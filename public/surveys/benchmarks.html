The Anatomy of a Benchmark

Every benchmark on the atlas — 126 evaluation papers spanning ImageNet (2009) to MCP-Atlas (2026) — classified along eight facets, then placed in a single taxonomy tree by the question each was built to answer, with lineage mined from the citation graph itself. Read one paper and you learn a benchmark; put all of them in one structure and you can see what no single paper shows — including nine connections that only appear across papers.

// the corpus

What counts as a benchmark here

The atlas tags a paper benchmarks when a named benchmark, evaluation dataset, or evaluation environment is its headline contribution — the thing a survey would cite it for. Models, training methods, and agent frameworks that merely report benchmark numbers don't qualify. That rubric yields 126 papers, every one of which has a full explainer on this site; this page is built from those explainers.

The corpus is curated, not exhaustive: it is centred on LLM, agent, and safety evaluation, with the classic CV and NLU generations (ImageNet, COCO, SQuAD, GLUE) present as ancestry rather than in full. 28 of the 126 also carry the evaluations tag — papers that shipped a new way to evaluate along with their dataset (Chatbot Arena's Elo protocol, HELM's scenario grid, FActScore's atomic-fact decomposition). Where the corpus skews, the findings inherit the skew; the method section spells this out.

// prior work

Two shelves of benchmark surveys, and the gap between them

Existing surveys of AI evaluation sit on two shelves that barely touch. The first shelf taxonomizes what benchmarks measure: Chang et al. (2023) organize the field by what / where / how to evaluate, Guo et al. (2023) by evaluated property (knowledge, alignment, safety, domain), and HELM (2022) — the most rigorous of the family — by a scenario × metric grid it then deliberately fills. The second shelf grades how well benchmarks are built: BetterBench (2024) scores 24 benchmarks on 46 lifecycle criteria, McIntosh et al. (2024) find 22 of 23 prominent benchmarks vulnerable on the same integrity grounds, Bean et al. (2025) audit construct validity across 445 benchmarks, and the Agentic Benchmark Checklist (2025) shows that 7 of 10 audited agentic benchmarks had unsound graders.

The agent-evaluation surveys that appeared in 2025 — Yehudai et al., Kapoor et al.'s AI Agents That Matter — added structural vocabulary (dataset vs. environment, offline vs. online, tool access) but each survey coins its own terms, and none quantifies the axes it names. Meanwhile the dimension that turns out to matter most for where benchmarks are going — how long the task is — was only made measurable by METR's time-horizon work (2025), and has never been applied to a benchmark corpus.

What no prior survey does is cross the shelves at paper level: place every benchmark in a capability map and record its grading machinery, task horizon, tool surface, provenance, and contamination posture — then look at the joint distribution over time. That cross is what this page contributes, on a corpus that is small (126) but densely annotated: every paper here has been read, summarized, and critiqued individually, and the underlying citation graph is browsable as a timeline. Two further anchors for what follows: Biderman et al.'s reproducibility lessons (2024) and the benchmark-saturation study (2026), which finds roughly half of all benchmarks saturated. This corpus's own pre-2021 cohort is saturated at a far higher rate — 82% (14 of 17) against 25% corpus-wide — and the gap is exactly what finding 07 predicts: saturation concentrates in short-horizon work, and every pre-2021 paper here poses an L1 or L2 task.

Defining “complexity”: the task horizon

“Complexity” is the word everyone reaches for and nobody defines. The literature operationalizes it at least five incommensurable ways — reasoning steps, compositional depth, context length, search-space size, IRT difficulty — none of which lets you compare a multiple-choice exam to a week of freelance software work. The definition used here is the one that does:

// definition

Task horizon — the wall-clock time a competent human domain-expert would need to complete one typical task instance, reading and artifact production included. It is the only complexity currency that spans the whole corpus, and it is the axis METR showed frontier-model ability climbs along at a measurable rate (a ~7-month doubling time). Five buckets: L1 <1 minute · L2 1–30 minutes · L3 30 minutes–1 day · L4 1 day–2 weeks · L5 >2 weeks.

The horizon ladder. Counts and canonical examples per rung, across all 126 papers. Only a handful of papers (GDPval, RE-Bench, the METR suites) measure human time; everywhere else the rung is estimated from the paper's own task descriptions — see method.

The six grading families

Every benchmark answers “who says the output is right?” with one of six mechanisms, and the choice is less methodological free will than economics: each step right on the ladder below buys generality — the ability to grade open-ended work — and pays for it in cost per item and in grader error that must itself be audited.

The grading-cost ladder. Ordered by what it costs to grade one item. The reliability annotations are from the papers themselves: MT-Bench measured GPT-4-judge/human agreement at 85% (humans agree with each other 81%); PaperBench's judge reaches F1 0.83 against expert grading; the ABC audit found unsound graders in 7 of 10 agentic benchmarks it checked.

Two structural notes. First, the left end of the ladder is what the RL-with-verifiable-rewards era trains on — a benchmark whose grader is mechanical is also a reward function, which means the most verifiable benchmarks are precisely the ones models are being optimized against (Goodhart exposure is a property of the grading family, not the dataset). Second, the model-graded middle created a regress the field is still funding: judges are now benchmarked by meta-benchmarks (JudgeBench, RewardBench), and rubric-based grading is the current attempt to make judge error inspectable by decomposing it into checkable criteria.

// the tree

A taxonomy by evolutionary role

Sorting benchmarks by what they test reproduces the domain facet; sorting them by why they exist produces a different — and more honest — structure. The placement question used here: “What question was this benchmark built to answer, in the ecosystem of models and benchmarks that existed at its birth?” Judged from each paper's own problem statement, that question has seven answers — seven kingdoms — and every one of the 126 papers sits at exactly one address.

The tree. Seven kingdoms, twenty-five families, 126 leaves. Bars are family sizes.

The tree's shape is itself a finding. Only 56 of 126 papers (44%) are capability probes — the thing "benchmark" is usually assumed to mean — and the share is falling: 64% of pre-2021 entries asked "can the model do X?", against 35% of 2024–25 entries. The majority of the modern corpus is doing something more reflexive: auditing other measurements (15), rehearsing deployments (23), probing hazards (14), building walls designed not to fall (7), manufacturing training signal (3), or working out how to evaluate at all (8). Benchmarking has become an ecosystem with predators, parasites, and decomposers — not a row of thermometers.

Placement is by role at birth, not later use — so HellaSwag sits in de-saturation sequels (it was built to resurrect a solved benchmark, and its adversarial-filtering construction matters more than its commonsense content — see connection 3), SWE-bench sits in program synthesis (the agents came later), and GSM8K sits in closed-form reasoning even though its deepest legacy is the verifier paradigm (connection 7). Hover any chip for the one-line placement reason; every chip links to the paper's explainer.

// findings

What the cross-tabs say

Seven findings, each read off a chart below. All numbers are shares of the 126-paper corpus by publication era; per-era counts are small enough that you should read slopes, not decimals.

finding 01

The grading waves — and the 2025 rubric turn

Headline grading mechanism by era. Eras pool small years (≤2018 n=10, 2019–20 n=7, 2021–22 n=17); single years from 2023 (n=18, 28, 39, 7).

Grading has moved in waves: proxy metrics gave way to exact-match (14 of 17 papers up to 2020), execution-based programmatic grading surged with the code benchmarks (8 of 18 in 2023), holistic LLM-judging peaked in 2024 — and then, in 2025, rubric-scored judging became the single largest grading family for new benchmarks: 14 of 39 papers (36%), up from two papers total before 2025 (FActScore 2023, Agent-as-a-Judge 2024). HealthBench, PaperBench, ResearchRubrics, FORTRESS, and the 2026 cohort (MCP-Atlas, SWE Atlas, HIL-Bench) all grade by expert-written, per-item criteria applied by a model.

The wave structure has a single driver: each new capability regime produced outputs the previous grader couldn't score. Exact match broke on generation; unit tests broke on anything that isn't code; holistic judges broke on expert domains where “sounds good” diverges from “is correct.” The rubric turn is the field's current answer — decompose expert judgment into criteria a model can check — and it is also a quiet confession that the thing we now want to measure no longer has an answer key.

finding 02

The verifiability frontier

Task horizon × grading family, all 126 papers. Hollow cells are empty. The deterministic families (exact match, programmatic) die out above L2; L4 is graded only by humans and rubrics.

Read the grid diagonally and a boundary appears: cheap grading does not survive long horizons. Exact-match grading owns L1 (21 of 24 papers) and thins to exactly two papers at L3 — and both are deliberate exceptions: BrowseComp engineered its questions to be hard-to-find but easy-to-verify, and TRAIL grades long agent traces against a fixed error taxonomy. Programmatic grading peaks at L2–L3 (unit tests, environment end-states) and vanishes at L4: nobody has built a test harness for “produce a competitive research paper.” Above the frontier, only judges, rubrics, and humans remain.

This is the survey's central claim: a benchmark can only pose tasks as long as it can afford to grade. Verification asymmetry — tasks that are hard to do but cheap to check — is the scarce resource of benchmark design, and the L4 row shows what happens when it runs out: GDPval pays experts for >1 hour of pairwise grading per item, PaperBench amortizes 33 person-weeks of rubric authoring. The grading-cost ladder from the taxonomy section is not a menu; at high horizons it is a budget constraint.

finding 03

The horizon climb — three rungs in seventeen years, top rung empty

Share of new benchmark papers per era posing L3+ tasks (≥30 expert-minutes per instance).

Benchmarks are climbing the ladder their subjects climb. Before 2021 no paper in the corpus posed a task above L2; the L3+ share was 6–11% through 2023–24, then jumped to 36% in 2025 and 5 of 7 papers in 2026. The mean rung moved from 1.2 (≤2018) to 2.7 (2026). That tracks the capability curve METR measured — as models' 50%-success horizon doubles every ~7 months, benchmarks below the frontier stop discriminating and the field re-builds one rung up.

But the climb is running out of ladder. L4 — day-to-week projects — contains exactly three papers (GDPval, PaperBench, RLI), all from 2025, and L5 is empty: no benchmark in this corpus poses work beyond two expert-weeks. The binding constraints are the two graders left at that altitude (finding 02) and item economics — a 1,000-item benchmark of week-long tasks is a thousand expert-weeks of ground truth. Whoever solves cheap verification of long-horizon work gets to write the first L5 benchmark, and probably the reward signal for the next training era with it.

finding 04

The interactive turn — and the missing middle

Task shape by era. Interactive environments: the model acts, the environment responds, repeat. Multi-turn: dialogue without an environment.

The corpus's first interactive environment is WebShop (2022). Three years later, interactive environments are 38% of new benchmarks (15 of 39 in 2025) and 6 of 7 in 2026 — the static single-turn dataset, 100% of the corpus through 2020, is on its way to being the minority format. This is the static→interactive migration the agent surveys narrate; the timeline lets you date it.

The anomaly is what got skipped: multi-turn dialogue is 7 papers out of 126 — MT-Bench, MathDial, the multi-turn jailbreak studies, MultiChallenge, AdvancedIF, Audio MultiChallenge — and never exceeds ~8% in any era. The field jumped from one-shot prompts straight to autonomous tool loops, mostly skipping the thing users actually do with these systems: talk to them, clarify, correct, change their minds mid-task. τ-bench-style simulated users exist inside a few agentic environments, but conversation as the object of evaluation remains a gap — the missing middle of task shape.

finding 05

Benchmarks stopped being scraped and started being commissioned

Provenance of task items by era. “Expert-authored” means items written or curated for the benchmark by recruited domain experts; “real-work-mined” means harvested from artifacts of real work (GitHub issues, freelance jobs, filed contracts).

Through 2018, benchmarks were built from what could be collected cheaply: crowdworkers and scraped exams (8 of 10 papers). By 2025, 54% of new benchmarks are expert-authored — physicians writing HealthBench criteria, IMO medalists writing FrontierMath-class problems, industry professionals with 14-year average experience writing GDPval deliverables — and another 15% are mined from real work products (SWE-bench's GitHub issues, SWE-Lancer's paid freelance jobs, RLI's real projects). The era of the annotation farm is over at the frontier.

The driver is the same as in finding 01: models passed the crowd. When the evaluated system outperforms a Mechanical Turk worker at the task, crowd-sourced ground truth stops being ground truth, and item-writing becomes skilled labor — which is why benchmark construction industrialized (Scale, Mercor, Surge are all in this corpus's author lists) and why item counts fall as horizons rise. The economics of expertise, not dataset fashion, is what moved this chart.

finding 06

The contamination cycle: the field forgot the private test set, then re-learned it

Strongest shipped contamination defense by era. “Private holdout” includes hidden test sets and evaluation servers; “live refresh” means post-cutoff or continuously regenerated items by design.

The most surprising row in the data is the oldest one. The CV era already had the answer: 7 of 10 pre-2018 papers shipped a hidden test set or an evaluation server (ILSVRC, COCO, VQA, SQuAD, GLUE's leaderboard). The web-scrape training era then abandoned it — 16 of 17 papers in 2021–22 published everything, MMLU and BIG-bench included, and the contamination crisis of 2023–24 (GSM1k measured up to 8-point inflation on GSM8k) was the bill for that amnesia. From 2024 the defenses return and diversify: private holdouts (15 of 39 in 2025), live refresh (LiveCodeBench, SciPredict's post-cutoff sourcing, Arena's user traffic), procedural generation (Reasoning Gym), canaries. In 2025, for the first time since 2018, more than half of new benchmarks shipped some defense; in 2026 it's 6 of 7.

The defenses also work differently well. Of the 8 live-refresh benchmarks, zero are saturated; private holdouts saturate at the corpus base rate (~26%) — a hidden test set stops leakage but not progress. Refresh-by-design is the only defense that also buys longevity, which is why the newest entries (SciPredict's “no paper before March 2025,” UPBench's live freelance feed) build refresh into the sampling frame rather than bolting privacy onto a frozen set.

finding 07

What saturates: horizon is destiny

Saturation status by task horizon, judged from the frontier scores each explainer quotes (mid-2026). Counts inside bars.

Saturation is almost perfectly ordered by the ladder: 62% of L1 benchmarks are saturated, 21% of L2, and 0% of L3 and above. Every saturated benchmark in this corpus poses tasks a human expert finishes inside half an hour. Domain matters less than you'd think and defense matters less than refresh (finding 06); what predicts whether a benchmark is still discriminating in 2026 is simply how much expert time its tasks embody.

That gives benchmark builders a clean actuarial rule — expected lifespan is bought in human-minutes per item — and it explains the corpus's own shape: the L1 rows are the field's history, the L2 rows its present workhorses (with a third already closing), and the L3–L4 rows, where every benchmark still has headroom, are where evaluation currently lives. The ladder from finding 03 isn't just being climbed; it's being consumed from the bottom.

// connections

Nine things no single paper shows

These were mined by tracing the corpus's internal citation graph (598 edges among the 126) against the full text of every explainer, then checking each claim's quotes and citation-structure assertions mechanically. Each is a connection you cannot see from inside any one paper.

connection 01 · the twin transplant

GSM1k is ImageNetV2 re-run on LLM math — and the method's flaw transplanted with it, sign-flipped

GSM1k (2024) is Recht et al.'s 2019 CV experiment — rebuild a benchmark's test set by replicating its creation process, measure the drop — transplanted to LLM arithmetic five years later; its explainer says the move is “borrowed straight” from the CIFAR/ImageNet rebuilds. What transplanted along with the method is its central hazard: a reconstructed twin never lands exactly on the original distribution. But the bias flipped sign in transit. ImageNetV2's construction mismatch inflated its headline drop — Engstrom et al. later showed roughly two-thirds of it was artifact — while GSM1k's twin came out slightly easier than GSM8k, so its measured overfitting is plausibly understated.

Same method, same flaw, opposite error bars — and, strikingly, the same meta-verdict about two different eras: rankings mostly transfer, progress is mostly real, and inflation concentrates in specific model families rather than the frontier.

connection 02 · the audit shadow

Every gravity well acquires an audit — and the lag is collapsing

Every benchmark that mattered in this corpus eventually acquired an audit, twin, or corrective sequel — and the wait has collapsed by an order of magnitude: ImageNet went unaudited for 10 years; MMLU, GSM8K, and HumanEval got 2–4; MT-Bench got one; MultiChallenge got its modality twin within months. A benchmark's expected unaudited lifetime is now shorter than a typical review-to-camera-ready cycle, which means every headline number carries a known depreciation schedule.

The mechanism of the shrink is visible in who runs the audits: early ones were third-party forensics (Berkeley auditing ImageNet), but by 2024–25 the shadow moved in-house and turned pre-emptive — OpenAI hardened its own SimpleQA into BrowseComp, LMSYS distilled its own Arena into Arena-Hard, Scale extended its own MultiChallenge into audio. Quality control became contemporaneous with publication.

connection 03 · the filter-model trap

HellaSwag's construction step became the 2024–25 standard — its documented failure mode got re-derived from scratch each time

Filter-by-model-failure — keep only items a reference model gets wrong — is HellaSwag's 2019 adversarial-filtering recipe, and it is the load-bearing construction step of three flagship evals in three domains: MMLU-Pro drops questions that 5+ of an 8-model panel answer correctly, HLE admits an item only if frontier models already fail it (70,000+ logged attempts), and Arena-Hard's BenchBuilder keeps only prompts a GPT-4 annotator scores ≥6. The lineage is contiguous in the citation graph — MMLU-Pro and Arena-Hard cite HellaSwag, HLE cites MMLU-Pro — yet none of the three names adversarial filtering.

The method propagated while its caveat got rediscovered independently each time: difficulty defined against a reference model is not intrinsic difficulty, so each benchmark is guaranteed to be climbed by anything stronger than its filter. HellaSwag's own ablation said so in 2019, and each descendant aged exactly as predicted — HellaSwag's “100,000 GPU-years to human level” fell in four years; HLE's sub-10% launch scores were tripled within months.

connection 04 · the pendulum forks

Web-agent evaluation swings between realism and gradability — and on the third swing it split in two

Each paper in the web lineage names the previous pole's grading failure as its motivation. Mind2Web (2023) buys exact-match gradability with static snapshots and pays in false negatives; WebVoyager (2024) swings to the live web with a GPT-4V judge and pays in irreproducibility and judge error. Then the retreat to verifiability happens twice, in incompatible directions: OSWorld keeps a live machine but gives up the open web (a controlled VM with per-task execution checks), while BrowseComp keeps the open web but gives up realistic tasks (questions inverted until the answer is a short verifiable string).

The pendulum doesn't return to one point — it forks, each branch sacrificing a different slice of realism. That fork is why GUI-agent evaluation and deep-research evaluation, both descended from a WebVoyager-shaped ancestor, no longer look related.

connection 05 · grader recursion

The same judging machinery scores ~90% at verdicts and 11% at explanations

Agent-as-a-Judge's machinery — hierarchical requirements checked one by one by an agentic judge that reads the workspace — was absorbed as PaperBench's rubric-tree grading infrastructure (which cites it). Then TRAIL (which also cites it) inverted the paradigm: the judge became the benchmarked system, asked not to check requirements but to localize faults inside real agent traces — and the ~90% human-alignment success story collapsed to 11% joint accuracy.

Verdict-checking and fault-explanation look like the same skill from a distance. The citation chain documents the field discovering, one paper at a time, that they are not — with direct implications for how much rubric-graded scores (finding 01) can be trusted when the rubric items get harder than the checkable kind.

connection 06 · the rubric fork

Per-task rubrics were invented twice in three months — and the two branches disagree about what the expensive part buys

2025's rubric wave is usually told as one movement; the citation graph splits it. PaperBench (April) and HealthBench (May) are genuine descent — linked papers, same lab, expert-written per-instance criteria (author-co-written rubric trees; 262 physicians). DeepResearch Bench's RACE (June) cites neither and is cited by neither: an independent convergence on per-task criteria that resolved the cost problem the opposite way — the judge writes its own criteria at evaluation time.

ResearchRubrics then ran the experiment that adjudicates the fork: letting an LLM augment expert rubrics degrades human alignment by 15–20%, while RACE's own ablation concedes its adaptive judge-written criteria buy only ~3 points over static ones. Expert-grounded versus judge-generated rubrics is now a falsifiable methodological fight between rival benchmarks — grader design has become an experimental science with adversaries.

connection 07 · the benchmark is the reward

The training flip was there from the start — and contamination travels through graders, not just test sets

The canonical benchmarks were never eval-only artifacts. GSM8K shipped as verifier-training infrastructure — a 7.5K train split plus a learned verifier, with the launch paper already documenting the verifier being reward-hacked. HumanEval arrived inside the Codex training paper with unit tests doubling as a sample-selection oracle; SWE-bench shipped 19K training instances and its own fine-tunes. When GSM1k audited the ecosystem, its most telling overfit case was contaminated through the grader lineage — a process-reward model leaking reasoning chains — with no test-set leak an n-gram checker could see.

Validity then had to be re-purchased, each time by moving the grader further from the training corpus: EvalPlus densified HumanEval's tests 80×; SWE-bench Pro bought commercial code models could never have seen — and its ~25–30-point public→commercial collapse, same models, same harness, is the field's cleanest single measurement of what training against a public grader family costs. By 2025 the flip is doctrine: MLGym's complaint about prior benchmarks is that they can't be trained on, and Reasoning Gym publishes its reward function. Goodhart doesn't disappear under procedural generation — it migrates into the grader's blind spots.

connection 08 · gravity selects portability

APPS was bigger, denser-tested, and tiered — HumanEval got the 43 citations. Wells get renovated, never abandoned

APPS and HumanEval are same-year, mutually-citing, execution-graded code benchmarks; APPS was 60× larger with twice the tests per problem. HumanEval became this corpus's biggest gravity well (43 in-corpus citations to APPS's 12) because what the field reused was the measurement kit — the unbiased pass@k estimator plus 164 cheap, self-contained tasks scoring in the discriminative range — while APPS's grader vocabulary failed to travel (its lenient metric flattered; its strict metric floored at 0.00% on the whole Competition tier). The clincher: when EvalPlus proved HumanEval's thin tests inflate pass@k by up to ~29%, the field patched HumanEval in place rather than migrate to APPS — which already had denser tests.

The pattern repeats one generation down: SWE-bench Pro's forkable Docker harness is already substrate for third-generation benchmarks, while SWE-Lancer's more rigorous but unforkable grader (triple-verified end-to-end tests on one proprietary repo) has no coding descendants — its framing (real-dollar difficulty) instead seeded the economic-rehearsal family (GDPval, RLI, UpBench). Benchmark genes travel through cheap reproducible harnesses; the ecosystem structurally selects reproducibility over rigor.

connection 09 · the noiseless human

AlpacaFarm proved a simulated human must be noisy to be faithful — the lineage then built noiseless ones

AlpacaFarm's deepest result is that fidelity means reproducing human imperfection: it rejected a single GPT-4 judge with higher raw agreement in favor of 13 disagreeing judges plus deliberate label noise, because only the noisy simulator reproduces reward over-optimization — the phenomenon the sandbox existed to study. The later simulated humans quietly inverted this. MathDial's simulated student errs in GPT-3.5's failure modes, not children's; by HiL-Bench (2026) the simulated collaborator is explicitly an oracle — instant, correct, and free — which its own authors concede makes every reported number a ceiling-free floor.

A fake human that never lies, stalls, or charges cannot surface the failure modes that only human noise reveals — the exact category of blindness AlpacaFarm demonstrated in 2023. The founding fidelity lesson of the simulated-human lineage was traded away, one convenience at a time.

// method & caveats

How this was built, and what to distrust

Corpus. The 126 papers are the atlas's benchmarks survey tag — the same set you see ringed on the timeline map. Membership follows the tag rubric (benchmark as headline contribution), applied when each paper joined the atlas. The corpus over-represents what the atlas covers deeply (LLM, agent, and safety evaluation, 2023–2026) and thins out toward classic CV/NLU; era-level trends should be read as trends within this kind of corpus, not a census of all benchmarks ever built. The corpus is papers only: the 2026 model-card canon also includes non-paper instruments this page doesn't cover — SWE-bench Verified, Aider Polyglot, AIME-as-eval, the ARC-AGI leaderboards, and needle-in-a-haystack probes among them — an exclusion of form, not a judgment that they don't matter.

Classification. Each paper was classified from its atlas explainer (and the paper itself where the explainer underdetermined a dimension) by a language-model pipeline: one full-schema pass over every paper, then a fully independent second pass re-classifying the six judgment-heavy dimensions with different paper groupings. The two passes agreed on 94.8% of the 750 compared values; all 39 disagreements (on 32 papers) were adjudicated by a third read of the source text. Saturation was the noisiest dimension (16 of 39 conflicts) — treat individual saturation calls as softer than the aggregate.

Tree placement. Each paper's kingdom/family address was assigned by the same two-pass protocol, judging role-at-birth from the paper's own problem statement, with lineage restricted to citation edges that exist inside the corpus (598 edges among the 126). The independent second pass covered 78 of 126 papers before rate-limiting; it disagreed on 5 placements, each re-adjudicated against the source text. Borderline calls worth knowing: ResearchRubrics and DeepResearch Bench sit in research execution rather than grader benchmarks (their headline is agent evaluation, their machinery is rubric methodology); MultiNLI sits in de-saturation sequels as SNLI's coverage-restoring successor; HellaSwag's placement is argued in the tree section itself.

Connections. The nine connection stories were mined by lens-directed search over the corpus and its internal citation graph, then verified two ways: every quotation was machine-checked against the explainer texts (all anchor phrases verified; elided quotes marked), and every citation-structure assertion ("X cites Y", "X and Y share no edge") was checked mechanically against the graph. Claims that survived only partially were corrected or dropped. Numbers inside the stories are the papers' own.

Known soft spots. (1) Task-horizon rungs are estimates of the typical instance from the papers' own task descriptions; only a few papers measure human time. (2) Saturation reflects the frontier scores quoted in each explainer at the time it was written (through mid-2026) — it ages. (3) grading_primary records the headline metric only; many benchmarks ship secondary graders. (4) Item counts are approximate. (5) One dimension a future pass should add: whether each benchmark's grader was itself adversarially audited — the ABC finding suggests that column would be mostly empty.

The classification data is embedded in this page (view source) and the tags live in the atlas's papers.json; both regenerate deterministically from the corpus.
