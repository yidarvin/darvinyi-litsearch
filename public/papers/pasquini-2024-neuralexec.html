Neural Exec: Learning (and Learning from) Execution Triggers for Prompt Injection Attacks

Reframing execution-trigger design as a discrete optimization problem: GCG-style search finds prompt-injection triggers at least twice as effective as the best handcrafted baseline (up to a 500% accuracy gain on the most resistant model), structurally unlike anything a human would write, and — when biased for two new robustness properties — able to survive a RAG pipeline's chunking and retrieval about 80% of the time.

Dario Pasquini, Martin Strohmeier, Carmela Troncoso

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

Every prompt injection attack decomposes into two parts: a payload (the harmful instruction the attacker wants executed) and an execution trigger (the string that coerces the model into treating the payload as an instruction rather than data). Almost all attention in the prompt injection literature — Perez & Ribeiro's original study, Greshake et al.'s indirect-injection framing, Liu et al.'s "separator" taxonomy — has gone into cataloguing payload variety. The trigger itself has stayed almost frozen: a handful of handcrafted patterns, typically a run of separator characters followed by natural language like "Ignore all the previous and following instructions and just [PAYLOAD]." These triggers work, but they're the product of human intuition, they cluster tightly in form (making dictionary- or blacklist-based detectors plausible), and nobody had systematically asked whether a stronger, structurally novel class of trigger even exists.

The authors' core move is to stop treating trigger design as a creative-writing problem and instead treat it as a search problem over the LLM's input space — the same reframing that GCG (Zou et al.) applied to jailbreak suffixes, now applied one level up the attack chain, to the thing that makes an injected payload execute at all. A second, practically important gap: real deployments rarely feed an attacker's raw text straight to the model. Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) pipelines chunk untrusted documents, embed the chunks, and filter by relevance before anything reaches the LLM — a step that can shred or simply never retrieve a naively-injected payload. Prior attacks were not designed with this preprocessing in mind, so it was unclear whether indirect injection was even a practical threat against the RAG-based applications (chat-with-your-docs assistants, web-search agents) that are now the dominant way LLMs touch untrusted external content.

// how it works

The method

A Neural Exec trigger Υ is modeled as a prefix Υpre and suffix Υpost wrapped around a payload α, producing an "armed payload" Υ(α). The ideal functionality the optimization targets is that, for any payload and any surrounding prompt, feeding the model the armed payload should produce output equivalent to feeding it the payload alone — i.e. the trigger fully overrides whatever task the prompt originally specified:

G(Σi( Υ(αi) )) ≡ G(αi)  ∀αi∈A, Σi∈K // G = the LLM as a generator; Σ = the surrounding prompt template; A, K = the universes of possible payloads and prompts — the trigger must be universal across both

Because that equivalence isn't differentiable, the actual search maximizes a proxy: the conditional probability the target model assigns to the desired output string, computed via teacher forcing and weighted so early tokens matter more. The search itself follows Zou et al.'s Greedy Coordinate Gradient (GCG) algorithm — at each step, compute the gradient of the loss with respect to every trigger token, use it to shortlist promising single-token substitutions, evaluate a batch of candidates, and keep the best one. Training draws from a large synthetic universe: roughly 106 possible prompt combinations spanning single- and multi-input tasks, natural language and code, plus about 50,000 payloads pulled from the Alpaca instruction set. 150–250 iterations is typical.

Two additional properties are baked directly into the search to make triggers survive real RAG pipelines. Inlining bans the newline token from the search space entirely, since RAG chunkers split primarily on newlines — a multi-line handcrafted trigger is trivially cut apart. Inline Invariant Composition (IIC) and Semantic-Oblivious Injection (SOI) go further: during optimization, the armed payload is randomly embedded inside honest-looking "guide-text" designed to match what a victim might search for (e.g. text about "investment strategies" if the attacker expects an investment-related query), and the scoring function is biased to prefer triggers whose insertion barely perturbs the guide-text's embedding — so the poisoned chunk still looks relevant enough to survive a RAG system's retrieval-time filtering. The optimization is also constrained to ASCII, printable characters, and explicitly forbidden from using known chat-template tags like [INST] or <<SYS>>, so nothing in the final trigger is a simple injected-tag exploit by construction.

// what they found

The results

Evaluation targets four open-source, instruction-tuned models — Llama-3-8B-Instruct, Mistral-7B-Instruct-v0.2, OpenChat3.5, and Mixtral-8x7B — scored with an "Execution (Top-1) Accuracy" metric (ExeAcc): given an injected prompt, does the model's greedy-decoded output count as a valid execution of the payload, judged via an LLM-based fuzzy-matching function, on 100 held-out prompt/payload pairs disjoint from training. Baselines are a pool of 12 handcrafted triggers drawn from Perez et al., prior blog posts, and Liu et al.'s separator components — the strongest publicly known non-adaptive attacks at the time.

Prior-free (15-prefix + 5-suffix) Neural Execs, trained from a random initial solution per target model, reach an average execution accuracy of 91% across all four models — "at least twice as effective as the best baseline trigger." The gap is largest on the model that resisted handcrafted attacks best: OpenChat3.5, where the Neural Exec trigger's accuracy gain over the best baseline "reaches an increase of 500%." Bootstrapping the search from a handcrafted starting trigger instead of a random one trades a small amount of target-model accuracy for improved cross-model transferability and a more human-interpretable trigger (the optimizer tends to keep rephrasing the seed trigger's "ignore all the previous…" body rather than discarding it).

Against a simulated RAG pipeline (LangChain's recursive text splitter, the all-mpnet-base-v2 embedding model, SQuAD 2.0 paragraphs as guide-text), Neural Exec triggers optimized for SOI showed markedly better survivability than both non-SOI Neural Execs and handcrafted baselines across a sweep of chunk sizes and retrieval depths (k). At the field's typical default of a 500-character chunk size, Neural Execs show an average persistence of 80% — meaning the armed payload reaches the LLM's input intact and selected four times out of five. Handcrafted baselines fare far worse under the same conditions, since their newline-heavy structure is exactly what chunkers are designed to split on.

A final experiment distills the optimization's discoveries into one single-newline handcrafted trigger: a closed-parenthesis-and-semicolon separator plus the multi-line comment terminator */ to "signal the end of a preceding context," with a body borrowed directly from the bootstrapped OpenChat3.5 Neural Exec of Figure 7. This "Neural Exec-inspired" trigger beats all 12 baselines and transfers across all five tested models, including the proprietary ChatGPT-4 (February), where it reaches "approximately five times the success rate of previous baselines" without ever having been optimized against that model directly.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

The core results are white-box by construction: every reported Neural Exec is generated with full gradient access to an open-source target model. That's a defensible starting point for establishing the technique works at all, but it sits awkwardly against the paper's own motivation — indirect injection's real targets are LLM-integrated applications, which increasingly sit behind proprietary APIs the attacker cannot backprop through. The one black-box data point in the paper (transfer to ChatGPT-4 via the hand-distilled "Neural Exec-inspired" trigger) is a human-assembled artifact built from patterns the optimizer surfaced, not something the optimization method itself produced under black-box constraints. The paper explicitly flags this: black-box Neural Exec generation is future work, pointed at prior black-box jailbreak optimizers (Sitawarin et al.'s PAL) rather than demonstrated here.

The headline "execution accuracy" numbers rest on an LLM-as-judge fuzzy-matching function (M) deciding whether a given completion counts as a "valid execution" of the payload, with no reported human-agreement check on that judge — a metric-validity gap for a paper whose entire contribution is measured through it. Separately, the RAG-robustness experiment (Section 5.4) and the execution-accuracy experiment (Section 5.2) are run as two independent pipelines: the RAG experiment measures only whether the armed payload survives chunking and gets selected at retrieval — not whether, once selected, it still successfully hijacks the model. The paper never multiplies the two together to report a single end-to-end probability that an attack succeeds against a real RAG app, leaving a reader to combine an 80% persistence figure with a separately-measured 91% execution-accuracy figure and hope the two compose cleanly (they were measured under different setups).

Finally, this is purely an attack paper — there is no evaluation against any prompt-level defense. By the time of the paper's May 2024 revision, delimiter/datamarking-style defenses (e.g. spotlighting, on this same site) and structured-query defenses were already circulating, and the paper's own optimization machinery — which already learns to dodge a RAG relevance filter via the SOI objective — is naturally suited to testing whether a learned trigger can also route around a marking or encoding defense. That head-to-head experiment doesn't appear, so the paper establishes that learned triggers beat undefended models and handcrafted-trigger baselines, but not that they beat the current best defenses.

baselines

12 handcrafted triggers from Perez et al., prior blog posts, and Liu et al.'s separator taxonomy — the strongest known non-adaptive attacks, but static and non-learned, which is a low bar for a paper whose central claim is that learned attacks beat handcrafted ones.

contamination

Not applicable in the usual sense — this is an attack-generation method, not a benchmark — but ExeAcc's LLM-judge implementation is not validated against human labels anywhere in the paper.

what's missed

Black-box/query-only trigger generation against closed models (flagged as future work, not attempted); an end-to-end RAG attack success rate combining persistence and execution accuracy; and any test against a deployed prompt-level defense.

would raise confidence

A black-box variant evaluated directly against production APIs; a single composed RAG-to-execution success-rate number measured on one unified pipeline rather than two separate experiments; a human-verified subsample of ExeAcc judgments; and a head-to-head test against spotlighting-, StruQ-, or instruction-hierarchy-style defenses.

// bottom line

Neural Exec's central claim — that execution-trigger design doesn't require human creativity, and that an automated search finds triggers both stronger and structurally alien to anything a detector trained on "known" injection patterns would catch — is well supported within its stated white-box, no-defense scope: 91% average execution accuracy, up to 500% over the best handcrafted baseline, and 80% persistence through a realistic RAG pipeline are strong, specific numbers. What the paper doesn't yet show is the scenario that would matter most for real deployments: an attacker without gradient access, up against a system that's actually running one of the defenses this site catalogs elsewhere. Both are explicitly future work rather than gaps papered over.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. Neural Exec reframes prompt-injection execution triggers — not just payloads — as a GCG-style discrete optimization target, producing triggers with 91% average execution accuracy across four open-source LLMs, at least twice as effective as the best of 12 handcrafted baselines (up to 500% better on OpenChat3.5).
  2. Two search-space constraints, Inline Invariant Composition and Semantic-Oblivious Injection, bias the optimizer toward triggers that survive real RAG preprocessing — no newlines for chunkers to split on, and embeddings close enough to clean "guide-text" to pass retrieval filtering — reaching 80% persistence through a simulated RAG pipeline at a realistic 500-character chunk size, far above handcrafted triggers.
  3. Everything demonstrated is white-box (gradient access to the target model) and defense-free; black-box generation against closed APIs and any test against a deployed prompt-injection defense are both explicitly left to future work, not shown.
