UnifiedQA: Crossing Format Boundaries With a Single QA System

One prefix-free text-to-text model, trained on four QA formats at once, matches eight format-specific experts across 20 datasets — and generalizes to unseen datasets well enough to beat fully-supervised state of the art on some of them.

Daniel Khashabi, Sewon Min, Tushar Khot, Ashish Sabharwal, Oyvind Tafjord, Peter Clark, Hannaneh Hajishirzi · Allen Institute for AI & University of Washington

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

By 2020, question answering had splintered into format silos. Extractive QA (SQuAD) wanted a span copied out of a paragraph; multiple-choice QA (ARC, RACE) wanted a letter chosen from candidates; yes/no QA (BoolQ) wanted a boolean; abstractive QA (NarrativeQA) wanted free-form text the paragraph never literally contains. Each format grew its own datasets, its own leaderboards, and — crucially — its own model architectures, with the format often hard-wired into the network: a span-pointer head here, a classification head there.

Efforts to train on more than one dataset almost always stayed inside a single format. MultiQA studied transfer only across extractive sets, and even those leave-one-out systems lagged dataset-specific models. The authors argue this division is artificial. The linguistic understanding a QA system needs — resolve a coreference, take a small arithmetic step, weigh a piece of commonsense — doesn't depend on whether the answer is finally delivered as a span, a letter, or a "yes." If that's true, a multiple-choice model should be able to learn from an extractive dataset. Yet, to the authors' knowledge, no single system targeted, let alone excelled at, all four formats at once.

// how it works

The method

UnifiedQA throws away the format-specific machinery and casts every QA instance as plain text in, plain text out, building on the text-to-text framework of T5 (and, in a second variant, BART). The encoding is deliberately human-like: the question always comes first, then any context paragraph and/or candidate answers, joined by literal \n separators. A multiple-choice item becomes question \n (A) c₁ (B) c₂ … [\n paragraph]; everything else is just question \n paragraph.

The load-bearing design choice is what's absent: unlike T5, UnifiedQA uses no task-, dataset-, or format-specific prefixes. The model is never told "this is multiple choice" or "extract a span" — it has to infer the format from the wording of the question itself (whether the phrasing demands a yes/no, whether candidate answers are present). Narrowing the scope from T5's grab-bag of NLP tasks down to the single coherent space of QA is exactly what lets the prefixes go: prior multi-task work, including T5's own, found that mixing very different tasks into one model usually underperformed a model-per-task.

Training is a simple balanced mix. All training instances from all eight seed datasets are pooled, and each instance is drawn with probability inversely proportional to its dataset's size — so every batch carries, on average, the same number of examples from each dataset, whether it has 1.4k or 130k training items.

P(draw q ∈ Tj)  ∝  1|Tj| // balanced mixing — each dataset contributes equally per batch, large or small

The eight seed datasets were picked, via a pilot study, to put a strong representative of each format in the pot: SQuAD 1.1 and SQuAD 2.0 (extractive), NarrativeQA (abstractive), RACE, ARC, OpenBookQA, MCTest (multiple-choice), and BoolQ (yes/no). The headline model fine-tunes the largest 11-billion-parameter T5; a smaller BART-large variant is also reported. Evaluation spans 20 datasets across the four formats, scored with each format's customary metric: token-level F1 for extractive, ROUGE-L for abstractive (exact match for open-domain NaturalQuestions), accuracy via the closest candidate by token overlap for multiple-choice, and yes/no label matching for boolean.

// what they found

The results

Three results carry the paper.

(1) One model ≈ eight experts. A single UnifiedQA performs on par with — and on average slightly above — an ensemble of nine equally-sized, T5-based models each fine-tuned on its own dataset (§6.1). That a single multi-format model doesn't pay the usual multi-task tax is the first surprise.

(2) Zero-shot generalization that beats supervised SOTA. Evaluated on 12 unseen datasets of seen formats, UnifiedQA averages 60.7, far above single-format siblings trained the same way — UnifiedQA[AB] 45.8, [MC] 42.3, [EX] 38.1, [YN] 24.2 — and it wins on 9 of 12 datasets against any single-format expert (Table 4). On several of these never-seen sets it even out-scores the fully-supervised previous best: ROPES 67.0 vs 61.1, ROPES-CS 45.5 vs 32.5, NP-BoolQ 81.3 vs 78.4, BoolQ-CS 80.4 vs 71.1.

(3) A better starting point for fine-tuning. Fine-tuning UnifiedQA, rather than vanilla T5/BART, sets a new state of the art on 10 datasets (§6.3), improving over fine-tuned T5 by 1% (CommonsenseQA) to as much as 13% (ARC-challenge): OBQA 86.0 vs prior best 75.7, ARC-easy 86.4 vs 69.9, ARC-challenge 75.0 (78.5 with retrieval) vs 55.9.

Underneath, a pilot study confirms the premise directly: adding an out-of-format dataset to a single anchor helps in nearly every case — training BoolQ jointly with SQuAD 2.0 lifts NP-BoolQ from 51.3 to 59.4. NarrativeQA turns out to be the single most useful donor, helping multiple formats; BoolQ the least. A leave-one-out ablation drives the lesson home: dropping BoolQ from the mix costs 8.4 average points (the whole yes/no format collapses), while dropping SQuAD 1.1 costs essentially nothing — it's redundant with SQuAD 2.0. What matters is having a representative of each format, not piling on data.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

The headline that a zero-shot model beats fully-supervised state of the art deserves a careful read, because two things are varied at once: multi-format training and raw scale. UnifiedQA is the 11B-parameter T5; many of the "previous best" baselines it leapfrogs are RoBERTa/ALBERT-class models two orders of magnitude smaller. The clean, scale-controlled comparison is UnifiedQA against its own single-format siblings — same 11B backbone, same recipe — and there the win is real but more modest (9 of 12 datasets, +14.9 average over the best single-format variant). The SOTA-beating framing rides partly on model size, a point the paper is honest about but the abstract softens.

The generalization story is also entangled with domain overlap, not just format transfer. The seed sets (SQuAD, RACE, ARC, OBQA) and the "unseen" sets (CommonsenseQA, QASC, ROPES, the BoolQ contrast sets) draw on overlapping sources — Wikipedia, grade-school science exams, the same crowd-sourcing pipelines. The paper itself notes single-format experts generalize best "only when the source and target datasets are very similar (SQuAD/Quoref)," which concedes that proximity, not pure format-agnosticism, drives much of the transfer. And nothing in the evaluation reaches a genuinely new format (e.g., closed-book direct-answer), so "format-agnostic" really means "agnostic across these four."

Finally, the scoring is lenient by construction. Multiple-choice accuracy matches the free-form generation to "the closest answer candidate based on token overlap," and yes/no is scored by checking the output "contains the correct label but not the incorrect one" — both forgiving relative to exact match, while abstractive answers lean on ROUGE-L, a weak proxy with no human check. Reproducibility, too, is gated on the 11B model and Google TPUs: the BART-large variant is markedly weaker (ARC-challenge 52.1 vs 75.0), so most of the magic needs the big model.

baselines

Internally fair vs same-size T5 experts (UnifiedQA wins on average); but the "beats supervised SOTA" claim pits an 11B model against ~300M-param RoBERTa/ALBERT baselines — scale is a confound.

contamination

No train/test instance leakage, but seed and "unseen" sets share sources (Wikipedia, science exams), so the generalization is partly domain proximity, not pure cross-format transfer — which the paper half-concedes.

what's missed

Generalization is only to unseen datasets of seen formats; lenient MC/YN matching and ROUGE-L for abstractive can flatter the scores; no genuinely new format is tested.

would raise confidence

Hold model size fixed across every comparison; evaluate on a held-out format the model never saw; replace token-overlap/ROUGE-L with exact-match or human judgement on the free-form outputs.

// bottom line

The core claim — that QA's format boundaries are artificial, and a single prefix-free text-to-text model can span all four while losing nothing to dedicated experts — is well supported by the scale-controlled comparisons and the leave-one-out ablation. The flashier "zero-shot beats supervised SOTA" line is real but leans on an 11B model and domain overlap; read it as "big model + diverse QA training generalizes remarkably," not as proof that format alone was the barrier. As a recipe and a starting checkpoint, UnifiedQA earned its influence.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. Format is not destiny. Casting extractive, abstractive, multiple-choice and yes/no QA into one prefix-free text-to-text interface lets a single model learn from all of them and match eight format-specific experts — undoing the usual multi-task penalty by keeping the task space coherent.
  2. Out-of-format data genuinely transfers. A balanced mix where each dataset contributes equally per batch generalizes to 12 unseen datasets (avg 60.7 vs ≤45.8 for any single-format variant), and a leave-one-out ablation shows you want one representative per format, not more data — dropping BoolQ costs 8.4 points, dropping redundant SQuAD 1.1 costs nothing.
  3. An influential precursor to "everything is text." UnifiedQA became a default QA starting checkpoint and a conceptual stepping-stone toward instruction-following, prompt-unified models (T0, FLAN, InstructGPT) — but its "beats supervised SOTA zero-shot" headline rests on an 11B model and source overlap, so credit the scale and diversity, not format-unification alone.
