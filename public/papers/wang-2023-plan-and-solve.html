Plan-and-Solve Prompting: Improving Zero-Shot Chain-of-Thought Reasoning by Large Language Models

Zero-shot-CoT's "let's think step by step" gets an LLM reasoning without hand-written examples — but it still drops steps and botches arithmetic. Plan-and-Solve swaps in a two-part trigger — devise a plan, then carry it out — and closes much of that gap with no extra exemplars at all.

Lei Wang, Wanyu Xu, Yihuai Lan, Zhiqiang Hu, Yunshi Lan, Roy Ka-Wei Lee, Ee-Peng Lim · SMU Singapore & SUTD

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

Few-shot chain-of-thought (CoT) prompting (Wei et al., 2022) gets LLMs to write out intermediate reasoning steps before an answer, but it needs a handful of hand-crafted, step-by-step demonstration examples for every task. Zero-shot-CoT (Kojima et al., 2022) removed that manual cost entirely — just append "Let's think step by step" to the question — and, remarkably, recovered most of few-shot CoT's benefit on arithmetic reasoning.

But Zero-shot-CoT is not free of failure modes. The authors sample 100 GSM8K problems Zero-shot-CoT with GPT-3 got wrong and manually bucket the errors: 7% are calculation errors (arithmetic slips), 12% are missing-step errors (the model skips a necessary intermediate step, especially on longer problems), and 27% are semantic-misunderstanding errors (the model misreads what the problem is actually asking). The generic trigger "let's think step by step" invites free-form reasoning but never explicitly asks the model to plan out the subtasks first — so nothing forces it to notice a missing step before it's too late. The paper's target is specifically the missing-step failure mode: can a differently-worded, still-zero-shot trigger make the model decompose the task before it starts solving it?

// how it works

The method

Plan-and-Solve (PS) prompting keeps Zero-shot-CoT's exact two-step recipe — one prompt generates a reasoning trace, a second prompt extracts the final answer from it — and changes only the trigger sentence embedded in the first prompt. Where Zero-shot-CoT appends "Let's think step by step," PS appends: "Let's first understand the problem and devise a plan to solve the problem. Then, let's carry out the plan and solve the problem step by step." That's the entire method: no exemplars, no fine-tuning, no external tools — just asking the model, in the trigger itself, to produce a plan before executing it.

To also address calculation errors, the authors extend the trigger with two more explicit instructions — "extract relevant variables and their corresponding numerals" and "calculate intermediate results (pay attention to calculation and commonsense)" — producing the PS+ variant. The extraction instruction is motivated by a correlation check (Figure 5 in the paper): across 100 sampled GSM8K predictions, whether the generated text contains an explicit "Variables" section correlates negatively with both calculation errors (r = −0.41) and missing-step errors (r = −0.56), while an explicit "Plan" section correlates even more strongly with fewer missing-step errors (r = −0.83).

Everything runs on GPT-3 (text-davinci-003) with greedy decoding (temperature 0), evaluated across ten datasets spanning three reasoning categories: six arithmetic-word-problem sets (MultiArith, GSM8K, AddSub, AQuA, SingleEq, SVAMP), two commonsense sets (CommonsenseQA, StrategyQA), and two symbolic sets (Last Letter Concatenation, Coin Flip). Baselines are Zero-shot-CoT, Zero-shot Program-of-Thought (PoT, which has the LLM write and execute a Python program instead of prose), and two few-shot references (8-shot Manual-CoT and automatically-selected Auto-CoT).

// what they found

The results

On the six arithmetic datasets, PS+ improves over Zero-shot-CoT by at least 5 points on every dataset except GSM8K, which sees a smaller +2.9-point gain (56.4% → 59.3%) — the paper attributes this to GSM8K's greater linguistic complexity. PS alone (without the extra calculation instructions) is more modest, at +2.5 points average accuracy over Zero-shot-CoT (70.4% → 72.9%). PS+ also beats the Zero-shot-PoT baseline on five of the six arithmetic datasets.

The headline framing is that zero-shot PS+ "has comparable performance with 8-shot CoT prompting" — its 76.7% average sits just below Manual-CoT's 77.6%, and PS+ actually beats Manual-CoT outright on GSM8K (59.3 vs 58.4), AddSub (92.2 vs 91.6), and SingleEq (94.7 vs 93.5), while trailing on the other three. On commonsense reasoning, PS+ beats Zero-shot-CoT on both CSQA (71.9% vs 65.2%) and StrategyQA (65.4% vs 63.8%) but still trails Few-shot-CoT (78.3%, 71.2%). On symbolic reasoning, PS+ beats both Zero-shot-CoT and Manual-CoT on Last Letter (75.2% vs 64.8% vs 70.6%), but on Coin Flip it edges out Zero-shot-CoT (99.6% vs 96.8%) while falling just short of Manual-CoT's perfect 100.0%.

Layering self-consistency (SC — sample N=10 at temperature 0.7, majority-vote the answer) on top improves both methods substantially and preserves PS+'s edge: on GSM8K, PS+ w/ SC reaches 73.7% versus CoT w/ SC's 70.7%; on SVAMP, PS+ w/ SC reaches 84.4% versus CoT w/ SC's 81.7%.

A separate error-type breakdown on the same 100 sampled GSM8K problems shows PS+ cutting the incorrect-answer count from 46/100 (Zero-shot-CoT) to 39/100, with calculation errors down to 5% and missing-step errors down to 7% (from 7%/12% for Zero-shot-CoT) — semantic-misunderstanding errors stay roughly flat at 27% for both, which the paper doesn't address since it's a comprehension failure a differently-worded trigger can't easily fix. A follow-up manual check found that 90 of 100 sampled PS predictions actually contained an explicit plan in the output.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

The comparison set is reasonable for its moment — Zero-shot-CoT, Zero-shot-PoT, Manual-CoT, and Auto-CoT were the standing zero/few-shot prompting baselines in early-to-mid 2023 — but every number in the paper comes from a single model family, OpenAI's text-davinci-003, with greedy (temperature-0) decoding and, as far as the paper documents, a single run per dataset per method outside the dedicated self-consistency ablation. There's no error bar, no variance across seeds, and no second model (open-weight or otherwise) to check whether the plan-then-solve trigger generalizes past one specific instruction-tuned GPT-3 checkpoint that OpenAI has since deprecated.

The paper's own prose and its own tables disagree with each other in more than one place. Section 4.2 gives the no-self-consistency PS+ accuracy on GSM8K as "58.7%," while Table 2 and Figure 4 both report 59.3% for the same zero-shot PS+/GSM8K cell. Separately, Section 4.1's prose gives Zero-shot-CoT's Last Letter accuracy as "65.2%," while Table 4 (and the corresponding number used to compute PS+'s reported margin) says 64.8%. Neither slip changes the paper's conclusions — both readings tell the same story — but two independent prose/table mismatches in one short paper is a reminder to check the tables directly rather than trusting the surrounding prose.

baselines

Fair and current for a 2023 zero-shot/few-shot CoT paper (Zero-shot-CoT, Zero-shot-PoT, Manual-CoT, Auto-CoT), but confined to a single closed model (text-davinci-003) — no open-weight model, and GPT-4 is only checked informally (90/100 plans present) rather than benchmarked on the actual accuracy tables.

contamination

Not discussed. GSM8K, AQuA, CommonsenseQA, and StrategyQA are all long-public benchmarks that plausibly sit inside GPT-3's training corpus; the paper offers no held-out or contamination-aware check, common for prompting papers of this era but a real caveat for the reported deltas.

what's missed

The "comparable performance with 8-shot CoT" framing is true only on the aggregate average — PS+ trails Manual-CoT on 3 of 6 arithmetic datasets and both commonsense sets, and the paper doesn't report statistical significance for any Δ, several of which (e.g. GSM8K's own text/table mismatch) are within a point or two.

would raise confidence

Multiple sampling runs with variance bars even outside the SC ablation; at least one open-weight model, to separate whether the trigger works in general from whether it merely works on text-davinci-003 specifically; and an explicit contamination check given several benchmarks' age relative to GPT-3's training cutoff.

// bottom line

The core empirical claim — that explicitly asking a model to plan before solving reduces missing-step and calculation errors relative to a bare "let's think step by step" — is well supported by the paired error-type analysis (46→39 wrong answers, missing-step errors 12%→7%) and is intuitive enough that it doesn't need heroic statistics to be believable. But the single-model, single-run evaluation and the "comparable performance with 8-shot CoT" framing (true on average, not on most individual datasets) mean the reported margins should be read as directional, not precise.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. Plan-and-Solve replaces Zero-shot-CoT's "let's think step by step" with a plan-then-execute trigger sentence — the entire method is a rewritten instruction, no exemplars or fine-tuning required.
  2. PS+ (the variant with added "extract relevant variables" and "calculate intermediate results" instructions) closes most of the gap to 8-shot Manual-CoT on average (76.7% vs 77.6%), while staying fully zero-shot, and cuts GSM8K error counts from 46/100 to 39/100 in the paper's own error-type breakdown.
  3. All results are single-model (GPT-3 text-davinci-003), single-run outside the self-consistency ablation — a useful prompting idea, but the reported margins shouldn't be over-read as precise or as validated beyond that one model.
