When combinations of humans and AI are useful: A systematic review and meta-analysis

A pre-registered systematic review and meta-analysis of 106 experiments (370 effect sizes, 2020–2023) finds that human-AI teams perform worse on average than the better of human-alone or AI-alone (Hedges' g = −0.23) — even though the same teams beat the human working alone (g = 0.64). The complementarity caution that "Generative AI at Work" and "GPTs are GPTs" both cite before claiming their own gains.

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

Human-AI collaboration research had split into two camps that never talked to each other's numbers. One camp — case studies of doctors reading scans, forecasters reading dashboards, radiologists reading pathology slides — reported dramatic gains from pairing people with algorithms. Another camp reported the opposite: humans anchoring on bad AI suggestions, ignoring good ones, or simply adding noise to an otherwise-accurate model. Both literatures were real, but neither told you what to expect from the next human-AI system you build, because no one had pooled the evidence at scale.

Part of the problem is that most published studies only ask one question: is the human-AI team better than the human working alone? That is human augmentation — a useful bar when full automation is off the table for legal, ethical, or safety reasons. But it is a low bar. The authors argue the harder and more diagnostic question is human-AI synergy: is the combined team better than either the best human alone or the best AI alone? If a team can't clear that bar, the rational move is to just deploy whichever standalone system is better and skip the collaboration machinery entirely. Prior reviews of this literature were qualitative — narrative surveys that could say "results are mixed" but not put a number on how mixed, or say which experimental design choices moved the needle.

// how it works

The method

The authors ran a PRISMA-style systematic review, pre-registered in advance, searching the ACM Digital Library, the AIS eLibrary, and Web of Science for experiments published between January 1, 2020 and June 30, 2023 (plus forward/backward citation chasing on every included paper). The search string combined four required facets — a human component, an AI component, a collaboration verb (assist, aid, interact, help), and an experiment/user-study component. That query returned 5,126 candidate papers; after applying inclusion criteria, 74 papers survived.

The inclusion bar was strict and is what makes the dataset usable for a meta-analysis rather than just a literature map: a study had to report an original human-participants experiment, and it had to report the performance of all three conditions — human alone, AI alone, and human+AI combined — on a comparable quantitative outcome, with enough information (means, SDs, N) to compute an effect size. Studies that reported only "human vs. human+AI," which is most of the augmentation-only literature, were excluded from the synergy analysis by construction. Where papers reported confidence intervals or standard errors instead of SDs, the authors back-calculated; where raw data was public, they recomputed from source; where numbers were only in a plot, they used WebPlotDigitizer or emailed the corresponding author.

The 74 papers yielded 106 distinct experiments (defined by distinct participant samples) and 370 individual effect sizes, because many experiments had multiple treatment arms or multiple outcome measures. The core statistic is Hedges' g — a bias-corrected standardized mean difference chosen because it lets accuracy, error rate, and quality-score outcomes all be compared on one unitless scale. Because effect sizes were nested (multiple effect sizes per experiment, multiple experiments per paper), a single-level meta-analysis would understate uncertainty by treating non-independent estimates as independent. The authors instead fit a three-level random-effects meta-analytic model — variance within an effect size, variance between effect sizes in the same experiment, variance between experiments — with robust variance estimation (RVE) and a Knapp–Hartung small-sample correction, using R's metafor package.

Eleven candidate moderators were coded for every effect size — task type (decide vs. create), task output format, task data modality, publication year, AI type (deep-learning, "shallow"/classical, or Wizard-of-Oz), whether the AI showed a confidence score, whether it showed an explanation, participant type (expert vs. crowdworker vs. student), experimental design, and division-of-labor structure — and tested one at a time via separate meta-regressions.

// what they found

The results

The headline split: against the synergy baseline (the better of human-alone or AI-alone), human-AI teams performed significantly worse on average — g = −0.23 (t(92) = −2.89, two-tailed p = 0.005, 95% CI [−0.39, −0.07]), a small-but-real negative effect. Against the augmentation baseline (human-alone only), the same teams performed significantly better — g = 0.64 (t(98) = 11.87, p < 0.001, 95% CI [0.53, 0.74]), a medium-to-large positive effect. Put plainly: AI reliably makes people better than they were on their own, but the resulting team usually still falls short of what the AI (or, less often, the human) could have delivered solo. As Figure 1 shows at the level of individual effect sizes, 213 of 370 (58%) synergy comparisons were negative versus 157 (42%) positive; for augmentation the split flips hard — 314 of 370 (85%) positive versus 56 (15%) negative.

Heterogeneity across the 370 effect sizes was extreme (I² = 97.7% for synergy, 93.8% for augmentation) — the average is masking wildly different outcomes depending on setup, which is exactly what the moderator analysis goes after. Two moderators dominated. First, task type: among decision tasks (choosing among a fixed set of options — n = 344, "the vast majority (about 85%)" of the sample per the authors' own description), synergy was significantly negative (g = −0.27, t(104) = −3.20, p = 0.002); among creation tasks (open-ended content generation — n = 34, "only about 10%"), synergy was positive, g = 0.19 (t(104) = 1.35, p = 0.180 — not itself significant, likely underpowered at n = 34, but significantly different from the decision-task estimate, F(1,104) = 7.84, p = 0.006).

Second, and more strikingly, who was better alone flips the sign of the whole effect. When the human alone beat the AI alone, the combined team beat both — g = 0.46 (t(104) = 5.06, p < 0.001, 95% CI [0.28, 0.66]). When the AI alone beat the human alone, the combined team lost to the AI — g = −0.54 (t(104) = −6.20, p < 0.001, 95% CI [−0.71, −0.37]) — even though augmentation over the human baseline was at its largest in exactly this case (g = 0.74, t(104) = 13.50, p < 0.001). The authors illustrate with one experiment run across three tasks with an identical interface: for fake-hotel-review detection, AI alone hit 73% accuracy against 55% for humans alone and 69% for the team — the team dragged the stronger partner down. For bird-species classification in the same experiment, AI alone hit 73%, humans alone hit 81%, and the team hit 90% — synergy, because the (better) humans apparently knew when to trust their own judgment over the algorithm's.

Two moderators that dominate the field's design conversation turned out not to matter: whether the AI showed a confidence score (F not significant) and whether it showed an explanation (F not significant) moved neither synergy nor augmentation. AI type (deep vs. shallow vs. Wizard-of-Oz) and publication year did move synergy modestly (p = 0.026 and p = 0.015 respectively); experimental design (dependent vs. independent samples) moved augmentation (p = 0.029). Only 3 of the 100+ experiments used a pre-determined division of labor — assigning specific sub-tasks to whichever partner is known to be better at them, rather than having the AI advise and the human decide everything — and those 3 experiments (4 effect sizes) produced a positive but not-quite-significant synergy estimate, g = 0.22 (t(104) = 0.69, p = 0.494, 95% CI [−0.42, 0.87]).

On robustness: excluding statistical outliers (11 for synergy, 9 for augmentation) barely moved the estimates (g = −0.25 and g = 0.60 respectively). Clustering at the paper level instead of the experiment level gave g = −0.22 and g = 0.65. Leave-one-out analysis at the effect-size, experiment, and paper level kept the synergy estimate between −0.28 and −0.19 (all p < 0.05) and augmentation between 0.61 and 0.66 (all p < 0.001) in every case. Dropping the effect sizes that had to be estimated via WebPlotDigitizer or author correspondence changed almost nothing (g = −0.21 and g = 0.64).

// does it hold up?

The eval read

This is one of the more self-scrutinizing meta-analyses in the human-AI literature, and it is worth taking seriously precisely because the authors ran the bias checks a lot of similar papers skip — and the results split in an interesting, non-flattering-to-the-field way.

On publication bias, the authors ran funnel-plot inspection, Egger's regression, and a rank-correlation test separately for each outcome. For the headline synergy finding — the negative, more surprising result — they found no evidence of bias (Egger's β = −0.67, t(104) = −0.78, p = 0.438; rank correlation τ = 0.05, p = 0.121). But for augmentation — the flattering, human-AI-beats-human-alone result that most papers in this literature actually report — they found significant evidence of publication bias (Egger's β = 1.96, t(104) = 3.24, p = 0.002; rank correlation τ = 0.19, p < 0.001). In other words, the number this literature usually leads with is the one their own bias tests flag as inflated, and the more damning number is the one that checks out clean. That is a genuinely sharp, self-aware result, and it argues for taking the g = −0.23 synergy estimate more seriously than the g = 0.64 augmentation estimate, not less.

The sample composition, though, narrows what "on average" can mean here. The authors' own text describes decision tasks (n = 344 of 370 effect sizes in Figure 2) as "the vast majority (about 85%)" of the sample and creation tasks (n = 34) as "only about 10%" — so the negative average is substantially a statement about classification/prediction-style decision aids circa 2020–2023, not about human-AI collaboration in general, and the one subgroup where synergy trended positive (creation tasks, g = 0.19) is both the smallest and the least statistically confident estimate in the paper. (Note: the per-moderator counts in Figure 2 — e.g., 344 + 34 = 378 for task type — don't sum exactly to the reported N = 370 total effect sizes; a minor internal inconsistency in the source, not one we introduced.) The search window itself (Jan 2020–Jun 2023) closes roughly seven months after ChatGPT's public launch, so the corpus is necessarily dominated by pre-generative-AI decision-support systems rather than the LLM-copilot pattern ("Generative AI at Work," "Navigating the Jagged Technological Frontier") that motivated citing this paper in the first place — an acknowledged sampling artifact of when the review was conducted, not a flaw in the review itself.

The other structural gap the authors flag themselves: almost none of the underlying experiments actually tested the theoretically-motivated route to synergy. "Only 3 of the 100+ experiments in our analysis explore such processes with a pre-determined delegation of separate sub-tasks to humans and AI" — meaning 97% of this evidence base is studies of the "AI recommends, human decides everything" pattern, which theory (and the AI-better-alone subgroup result, g = −0.54) suggests is close to the worst-case design for generating synergy. The negative headline number may be less a verdict on human-AI collaboration as a concept than a verdict on how narrow the field's experimental repertoire still is.

baselines

Two explicit, well-motivated baselines (max(human,AI) for synergy; human-alone for augmentation) — a real strength versus papers that report only one. But the inclusion criterion (paper must report all three arms) self-selects toward lab studies designed to isolate components, not production delegation systems.

contamination

Formally tested via funnel plots + Egger's regression + rank correlation, separately per outcome. Clean for the negative synergy result; flagged as biased for the positive augmentation result the field usually reports — a rare instance of a meta-analysis undercutting its own more publishable finding.

what's missed

93% decision tasks, ≤10% creation tasks; search window ends mid-2023, before generative-AI copilots were widely studied; only 3 experiments (4 effect sizes) test deliberate task delegation, the design theory says should work best.

would raise confidence

A 2023–2026 replication dominated by LLM-copilot studies; more experiments with a priori sub-task delegation instead of AI-advises/human-decides; the "commensurability criteria" (standardized tasks, metrics, and a shared open-data repository) the authors themselves call for in their roadmap.

// bottom line

The negative headline (g = −0.23) survives every robustness check the authors threw at it and is the one their own bias tests say is clean — that's unusually rigorous for this literature. But it is a snapshot of mostly pre-generative-AI, mostly "AI-advises" decision tasks; treat it as a strong prior about a specific, narrow experimental pattern, not as a verdict on whether human-AI complementarity is achievable in general.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. Synergy is rare; augmentation is common. Across 370 effect sizes from 106 experiments, human-AI teams beat the better of human-alone or AI-alone only 42% of the time (g = −0.23 on average) but beat the human alone 85% of the time (g = 0.64 on average) — AI reliably helps people, it just doesn't reliably beat the best available option.
  2. Who's better alone decides the sign. When AI alone outperforms the human alone, teaming up costs performance (g = −0.54); when the human alone is better, teaming up gains performance (g = +0.46). The team can't just average its two members — how well humans calibrate trust in the algorithm is doing the real work.
  3. The field's favorite design knobs don't matter, and its favorite design pattern is undertested. Showing an AI's confidence or explanation moved neither outcome. Meanwhile only 3 of 100+ experiments tried deliberately delegating different sub-tasks to whichever partner is better at them — the one design synergy theory says should actually work, and almost nobody has run it.
