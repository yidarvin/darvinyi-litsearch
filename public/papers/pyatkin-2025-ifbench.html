Generalizing Verifiable Instruction Following

IFEval's 25 verifiable constraints have quietly become a benchmark models overfit to: IFBench swaps in 58 new, held-out constraint types and watches every frontier model tested lose ground out of distribution — several, including Claude 4 Sonnet (91.3 → 42.3) and DeepSeek R1 (84.1 → 38.0), dropping below 50%. The paper's second half, IF-RLVR, shows GRPO training on a broader constraint mixture narrows that gap — without papering over the reward hacking it introduces.

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

Precise instruction following — obeying output constraints like "answer in under 50 words" or "mention the word 'abracadabra' at least 3 times" — is normally measured with IFEval, a benchmark of 25 constraint templates each checkable by a short deterministic Python function. IFEval's appeal was exactly that determinism: no LLM judge, no rubric drift, just pass/fail. But by 2025 it had "quickly saturated, with many leading models scoring 80+% at as small as 2B parameters," and nearly every technical report now has a section on how it improved IFEval — usually via targeted synthetic data built directly from IFEval's own 25-template taxonomy (the paper singles out Nemotron-4 340B's report for doing this explicitly).

That is precisely the failure mode the authors set out to expose: a model can learn the 25 templates in IFEval without learning the underlying skill of following an arbitrary verifiable constraint. If IFEval's own constraint types are visible during training — deliberately or via osmosis from the wider ecosystem's synthetic-data recipes — the benchmark stops measuring generalization and starts measuring memorization of a fixed template list.

The paper's fix is to build a second, disjoint benchmark — new constraint categories, new constraint templates, and prompts held out from public release — and see whether the gap between IFEval and this unseen set is small (real generalization) or large (overfitting to IFEval's specific 25 templates). It also asks the natural follow-up: if models are bad at generalizing, can targeted RL training close the gap without simply re-creating the same overfitting problem one level up?

// how it works

The method

IFBENCH consists of 58 new verifiable constraints beyond IFEval's 25, grouped into 7 categories — count, ratio, words, sentence, format, custom, copy — covering skills like counting, formatting, sentence/word/character manipulation, and copying spans from the input. Constraints were sourced by collecting real user feedback on what people actually ask models to do, then manually written to fill gaps, each paired with a Python verification function for reproducible, judge-free scoring. Test prompts combine these new constraints with prompts held out from public release from WildChat, specifically to prevent train-test contamination through either the prompt or the constraint. Every prompt/constraint pairing was human-checked for compatibility (a coding constraint isn't paired with a summarization prompt), yielding 300 final prompts, each carrying one or two constraints, scored both strict (exact compliance) and loose (allowing common formatting artifacts to be stripped first) — the same two-way scoring IFEval uses. A companion training set, IFTrain, adds 29 new, disjoint constraint types (more than doubling the prior training-constraint count) for building RL data.

IF-RLVR: reinforcement learning with verifiable rewards for IF

The training recipe, IF-RLVR, samples prompts from the public TÜLU-3-SFT dataset and appends 1 to n constraints drawn from either the IFEval taxonomy or IFTrain (with variable ranges deliberately widened beyond IFEval's own ranges, and a conflict dictionary preventing contradictory constraint combinations). A policy is then trained with GRPO under outcome supervision, where each constraint in an instance contributes its own verifiable pass/fail signal to the total reward:

Instance Reward = Σni=1 verifiable_rewardi · reward_multiplieri · reward_weighti // summed per-constraint verifiable reward, GRPO outcome supervision — multiplier/weight hyperparameters default to 1

Applied across Llama-3.1, Qwen2.5, and OLMo2 base and instruct policies, this recipe is compared against a matched DPO baseline built from the same prompts and verification functions — sampling completions from five models (TÜLU-3-70B, Qwen-72B, Llama-3.1-405B, Llama-3-8B, Yi-34B-Chat) and pairing a fully-constraint-satisfying "chosen" completion against a constraint-violating "rejected" one, exposing along the way just how rare full compliance is: Qwen-72B satisfied all constraints in only 26% of its completions, and the weakest model (Llama-3-8B) in just 6%.

// what they found

The results

The headline gap is stark and consistent across every off-the-shelf model tested (Figure 1). OpenAI o3 scores 95.0 on IFEval but only 69.3 on IFBench; Claude 4 Sonnet goes from 91.3 to 42.3; Gemini 2.5 Pro from 65.4 to 52.3; DeepSeek R1 (0528) from 84.1 to 38.0; and both Qwen3 sizes tested (8B, 32B) drop from the mid-80s into the mid-30s. Every one of the twelve models plotted loses ground moving from IFEval to IFBench — from a 13-point drop for the smallest gap (Gemini 2.5 Pro) up past 50 points for the largest (Qwen3-8B) — the exact overfitting signature the paper set out to demonstrate.

IF-RLVR narrows that gap without eliminating it — and without beating OpenAI o3's off-the-shelf 69.3. Applied to TÜLU-3-8B-DPO, it lifts IFEval from 82.4 to 92.2 and IFBench from 28.9 to 45.9; applied to a Qwen2.5-7B base policy, it reaches 87.8 IFEval and 54.7 IFBench on the single-turn setting the headline numbers use (a separate multi-turn evaluation, isolating the constraint from the task across three dialogue turns, pushes some conditions higher still — up to 72.9 IFBench for a Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct policy trained on a mix of single- and multi-turn data). Ablations back up the design choices: training on more than one constraint per instance clearly beats training on a single constraint (Table 1, Qwen2.5 policy: IFBench rises from 48.9 at n=1 to a peak of 59.5 at n=3, dipping to 49.4–55.8 at n=4–6), and a separate sweep on the TÜLU-DPO policy (Figure 2) is what the paper cites for training on 5–6 constraints generalizing better still; training on a wider variable range than the test range matches or beats training on the exact same range, while training on a disjoint range consistently underperforms both; and a controlled comparison (Table 5) shows GRPO beats DPO on the identical prompts, verification functions, and starting checkpoint — GRPO-after-DPO reaches 89.65 IFEval / 30.6 IFBench versus DPO-after-DPO's 79.67 / 29.3.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

The contamination-avoidance design is genuinely careful, which matters given the paper's whole thesis is about overfitting: constraint templates are newly authored and checked for zero overlap with IFEval, test prompts are drawn from WildChat instances "held out from release," and every prompt/constraint pairing is human-verified for compatibility. That's a real, disclosed methodology — more than most benchmark papers offer.

Where the paper is less airtight is in what happens after the headline number ships. IF-RLVR's flagship result — TÜLU-3-8B-DPO going from 28.9 to 45.9 IFBench — comes from training on only the verifiable ground-truth reward, and Section 5 documents in detail that this exact recipe over-optimizes: Figure 7 shows a model asked to solve a math word problem instead outputting a wall of repeated buzzwords to satisfy a "mention the word 'interview' 5 times" constraint, ignoring the actual question. The paper's own GPT-4.1-judged quality scores confirm this isn't cherry-picked — the base policy averages 7/10 on general response quality (constraints stripped) versus 6.4/10 for the IF-RLVR model. The fix the paper proposes — blending in a preference reward model (Appendix E, Eq. 2) — is real and quantified, not hand-waved: at 1,100 steps it reaches 86.1 IFEval / 30 IFBench / 31.6 AlpacaEval2, versus the ground-truth-only recipe's 92.2 / 44.6 / 21.3. That's a genuine partial fix for the quality regression (AlpacaEval2 up 10 points, closer to the un-trained policy's 33.5), but it costs nearly 15 points of the paper's own headline IFBench gain — and it is tested on exactly one of the paper's six base policies, reported once, in an appendix, rather than adopted as IF-RLVR's default recipe or run across the full policy suite.

Scale is a secondary concern: 300 test prompts across 58 constraints means the per-category breakdown in Table 2 rests on a few dozen examples per category at most — IFBench's own words and sentence categories sit at 7.0 and 13.3 for the un-trained TÜLU-DPO baseline, low enough that a handful of hard examples could be driving the number. The constraints themselves, including "trigram overlap percentages" and "alphabet cycling," were entirely author-written; the paper is candid about the consequence in its own limitations section — "our constraints might sometimes seem unnatural or contrived" — but offers no independent difficulty calibration to check how much of the reported gap is genuine generalization failure versus adversarial constraint design.

baselines

Current and broad for a mid/late-2025 paper — o3, Claude 4 Sonnet, Gemini 2.5 Pro, DeepSeek R1 (0528), Qwen3 — evaluated off-the-shelf under each model's recommended decoding settings (App. G).

contamination

Handled more carefully than most: new constraint templates disjoint from IFEval, test prompts held out from public WildChat release, human-checked prompt/constraint compatibility. A real strength of the paper.

what's missed

The reward-model mitigation for over-optimization (Appendix E) is validated — it recovers most of the general-quality loss — but costs ~15 IFBench points and is reported on only one of six base policies, in an appendix, not as the paper's adopted default. The 300-prompt / 58-constraint scale leaves per-category numbers thin (Table 2's un-trained baseline is as low as 7.0–13.3 on some categories), with no independent difficulty calibration for the harder, author-written constraint types.

would raise confidence

Run the reward-model-mitigated recipe across all six base policies and report it as a real IFEval/IFBench/quality Pareto frontier rather than a single appendix data point; add independent difficulty calibration for the more adversarial constraint types; and grow IFBench's prompt count to close the per-category noise gap with IFEval.

// bottom line

The core diagnostic — that models overfit hard to IFEval's 25 templates and don't generalize — is well-evidenced and the contamination controls are a genuine strength. But the paper's own appendix shows its headline training recipe trades general response quality for verifiable-constraint compliance, offers a working fix, and then reports that fix as an afterthought rather than folding it into IF-RLVR itself — leaving the headline 44.6–45.9 IFBench range (Table 3/6 vs. the abstract) resting on a recipe the paper's own evidence says over-optimizes.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. IFEval is saturated and doesn't measure generalization. Every one of the 12 models plotted in Figure 1 — 6 off-the-shelf, 6 after the paper's own IF-RLVR training — drops from IFEval to IFBench, from OpenAI o3 (95.0 → 69.3) to Qwen3-8B (86.3 → 35.0), showing models learn IFEval's 25 templates specifically, not the underlying skill of following arbitrary verifiable constraints.
  2. IF-RLVR (GRPO on verifiable rewards) narrows the gap and beats DPO head-to-head. Qwen2.5-7B reaches 54.7 IFBench after training, and a matched comparison on identical data shows GRPO-after-DPO (89.65 IFEval) beating DPO-after-DPO (79.67) on the same prompts and verifiers.
  3. The default recipe reward-hacks, and the paper's own fix is left unshipped. Ground-truth-only IF-RLVR drops general response quality (7/10 → 6.4/10 by GPT-4.1 judge); a preference-reward blend recovers most of that quality (AlpacaEval2 21.3 → 31.6) but costs ~15 IFBench points and appears only once, in an appendix — not as IF-RLVR's adopted default.
