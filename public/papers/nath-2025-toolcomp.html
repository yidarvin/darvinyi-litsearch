ToolComp: A Multi-Tool Reasoning & Process Supervision Benchmark

Scale AI's tool-use benchmark grades the trajectory, not just the answer: 485 human-verified prompts that chain calls across 11 tools, paired with 1,731 per-step process-supervision labels. The best model (o1-preview) reaches only 61.83% — and a process-supervised reward model beats an outcome-supervised one by 19 points at picking the right trajectory, early evidence that supervising the steps is what improves agentic tool use.

Vaskar Nath, Pranav Raja, Claire Yoon, Sean Hendryx · Scale AI

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

By late 2024, tool-use benchmarks each measured a slice of the problem and missed the rest. The earliest ones tested whether a model could retrieve and invoke a single tool correctly — general QA, fact verification, temporal queries. Multi-step benchmarks that did exist, like GAIA, scored only the final answer: get the right number and you pass, regardless of how you got there.

The benchmarks that did probe long tool chains mostly leaned on stateful environments — a codebase or a live database (SWE-bench, the Berkeley Function Calling Leaderboard, AgentBench). That conflates two things: a general-purpose model is penalized for being unfamiliar with the specific environment, not for being bad at reasoning. Others scored the final state of a system, or computed win-rates against a reference model — neither of which gives you a human-verified ground-truth answer to check against.

The hole, then, was a benchmark that combined all three: (1) genuinely multi-step, dependent tool chains; (2) human-verified, programmatically checkable final answers; and (3) step-wise labels marking which intermediate moves were right or wrong. Without (3) you can't tell whether a model got the right answer for the right reasons, or pinpoint where in a 10-step trajectory it went off the rails — and you can't apply process supervision, the technique that (via Lightman et al.'s "Let's Verify Step by Step" and Math-Shepherd) had just delivered large gains on mathematical reasoning. Nobody had built the per-step labels needed to test whether that idea transfers from math to tool use. ToolComp is built to fill exactly that hole.

// how it works

The method

ToolComp is 485 prompts split into two subsets. ToolComp-Enterprise (287 prompts) exposes all 11 tools — Date, Current and Historical Weather, Calculator, Wiki Search, Google Search, Wolfram Alpha, intra-day and daily Stock Info, Stock Symbol Search, and Python — to emulate an agent composing many expressive APIs. ToolComp-Chat (198 prompts) restricts the agent to just Google Search and Python, the minimal toolkit a general chatbot ships with. Every prompt is written to satisfy two hard constraints: its solution requires a chain of dependent tool calls, and its final answer can be programmatically verified. Candidate prompts are generated few-shot, then iteratively filtered (rejected prompts are recycled as negative in-context examples to push generation harder), and finally hand-refined.

Annotating the trajectory, step by step

The signature move is the label-creation loop. Rather than just record a final answer, ToolComp captures an entire ReAct trajectory — a sequence of Thought → Action → Action Input → Observation steps, preceded by an Action Plan — and has humans audit it move by move. A policy model (FireFunction-V1, the strongest open tool-use model at the time) proposes each plan and step; an annotator marks every component Correct or Incorrect, and rewrites any incorrect component before feeding the corrected step back into the model's context. The loop repeats until the model (or the annotator) issues Finish. The output per task is a "golden" trajectory plus dense supervision: 1,731 per-step correct/incorrect labels with paired rewrites — the human-corrected step and the model's original mistake, side by side.

Two ways to grade

Final answers are scored by an LLM judge (GPT-4 Turbo), which sees the prompt, the ground-truth answer, and the model's answer and returns Correct, Correct with Bad Formatting, or Incorrect — the first two both count as a win, so models aren't punished for formatting their complex, multi-element answers differently (an exact-match check is reported separately in the appendix). The process labels are turned into a pairwise judging task: can a model, shown a human-corrected step and the original incorrect step, pick the corrected one? Order is swapped to cancel position bias, ties are allowed, and scores follow RewardBench's convention (loss = 0, tie = 0.5, win = 1).

The second half of the paper asks whether these step labels are useful for training. The authors equip Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct with a linear reward head and train two reward models on additional synthetic data (policy generator: Llama-3.1-8B; per-step critic: GPT-4o). An outcome-supervised reward model (ORM) scores a whole trajectory; a process-supervised reward model (PRM) scores each step. To rank a set of candidate trajectories with the PRM, per-step scores must be collapsed into one number — and the choice of aggregator turns out to matter enormously:

s(τ) = max t rφ(stept)       rank@1 = correct ( argmax τ s(τ) ) // PRM trajectory score = the single best step's reward; rank@1 = is the top-scored trajectory's final answer correct?
// what they found

The results

Across 16 models from 6 families, ToolComp is hard: the majority score under 50% on final answers. o1-preview leads at 61.83%, trailed by a tight cluster — GPT-4o (Aug 2024) 58.68%, GPT-4o (May 2024) 58.44%, Claude 3.5 Sonnet 58.03%, GPT-4 Turbo 57.61%, Gemini 1.5 Pro (Aug 2024) 56.61% — with a benchmark average of 46.64%. The floor is steep: Llama 3.1 8B manages just 12.81% and Command R+ 26.13%.

The most telling cut is the subset split. ToolComp-Chat (2 tools) is harder than ToolComp-Enterprise (11 tools) — average 41.08% vs 50.46%. More tools don't make the task harder; the wall is planning and chaining open-ended reasoning, not the mechanics of having many APIs available.

The steps carry signal the answer misses

Judged on the process labels — can the model spot the human-corrected step? — o1-preview again tops out at 80.19%, with a benchmark average of 67.54% (Mistral Large 2 and GPT-4o (Aug) both near 72.6%). Crucially, step-wise reasoning quality and final-answer accuracy are correlated but only moderately: Pearson r = 0.63 (p = 0.0084). The gap is the point — there is real information in the trajectory that a final-answer-only score throws away, which is exactly why per-step labels are worth collecting.

Process supervision beats outcome supervision

The headline training result: a PRM ranks trajectories far better than an ORM. On raw base-model generations, the best PRM hits 42.65% rank@1 vs the ORM's 23.89% — a +19-point gap. On generations from an SFT'd model, the PRM pushes rank@1 to 60.25%, nearly matching the generator's pass@30 ceiling, an +11-point edge over the ORM. And the PRM dominates at every training-data scale (10/25/50/100%), with the widest margins on the noisy base-model trajectories — exactly where being able to fish a good trajectory out of many bad ones is most valuable.

Two design ablations sharpen the recipe. Supervision granularity: rewarding the Full ReAct step with its Observation (the tool's output) wins at 60.25% rank@1, ahead of full-step-without-observation (55.43), and well ahead of finer sub-step labels (49.48 / 45.70) — the tool's output is load-bearing signal, and coarser labels generalize better than over-detailed ones. Aggregation: max is dramatically the best collapse function (60.25%), while min (23.68), average (25.74), and product (23.06) all crater — they punish any single wrong step even when the model recovers. The error analysis (below) shows where models actually fail: hallucinated information, final answers that omit requested details, and malformed tool calls dominate, rather than picking the wrong tool.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

ToolComp's data design is genuinely ahead of its time, but several of its load-bearing claims rest on a narrow, now-dated setup that's worth reading carefully.

The leaderboard is stale, and the model lineup is small for the headline claim. This is a January 2025 paper: the frontier here is o1-preview, GPT-4o, Claude 3.5 Sonnet, Gemini 1.5 Pro, Llama 3.1 — none of the reasoning-heavy 2025 models that would now obviously move these numbers. "Most models score under 50%" is a 2024-vintage statement. And the entire process-vs-outcome story is run on a single base model (Llama-3.1-8B + a linear head) with no sweep over model size — so "PRM > ORM" is demonstrated at one scale, not shown to be a law.

One judge, and it shares a family with the winners. Final answers are graded by GPT-4 Turbo while OpenAI models top the chart — the textbook self-preference setup. The authors defend it with an exact-match cross-check (Appendix A.1) showing the top-5 and bottom-4 are unchanged and that judge/exact-match disagreements are formatting-only, which is reassuring but not the same as a cross-family judge panel. Note too that the "process supervision" scores are themselves an LLM-as-judge task, so they partly measure a model's judging ability, not purely its reasoning.

The PRM win has a synthetic-label confound. ToolComp's evaluation labels are human, but the PRM's training labels are generated by a GPT-4o "critic," not people. So the cleanest reading of the result is "GPT-4o-distilled process supervision beats outcome supervision for a small reward model" — strong and useful, but not a human-grounded claim. It also leans on the aggregation finding: max wins precisely because the highest-scoring step tends to sit near the end of the trajectory (Figure 4), which means the PRM's edge is partly "is the late/near-final reasoning good?" — uncomfortably close to outcome supervision, and consistent with min/avg/product collapsing back to roughly the ORM's level.

Closed by design, and time-sensitive. The benchmark is deliberately withheld — to be released only once a model clears 85% or at the end of 2026, whichever is first — which buys contamination resistance at the cost of external auditability: no one outside Scale can inspect the 485 prompts or 1,731 labels for ambiguity or label noise. Worse for reproducibility, several tools are live and time-varying (weather, intra-day stocks, Google Search; "Accessed February 2023–September 2024"). Ground-truth answers were frozen at annotation time, so re-running an evaluation today can disagree with the gold answer for reasons that have nothing to do with the model. Finally, the trajectories being annotated were generated by FireFunction-V1, a weak/dated policy — so the step-judging difficulty and error distribution are shaped by that one model's failure modes. With 485 prompts (Chat only 198) the confidence intervals are wide (±4.34 overall, ~±7 on Chat), and the top cluster's differences sit within overlapping CIs — so "o1-preview is best" is a soft ranking, not a decisive one.

baselines

16 models / 6 families, but all pre-2025 (o1-preview is the ceiling); the PRM-vs-ORM study uses a single Llama-3.1-8B + linear head with no reward-model size sweep.

contamination

Deliberately held out until a model hits 85% or end-2026 — contamination-resistant but unauditable; live weather/stock/search tools make the frozen ground truth and reproducibility drift over time.

what's missed

A single GPT-4 Turbo judge with OpenAI models on top (self-preference); the PRM's training labels are GPT-4o-generated, not human; "max" aggregation leans on late steps, partly collapsing the PRM-vs-ORM distinction.

would raise confidence

A cross-family judge panel; human (not GPT-4o) step labels in the training set; a reward-model size sweep; current frontier models; and an aggregator that credits genuine mid-trajectory recovery rather than the best single step.

// bottom line

The numbers age fast and the PRM result rests on a GPT-4o-labeled, single-small-model setup — but the durable idea is the data shape. ToolComp is one of the first tool-use benchmarks to pair human-verified final answers with dense per-step process-supervision labels, and it gives early, credible evidence that process supervision carries from math reasoning to agentic tool use. That gradable-trajectory design is a direct ancestor of Scale AI's later process- and rubric-reward line of work.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. Grade the trajectory, not just the answer. ToolComp's 1,731 per-step labels let it separate "right answer" from "right reasoning" — and the two correlate at only r = 0.63, so there's real signal in the steps that final-answer accuracy misses.
  2. Process supervision transfers from math to tool use. A process-supervised reward model beat an outcome-supervised one by +19 points (rank@1) on base-model trajectories and pushed an SFT model to 60.25%, near its pass@30 ceiling — early, concrete evidence that the "Let's Verify Step by Step" idea generalizes to multi-step tool calling.
  3. Tools aren't the bottleneck; reasoning is. The 2-tool Chat subset (41.08% avg) is harder than the 11-tool Enterprise subset (50.46%), and most of 16 models score under 50% — the wall is planning and chaining, not API mechanics.
  4. Recipe details matter. Among PRM choices, full-step labels that include the tool's Observation win, and max aggregation dominates min/average/product — but max works largely because the best step sits near the end, a caveat on how distinct the PRM's signal is from outcome supervision.
