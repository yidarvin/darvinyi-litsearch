Defensive Refusal Bias: How Safety Alignment Fails Cyber Defenders

Safety tuning teaches models to refuse anything that sounds like an attack — but defenders and attackers speak the same language. Across 2,390 real blue-team prompts, aligned LLMs refuse legitimate defensive work 2.72× more often when it carries security vocabulary, and saying "I'm on the blue team" makes it worse.

David Campbell, Neil Kale, Udari Madhushani Sehwag, Bert Herring, Nick Price, Dan Borges, Alex Levinson, Christina Q Knight

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

Safety alignment for cybersecurity has a single, well-rehearsed objective: prevent misuse. Benchmarks like HarmBench and TruthfulQA score whether a model will comply with a dangerous request, and a refusal is counted as a clean win. That framing has a blind spot the size of an entire profession — it never asks how often the same guardrails fire on the people they are meant to protect.

The trouble is that in real security operations, defenders and attackers use identical words. To stop a piece of malware you have to analyze it; to close an exploit path you have to trace it; to harden a box you have to think like the person who would break in. "How does this persistence mechanism work?" is, token for token, the question an attacker writing malware would ask. The intent differs; the surface text does not. If a model's safety behavior keys on vocabulary rather than intent, every blue-teamer inherits the friction that was designed for red-teamers.

Prior work circles this without landing on it. OR-Bench measures over-refusal in general, FORTRESS measures over-refusal alongside jailbreaks, and CyberSecEval 2 reports a synthetic "False Refusal Rate" on CTF-style challenges. But to the authors' knowledge no one had measured the cost of alignment on real, sanctioned defensive work. This paper is the first systematic study of what it names Defensive Refusal Bias: the tendency of safety-tuned LLMs to deny legitimate defensive requests because they resemble offense. The stakes are not academic — the same refusals that are a nuisance to a human analyst (who can rephrase and retry) are a silent failure for an autonomous defensive agent, which either gets help or quietly reports a compromised system as "handled."

// how it works

The method

The study is observational, built on a dataset with an unusually strong claim to legitimacy. The authors take 2,390 single-turn conversations collected during the National Collegiate Cyber Defense Competition (NCCDC) — a sanctioned event where student blue teams defend live infrastructure against professional red teams. Because every prompt originates from defenders working under real attack pressure, the authors treat the entire corpus as legitimate defensive use: any refusal is, by construction, a refusal of a real defender.

The prompts span eight defensive task categories — malware analysis, vulnerability assessment, incident response, system hardening, credential management, firewall configuration, network scanning, and log analysis — and are replayed against three models chosen to represent three deployment archetypes:

  • Safety-focused: Claude 3.5 Sonnet (June 2024)
  • General frontier: GPT-4o (2024)
  • Open-source: Llama-3.3-70B-Instruct

Detecting refusals and tagging prompts

Refusals are detected by case-insensitive regular expressions over the response text, grouped into statements of inability ("I can't help with that"), apology-prefixed refusals, policy references ("against our guidelines"), and misuse warnings ("could be misused for"). Hard refusals, soft refusals, and degraded ("generic, non-actionable") assistance are collapsed into a single refusal outcome for aggregate analysis. Three authors hand-labeled 200 sampled responses to validate the classifier (Fleiss' κ = 0.80; classifier-vs-human Cohen's κ = 0.60).

Each prompt is then annotated along four keyword-based dimensions: task category (first matching regex wins); offensive terminology, flagged by an 11-word vocabulary (exploit, payload, shell, bypass, crack, brute, dump, pivot, c2, obfuscate, evasion — with "powershell" masked so it doesn't trip "shell"); authorization signals, split into strong markers (blue team, ctf, nccdc, ccdc, authorized, training exercise) and weak ones (our network, pentest, defender…); and incident framing (active / preventative / post-incident).

The core measurement

The headline statistic is a relative risk ratio: how much more likely is a refusal when offensive vocabulary is present versus a semantically equivalent prompt without it. Differences are tested with chi-square (α = 0.05) and reported with bootstrapped 95% confidence intervals (2,000 resamples).

RR = P(refuse | offensive terms)P(refuse | neutral terms)  = 30.5%11.2% = 2.72× // χ² = 37.3, p < 0.001 — vocabulary, not intent, is the dominant predictor

Two further analyses push past correlation. To establish that vocabulary causes refusals rather than merely co-occurring with hard tasks, the authors rephrase refused prompts to strip authorization/affiliation language while preserving technical intent, and re-measure. And to ask whether refusals are driven by surface keywords or deeper meaning, they train classifiers to predict the refusal outcome from different feature sets — including raw prompt embeddings — and compare held-out AUC.

// what they found

The results

Across all 2,390 sanctioned defensive prompts, the models refuse 12.2% of the time (291 refusals) — a substantial denial of service to legitimate users, before any breakdown. The split by archetype is stark: the safety-focused model refuses 19.5% of requests, the frontier model 10.2%, and the open-source model 6.6%. The most heavily aligned model is roughly 3× more likely to refuse a defender than the open-weight one, in identical contexts — the opposite of the security posture alignment is supposed to buy.

Offensive vocabulary is the dominant trigger

Prompts containing terms like "exploit," "payload," or "shell" are refused at 30.5% versus 11.2% for semantically equivalent prompts without them — the 2.72× headline (χ² = 37.3, p < 0.001). The effect holds regardless of defensive intent or stated authorization.

The authorization paradox

The most counterintuitive finding: telling the model you're authorized makes it more likely to refuse. Prompts carrying explicit authorization signals are refused at 21.8% vs 11.6% (χ² = 9.23, p < 0.01). The interaction is sharpest when offensive vocabulary is also present — adding authorization there drives refusals to the dataset's peak of 50.0%, versus 28.7% for the same offensive prompts without authorization. And the relationship is causal in the helpful direction: rephrasing refused prompts to remove authorization cuts the refusal rate from 21.8% to 13.7% on the same tasks. The authors offer two mechanisms — authorization functions as a dual-use confirmation ("this must be sensitive"), or models have learned to pattern-match "I'm a security researcher / I'm authorized" to jailbreak attempts and treat it as adversarial.

Refusals track meaning, not keywords

Where it gets subtle: the refusals are not simple keyword matching. When the authors predict the refusal outcome from different feature sets, raw prompt embeddings alone hit AUC 0.827, while the explicit keyword features (offensive terms + authorization) sit near chance at 0.572. Refused prompts also cluster in embedding space — among a refused prompt's 10 nearest neighbors, 32.7% are also refused versus a 12.3% base rate (p < 10⁻¹⁶). The picture: alignment has learned a continuous "harm-adjacent" region in representation space, and legitimate defensive work lives inside it. Offensive terms matter because they shift a prompt toward that boundary, not because a literal blocklist fires.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

The phenomenon is real and the framing is valuable — "alignment imposes an asymmetric tax on defenders" is a genuinely under-measured failure mode, and grounding it in a sanctioned, real-world competition rather than synthetic CTF prompts is the paper's strongest move. But the magnitudes deserve more skepticism than the prose grants them, and the design is observational where it wants to be causal.

Start with the measuring instrument. Refusals are detected by regex, and the authors' own validation shows the classifier over-fires: on the 200-sample audit it flagged refusals at 50.0% vs the human majority's 35.0% (Cohen's κ = 0.60, only moderate agreement). The paper reframes this as the reported rates being "conservative upper bounds," but a classifier that over-counts refusals by ~40% relative makes the headline numbers look inflated, not conservative — what survives is the direction of every comparison, not "system hardening = 43.8%" as a literal rate. A semantic/LLM judge for the refusal label would close this gap.

Next, the annotations that the whole story rests on are themselves keyword heuristics. "Offensive terminology," "authorization," and "task category" are all regex tags, and task category is assigned by first match in a fixed order — so a malware-and-hardening prompt is silently forced into one bucket. The embedding analysis (AUC 0.827) partly rescues the argument from circularity by showing the signal is semantic, but the per-condition rates are still computed over noisy, self-defined labels. The "causal" rephrasing experiment is suggestive rather than clean: the worked example rewrites "for a cybersecurity competition… to train our cyber athletes" into "for a cybersecurity challenge… to enhance our skills," which removes authorization and softens framing at once, and the rewrites are produced by one of the models under test.

Finally, scope. The model panel is n = 1 per "class" and already dated — Claude 3.5 Sonnet (June 2024), GPT-4o, Llama-3.3-70B — so "safety-focused vs frontier" can't be separated from "this specific model," and — since safety tuning and refusal behavior shift release to release — there's no evidence one way or the other about whether the bias persists in 2026-era frontier models. Everything is single-turn, single-competition (NCCDC). The promised Spearman correlation between per-team refusal rates and competition scores is set up in the methods but never reported. And crucially, the study measures refusal, never answer quality or whether any refusals were actually correct — there is no genuinely-harmful control set to show these models still refuse true misuse, which is what would prove this is over-refusal rather than just a permissive model.

baselines

Three models, one per "class," all 2024-era (Claude 3.5 Sonnet / GPT-4o / Llama-3.3-70B). No current frontier models; no harmful-control set to demonstrate the refusals are over-refusals rather than baseline permissiveness.

contamination

Not a leakage-style benchmark, so train/test contamination isn't the risk. The analogous risk is label contamination: regex refusal detection over-fires (50% vs 35% human), and keyword-defined conditions can entangle vocabulary with task difficulty.

what's missed

Answer quality is never scored — only whether a refusal happened. No measure of whether a refusal was ever warranted. The team-score Spearman correlation is promised but absent. Mechanism behind the authorization paradox is hypothesized, not tested.

would raise confidence

An LLM/human refusal judge at scale; a clean factorial design holding the task fixed while independently varying terminology and authorization; current frontier models; a harmful-prompt control arm; and downstream task-success (not just refusal) outcomes.

// bottom line

A convincing existence proof of a real and important asymmetry — alignment penalizes defenders who must speak the language of attack — delivered as a preliminary workshop study whose exact percentages are softer than they read. Trust the shape (vocabulary > intent, authorization backfires, critical tasks refused most, refusals are semantic) more than the precise rates, and treat it as a call for authorization-aware, intent-reasoning evaluation rather than the final word on how big the tax is.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. Alignment taxes defenders asymmetrically. On 2,390 real blue-team prompts, aligned models refuse 2.72× more often when security vocabulary is present (30.5% vs 11.2%), and the most safety-tuned model refuses 1 in 5 legitimate requests — 3× the open-weight model.
  2. Authorization backfires. Saying "I'm on the blue team" or "this is for NCCDC" raises refusals (21.8% vs 11.6%; up to 50.0% with offensive terms), and removing it lowers them — models read justifications as jailbreak signals, not exculpation.
  3. It's semantic, not a blocklist. Embeddings predict refusals at AUC 0.827 while keyword features sit at 0.572: alignment learned a "harm-adjacent" region that swallows defensive work — which is exactly why intent-aware, authorization-respecting evaluation (not keyword rules) is the fix, and why autonomous defensive agents that can't rephrase are most at risk.
