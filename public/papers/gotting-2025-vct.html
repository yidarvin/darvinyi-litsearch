Virology Capabilities Test (VCT): A Multimodal Virology Q&A Benchmark

A 322-question, expert-sourced benchmark for practical virology troubleshooting finds OpenAI's o3 outperforming 94% of PhD-level virologists in their own sub-specialty — a direct capability comparison with real governance stakes.

Jasper Götting, Pedro Medeiros, Jon G. Sanders, Nathaniel Li, Long Phan, Karam Elabd, Lennart Justen, Dan Hendrycks, Seth Donoughe

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

Standard LLM science benchmarks — MMLU, GPQA — are multiple-choice and text-only. They're easy to build, grade, and scale, but as the authors put it, they "do not capture rare, tacit, and search-proof knowledge," and they don't test reasoning over images despite multimodality now being a standard model capability with obvious relevance to a lab bench, where troubleshooting often starts with "here's what my plate looks like." That gap is a particular problem for biosecurity risk assessment: existing biosecurity-focused evaluations of AI models have leaned on "few benchmarks and isolated human red-teaming studies," which are slow, expensive, and hard to replicate, and existing biology benchmarks mostly probe broad, published textbook knowledge rather than the hard-to-find, tacit troubleshooting expertise a practicing virologist actually draws on.

That distinction matters because expert-level virology troubleshooting ability is inherently dual-use: the same knowledge that helps a scientist fix a failed experiment in pursuit of a vaccine or a diagnostic can, in principle, help someone attempting to cause harm with a virus. Deciding what governance an AI capability needs first requires being able to measure whether that capability has arrived — which is what VCT sets out to do, while deliberately keeping the specific hazardous procedural content it tests around out of the questions themselves.

// how it works

The method

VCT's questions were written entirely by externally recruited, PhD-level (or PhD-track) virologists — not generated synthetically. Recruitment funneled from 184 experts who registered interest, through 105 who attended a training webinar, 86 fully onboarded, down to 68 who began submitting questions. Contributing experts averaged 5 years and 10 months of virology experience (interquartile range 4–9 years), based primarily in the US (37%), Europe (22%), and South America (22%). Every expert's first three submissions were scored by at least two project supervisors; 11 of the 68 were excluded during this vetting step for insufficient quality or guideline adherence — including one case of suspected LLM-assisted question writing, which was explicitly disallowed. That left 57 active experts.

Each question poses a detailed lab-troubleshooting scenario, sometimes paired with an image the answerer must interpret, followed by 4–10 true/false answer statements — the correct subset must be identified exactly (a "multiple-response" format, modeled on GPQA's review structure). This format was chosen deliberately over standard 4-option multiple choice because the guessing probability for n statements drops to 1/2n−1, and it lets the authors compute a Hamming-distance "mismatch count" between a submitted answer set and the correct one, tracking how close a wrong answer actually was.

Every submitted question passed two rounds of expert peer review, was polished by a team of trusted editors, and was then tested on non-experts: 3–4 graduate-level scientists from fields other than biology attempted each question, and any question that ≥2/3 of them answered correctly was dropped as insufficiently "Google-proof."

Of 1,042 total peer reviews, 72.6% approved the question under review. The ten most productive experts contributed 51% of all 507 submissions, and the dataset carries a canary string (a superset of the BIG-bench canary) to let future training-data audits filter it out. The shared 322-question set is not released publicly; it is conveyed directly only to organizations with a track record in AI safety work, while the 43-question holdout stays private to check for leakage over time.

Evaluation. Models were scored zero-shot, in the multiple-response format, via the UK AI Safety Institute's Inspect framework (for Anthropic, Google, and OpenAI's non-reasoning models) or custom scripts otherwise, under all-or-nothing grading — a model must select every true statement to get credit. For the human baseline, 36 of the 57 active experts answered a personally tailored subset of at least 10 questions matched to their own self-reported top areas of expertise, with 15–30 minutes and internet access per question but no help from colleagues or LLMs.

// what they found

The results

The headline comparison: OpenAI's o3 (Apr '25) scored 43.8% accuracy, the highest of any model evaluated, while tailored expert virologists averaged 22.1% — lower than every model tested except GPT-4o (18.8%). Because different experts answered different, personally-tailored question subsets, the authors ran a matched comparison: score each model on the exact same subset each expert answered, then rank the model within that expert's pool. On that matched analysis, o3 outperformed 34 of the 36 participating experts — the 94th percentile. Every reasoning model tested beat the median human expert.

The mismatch-count metric (average Hamming distance between a submitted and correct answer set — lower is better) tracks accuracy closely: o3's 1.06 is the best of any model or human baseline; expert virologists' 1.71 is the worst, meaning models aren't just winning on lucky exact matches — they're also closer on the questions they get wrong. Models also outperform virologists on VCT's 101-question text-only subset (e.g. the text-only DeepSeek-R1 reaches 38.6% accuracy there). Across all evaluations, the authors report zero refusals — no model declined to answer, making jailbreak-style prompting unnecessary to elicit responses. The authors read the broader trend, not just the single o3 data point, as the important signal: models have matched or exceeded expert troubleshooting ability "for over a year" (dating to Gemini 1.5 Pro in February 2024), and "the disparity between humans and models is widening."

// does it hold up?

The eval read

VCT is methodologically careful for what it is — a benchmark built almost entirely from vetted human expert labor rather than synthetic generation, with real peer review and a documented exclusion policy for the most sensitive content. That care produces a genuinely load-bearing finding for AI governance. But because the material is sensitive by design, the benchmark trades public verifiability for safety, and several of its evaluation choices are worth reading carefully rather than taking the headline percentile at face value.

baselines

Evaluated models (o3, o4-mini, Gemini 2.5 Pro, Claude 3.7 Sonnet, GPT-4.5 Preview) were current as of April 2025. As with any fast-moving capability benchmark, specific percentile numbers will already be stale for the newest models by the time a reader sees this page — the paper itself frames the finding as a widening trend rather than a fixed score, which is the right way to read it.

contamination

The shared 322-question set is deliberately not released publicly — it is conveyed only to organizations with an AI-safety track record — with a separate 43-question holdout reserved specifically to check for future leakage. That is a defensible tradeoff given the subject matter, but it also means outside readers cannot independently inspect question difficulty, re-verify the reported accuracy numbers against the raw items, or audit for grading errors; the benchmark's rigor has to be taken substantially on trust.

what's missed

VCT measures Q&A accuracy, not real-world uplift — whether pairing a person with a model actually improves their success at a physical wet-lab task remains untested, and the authors say so explicitly, proposing a wet-lab uplift study as necessary follow-up. The reported zero refusals is itself a notable finding about current model safety training, but it means VCT alone says nothing about whether deployed guardrails would intervene in practice.

would raise confidence

The wet-lab uplift study the authors themselves propose would connect Q&A accuracy to actual capability uplift. The baseline pool is fairly concentrated — 36 experts for percentile estimates, and the ten most prolific of 68 question-writers produced 51% of all 507 submissions — so a larger, more diverse contributor and baseline pool would tighten the percentile claims and reduce the chance that benchmark content skews toward a handful of contributors' specialties.

// bottom line

VCT is a careful, expert-sourced benchmark whose central finding — frontier models already match or exceed trained virologists on practical troubleshooting knowledge — is significant for dual-use governance regardless of the exact percentile. But it is a capability proxy, not an uplift measurement, and its safety-motivated lack of public release means its numbers must be trusted rather than independently reproduced.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. On VCT's 322-question benchmark, OpenAI's o3 (43.8% accuracy) outperformed 34 of 36 expert virologists on their own personally-tailored question sets — the 94th percentile — while every reasoning model tested beat the median human expert (who averaged 22.1%).
  2. The benchmark was built almost entirely from vetted human expert labor: 68 PhD-level virologists submitted questions, each passing two rounds of expert peer review plus non-expert "Google-proof" testing, narrowing 507 submissions to a 322-question shared set and a 43-question private holdout.
  3. The authors frame the result as a governance signal, not a leaderboard entry: because expert-level virology troubleshooting is inherently dual-use, they argue this kind of AI capability should be folded into existing dual-use life-sciences oversight frameworks, noting the gap between models and experts has been widening since Gemini 1.5 Pro first crossed this threshold in February 2024.
