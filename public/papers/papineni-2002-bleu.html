BLEU: a Method for Automatic Evaluation of Machine Translation

Measuring translation quality used to take human judges weeks or months. BLEU replaced it with a script you can run every night: clipped n-gram precision against reference translations, a brevity penalty standing in for recall, and a reported 0.99 correlation with human judges. The recipe outlived machine translation and became NLP's default — and most over-trusted — yardstick.

What problem is this solving?

Human evaluation of machine translation weighs adequacy, fidelity, and fluency — and in 2002 it was the only trustworthy yardstick. It was also ruinously slow. A careful human study "can take weeks or months to finish," and the labor "can not be reused": each new system version means a fresh round of judges. For a team shipping daily changes to an MT system, that is fatal. You cannot "weed out bad ideas from good ideas" if every comparison costs a month.

The authors frame this as an evaluation bottleneck — "a logjam of fruitful research ideas waiting to be released." The missing piece was a translation-quality metric that was simultaneously quick, inexpensive, language-independent, and correlated with human judgment, with near-zero marginal cost per run. Prior automatic ideas borrowed from speech recognition (word error rate) didn't transfer cleanly: a sentence has many equally good translations that differ legitimately in word choice and word order, so a single-reference, position-sensitive error rate punishes valid variation.

BLEU's pitch is deliberately modest. It is offered not as a replacement for human judgment but as an "understudy" — the bilingual evaluation understudy — that stands in for skilled judges "when there is need for quick or frequent evaluations." The bet: if an automatic number tracks human ranking closely enough, developers can iterate against the number and reserve expensive human studies for milestones.

// how it works

The method

The central idea is a single sentence: "The closer a machine translation is to a professional human translation, the better it is." So BLEU needs two ingredients — a numerical "closeness" metric, and a corpus of good human reference translations — and it measures closeness by counting matching word sequences. Two design moves make that counting robust.

1 · Modified (clipped) n-gram precision

Plain unigram precision — candidate words that appear in any reference, divided by candidate length — is trivially gamed. The paper's own example: a candidate of "the the the the the the the" scores a perfect 7/7 against references that merely contain "the." The fix is to clip each candidate n-gram's count to the maximum number of times it appears in any single reference:

Countclip = min( Count, Max Ref Count ) // "the" appears at most twice in a reference → contributes at most 2, so precision = 2/7

Unigram matches reward adequacy (did the right words show up?); longer n-grams reward fluency (are they in a plausible order?). On the paper's worked example, the good candidate scores a modified unigram precision of 17/18 and bigram 10/17, while the bad candidate scores 8/14 and 1/13 — the gap that lets a program rank them correctly. Over a test corpus, the modified precision for order n sums clipped matches across all candidate sentences and divides by the total candidate n-gram count:

pn = ΣC Σn-gram∈C Countclip(n-gram)ΣC′ Σn-gram′∈C′ Count(n-gram′)

2 · Combining orders: a geometric mean

Modified precision decays roughly exponentially with n (unigram ≫ bigram ≫ trigram), so a plain arithmetic average would be dominated by unigrams. BLEU instead takes a weighted average of the logarithms — equivalently, the geometric mean of p1…pN — with uniform weights wn = 1/N and a maximum order of N = 4 (which gave the best correlation with human judgment; 3- and 5-grams were comparable).

3 · The brevity penalty: recall, replaced

Precision alone never punishes a translation for being too short — a candidate of just "of the" can score 2/2 unigram, 1/1 bigram. The classical fix is recall, but with multiple references recall misbehaves: a good translation uses one acceptable word choice, not all of them ("I always invariably perpetually do" recalls more reference words yet is plainly worse than "I always do"). BLEU drops recall entirely and adds a multiplicative brevity penalty computed over the whole corpus, where c is total candidate length and r is the effective reference length (the sum of best-match reference lengths):

BP = 1 if c > r,  else e(1 − r/c)

Putting the pieces together gives the metric in one line (and a cleaner form in the log domain):

BLEU = BP · exp( Σn=1N wn log pn ) // log BLEU = min(1 − r/c, 0) + Σ wₙ log pₙ  ·  case-folding is the only normalization

The score ranges from 0 to 1. Because it demands matches at every n-gram order, few translations reach 1 — even a human translator generally won't. Crucially, BLEU is designed to be valid averaged over a corpus, where idiosyncratic sentence-level errors cancel out across many sentences and multiple reference styles; the authors are explicit that individual-sentence scores "will often vary from human judgments."

// what they found

The results

The validation runs on five "systems" — two human translators (H1, a non-native speaker of both Chinese and English; H2, a native English speaker) and three commercial MT systems (S1, S2, S3) — scored against two professional reference translations on a 500-sentence Chinese→English news corpus (40 stories). Every single n-gram order produces the same ranking: H2 > H1 ≫ S3 > S2 > S1, with a large gap between the best machine system and the worst human.

Aggregated to a single number, BLEU spreads the five systems out and the differences are statistically real. Splitting the 500 sentences into 20 blocks of 25 and running paired t-tests between each system and its neighbor, every gap clears the 95% bar (a paired t of 1.7 or above is significant) — including the genuinely close S2/S3 pair:

S1 (machine)0.05270.051—
S2 (machine)0.08290.0816
S3 (machine)0.09300.0903.4
H1 (human, non-native)0.19340.19224

Two practical caveats fall out of the numbers. First, scores are not comparable across reference counts: the same human translator scored 0.3468 against four references but 0.2571 against two. Second, a single reference is workable — randomly assigning one of the four references per story preserved the system ranking — provided the references don't all come from one translator.

Does the number track humans?

Two panels of ten judges rated 250 randomly ordered source–translation pairs from 1 (very bad) to 5 (very good): a monolingual group (native English, judging fluency/readability) and a bilingual Chinese–English group. Pairwise within-judge comparisons confirmed the ranking — for the monolingual group, S2 beat S1 by 0.326 mean-opinion-score points and S3 beat S2 by 0.114, both significant at 95%. Regressing the human scores on BLEU across the five systems gives the headline numbers: a correlation of 0.99 for the monolingual group and 0.96 for the bilingual group.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

BLEU is one of the most consequential evaluation papers ever written, so it is worth being precise about what it actually demonstrated — because the field went on to lean on it far harder than the evidence here supports.

Start with the headline. The famous 0.99 correlation is a regression over five data points — two humans and three MT systems — on one language pair (Chinese→English) and one domain (40 news stories). Five points is a fragile basis for a correlation, and these five are deliberately spread out: a large human-vs-machine chasm sits at one end, which mechanically inflates any correlation coefficient. The honest, harder test is whether BLEU separates the two closely matched commercial systems S2 (0.0829) and S3 (0.0930), and there the evidence is a paired t of 3.4 — real, but a long way from the comfort the "0.99" implies.

There is also a mild tune-on-the-test-set flavor: the choices that define BLEU (maximum order N = 4, uniform weights, geometric over arithmetic mean) are all justified as giving "the best correlation" on this same corpus, then reported as correlating well with it. And there is no competing automatic metric in the comparison — BLEU is validated against humans, not benchmarked against alternatives (few existed in 2002). So the paper never claims, and never tests, that BLEU is the best automatic metric; it claims it correlates with humans on this data.

What the metric structurally misses is, to the authors' credit, half-conceded in the paper itself. Footnote 4 admits sentence-level scores "often vary from human judgments" — BLEU is only valid averaged over a corpus, yet two decades of leaderboards quote it per system as if it were a fine-grained quality score. By construction it has no recall, no synonym or paraphrase awareness (a legitimate alternative wording is penalized unless a reference happens to use it), and no grammaticality beyond local n-gram overlap; the geometric mean drops a sentence's score to zero the instant any n-gram order has no match. And because the same human scored 0.3468 vs four references but 0.2571 vs two, a bare BLEU number is meaningless without its reference count, tokenization, and smoothing — precisely the cross-paper comparison the metric was later, routinely, abused for. Finally, the conclusion's gesture toward multi-language generality (Arabic, Chinese, French, Spanish) rests on a companion "to appear" paper, not on evidence here.

baselines

No competing automatic metric in the loop — BLEU is validated against human judges only (the field had few alternatives in 2002). The result is "tracks humans," not "beats metric X."

contamination

No data leakage in the modern sense, but the metric's free parameters (N = 4, uniform weights, geometric mean) are chosen for best correlation on the very corpus then used to report that correlation.

what's missed

Sentence-level validity (conceded in fn. 4), recall, synonyms/paraphrase, grammaticality. Scores are not comparable across reference counts, tokenization, or smoothing — yet are constantly compared anyway.

would raise confidence

More closely-spaced systems near human quality; multiple language pairs and domains in this paper; reported sentence-level correlations; an explicit reference-count / tokenization sensitivity study.

// bottom line

BLEU's enduring contribution is operational, not statistical: it turned MT evaluation from a months-long human study into a metric you can run every night, which is why the clipped-precision + brevity-penalty recipe became the template for a generation of automatic metrics. But the original validation is thin by modern standards — five systems, one language pair — and the field's habit of treating a single corpus-level BLEU number as a portable, cross-setup quality score is exactly the over-reading the paper's own caveats warned against. The fixes that followed (METEOR, chrF, BERTScore, COMET) are, in effect, a long list of the things this table of five numbers could not see.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. The recipe. BLEU = a corpus-level brevity penalty × the geometric mean of clipped (modified) n-gram precisions up to 4-grams. Clipping kills the "the the the" exploit; the brevity penalty replaces ill-behaved recall.
  2. The evidence. A 0.99 (monolingual) / 0.96 (bilingual) correlation with human judges — but over only five systems on one Chinese→English news corpus, and explicitly valid only when averaged over a corpus, never per sentence.
  3. The legacy. Its real win was making MT evaluation cheap, language-independent, and reusable, unblocking the research loop — and in doing so it became NLP's default and most over-trusted yardstick, the baseline every later metric was built to repair.
