Prometheus: Inducing Fine-grained Evaluation Capability in Language Models

Give GPT-4 a custom score rubric and a reference answer and it can grade almost anything — the catch is you’re locked into a proprietary API. Prometheus fine-tunes an open 13B model on 100K GPT-4-written (rubric, response, feedback, score) examples, and the result correlates with human raters at 0.897 Pearson, edging out GPT-4’s own 0.882 on the same 45 rubrics.

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

By late 2023, using GPT-4 as an evaluator for long-form LLM responses had become the default. It works well when appropriately prompted, but the paper identifies three structural problems with leaning on a proprietary model as the field’s evaluation backbone: its closed-source nature keeps the academic community from inspecting or improving it; its uncontrolled versioning means the same prompt can silently produce different judgments as the API updates, undermining reproducibility; and its cost is real at scale — the paper notes that evaluating four LLM variants across four sizes (7B–65B) on 1,000 instances with GPT-4 can run over $2,000.

There’s a second, more specific gap beneath the cost problem: existing evaluation setups are coarse-grained. A prompt like AlpacaFarm’s, which asks a judge to pick the response that’s “generally preferred,” collapses everything a user might care about — humor, cultural sensitivity, technical precision, tone — into one undifferentiated preference judgment. Real evaluation needs often look like “which response is more playful and humorous” or “which response shows more care for cultural sensitivity” — customized, fine-grained criteria, not a single default. The paper’s own pilot found that even the largest open model available at the time (70B) couldn’t reliably evaluate against a customized rubric the way GPT-4 could.

// how it works

The method

Prometheus is a 13B evaluator fine-tuned from Llama-2-Chat on a new dataset, the Feedback Collection: 1K fine-grained score rubrics × 20 instructions each (20K instructions total) × 5 responses per instruction (100K responses, one per score level 1–5), each response paired with GPT-4-written feedback and a score. Every training instance packages four inputs — instruction, response to evaluate, customized score rubric, and reference answer — and asks the model to produce two outputs: a natural-language feedback (a chain-of-thought-style rationale) and an integer score from 1 to 5, separated by a literal [RESULT] marker to prevent degeneration at inference.

Why the reference answer matters as much as the rubric

The paper’s central methodological bet is that giving the evaluator LM reference materials — not just the instruction and response — is what makes fine-grained evaluation tractable for a model far smaller than GPT-4. Without a rubric, the model has to infer what aspects matter; without a reference answer, it effectively has to solve the instruction itself before it can judge whether a candidate response is good. Supplying both lets a 13B model focus purely on comparison rather than joint task-solving-and-judging. An ablation later confirms this is not a minor effect: removing the reference answer is the single most damaging cut to performance (Table 6).

Building 1K diverse rubrics, then 100K balanced examples

The dataset is built entirely by prompting GPT-4 in four steps: (1) the authors hand-write 50 seed rubrics; (2) GPT-4 brainstorms and paraphrases from 4 sampled seeds at a time, iterated for 10 rounds, expanding to 1,000 rubrics; (3) GPT-4 generates 20 rubric-relevant instructions per rubric (20K total); (4) GPT-4 generates the response/feedback/score for each of the five score levels per instruction, holding the count exactly equal across scores 1–5 (20K each) to avoid decision bias, and generating two independent score-5 responses so one can serve as the reference answer without leaking the exact eval response.

// what they found

The results

The headline test measures Pearson correlation with human evaluators across 45 customized rubrics sampled from Feedback Bench (unseen rubrics), MT Bench, and Vicuna Bench.

Beyond the scalar score, human annotators also compared feedback quality head-to-head in a pairwise setting. Prometheus’s feedback was preferred over GPT-4’s 58.62% of the time (excluding ties) and over GPT-3.5-turbo’s 79.57% of the time.

Correlation with GPT-4-as-judge (rather than humans) across a much larger sweep — 1,222 rubrics, 2,360 instances across Feedback Bench, Vicuna Bench, MT Bench, and FLASK Eval — tells a more mixed story. On the in-distribution Feedback Bench splits, Prometheus 13B reaches 0.861 Pearson (seen rubrics) and 0.860 (unseen rubrics), clearing every baseline including all three GPT-4 snapshots tested (0.733–0.754 across GPT-4-0314/0613/RECENT). But on Vicuna Bench, MT Bench, and FLASK Eval — datasets with a different instruction style than the Feedback Collection’s training distribution — Prometheus trails GPT-4 clearly: 0.466 vs. 0.925 on Vicuna Bench, 0.473 vs. 0.952 on MT Bench.

On two ranking-grading human-preference benchmarks it was never directly trained for — HHH Alignment and MT Bench Human Judgment — Prometheus 13B beats two specialist reward models (StanfordNLP Reward Model, ALMOST) and its own base model by +5.43 and +5.38 percentage points respectively, evidence that absolute-grading training transfers to ranking without ever seeing a ranking example.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

The paper is candid about the biggest confound in its own headline number, and it’s worth restating plainly: every training feedback and score in the Feedback Collection was generated by GPT-4, and the human-correlation eval set overlaps in style with that same distribution (Feedback Bench is “crafted with the exact same procedure” as the training data; two of the three human-eval subsets, MT Bench and Vicuna Bench, still share short-instruction, conversational framing). A 0.897 human-correlation for a model distilled from GPT-4’s own scoring judgment is partly a measurement of how well Prometheus imitates GPT-4’s grading style, not purely independent evidence of “fine-grained evaluation capability” from first principles. The paper’s own Section 6.2 acknowledges this directly, attributing part of the GPT-4-beating result to self-consistency effects in how GPT-4 was sampled for comparison.

The out-of-distribution numbers are the more honest signal, and they cut the other way from the abstract’s framing. On Vicuna Bench, MT Bench, and FLASK Eval — datasets whose instructions look different from the Feedback Collection’s — Prometheus’s correlation with GPT-4 drops to 0.466–0.473, well below GPT-4’s own 0.785–0.952 self-consistency range (across its three snapshots) on those same sets. The paper explains this as a distribution-shift effect (Feedback Collection instructions run long and detailed; MT/Vicuna prompts run short), which is a fair diagnosis, but it means “on par with GPT-4” holds specifically for the human-evaluation setting the paper foregrounds, and does not hold for GPT-4-agreement on standard chat benchmarks.

The human-preference-quality result is genuinely interesting but has a documented downside: Figure 6 shows Prometheus’s feedback is rejected mainly for being “overly critical” (31.43% of rejections), versus GPT-4’s feedback being rejected mainly for being “too general and abstract” (44.00%). Those are different failure modes, not a strict quality ordering — a downstream user who wants encouraging, general feedback may prefer GPT-4’s failure mode over Prometheus’s.

baselines

Fair and current for the period — GPT-3.5-turbo, multiple GPT-4 snapshots (0314/0613/RECENT), Llama-2-Chat at 7B/13B/70B, and two purpose-built reward models (StanfordNLP, ALMOST). The Llama2-Chat-13B+Coarse ablation is a genuinely useful control isolating the value of fine-grained (vs. generic) rubrics.

contamination

Not leakage in the traditional sense, but a real distillation confound: training feedback/scores and a chunk of the eval protocol both originate from GPT-4, so high GPT-4-style agreement partly reflects imitation of the teacher rather than independent judgment quality. Feedback Bench is explicitly in-distribution with training (same generation procedure), and the paper reports it separately rather than folding it into the headline — a good practice worth noting.

what’s missed

No error analysis of which kinds of rubrics or instructions Prometheus fails on beyond the coarse Vicuna/MT-Bench/FLASK split; no test of robustness to adversarial or malformed rubrics; the human evaluation explicitly excluded coding and math questions, so the correlation numbers say nothing about technical-domain grading (later addressed qualitatively, not quantitatively, via the Code-Llama ablation in Figure 19).

would raise confidence

Human correlation on genuinely out-of-distribution, long-tail rubrics (not just Feedback Bench’s unseen-but-same-procedure subset); a feedback-faithfulness check verifying the written rationale actually matches the assigned score; results with a training feedback source other than GPT-4, to separate “can imitate a strong judge” from “can evaluate independently.”

// bottom line

Prometheus is a well-executed, influential proof of concept: pairing a customized rubric with a reference answer really does let a 13B open model approach GPT-4’s evaluation behavior on the specific human-correlation test the paper designs around, and the resulting Feedback Collection recipe became the template the field reused (including the paper’s own successor, Prometheus 2). But the “on par with GPT-4” claim is narrower than the abstract suggests — it holds on in-style, GPT-4-flavored evaluation and degrades substantially (0.47 vs. GPT-4’s own 0.79–0.95 self-consistency) on standard chat benchmarks with a different instruction distribution. Read it as a strong existence proof for rubric-conditioned open evaluators, not a drop-in GPT-4 replacement for arbitrary evaluation tasks.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. The recipe: condition an evaluator LM on a user-defined, fine-grained score rubric and a reference answer, not just the instruction and response — the reference answer ablates worst when removed (Table 6), because it lets the evaluator skip solving the instruction and focus purely on comparison.
  2. The headline number is real but distillation-flavored: Prometheus 13B reaches 0.897 Pearson correlation with humans (vs. GPT-4’s 0.882) — but its training feedback and much of its eval protocol trace back to GPT-4, and its GPT-4-agreement collapses to 0.47 on standard chat benchmarks (Vicuna/MT-Bench) outside the Feedback Collection’s instruction style.
  3. Absolute-grading training transfers to ranking: despite never training on pairwise comparisons, Prometheus beats specialist reward models on HHH Alignment and MT Bench Human Judgment — evidence a single well-built evaluator can serve both scoring and ranking use cases.
  4. Reusable artifact: the Feedback Collection — 1K rubrics × 20K instructions × 100K GPT-4-scored responses, balanced across score levels 1–5 — became the direct ancestor of Prometheus 2’s Feedback + Preference Collections.
