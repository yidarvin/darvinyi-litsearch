Adversarial NLI: A New Benchmark for Natural Language Understanding

A three-round, human-and-model-in-the-loop adversarial collection procedure for NLI: annotators try to fool the current best model, a second annotator verifies the failures, the verified examples become the next round's train/dev/test split, and a stronger model is retrained to be the next round's adversary.

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

By late 2019, natural language inference (NLI) — deciding whether a hypothesis is entailed by, contradicts, or is neutral with respect to a premise — had a saturation problem. SNLI (2015) and MultiNLI gave rise to combined benchmarks like GLUE, which "did not last as long as we would have hoped after the advent of BERT," and had to be hastily extended into the harder SuperGLUE within about a year. Contrast that with vision: it took roughly 15 years to reach near-human performance on MNIST and about 7 years to surpass humans on ImageNet. NLU benchmarks were instead being solved almost as fast as they were released — which raises a second, sharper question: were models actually getting better at understanding language, or just better at exploiting the benchmark?

The evidence pointed at the latter. A growing line of work — Gururangan et al.'s "Annotation Artifacts in Natural Language Inference Data" chief among it, alongside Poliak et al., Tsuchiya, Glockner et al., and others — showed state-of-the-art NLI models learn to exploit spurious statistical patterns baked into how crowdworkers phrase hypotheses (certain words correlate suspiciously well with "contradiction" or "entailment" regardless of the premise), rather than learning the flexible, generalizable meaning humans do. A benchmark collected once, by asking annotators to freely write premise/hypothesis pairs, encodes whatever unconscious shortcuts those annotators fell into — and a fixed test set can never catch a model that has learned to exploit those shortcuts, because the artifacts are baked into the test set too. Two problems, then, sit on top of each other: static NLI benchmarks stop being difficult within roughly a year of a strong new pretrained model, and even while they're "unsolved" their headline accuracy numbers may be measuring artifact-exploitation more than genuine language understanding. ANLI's premise is that both problems have the same fix: stop collecting the benchmark once, and instead make data collection adversarial and continuous, explicitly targeting whatever the current best model gets wrong.

// how it works

The method

The paper introduces HAMLET — Human-And-Model-in-the-Loop Enabled Training — a four-step loop repeated once per collection round (Figure 1). Step 1 (write): a human "writer" is given a context (premise) and a target label (entailment, neutral, or contradiction) and must write a hypothesis that a live, current-best NLI model will misclassify — effectively playing a "white-hat hacker" trying to break the model. Step 2 (feedback): the model scores the hypothesis in real time and shows the writer its prediction; if the model gets it right, the writer can revise and retry (up to a fixed number of tries per context before moving on). Step 3 (verify and split): once a writer produces an example the model gets wrong, a separate "verifier" annotator checks whether the example's label is actually correct. Model-correct examples are folded into the training set; only verified, model-wrong examples become development/test data, and the test set is further restricted to "exclusive" annotators never seen in training and rebalanced across label classes, so a model can't simply memorize a small pool of writers' idiosyncrasies. Step 4 (retrain): a new, stronger model is trained on all data collected so far and becomes the adversary for the next round — each round is collected against a harder target than the last, by construction.

Three rounds were collected this way. Round 1 (A1) used Wikipedia passages (retrieved via HotpotQA) as contexts and a BERT-based model trained on SNLI + MultiNLI as the adversary. Round 2 (A2) reused Wikipedia contexts but swapped in a stronger adversary — an ensemble of RoBERTa models fine-tuned on SNLI, MultiNLI, FEVER, and round 1's data, with a different ensemble member sampled per turn so writers couldn't over-fit to one model's specific blind spots. Round 3 (A3) diversified contexts beyond Wikipedia — news (Common Crawl), fiction (StoryCloze, CBT), formal spoken text (court and presidential-debate transcripts from the MASC corpus), procedural text (WikiHow), and the longer premises from GLUE's RTE task — against an even stronger RoBERTa ensemble trained with A1 and A2 folded in. Verified model-wrong examples also came with a short, free-text explanation from the writer for why the model failed, plus (on a 500-example sample per round) linguist-annotated tags for the kind of reasoning required (numerical/quantitative, reference/coreference, standard logical inference, lexical, "tricky" wordplay, and outside-knowledge reasoning).

The collection cost is visible directly in the dataset statistics. Round 1 produced 16,946 / 1,000 / 1,000 train/dev/test examples from 2,080 contexts, with an unverified model error rate of 29.68% (18.33% once verified) and a mean of 3.4 tries and 199.2 seconds per good verified example. Round 2, against a stronger adversary, needed nearly 6.4 tries and 355.3 seconds on average to produce a verified error, and yielded a lower error rate (16.59% unverified / 8.07% verified) from 45,460 / 1,000 / 1,000 examples over 2,694 contexts. Round 3 — more contexts (6,002) across more domains — produced the largest split (100,459 / 1,200 / 1,200) at a broadly similar per-example cost (6.4 tries, 284.0 seconds) and error rate (17.47% / 8.60%). Combined, ANLI totals 162,865 / 3,200 / 3,200 train/dev/test examples over 10,776 unique contexts.

// what they found

The results

The headline result is that difficulty compounds across rounds, and that difficulty transfers as a training signal. A BERT model trained only on SNLI + MultiNLI (the round-1 adversary itself) scores 00.0% on A1 by construction — every example in the test set was, tautologically, something that exact model got wrong — but its performance craters just as hard out of round: 28.9% on A2 and 28.8% on A3, both near or below the 33.3% three-way chance rate. The RoBERTa ensemble used as the round-2 adversary (trained on SNLI+MultiNLI+FEVER+A1) reaches 68.7% on A1 but only 19.3% on A2 — worse than random guessing on the very data it was built to be fooled by, and the round-3 adversary (add A2 to training) scores 71.2% on A1, 44.3% on A2, but only 20.4% on A3. Each round is, by design, exactly the set of examples the previous round's best model cannot solve.

Training on more rounds recovers robustness and sets new state of the art on the older, static benchmarks it was never targeting. RoBERTa fine-tuned on SNLI + MultiNLI + FEVER + all three ANLI rounds reaches 73.8% / 48.9% / 44.4% on A1/A2/A3 (53.7% on the combined ANLI test set), and — despite ANLI data looking nothing like SNLI or MultiNLI — actually improves on those two static benchmarks rather than trading off against them: 92.9% on SNLI (versus the prior state of the art, MT-DNN's 91.6%) and 91.0% / 90.7% on MNLI-matched/mismatched (versus 90.2%/90.2% reported for RoBERTa's own paper). Interestingly, the RoBERTa base models for rounds 2 and 3 are actually outperformed on their own target rounds by plain BERT and XLNet trained on the full A1+A2+A3 stack (BERT: 49.0% on A2, 46.1% on A3; XLNet: 50.7% / 48.3%) — evidence that ANLI's rounds captured architecture-specific weaknesses in RoBERTa specifically, not weaknesses common to all transformers.

Training on ANLI also transfers to benchmarks it was never designed for. On the pre-existing NLI stress tests (Naik et al., 2018) and SNLI-Hard, RoBERTa trained on everything including ANLI reaches 84.7% on SNLI-Hard (versus 72.7% for prior models), 80.6% on the Numerical Reasoning stress test (versus 28.8%), and clear gains on Negation (62.2%/61.9% vs. 48.8%/46.6%) and Word Overlap (67.4%/65.6% vs. 50.0%/50.2%). A hypothesis-only ablation — training a model on ANLI hypotheses with the premise hidden — performs far worse on ANLI (highest 51.0% on A1, dropping to 41–43% on A2/A3) than the same hypothesis-only setup trained on SNLI/MNLI-inclusive data reaches on SNLI/MNLI itself (59.8–71.8%), evidence that ANLI resists the hypothesis-only artifact exploitation that motivated the whole project in the first place.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

The paper's own numbers show the "never-ending" framing has a cost curve, and it is not flat. The mean number of tries needed to produce one good, verified adversarial example nearly doubles from round 1 (3.4) to rounds 2 and 3 (6.4 each), and mean time per good example rises from 199.2 seconds to 355.3 (round 2) and 284.0 (round 3). That is exactly what you'd expect — a stronger adversary is harder to fool — but it means each additional round of "never-ending learning" gets more expensive to sustain per usable example, and the paper never reports what that translated to in annotator pay or total compute/labor budget. A scheme whose selling point is that it doesn't saturate the way static benchmarks do still needs its per-round cost curve extrapolated a few rounds out before "never-ending" is more than aspirational; three rounds is a proof of concept, not evidence the loop stays economical indefinitely.

The composition of what counts as "hard" also drifts across rounds in a way that reflects annotator incentives as much as any principled notion of NLU difficulty. The 500-example-per-round linguistic tagging (Table 7) shows Numerical & Quantitative reasoning collapsing from 38% of round-1 examples to 32% (round 2) to just 10% (round 3), while "Reasoning & Facts" (outside-knowledge inference) climbs from 53% to 59% to 63% and stays the dominant category throughout. Because writers are non-expert crowdworkers paid per successful adversarial example within a fixed, gamified UI (Figure 9's "Beat the System!"), the mix of attack strategies that gets discovered is shaped by whatever non-experts find fastest and most reliably profitable to write against a specific model family — not by a principled sampling of the space of genuine reasoning failures. A model that becomes robust to ANLI-style adversarial hypotheses is robust to what crowdworkers, under a specific incentive structure and UI, converged on — which is a real but narrower claim than "robust to hard natural language inference" in general.

baselines

Reasonable and honestly framed for the moment: BERT was the appropriate SOTA adversary for round 1 (Oct 2019), and RoBERTa ensembles for rounds 2–3 as the field's frontier moved. But each round's difficulty is therefore bespoke to one specific model family's blind spots — the paper's own Table 3 shows BERT and XLNet actually beat RoBERTa's own base models on A2/A3, meaning some of what ANLI collects is RoBERTa-specific brittleness rather than a universal transformer weakness.

contamination

Low risk by construction — dev/test come only from verified, model-wrong examples, with an "exclusive" annotator subset (ANLI-E) held fully out of training, and the paper reports negligible ANLI vs. ANLI-E gaps. The more relevant leakage question is temporal: as pretraining corpora grow to include ANLI itself and its GitHub release, later models could see round-1/2 examples during pretraining in a way this paper couldn't anticipate.

what's missed

Cost accounting (no reported annotator pay or total collection budget despite tries/time both roughly doubling by round 2–3), and any test of whether the loop keeps producing genuinely new failure modes past round 3 versus converging on variations of the same "Reasoning & Facts" template that already dominates 58% of examples on average.

would raise confidence

A fourth and fifth round run against a materially different model family (not just a stronger member of the BERT/RoBERTa lineage), reporting whether error rate keeps falling and whether the linguistic-tag distribution keeps drifting or stabilizes — plus an explicit per-round cost figure so "never-ending" can be evaluated as a budget claim, not just a methodology claim.

// bottom line

ANLI is a genuinely well-instrumented answer to the annotation-artifacts and benchmark-saturation critiques it cites — the human-and-model-in-the-loop design directly targets the failure mode (a fixed test set eventually gets gamed) rather than just building a harder one-shot dataset. But "never-ending learning" is a methodology, not yet a demonstrated property: the paper shows three rounds work and get more expensive to run, and leaves open — for later work, including the Bowman & Dahl line of "what will it take to fix benchmarking" critique this dataset itself gets cited by — whether round N+1 keeps finding real gaps or just harder-to-write busywork against whatever model happens to be the adversary that year.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. ANLI operationalizes "the benchmark should chase the model": HAMLET runs a four-step human-and-model-in-the-loop loop (write → get model feedback → verify and split → retrain) across three rounds, collecting 162,865 / 3,200 / 3,200 train/dev/test NLI examples where every dev/test example is, by construction, something a strong contemporary model got wrong and a second human confirmed was actually right.
  2. Difficulty compounds and generalizes: the round-2 adversary model scores just 19.3% on A2 (below chance), while stacking all three rounds into training pushes RoBERTa to new state of the art on benchmarks it wasn't built for — 92.9% SNLI, 91.0%/90.7% MNLI — and to large gains on independent NLI stress tests (84.7% SNLI-Hard, up from 72.7%).
  3. The scheme buys durability at a real, rising annotator cost (tries and time per good adversarial example roughly double from round 1 to rounds 2–3) and a documented shift in what "hard" means as rounds progress (numerical reasoning drops from 38% to 10% of examples by round 3) — a reminder that even a self-described "never-ending" benchmark needs its per-round economics and drift interrogated, not assumed.
