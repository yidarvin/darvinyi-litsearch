LiveBench: A Challenging, Contamination-Limited LLM Benchmark

A benchmark built to keep moving: monthly-refreshed math, coding, reasoning, data-analysis, instruction-following, and language tasks graded by objective ground truth instead of an LLM judge — and still hard enough that the best model at release, o1-preview, clears only 64.7%.

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

By mid-2024, benchmark evaluation for LLMs had a well-known contamination problem, and both of the era's standard fixes were themselves compromised. The contamination problem is straightforward: benchmarks get published on the open internet, and modern LLMs are trained on huge scrapes of that internet, so a model has often already seen the test questions — sometimes the answers too — before it's "evaluated" on them. The paper cites concrete evidence for this: LLM performance on Codeforces problems plummets sharply right around each model's training cutoff date, and before that cutoff, performance correlates strongly with how many times a given problem appears on GitHub, a signature of pattern-matching against memorized solutions rather than solving from scratch. GSM8K, one of the most widely cited math benchmarks in the field, shows the same tell: hand-crafted variants of its problems reveal that several prominent models have measurably overfit to the original question set.

The standard response has been to abandon static questions altogether and crowdsource fresh prompts instead, judged either by humans (Chatbot Arena) or by another LLM (AlpacaEval, MT-Bench, Arena-Hard). Both routes trade one problem for another. LLM judges are fast and can score open-ended answers that have no single correct string, but they are demonstrably biased — favoring their own model family's outputs, favoring longer and more verbose answers regardless of correctness — and, as this paper's own appendix ablation later shows, they are simply unreliable graders on hard math and logic problems even when explicitly told to check against ground truth. Human judging avoids that particular bias but introduces its own: it is slow and expensive to scale, annotators may not probe the full breadth of a model's capability or may construct poorly-designed prompts, and human-to-human annotation variance is itself a well-documented source of unpredictable outcomes.

So the actual gap LiveBench targets is narrower and sharper than "benchmarks get contaminated": can a benchmark stay fresh — immune to the "the model has already seen this" failure mode — without falling back on a judge that introduces its own bias? The paper's answer is to refuse both the static-benchmark route and the LLM-judge/human-crowdsourcing routes, and instead build tasks that draw on genuinely new source material on a recurring schedule while being scored by a mechanically checkable, objective ground truth — no judgment call required, human or machine.

// how it works

The method

LiveBench organizes 18 tasks into six categories — math, coding, reasoning, data analysis, instruction following, and language comprehension — and builds each task one of two ways: either it mines a task-specific live information source (a recent Kaggle or Socrata dataset, a fresh arXiv abstract, a Guardian news article, an IMDb synopsis for a movie released after January 1, 2024) so the underlying content genuinely postdates any plausible training cutoff, or it takes an existing, well-known benchmark and builds a demonstrably harder, differently-shaped variant of it (Big-Bench Hard's Web of Lies, the AMPS math generator, IFEval). Every task targets 40–100 questions and is tuned toward a 30–70% success rate on the strongest contemporary models: hard enough to leave room to differentiate, not so hard that scores collapse to the floor.

Math draws on three distinct question types. Competitions repurposes AMC12 2023 (both A and B), the UK's Senior Mathematical Challenge 2023, and AIME 2024 (I and II) — genuine, expert-authored contest problems — but rewrites the prose, reorders the multiple-choice answer options, and changes the required output format, so a model can't simply pattern-match the original problem string. Olympiad is the most structurally novel task in the paper: rather than trusting an LLM (or an auto-formalizer into Lean) to judge whether a USAMO/IMO 2024 proof is correct — a task with no available formal-correctness guarantee either way — LiveBench masks out a subset of the equations from a genuinely correct proof, scrambles their order, and asks the model to reinsert them in the right positions, generating three hardness variants per problem (10%, 50%, and 80% of formulae masked) and scoring by the edit distance between the predicted and true ordering. AMPS_Hard procedurally synthesizes fresh math questions (derivatives, integrals, completing the square, factoring polynomials) using a harder distribution of primitives than the original AMPS/MATH generators, and grades them with Sympy for symbolic and numeric equivalence rather than exact string match.

Coding reuses LiveCodeBench's own contamination-conscious pipeline: 78 problems pulled from LeetCode and AtCoder released in or after November 2023 (LCB Generation), plus a new Completion task that strips the last 15–70% of a partially-complete LeetCode solution and asks the model to finish it — testing whether it can parse both the problem statement and the existing code's intent, rather than regurgitate a memorized full solution.

Reasoning includes Web of Lies v2, a substantially harder rebuild of the Big-Bench-Hard truth-teller/liar puzzle that adds red-herring characters, evaluates more people at once, and layers in an extra deductive step; procedurally generated Zebra Puzzles (3–4 people, 3–4 attributes, constraint statements drawn from the harder half of a 20-level difficulty scale, with a guaranteed-unique solution verified by an included solver); and 50 handwritten Spatial Reasoning questions that ask models to reason about the intersections and orientations of 2D/3D shapes with no diagram provided at all.

Data Analysis turns three practitioner workflows into graded tasks against fresh Kaggle and Socrata tables: column type annotation (given a name and a handful of sampled values, name the true column type), table reformatting (rewrite a table losslessly across JSON, JSONL, Markdown, CSV, TSV, and HTML), and join-column prediction (propose the correct join-key mapping between two column-anonymized, overlapping tables), scored by accuracy@1 or F1 depending on the task.

Instruction Following borrows IFEval's idea of verifiable constraints ("write more than 300 words," "end with this exact phrase"), but trims IFEval's original 25-instruction list down to the 16 the authors judge both genuinely real-world and unambiguously checkable by script, then — instead of IFEval's generic "write a travel blog" prompts — grounds every question in the opening sentences of a real Guardian news article and asks the model to paraphrase, simplify, summarize, or generate a story from it while obeying two or three randomly composed, deconflicted instructions at once. The score blends prompt-level accuracy (did the model satisfy every instruction in the prompt) with instruction-level accuracy (what fraction of the individual instructions it satisfied), averaged into one number per question.

Language Comprehension is the most linguistically playful category: Connections reimplements the New York Times word-grouping puzzle at three difficulties (15 questions of eight words, 15 of twelve, 20 of sixteen); Typos programmatically re-corrupts recent, typo-free arXiv abstracts by replacing correctly spelled words with common human misspellings at a randomly sampled rate p ~ U(0.5, 0.7) per question, then asks the model to restore only the misspellings while leaving every other stylistic choice — commas, US-vs-British spelling — untouched; and Plot Unscrambling shuffles the sentences of post-January-2024 movie synopses (7 to 66 sentences long) and asks the model to restore the original causal order, scored with a Levenshtein-distance-based fuzzy match rather than requiring verbatim transcription:

score = 1 − dnsentences,gt // d = Levenshtein edit distance between predicted and ground-truth sentence order; a perfect match scores 1, and the metric tolerates minor transcription slips instead of demanding verbatim reproduction

Underlying all six categories is a maintenance commitment, not a one-time release: the authors replace roughly a sixth of the question set every month (a full refresh cycle of about six months), choosing which tasks to update based on which are oldest and which have become easiest for top models, and they hold each new batch of questions private for one month before publishing it — so the public leaderboard always has a guaranteed sixth of completely undisclosed questions in play. The result, before a single model is even run, is a task list explicitly engineered so that five structurally different notions of "correct" — accuracy@1, F1, Sympy-checked symbolic equivalence, Levenshtein-based edit distance, and a blended prompt/instruction-level accuracy — all collapse into regex-parseable, judge-free grading.

// what they found

The results

Across the paper's frozen 40-model snapshot — from 0.5B-parameter minis up to Llama-3.1-405B and the newly-released o1 reasoning models, spanning roughly September–November 2024 — the headline LiveBench Score, an unweighted mean of the six category averages, tops out at 64.7, held by o1-preview-2024-09-12, which the authors report as 6% better than every other model. That is a comfortable margin at the top but nowhere near saturation: on a benchmark whose grading is entirely mechanical, with no judge to please and no partial credit for a confidently wrong-formatted answer, the strongest model in the world at the time still misses roughly a third of the questions.

The category breakdown shows real disagreement about who is actually "best" depending on which skill matters to you. o1-preview leads Data Analysis (64.0), Language (68.7), and Math (62.9), but claude-3-5-sonnet-20241022 (58.5 overall) posts the single best Coding score of the top 15, 67.1, well clear of o1-preview's 50.8 — a reminder that o1-preview's edge is concentrated, not uniform. o1-mini-2024-09-12 (56.7 overall) is the strongest reasoner by a wide margin at 72.3, consistent with a model built to spend more inference-time compute deliberating; gemini-exp-1121 (56.0 overall) is, somewhat surprisingly, the best Instruction Follower of the entire field at 80.1 despite a mediocre 40.0 on Math. Among open-weight models, Llama-3.1-405B-Instruct-Turbo posts 51.1 overall — reported by the authors as essentially tying Qwen2.5-72B-Instruct for the best open-source result and clearing GPT-4-Turbo (49.6) — while Phi-3.5-MoE-Instruct, at only 6.6B active parameters, is reported to land on par with the much larger Mixtral-8x22B and ahead of GPT-3.5.

Which single task best predicts overall LiveBench performance also matters for how much to trust the aggregate score. Category-level correlations against the overall average run from math (r = 0.9477) and reasoning (r = 0.9439) at the top down to instruction following at the bottom (r = 0.8174) — the category that correlates most weakly with everything else. At the individual-task level, web_of_lies_v2 (r = 0.9136) and math_comp (r = 0.9035) are the two most representative single tasks of a model's overall standing, while tablereformat (r = 0.6840) and spatial (r = 0.7294) are the least representative.

The paper also checks LiveBench against two prominent judge-based alternatives: Chatbot Arena (human preference) and Arena-Hard (GPT-4-judged). LiveBench correlates at r = 0.91 with Chatbot Arena and r = 0.88 with Arena-Hard across the models common to both — strong agreement in aggregate — but the disagreements are the interesting part. GPT-4-0125-preview and GPT-4-Turbo-2024-04-09 score substantially better on Arena-Hard relative to LiveBench than the trend line predicts, which the authors attribute to Arena-Hard's own use of GPT-4 as its judge — a same-family self-preference effect. Gemini-1.5 models, conversely, punch above their LiveBench weight on Chatbot Arena, which the authors read as evidence of a human-preferred output style that doesn't track raw correctness.

An appendix ablation puts a number on exactly how bad LLM judging gets on the hardest material. Using GPT-4-Turbo as judge to grade GPT-4-Turbo's and Claude-3-Opus's own answers on AMC12 2024, AIME 2024, SMC 2023, and Zebra Puzzles, the ground-truth-vs-LLM-judge disagreement is large and inconsistent in direction: on AIME 2024, GPT-4-Turbo's true accuracy is 13.793%, but the LLM judge scores it at 35.714% — more than double; on Zebra Puzzles, ground truth is 38% but the judge reports 68%. For Claude-3-Opus the judge sometimes swings the other way (AMC12: ground truth 56% vs. judge 42.857%). Judge error rates run from 10.3% up to 46.0% across all four tasks for both judge/model pairs — "far above a reasonable value," in the authors' own assessment, for using an LLM to grade math and logic problems it can't reliably solve itself.

The maintenance record so far backs up the "live" half of the name: rank correlation between the original release and the first monthly update, and between the first and second updates, both exceed 0.997 — model rankings hold steady even as roughly a sixth of the question pool turns over — while the mean and median score across models present in every iteration dropped about 1.2%, consistent with the benchmark growing modestly harder as easy, memorizable content cycles out.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

LiveBench's central bet is that objective ground-truth grading and frequent refreshing together solve contamination and judge-bias at the same time. Both halves of that bet deserve scrutiny — and the paper is unusually candid about the weak points of its own answer. Its Appendix A.7, "Contamination in LiveBench," is worth reading before taking the "contamination-limited" half of the title at face value.

Does monthly refresh actually prevent contamination, or just delay it? The appendix distinguishes test-set contamination (the definition used everywhere else in the paper) from "task contamination" (train-test distribution similarity), and admits LiveBench does not fully satisfy even the narrower, first definition: the Math Competitions task's AMC questions have undergone only a low level of modification from their original November 2023 source, and some coding questions are likewise pulled from a November 2023 LiveCodeBench release — meaning a chunk of the "live" benchmark was already seven-plus months old at the paper's June 2024 release. And by construction, once any batch of questions goes public it sits on GitHub and HuggingFace indefinitely, fully crawlable by every subsequent pretraining run. The one-month embargo buys exactly one month of protection for a sixth of the question pool; the other five-sixths is permanently exposed the moment it's posted. "Live" here means the benchmark's frontier keeps moving forward, which is a real and useful property — it stops the kind of retroactive gaming that the GSM8K variants exposed — but it's a weaker guarantee than "contamination-limited" might suggest on first read, and whether the promised every-six-months full refresh has actually kept pace with newer, more voracious pretraining corpora since 2024 is a question the paper can't answer prospectively.

Are the objectively-gradable, largely synthetic tasks actually diverse enough? By construction, LiveBench only asks questions with a single checkable answer — the authors say so themselves in their own Limitations section, flagging that ground-truth scoring cannot be used for open-ended cases like writing a travel guide. That's an honest, load-bearing exclusion: the entire open-ended-generation surface that Chatbot Arena and AlpacaEval exist to measure — the conversational and creative-writing use cases many people actually use these models for — is out of scope by design, not oversight. Within what remains, several tasks are also synthetic and procedurally generated rather than naturally occurring (Zebra Puzzles, Spatial Reasoning, AMPS_Hard, Web of Lies v2), which buys clean, unambiguous grading at the cost of testing a fairly narrow, templated puzzle-solving skill whose relationship to broader reasoning ability is asserted rather than independently validated in the paper.

What does the single headline number miss? The LiveBench Score is an unweighted mean-of-means across six categories that use five structurally different scoring functions — accuracy@1, F1, Sympy-checked equivalence, Levenshtein-based fuzzy match, and blended prompt/instruction-level accuracy — so a five-point swing in Data Analysis and a five-point swing in Reasoning are treated as equivalent even though the paper's own correlation table shows they are not equally informative (Reasoning r = 0.94 vs. Instruction Following r = 0.82). The categories aren't equally sized or equally noise-free either — Olympiad has only 36 questions, Plot Unscrambling has 40 — and the 95% bootstrap confidence intervals visible in the paper's own bar charts genuinely overlap for several adjacent models in the middle of the pack, meaning the precise leaderboard ordering there is noisier than a single ranked list implies. And because the 40-model comparison spans both traditional single-pass models and the newly-introduced o1 reasoning-compute family, the headline score doesn't normalize for the wildly different cost of achieving it: the paper's own pricing table puts o1-preview at roughly $47.87 to run the full benchmark versus about $0.90 for Claude-3-Haiku, a 53x spread the LiveBench Score alone never surfaces.

On the credit side of the ledger, the paper's own LLM-judge ablation is genuinely strong supporting evidence for the ground-truth-over-judging design choice: a 10–46% judge error rate on hard math and logic is a real, measured cost of the alternative, not a hypothetical one — and the authors are careful to flag the ablation as preliminary rather than definitive.

baselines

Reasonably broad for its 40-model snapshot (0.5B–405B parameters, proprietary and open) and re-run monthly as new models release, but the paper doesn't cost-normalize: o1-preview costs roughly $47.87 to run the full 1,000-question set versus about $0.90 for Claude-3-Haiku, a 53x spread the LiveBench Score doesn't reflect.

contamination

Explicitly acknowledged as incomplete in the paper's own Appendix A.7 — some AMC math questions are only lightly modified from a November 2023 source, and some coding questions are pulled from the same month, a limited but real contaminated slice by the authors' own admission.

what's missed

Anything without a single checkable answer — open-ended writing, chat quality, the use cases Chatbot Arena and AlpacaEval target — is out of scope by design. The one aggregate score also averages five different scoring functions across categories as small as 36–40 questions.

would raise confidence

A retrospective re-score of the original June 2024 questions against models released well after 2024, to check whether old, now-public questions show suspiciously inflated gains versus the always-private sixth; independent confirmation the promised six-month refresh cadence has actually held; a cost- or compute-normalized leaderboard alongside the raw score.

// bottom line

LiveBench is more candid about its own limits than most benchmark papers manage — its appendix names the exact slice of itself that isn't actually contamination-free. That candor doesn't make the underlying tension go away: a "live" benchmark whose five-sixths of questions are permanently public the moment they're posted is fundamentally racing forward, not eliminating the problem it names in its title. But it does mean the paper gives you everything you need to weigh how much to trust any single LiveBench number.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. LiveBench swaps subjective judges for mechanically-checkable ground truth across 18 tasks in six categories (math, coding, reasoning, data analysis, instruction following, language), then treats freshness as an ongoing maintenance commitment — about a sixth of questions replaced monthly, held private for a month before going public — rather than a one-time release.
  2. Even the strongest model at release, o1-preview-2024-09-12, clears only 64.7% overall, and no single model dominates every category: Claude-3.5-Sonnet leads Coding (67.1), o1-mini leads Reasoning (72.3), and Gemini-exp-1121 leads Instruction Following (80.1) despite trailing badly on Math (40.0).
  3. The paper's own contamination appendix is its most valuable section: it names the specific fraction of LiveBench — some AMC math and November-2023 coding questions — that isn't actually contamination-free, which is the honest caveat worth remembering for any benchmark that brands itself "live."
