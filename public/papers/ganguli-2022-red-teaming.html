Red Teaming Language Models to Reduce Harms: Methods, Scaling Behaviors, and Lessons Learned

Anthropic's early, exhaustively-documented manual red-teaming study: 324 crowdworkers produced 38,961 adversarial attacks against language models spanning 3 sizes (2.7B/13B/52B) and 4 safety interventions, released publicly as one of the first red-team datasets against an RLHF-trained model. The headline scaling result — RLHF gets harder to red-team as it scales, a static HHH prompt does not — became a load-bearing input to Anthropic's subsequent safety work.

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

By 2022, Anthropic (and others) had built language assistants with a specific safety intervention — reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF) against a harmlessness preference model — but had almost no systematic evidence for whether that intervention actually holds up as models get bigger, or how it compares to cheaper alternatives like a static "helpful, honest, and harmless" (HHH) prompt. Static benchmarks (toxicity classifiers scored against fixed prompt sets like RealToxicityPrompts) don't capture what a motivated human adversary can do in an open-ended, multi-turn conversation, and nobody had published a red-team dataset built against a model of that size trained specifically to be harmless via RLHF. The paper puts it plainly: "these types of models are already deployed and we believe our data can help shed further light on their strengths and weaknesses" — but at the time, the field lacked both the data and the methodology to make that assessment rigorous.

The deeper gap is procedural rather than just empirical: red teaming is inherently expensive, ethically fraught (someone has to be paid to try to make a model say something harmful, and someone else to review the results), and organizations that do it have "strong counter-incentives to share their findings" since the output is, by construction, a catalog of a company's own model failures. The paper's third contribution — publishing the full methodology, the annotator-wellbeing protocol, the statistical approach, and the actual dataset — is presented as a direct fix for that: making red teaming a shared, comparable practice rather than an internal, undisclosed one.

// how it works

The method

The core experimental design crosses 3 model sizes (2.7B, 13B, 52B parameters) with 4 safety interventions: a plain LM (1-shot prompted into dialogue format, no safety intervention — the control), a prompted LM (14-shot HHH-prompted, "helpful, honest, and harmless," a cheap static intervention), rejection sampling (RS) (generate 16 candidate responses from the prompted LM, rank them with a learned harmlessness preference model, serve the 2 least-harmful), and RLHF (a prompted LM further trained by reinforcement learning to directly maximize the same preference model's score). RS and RLHF both depend on that harmlessness preference model, which was itself trained on comparison data collected from red-teaming the 52B prompted LM — which is why the paper collected roughly 10× more attacks against that specific configuration (17,739 of the 38,961 total) than against any other cell in the grid.

324 US-based crowdworkers (307 recruited via Amazon Mechanical Turk, 17 via Upwork) were told to "make the AI behave badly, to get it to say obnoxious, offensive, and harmful things" in open-ended, multi-turn conversations, assigned at random to one of the 12 model-size × intervention cells without being told which model they were talking to. After each turn, workers were shown two candidate model responses and asked to pick the more harmful one — a design that both doubles the chance of catching a harmful generation per turn and directly produces the pairwise comparison data used to train the harmlessness preference model. At the end of each conversation — five per task — workers self-rated their success on a 5-point Likert scale (0 = not successful, 4 = very successful). MTurk workers were paid $7.50–$9.50 per set of 5 conversations (at or above California's $15/hour minimum wage at ≥2 tasks/hour); Upwork workers were paid $20/hour.

Every assistant utterance in every transcript was also scored automatically by the harmlessness preference model itself — producing one harmlessness score per turn, which the authors aggregate per-conversation as either the mean or the minimum (their preferred choice, "as a lower bound on the overall harmlessness of the model," though they report both give qualitatively similar results). To characterize what kinds of harm the 38,961 attacks actually contained, the authors embedded each transcript using the residual stream of the 52B prompted LM's 48th layer, projected to 2D with UMAP, and manually annotated the resulting clusters with topic labels — a bottom-up taxonomy built by inspecting the data rather than by pre-specifying categories.

// what they found

The results

The headline scaling result (Fig. 1, middle): as model size increases from 2.7B to 52B, RLHF models become significantly harder to red-team — mean minimum harmlessness rises from roughly 0.5 to roughly 1.7 — while the plain LM and prompted (HHH) LM show a flat trend with scale and are not significantly different from each other by self-reported attack success at any size (the paper's own footnote notes one exception: at 52B, the plain LM scores somewhat less harmful than the prompted LM on the automated minimum-harmlessness metric specifically). That second finding directly contradicts the authors' own prior work, which had used static toxicity evaluations to show HHH prompting was an effective safety intervention; the paper's explanation is that "static prompts from the RealToxicityPrompts dataset are less adversarial than the dialogue-based attacks employed by red team members" — i.e., a fixed prompt-completion benchmark understates how hard a determined, adaptive human adversary can push a model. Rejection sampling is the hardest configuration to red-team at every scale, but the paper notes this is achieved partly "by being evasive" rather than through more substantive harm avoidance — a caveat directly relevant to reading the RS numbers as a safety win. At 52B, RS and RLHF converge to statistically indistinguishable harmlessness.

The released dataset totals 38,961 red team attacks (Table 1: e.g. 17,739 against the 52B prompted LM alone, versus roughly 1,150–3,100 per cell everywhere else). Workers self-reported a successful attack (rating ≥ some threshold on the bimodal 0–4 scale) roughly 35% of the time on average. A separate review experiment — 3 independent reviewers rating a random sample of 500 attacks each on the 52B prompted LM and the 52B RLHF model — found low inter-annotator agreement on what even counts as a successful attack: Fleiss's κ of 0.32 on the raw 5-point Likert scale, rising to 0.49 when binarized (rating ≥ 3 = success); dropping the original red-team author and keeping only the 3 independent reviewers raised the binary agreement further, to a maximum of 0.55. Manual topic-tagging of that same ~1,000-attack review sample (~3% of the full dataset) found the five most common attack categories were discrimination & injustice, hate speech & offensive language, violence & incitement, non-violent unethical behavior, and bullying & harassment (Fig. 3 below) — with non-violent-unethical-behavior attacks succeeding at a disproportionately higher rate, which the authors attribute to that category being more subtle than overtly offensive requests. A separate UMAP-cluster analysis found a distinct cluster of 916 attacks soliciting personally identifiable information, some of which elicited fabricated ("hallucinated") addresses from the model rather than real ones.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

The single biggest measurement-validity issue in this paper is one the authors surface themselves but don't fully resolve: the harmlessness preference model used to score every transcript is the same model both RLHF and rejection sampling are built around — RLHF trains directly against it, and rejection sampling uses it at inference time to select the least-harmful of 16 candidate responses. The paper says so directly: RLHF is built by training a prompted LM "to maximize the scores given by the preference model described above," and the authors separately note, in a footnote to their own results, that "the RLHF model is explicitly trained to maximize harmlessness, as such, we expect these models to have low harmlessness scores by design" — meaning the headline scaling curve (Fig. 1, middle/right) is partly a demonstration that RL against a reward model increases that same reward model's score, which is close to definitional rather than an independent measurement of harm reduction. The paper does partially triangulate against a second signal — red team members' own self-reported success ratings (Fig. 1, left) — but that metric has its own problem: independent reviewers only agreed on what constitutes a "successful" attack at κ = 0.32–0.55, poor-to-fair by conventional interpretation of Fleiss's κ. With neither the automated metric nor the human metric individually trustworthy, and the two only partially correlated (the paper reports the correlation is real but "not perfect"), the scaling conclusion rests on convergence between two noisy, coupled signals rather than one clean one.

Representativeness is a second real limitation, and again one the authors quantify rather than hide: their crowdworker pool skews toward "White or Caucasian" self-identification (79% vs. 75.8% U.S. Census) and college-plus education (66% vs. 32.9% Census) — a population whose sense of what's "obnoxious, offensive, and harmful" is not neutral with respect to the wide space of possible harms. Consistent with that, the paper reports ~80% of attacks came from just ~50 of the ~300 red-teamers (out of the full 324-person pool), so the dataset's texture is disproportionately shaped by a small number of especially prolific (and, per the paper's own linear-mixed-effects analysis, especially effective) red-teamers rather than a broad population sample. The authors themselves flag concrete gaps this produced: no code-generation attacks appear in the dataset despite the models being trained partly on Python, and an internally-discovered attack class ("roleplay attacks," e.g. asking the model to enter "4chan mode") was found to work on the RLHF model but isn't represented in what was released — meaning published red-team coverage is a strict subset of what the authors themselves know the model is vulnerable to.

Finally, this is explicitly framed as an early, exploratory study, and it shows in the sample sizes underlying the paper's more granular claims: the entire inter-annotator-agreement and topic-tag analysis is built on 1,000 reviewed attacks — about 3% of the released dataset — so the tag-frequency breakdown (Fig. 3, the paper's Figure 9) and the qualitative claim about which harm types succeed more often are both extrapolations from a modest subsample, not the full 38,961.

baselines

The four-way comparison (plain / HHH-prompted / rejection-sampling / RLHF) is well-designed to isolate which safety intervention actually helps, and the plain-LM control is a genuinely useful floor — the surprising null result (HHH prompting ≈ plain LM under adversarial dialogue, despite being effective on static benchmarks) is one of the paper's most credible findings precisely because it's a negative result the authors weren't looking for.

contamination

Not a leakage concern in the usual benchmark sense, but a real circularity concern: RLHF is trained directly against, and rejection sampling selects via, the same harmlessness preference model used to evaluate them, so the automated half of the headline result is partly measuring optimization/selection pressure rather than independent harm reduction.

what's missed

Domain-expertise-requiring attacks (bomb-making, drug synthesis specifics) that crowdworkers may not have been equipped to correctly judge; code-generation attacks entirely; internally-known failure modes (roleplay/persona attacks) that the authors chose not to release; and any assessment of whether attack success correlates with red-teamer demographics, which the anonymous collection process makes impossible to check.

would raise confidence

A harmlessness metric fully decoupled from the RLHF/RS training signal (e.g., a held-out classifier never used in training either model), a larger and more diverse review sample for the inter-annotator-agreement study, and a released comparison of manual versus automated (LM red-teamer) attack diversity — which the paper flags as planned future work but does not itself contain.

// bottom line

This paper's lasting value is less the specific harmlessness numbers and more the methodology, the dataset (still one of the largest public manual red-team corpora against an RLHF model), and the field-shaping honesty of its limitations section — the same transparency the authors argue the field needs more of. The core scaling claim (RLHF improves with size, static prompting does not) is credible and has held up as an influential early data point, but it should be read as directionally right rather than precisely quantified, given the metric circularity and modest annotator agreement underneath it.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. 324 crowdworkers produced 38,961 manual red-team attacks across 3 model sizes (2.7B/13B/52B) and 4 safety interventions (plain LM, HHH-prompted LM, rejection sampling, RLHF) — one of the first public red-team datasets built against a model trained to be harmless via RLHF.
  2. RLHF models get significantly harder to red-team as they scale; a static HHH prompt does not — contradicting the authors' own earlier static-benchmark evidence that HHH prompting was an effective safety intervention, and illustrating that adversarial dialogue testing surfaces failures that fixed prompt-completion benchmarks miss.
  3. The paper's own numbers cut both ways: the automated harmlessness metric is confounded by being the same model RLHF trained against and RS selected via, and human inter-annotator agreement on what constitutes a successful attack was only poor-to-fair (Fleiss's κ 0.32–0.55) — a reminder that red-teaming rigor is as much about the measurement's validity as about the attack methodology itself.
