G-Eval: NLG Evaluation using GPT-4 with Better Human Alignment

Prompt GPT-4 with a task description and criteria, have it auto-write its own chain-of-thought evaluation steps, then read a fine-grained score off the probabilities of the rating tokens. A reference-free metric that hit 0.514 Spearman with humans on summarization — beating the trained SOTA — and put a name to the LLM judge's quiet preference for machine-written text.

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

Measuring the quality of generated text is the unglamorous bottleneck of NLG research. The workhorse metrics — BLEU, ROUGE, METEOR — score a candidate by its n-gram overlap with a human-written reference. That correlates poorly with what people actually judge, especially for open-ended, creative tasks where many good outputs share almost no surface words with the reference. And it presumes you have references, which are expensive to collect for every new task.

By early 2023 the obvious move was to ask a large language model to score text directly, reference-free. Methods like GPTScore did this by treating the model's generation probability of the candidate as a proxy for quality — high-quality, fluent text should be assigned higher probability. The catch: when meta-evaluated against human ratings, these LLM-based evaluators still trailed a much smaller trained neural evaluator, UniEval (a 770M T5 fine-tuned to predict quality as a QA task). So the prompt-an-LLM approach was convenient and reference-free, but not yet good — and nobody had systematically asked whether LLM evaluators were reliable, or what failure modes they hide.

G-Eval's bet is that the gap was in how the LLM was being asked, not in the LLM. Give GPT-4 an explicit task definition, an auto-generated rubric of evaluation steps, and a structured scoring format — then recover a continuous score from its token probabilities — and a frozen, reference-free LLM can overtake the trained specialist.

// how it works

The method

G-Eval is a prompt-based evaluator with three moving parts. First, a prompt that states the evaluation task and the criterion in natural language — for summarization coherence, for example, it pastes in the DUC quality definition: "the summary should be well-structured and well-organized … build from sentence to sentence to a coherent body of information." Second, an auto chain-of-thought: feeding only the task intro and criteria, the model is asked to generate its own detailed Evaluation Steps — "1. Read the news article and identify the main topic and key points. 2. Read the summary and check it covers them in a clear, logical order. 3. Assign a 1–5 score." This auto-rubric replaces hand-written, per-task instructions and gives the model explicit reasoning scaffolding before it commits to a number.

Third, a form-filling scoring function. G-Eval concatenates the prompt, the generated CoT, the input context (the article) and the target (the summary), and asks the model to fill in an evaluation form — scores only. This is the key contrast with GPTScore: rather than back out a number from the conditional probability of generating the target, G-Eval makes scoring the task itself.

Naive integer scoring has two pathologies the authors call out. One score tends to dominate the distribution (everything is a "3" on a 1–5 scale), collapsing variance; and LLMs emit integers even when asked for decimals, producing ties that wash out real differences between texts. The fix is probability-weighted scoring: read the model's probability for each possible score token and take the expectation.

score = Σi=1..n p(si) · si // expectation over the predefined score set S = {s₁,…,sₙ}, weighting each rating by the LLM's probability of emitting it — a continuous score in place of a quantized integer

Implementation detail that matters for the critique: GPT-3.5 (text-davinci-003, temperature 0) exposes output token probabilities directly. GPT-4 does not — so to estimate p(si) the authors sample the model 20 times at temperature 1 and read the empirical frequency of each score. "G-Eval-4" is therefore a 20-sample Monte-Carlo estimate of a distribution the method assumes it can see exactly.

// what they found

The results

Following Zhong et al. (2022), G-Eval is meta-evaluated on three benchmarks across two tasks: SummEval (summarization, rated on coherence / consistency / fluency / relevance), Topical-Chat (knowledge-grounded dialogue), and QAGS (summary factual consistency / hallucination). The metric is judged by how well it correlates with human ratings — Spearman (ρ) and Kendall-Tau (τ) at the summary level.

On SummEval, G-Eval-4 reaches an average Spearman of 0.514 (τ = 0.418), ahead of the trained SOTA UniEval (0.474), GPTScore (0.417) and BARTScore (0.385) — and far above similarity metrics like ROUGE-1 (0.192). The per-aspect wins are largest where quality is most semantic: coherence 0.582 and relevance 0.547.

Ablations. Removing the auto-CoT drops SummEval ρ from 0.514 to 0.500 (fluency falls hardest, 0.455 → 0.403). Removing probability weighting drops ρ to 0.502. One honest wrinkle the paper flags itself: the no-probability variant scores a higher Kendall-τ (0.446 vs 0.418), but only because integer ties aren't counted as concordant or discordant — an artifact, not a real gain, which is why that row is italicized in the original Table 1.

The most-cited finding isn't a correlation at all. Reusing Zhang et al. (2023)'s set of human-written vs. GPT-3.5 summaries — split by which humans preferred — G-Eval-4 assigns higher scores to the GPT-3.5 summaries in every group, even the group where human judges preferred the human-written summary. The authors raise this as a self-reinforcement risk: an LLM judge that favors LLM text would, used as a reward signal, train models toward their own taste rather than human quality.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

As a meta-evaluation the core result is clean and the baselines are the right ones for 2023: UniEval was the strongest trained evaluator, and beating it reference-free is a real result. But the headline "by a large margin" deserves unpacking. On SummEval the average edge over UniEval is 0.514 vs 0.474 — about four hundredths of a Spearman, bought with a closed, roughly two-orders-of-magnitude larger model behind a paid API. The margin is genuinely large only on abstractive consistency (QAGS-XSum); on the extractive QAGS-CNN split, UniEval's ρ (0.662) is within a whisker of G-Eval-4's (0.685). The win is most real exactly where small trained evaluators were weakest.

Two pillars hold up less well than their citation count suggests. The probability-weighting gain is ~0.01 Spearman and rests, for GPT-4, on a 20-sample estimate at temperature 1 — a coarse proxy (a score with true probability 0.05 is essentially invisible in 20 draws) for the exact logprobs the method's equation assumes, and a different decoding regime than the temperature-0 GPT-3.5 backbone it's compared against. And the famous self-preference result sits on shaky ground: the supporting data has near-zero human inter-annotator agreement (Krippendorff's α = 0.07, which the authors disclose), the absolute score gaps are tiny, and Figure 2's truncated 3.75–4.0 axis visually magnifies them. The direction later proved important to the field — but as evidence in this paper it's a flagged hypothesis, not a measured effect size.

The deepest tension is internal. Contribution #2 of the paper is that LLM metrics are "sensitive to the instructions and prompts" — yet every benchmark is run with a single prompt and criteria phrasing per aspect, with no variance reported over paraphrases. For a metric whose entire premise is a prompt, robustness to that prompt is the central reliability question, and it's named but never stress-tested. Add the usual closed-model caveats — no pinned GPT-4 version, documented model drift over time, all three benchmarks built on public pre-2021 corpora (CNN/DailyMail, XSum) that are almost certainly in pretraining — and the method reproduces but the exact numbers don't.

baselines

The right strong baseline — UniEval, the trained SOTA evaluator — and G-Eval beats it reference-free. But the average edge is ~0.04 Spearman, large only on abstractive XSum, and the comparison pits a closed ~100×-larger API model against a 770M T5.

contamination

SummEval / Topical-Chat / QAGS all build on public pre-2021 corpora (CNN/DM, XSum) near-certainly in GPT-4's pretraining; no leakage analysis. Matters less for a predict-human-ratings task than a recall task, but unmeasured.

what's missed

A prompt-based metric tested on one prompt per aspect, despite the paper's own "prompt-sensitive" warning → robustness untested. GPT-4 scores are 20-sample temp-1 estimates standing in for the logprobs the method assumes; the probability-weighting and CoT gains are each ~0.01–0.015 ρ.

would raise confidence

Prompt-paraphrase variance bands; a higher-agreement human-preference set for the self-bias claim (α = 0.07 is noise); version-pinned models; reported effect sizes instead of a truncated-axis bar chart.

// bottom line

G-Eval is the paper that made "GPT-4 as a CoT-guided, form-filling, probability-weighted judge" the default reference-free NLG metric, and it backs the recipe with a clean meta-evaluation win over the trained SOTA. The methodological core is sound; the two softest of its widely-quoted claims — that probability weighting matters and that the judge favors machine text — rest on a ~0.01 Spearman bump and an α = 0.07 dataset, and the metric's own Achilles' heel, prompt sensitivity, is named but never tested.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. The recipe that became the default. Prompt (task + criteria) + auto-generated CoT evaluation steps + form-filling score, refined by the probability-weighted sum Σ p(sᵢ)·sᵢ. It hit 0.514 Spearman on SummEval, beating trained SOTA UniEval (0.474) and GPTScore (0.417), and effectively defined the modern LLM-as-judge scoring loop.
  2. The gains are uneven and the ablations are small. Huge on abstractive consistency (QAGS-XSum), modest on easy/extractive splits where GPT-3.5 ≈ GPT-4. CoT and probability-weighting each move Spearman only ~0.01–0.015; the real jump comes from GPT-3.5 → GPT-4 on the hard aspects (consistency, relevance).
  3. It named the self-preference bias — on thin evidence. "LLM judges prefer LLM-written text, risking self-reinforcement as a reward signal" is the paper's most-cited line, but here it's backed by α = 0.07 human labels and a truncated-axis chart. Treat it as a hypothesis the field later confirmed, not a measured result from this paper.
