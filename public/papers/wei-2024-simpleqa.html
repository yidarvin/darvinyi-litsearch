Measuring short-form factuality in large language models

SimpleQA: 4,326 short, fact-seeking questions with a single indisputable answer, adversarially collected against GPT-4 and trivially gradable — built to measure not just what a model knows, but whether it knows what it knows.

Jason Wei, Nguyen Karina, Hyung Won Chung, Yunxin Joy Jiao, Spencer Papay, Amelia Glaese, John Schulman, William Fedus · OpenAI

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

Hallucination — confidently stating things that are false or unsupported — is one of the main barriers to trusting language models. But factuality is notoriously hard to measure: a model's long answer can contain dozens of independent claims, each of which has to be checked against the world, and many claims are genuinely open to interpretation. There is no clean scalar to optimize.

SimpleQA's move is to radically narrow the scope. Instead of grading sprawling completions, it asks only short, fact-seeking questions that have one, single, indisputable answer — "Who received the IEEE Frank Rosenblatt Award in 2010?" (Michio Sugeno). That reduction makes grading tractable, at the explicit cost of leaving open whether being right about short facts generalizes to being right across a long, claim-dense essay.

The existing short-form QA benchmarks were no longer fit for purpose. TriviaQA (2017) and Natural Questions (2019) are saturated — too easy for frontier models to be discriminative. SimpleQA was designed around four properties to fix that: high correctness (every reference answer agreed by two independent annotators), good researcher UX (short Q&A, fast to run, low run-to-run variance over 4,326 items), challenging for frontier models (GPT-4o and Claude both score under 50%), and diversity across history, science, art, geography, TV, and more.

// how it works

The method

Data collection ran in two human stages. First, AI trainers wrote question–answer pairs against a strict rubric. Then a second, independent trainer answered each question blind, and the item was kept only if both trainers' answers matched. The authoring rubric is where most of the engineering lives:

  • Single indisputable answer. The question must pin down the scope of the answer — ask "which city" or "what year," not an open "where" or "when" that admits several correct responses.
  • Evergreen. Answers must not change over time (specify "Season 13" rather than "currently"), but questions may not cheat by tacking on "as of 2023," which would feel contrived.
  • Evidence-backed. Every reference answer is tied to a supporting web page, and the authors kept a question only if the two trainers cited at least two distinct source domains.
  • Adversarially hard. Trainers reviewed four OpenAI model completions per question and could keep it only if at least one of the four was wrong. Most of the four came from GPT-4 variants; near the end one was swapped to GPT-3.5 so the set would give some signal on smaller models.
  • Answerable as of December 31, 2023, so models with knowledge cutoffs up to that date can be compared fairly.

Quality control layered automated and human checks: few-shot ChatGPT classifiers flagged rubric violations (missing units, time-varying or multi-answer questions) for revision, and a light ChatGPT rewrite fixed grammar without touching content. A final audit had a third trainer re-answer 1,000 random items; that trainer scored 94.4% by the autograder, and after manually triaging the disagreements the authors estimate the benchmark's own error rate at roughly 3%.

The resulting 4,326 questions span ten topics — Science & technology is the largest at n=858, followed by Politics (709) and Art (550). By answer type, 32.8% are dates, 24.1% a person, 15.3% a number, and 9.9% a place. Wikipedia is a source for ~3.5k of the 4.3k questions, with fandom.com (410), ac.uk (154), and imdb.com (121) trailing far behind.

Grading is itself a prompted ChatGPT classifier that sees the question, the gold target, and the model's answer, and returns one of three labels: correct (the prediction fully contains the reference answer with no contradiction), incorrect (it contradicts the reference in any way, even hedged), or not attempted (the reference answer isn't given, but nothing contradicts it — "I don't know"). Those collapse into two metrics: overall correct (recall-like: percent of all questions right) and correct given attempted (precision-like: percent right among questions the model actually tried). The headline single number is their harmonic mean:

F-score = 2c2c + 2i + n // c = correct, i = incorrect, n = not attempted · the harmonic mean of overall-correct and correct-given-attempted

The reward for abstention is the whole point: a model that knows a fact and knows when it doesn't is rewarded over one that blurts a guess every time. The authors flag one wrinkle — the F-score has a guessing loophole (it pays to answer whenever you're more than 50% sure), so they also propose a penalty metric that scores correct = +1, not-attempted = 0, incorrect = −p. At p = 9, a model only nets positive if it gets ≥90% of attempted questions right — a bar none of the evaluated models clear.

// what they found

The results

SimpleQA is hard. The best model evaluated, OpenAI o1-preview, answers just 42.7% of questions correctly (F-score 44.8); GPT-4o gets 38.2% correct and Claude-3.5 Sonnet 28.9%. Every frontier model lands well under 50% overall-correct. Because questions were filtered to be hard for GPT-4o, the authors use Claude as a sanity check: Claude also scores low, suggesting the questions are hard for frontier models generally rather than overfit to one family.

The more interesting signal is how models fail. Two opposite pathologies show up. GPT-4o-mini barrels ahead — it attempts almost everything (only 0.9% not-attempted) and is wrong 90.5% of the time, the picture of overconfidence. The Claude models abstain heavily — Claude-3-haiku declines 75.3% of questions. Claude-3.5 Sonnet answers far fewer questions correctly than GPT-4o, but because it also attempts far fewer, the two end up with a similar F-score (35.0 vs 38.4). The benchmark thus separates raw knowledge from the willingness to bet on it.

Because every question has a verifiable answer, SimpleQA doubles as a calibration probe — does a model's confidence track its accuracy? Two readings (Figure 2). When models are asked to state a confidence, all of them sit well below the diagonal: they consistently overstate how sure they are. When instead you sample the same question 100 times and read confidence off answer frequency, calibration looks much better — accuracy rises smoothly with frequency. On both axes, larger models are better calibrated (o1-preview > o1-mini, gpt4o > gpt4o-mini), and o1-preview's answer-frequency curve comes closest to the ideal y = x line.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

SimpleQA is a tidy, honest, deliberately minimal benchmark — and most of its limits are ones the authors name themselves. The contribution that will last is the abstention framing: by scoring "not attempted" as a first-class outcome and rewarding it over a wrong guess, it turns a factuality test into a test of whether a model knows the boundary of its own knowledge. The places to push are the panel, the grader, and the shelf life.

The model panel is narrow and already dated: eight models, all from OpenAI and Anthropic, mostly early-to-mid-2024 releases. No open-weight models (Llama, Mistral, Qwen), no Gemini. More structurally, the difficulty filter selected questions that at least one of four OpenAI models got wrong — so "hard" is defined relative to GPT-4-class models. The Claude sanity check is a reasonable hedge against single-family overfitting, but it doesn't fully escape the fact that the dataset is curated on OpenAI-model failure.

The grader is trusted on thin evidence. Grading is a prompted ChatGPT classifier, and the validation is informal: the authors manually read 100 correct / 100 incorrect / 100 not-attempted completions and found "only two disagreements," plus the 1,000-item third-trainer audit. There's no inter-annotator agreement statistic, no test of grader robustness to adversarial phrasing or to the hedging cases the rubric itself flags as tricky. Since the same autograder both filters the data and scores the models, its blind spots are baked in twice.

Finally, contamination will catch up to it. The questions are public web facts — Wikipedia sources ~3.5k of 4.3k — released openly on GitHub. Evergreen design keeps answers stable, but it does nothing to stop the dataset itself from entering future pretraining corpora; the hope that it "remains relevant for the next few generations" is in tension with open release. And the headline F-score carries the guessing loophole the authors disclose (Appendix B), while the ~3% estimated benchmark error rate sets a hard ceiling on how finely models can be separated near the top.

baselines

Only 8 OpenAI + Anthropic models, mostly early/mid-2024; no open-weight or Gemini. Difficulty is defined by failures of four OpenAI models, so "hard" is GPT-4-relative — Claude is used as a partial sanity check.

contamination

Evergreen answers but public, Wikipedia-heavy facts on GitHub; nothing prevents the dataset leaking into future training sets, eroding difficulty over time. No held-out private split to track this.

what's missed

Short-form, single-answer only — by the authors' own admission says nothing about long-form factuality. The grader has no formal validation (no IAA stats); F-score has a documented guessing loophole; ~3% benchmark error caps top-end resolution.

would raise confidence

Broader, current model panel (open-weight + Gemini + later frontier); a formal grader-validation study; correlation of SimpleQA scores with long-form factuality (LongFact / FActScore); a private split and an audit of the Wikipedia-source skew.

// bottom line

A cheap, low-variance, trivially gradable probe whose real value is reframing factuality as calibration — separating "knows the fact" from "knows that it knows." Its narrowness is both the strength (clean signal) and the ceiling (no long-form claim, and a public dataset whose difficulty will decay as it leaks into training).

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. Factuality reframed as calibration. SimpleQA scores abstention as a first-class outcome, rewarding a model that knows when to say "I don't know" over one that guesses — and finds frontier models systematically overstate their stated confidence.
  2. Frontier models genuinely struggle. The best model evaluated, o1-preview, answers only 42.7% correctly; all of GPT-4o, o1, and Claude land under 50% overall-correct on these adversarially-filtered, long-tail, single-answer questions.
  3. Tractability bought with scope. Single short answers are cheap to grade and low-variance, but the paper is explicit that this measures one constrained slice of factuality — and heavy Wikipedia sourcing plus public release means contamination will steadily eat its difficulty.
