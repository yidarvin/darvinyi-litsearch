Llama Guard: LLM-based Input-Output Safeguard for Human-AI Conversations

Meta fine-tunes a single Llama2-7B into a content-moderation classifier that judges the user's prompt and the model's reply as two separate tasks — and exposes the safety taxonomy in the prompt, so you can swap policies at inference time without retraining.

Hakan Inan, Kartikeya Upasani, Jianfeng Chi, Rashi Rungta, Krithika Iyer, Yuning Mao, Michael Tontchev, Qing Hu, Brian Fuller, Davide Testuggine, Madian Khabsa · GenAI at Meta

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

By late 2023, every chat assistant needed a guardrail — something that screens what the user sends in and what the model sends back. The obvious move was to reuse the content-moderation tools the industry already had: the Perspective API, OpenAI's Content Moderation API, Azure's Content Safety API. Llama Guard's argument is that all of them are the wrong shape for the job.

Four gaps, per the paper. First, none distinguishes the user from the AI — yet moderating a human's request ("how do I buy a tiger?") and moderating a model's answer ("go to the zoo, steal one") are genuinely different classification problems. Second, each enforces one fixed policy; you cannot bend a third-party API to a new category of harm as policies evolve. Third, they are API-only — you cannot fine-tune them to your own use case. Fourth, they are built on small, conventional transformer backbones, leaving the linguistic competence of modern LLMs on the table.

Llama Guard's bet is that an instruction-tuned LLM closes all four at once: it can be told who is speaking, it can be handed an arbitrary taxonomy in its prompt, its weights are open for further fine-tuning, and it inherits a 7B model's language understanding. The deliverable is not just a paper but an openly released model — pitched as a "strong baseline" for the community to build on.

// how it works

The method

Llama Guard reframes moderation as an instruction-following task. A single prompt to the model carries four ingredients: (1) a set of guidelines — numbered categories with plain-text descriptions of what is safe and unsafe; (2) the classification type — whether to judge the user "prompt" or the agent "response"; (3) the conversation itself (single- or multi-turn); and (4) the desired output format. The model then generates "safe" or "unsafe", and if unsafe, a second line listing the violated category codes (e.g. O3).

The "safe"/"unsafe" verdict is a single token in the SentencePiece tokenizer, which is the trick that turns a generative model into a calibrated classifier: the classification score is just the probability of that first token. The same format flexes between binary, multi-label, and one-vs-all classification — for a per-category score you put only that one category in the prompt and read off the binary decision.

ŷi = maxc ∈ {c1…cn} ŷc,i // overall "unsafe" score for example i = the max probability across all positive categories

The taxonomy itself is deliberately a sample, not a standard — six categories: Violence & Hate, Sexual Content, Guns & Illegal Weapons, Regulated or Controlled Substances, Suicide & Self-Harm, and Criminal Planning. The point is not the categories but the mechanism: because the policy lives in the prompt, you can do zero-shot (swap in a new taxonomy's names/descriptions) or few-shot (add 2–4 in-context examples per category) adaptation with no training at all.

Training data & recipe

The supervised data is small and synthetic-but-human-labeled. They take the first human prompt from Anthropic's harmlessness preference data, generate a mix of cooperating and refusing replies with an internal Llama checkpoint, and have an in-house red team annotate four labels per example: prompt-category, response-category, prompt-label, and response-label. The result is 13,997 prompt+response examples (Criminal Planning dominates at 3,915 prompts / 4,292 responses; Self-Harm is thinnest at 89 / 96), split 3:1 between training and evaluation.

The model is Llama2-7B — the smallest of the three sizes, chosen for cheaper inference — fine-tuned on a single 8×A100-80GB machine, batch size 2, sequence length 4096, learning rate 2×10⁻⁶, for just 500 steps (≈1 epoch). Two data augmentations teach it to honor the prompt's taxonomy: randomly dropping non-violated categories, and dropping all violated categories while flipping the label to "safe" — plus shuffling category indices so it can't memorize the format.

// what they found

The results

Everything is scored with AUPRC (area under the precision-recall curve), following the OpenAI moderation paper. Two headline claims. First, Llama Guard is excellent on its own taxonomy: 0.945 AUPRC for prompt classification and 0.953 for response classification on its in-house test set — well above the OpenAI Moderation API (0.764 / 0.769) and Perspective API (0.728 / 0.699). Second, it adapts: on OpenAI's own moderation dataset it scores 0.847 zero-shot, a hair under OpenAI's own API (0.856), and on ToxicChat — which none of the models were trained on — it leads at 0.626 vs OpenAI's 0.588 and Perspective's 0.532.

The adaptation story has two levers. Prompting: on the OpenAI dataset, Llama Guard goes from 0.837 (no adaptation) → 0.847 (zero-shot with OpenAI's category names) → 0.872 few-shot — enough to edge out OpenAI's own API (0.856) on OpenAI's own benchmark. Fine-tuning: adapting to ToxicChat's taxonomy, Llama Guard needs only 20% of the ToxicChat training data to match a vanilla Llama2-7B trained on 100% of it — evidence that the safety pre-fine-tuning transfers. (Vanilla Llama2-7B, prompted zero-shot, only emitted malformed outputs, scoring 0; Llama Guard scores 0.626 in the same zero-shot setting.)

// does it hold up?

The eval read

The engineering is clean and the open release was genuinely field-shaping. But the headline "matches or exceeds existing tools" rests on an evaluation that is structurally tilted toward the home team, and the most quotable numbers are the least comparable ones.

Start with the 0.945 / 0.953 on the in-house test set. Those are in-policy numbers: Llama Guard wrote the taxonomy, Meta's red team wrote the labels, and an internal Llama checkpoint even generated the responses being judged. The baselines are scored off-policy against that same foreign taxonomy, and several of Llama Guard's categories have no counterpart in theirs at all — Perspective has no self-harm, weapons, or controlled-substances class — so those tools structurally cannot flag whole swaths of the test set. A near-perfect score on a test set whose ground truth you authored is a ceiling, not a comparison — the paper says as much, but the abstract's "matches or exceeds" leans on it anyway.

The one apples-to-apples comparison — a third-party taxonomy and dataset — is the OpenAI Moderation set, and there the clean zero-shot number (0.847) actually trails OpenAI's own API (0.856). Llama Guard only pulls ahead (0.872) once it gets few-shot examples that the API never gets. The genuinely uncontaminated win is ToxicChat (0.626 vs 0.588) — but 0.626 AUPRC is low in absolute terms, a reminder that moderating real user-AI traffic is far from solved.

In the per-category off-policy breakdowns (the headline Table 2 numbers use a single max-over-categories rule), the methods aren't even scored alike: Llama Guard is measured one-vs-all, while the baseline APIs are measured one-vs-benign — which the authors concede "can produce optimistic results" for those baselines by dropping hard negatives. Different rules per method make that head-to-head fragile in either direction. Figure 2 below shows the more fundamental cost — the coarse six-category taxonomy: on OpenAI categories that don't map onto Llama Guard's (Violence, Harassment, Sexual-Minors, Graphic Violence), its per-category AUPRC collapses while the OpenAI API stays high.

baselines

Reasonable for 2023 (OpenAI Mod, Perspective, Azure, GPT-4) but compared unevenly: in-policy vs off-policy taxonomies, and — in the per-category breakdowns — 1-vs-all vs 1-vs-benign scoring. GPT-4 — arguably the strongest baseline — can't emit probabilities, so it gets no AUPRC and is exiled to an appendix P/R/F1 table where it over-flags (near-total recall — 0.947 overall on prompts — against precision that on several categories falls below 0.2).

contamination

Low risk for a classifier, but unaudited: Llama 2's pretraining corpus and the public eval sets (ToxicChat, OpenAI Mod) could overlap, and the in-house eval set shares its generator and annotators with the training set.

what's missed

No inter-annotator agreement, no confidence intervals, no significance tests, single seed. Eval sets are small (~3,500 in-house, 1,680 OpenAI Mod). English-only by the authors' own admission, and the data is synthetic (Anthropic prompts + internal-model responses) rather than real deployment traffic.

would raise confidence

A held-out third-party benchmark scored with one consistent rule for every method; GPT-4 compared on AUPRC via logprobs; multilingual and real-traffic evaluation; reported annotator agreement and CIs on the headline gaps.

// bottom line

Llama Guard's lasting contribution is the recipe and the open weights, not the leaderboard: separate the prompt and response tasks, put the policy in the prompt so it's re-promptable, and read the score off the first token. The benchmark numbers are real but flattering — the in-policy scores are a ceiling you authored, the one clean head-to-head is a narrow loss, and the uncontaminated win sits at a modest 0.626. Adopt the design; treat "state of the art" as load-bearing only on its own taxonomy.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. Two tasks, one model. Moderating the user's prompt and the model's response are distinct problems; Llama Guard was the first to carve them apart, switching between them with a single word in the instruction rather than two separate models.
  2. Policy-in-the-prompt is the real idea. Because the taxonomy is an input, not a trained-in head, you can re-prompt Llama Guard onto a new policy zero-shot or few-shot — and the open weights mean you can fine-tune it further (only 20% of ToxicChat data needed to match a from-scratch Llama2-7B).
  3. Read the benchmark numbers with the taxonomy in mind. The 0.94+ scores are in-policy on Meta's own test set; on a neutral third-party set Llama Guard's clean zero-shot score (0.847) slightly trails OpenAI's API, and the truly uncontaminated ToxicChat win (0.626) is modest. The contribution is the design, not the leaderboard.
