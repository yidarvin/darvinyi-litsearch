Unsupervised Dense Information Retrieval with Contrastive Learning

A bi-encoder dense retriever trained on raw text with zero relevance labels. Positives come from cropping two spans of the same document; negatives come from a MoCo queue. The result — nicknamed Contriever — is the first unsupervised dense retriever to beat BM25 across most of BEIR, and it transfers across languages and even scripts.

Gautier Izacard, Mathilde Caron, Lucas Hosseini, Sebastian Riedel, Piotr Bojanowski, Armand Joulin, Edouard Grave

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

By 2021, dense retrievers — neural bi-encoders like DPR that embed a query and a document into the same vector space and rank by dot product — had overtaken classical term-frequency methods on the datasets where they were trained. Give DPR a few hundred thousand labeled query–passage pairs and it beats BM25 on that distribution.

The catch is generalization. Point the same dense retriever at a new domain with no training data — a setting the BEIR benchmark was built to measure — and it falls apart. In strict zero-shot transfer, a 50-year-old sparse baseline, BM25, still wins. That is an awkward result: the expensive neural method is the less robust one exactly where robustness matters most, because most real retrieval problems (a fresh corpus, a niche vertical, a low-resource language) come with no labels at all.

So the question this paper asks is sharp: can you train a dense retriever with no supervision whatsoever — no queries, no relevance judgments — and still match or beat BM25's out-of-the-box robustness? If yes, dense retrieval stops being a thing you can only afford where labels exist, and becomes a general-purpose drop-in.

// how it works

The method

The trick is to manufacture training signal out of unlabeled text using contrastive learning. With no real query–document pairs to learn from, the model invents its own positives: take a single document, cut out two random spans, and declare them a matching pair. The two spans share topic and vocabulary, so a good encoder should map them near each other; everything else in the batch is a negative that should be pushed away.

The authors call this independent random cropping. It differs from the earlier Inverse Cloze Task (ICT) in one important way: cropping is symmetric — the query span and the document span are drawn from the same distribution, rather than ICT's asymmetric "sentence vs. its surrounding context." They layer on light extra augmentation (random word deletion) and sample spans from a mix of Wikipedia and CCNet. The encoder is initialized from BERT (and from mBERT / XLM-R for the multilingual variant).

Scoring and the contrastive loss

Retrieval score is just a dot product between the query and document embeddings produced by the bi-encoder. Training minimizes the InfoNCE contrastive loss: pull the positive pair together, push the query away from a large bank of negative keys, all sharpened by a temperature τ.

ℒ(q, k+) =  − log  exp( s(q, k+) / τ ) exp( s(q, k+) / τ ) + Σi exp( s(q, ki−) / τ ) // InfoNCE: s(q,k) = E(q)·E(k) is the dot-product score; k⁺ is the cropped-span positive, kᵢ⁻ the negatives, τ the temperature

Where the negatives come from: MoCo

Contrastive learning works better with more negatives, but stuffing more negatives into a batch means a bigger batch, which is expensive. Contriever borrows the MoCo answer from computer vision: keep a queue of key embeddings from previous batches and reuse them as negatives, so the effective negative count is decoupled from batch size. To keep those stale queued keys consistent, the keys are produced by a slowly-moving momentum encoder whose weights track the query encoder via an exponential moving average:

θk  ←  m·θk  +  (1 − m)·θq // momentum update — the key encoder drifts slowly (m ≈ 0.999) so queued negatives stay comparable

In the headline configuration the queue holds 131,072 negatives (32,768 for the multilingual model, which needs the extra stability). Once a corpus is encoded, retrieval at query time is a fast approximate k-nearest-neighbors search with FAISS. The ablation below shows the payoff of scale: across BEIR, raising the number of negatives steadily improves nDCG@10, and the effect is strongest in the unsupervised setting.

// what they found

The results

The headline claim is the one the field remembered: trained with no annotated data at all, Contriever outperforms BM25 on 11 of 15 BEIR datasets for Recall@100. It is competitive on every dataset except two with idiosyncratic features — TREC-COVID (its training text predates the COVID outbreak) and Touché-2020 (very long documents) — where BM25 wins decisively. It also clears the previous unsupervised dense retrievers (ICT, Masked Salient Spans), which generally sat below BM25.

On open-domain QA retrieval the same story holds. Fully unsupervised, Contriever beats the BM25 numbers of Ma et al. (2021) on NaturalQuestions — a roughly 4-point Recall@100 gain — and ties it on TriviaQA, while still trailing the supervised DPR ceiling:

Pre-training that pays off twice

Contriever is even more valuable as an initialization than as a finished model. Used as pre-training before fine-tuning, it improves BEIR transfer over a plain BERT start, and in a few-shot regime (a few thousand in-domain examples) it beats the strategy of transferring a model trained on the large MS MARCO dataset.

Languages and scripts

The multilingual mContriever (jointly pre-trained on 29 languages) carries the idea further. Averaged over languages, unsupervised mContriever already reaches Recall@100 ≈ 77.2 (nDCG@10 ≈ 25.0). Fine-tune it on English supervised data only and the cross-lingual average jumps to Recall@100 ≈ 87.0 (nDCG@10 ≈ 38.4); fine-tune further on Mr. TyDi and Recall@100 exceeds 94, setting state-of-the-art on that benchmark. Most strikingly, it does cross-script retrieval — fetching English Wikipedia documents from Arabic queries — something term-matching methods like BM25 simply cannot do.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

This is a well-run paper with an honest results section — the failures are reported, not hidden. But two things deserve a harder look before you read "beats BM25" as "dense retrieval is solved unsupervised": which metric the headline uses, and how zero-shot the zero-shot setting really is.

The famous win is specifically Recall@100 — a recall-heavy metric that only asks whether a relevant document landed somewhere in the top 100. On nDCG@10, which scores the precision of the actual top-10 ranking a human would see, the paper concedes that BM25 still beats Contriever on the BEIR average (its Table 11), with the gap driven mostly by the two domain-shift losses. So the cleaner statement is: unsupervised Contriever is a better recall engine than BM25, not yet a better ranker. The two failure cases reinforce the point — where the pre-training corpus doesn't resemble the target domain (a post-cutoff COVID corpus; unusually long argument documents), the method degrades sharply, which is exactly the brittleness it was meant to cure.

And the "zero-shot" framing is generous. Contriever pre-trains on Wikipedia + CCNet, and several of its strongest targets — NaturalQuestions, HotpotQA, FEVER, DBPedia — are themselves Wikipedia-derived. Part of the BM25-beating margin is therefore distributional familiarity, not pure out-of-domain generalization. That doesn't make the result wrong; it makes it narrower than the word "zero-shot" implies.

baselines

Strong and contemporary for 2021: BM25 (the zero-shot champ it has to beat), REALM, SimCSE-RoBERTa-large, ICT/MSS as unsupervised rivals, and supervised DPR / FiD-KD as a ceiling. The comparison is fair.

contamination

No label leakage, but real domain overlap: the Wikipedia/CCNet pre-training distribution coincides with several BEIR/QA eval domains, softening the "zero-shot" claim. The honest TREC-COVID and Touché-2020 losses show what happens when that overlap is absent.

what's missed

The headline rides Recall@100; on nDCG@10 ranking precision BM25 still leads the BEIR average. And there's no latency/cost comparison — dense retrieval needs a GPU-built FAISS index, BM25 is a cheap inverted index. The win is quality-at-high-recall, not efficiency.

would raise confidence

Lead with nDCG@10 head-to-head, not Recall@100; report a BM25+dense hybrid (often the true SOTA); and carve out eval domains with zero Wikipedia/CCNet overlap to isolate genuine zero-shot transfer.

// bottom line

The caveats are about which metric and which domains, not whether the method works. Contriever proved that an unsupervised dense retriever can be a credible BM25 replacement and a powerful pre-training initialization — and history settled the matter: "Contriever, fine-tuned on MS-MARCO" became the default off-the-shelf retriever behind countless RAG pipelines, including the one that builds the distractor sets in Lost in the Middle.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. Contrastive learning on two cropped spans of the same unlabeled document yields a dense retriever that beats BM25 on Recall@100 for 11 of 15 BEIR datasets — no relevance labels, queries, or human annotation required.
  2. The enabling trick is MoCo: a momentum key-encoder plus a queue of 131,072 negatives decouples the negative count from batch size, and more negatives reliably help — most of all in the unsupervised regime.
  3. It's worth more as an initialization than as a final model: contrastive pre-training improves few-shot and MS MARCO-fine-tuned transfer, and unlocks cross-lingual, cross-script retrieval (English documents from Arabic queries) that term matching can't touch.
  4. Read the metric before you cite the headline: the BM25 win is Recall@100; on nDCG@10 ranking precision BM25 still leads the BEIR average, and the "zero-shot" gains lean partly on Wikipedia/CCNet domain overlap.
