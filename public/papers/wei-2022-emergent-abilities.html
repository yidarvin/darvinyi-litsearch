Emergent Abilities of Large Language Models

Some abilities are absent in small models and present in large ones — appearing not as a gentle slope but as a sharp jump once compute crosses a threshold. The survey that named the phenomenon, catalogued the thresholds, and seeded the still-running argument over whether the jump lives in the model or in the metric.

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

By 2022 the dominant story about scale was predictability. Scaling laws (Kaplan et al., 2020; Hoffmann et al., 2022) showed that cross-entropy loss falls smoothly as a power law in compute, parameters, and data — curves that hold over more than seven orders of magnitude. If loss is that forecastable, you ought to be able to plan capability the same way: train a few small models, fit the trend, read off what the big one will do.

Except some downstream tasks refuse to cooperate. On a handful of benchmarks a model sits at random-chance accuracy across several orders of magnitude of compute — and then, past some critical scale, performance leaps to well above random. That jump is invisible in the small-model trend: nothing in the flat region tells you it is coming. The field had a vocabulary for the smooth part and none for the discontinuity.

The paper's contribution is to name and survey that discontinuity. It calls an ability emergent if it is not present in smaller models but is present in larger models, and therefore cannot be predicted by extrapolating from smaller models. Why it matters: if new abilities — and new risks — appear unannounced as you scale, then no amount of small-model evaluation tells you what the next model can or cannot do. Capability planning and safety forecasting both inherit that blind spot.

// how it works

The method

This is a survey, not a new experiment: the authors collect scaling curves from prior work and read a single shape off them. The operational test is deliberately simple — plot task performance against model scale (training FLOPs on the x-axis, with parameter-count and WikiText103-perplexity versions in the appendix) and look for a curve that hugs random until a threshold, then rises sharply. Borrowing from physics (Philip Anderson's 1972 essay "More Is Different"), they frame this as a phase transition: quantitative changes in a system producing a qualitative change in behavior.

performance(scale) ≈ random  for scale < scale*   →   ≫ random  for scale ≥ scale* // an ability is emergent if a sharp threshold scale* separates chance-level from well-above-chance — no smooth ramp to extrapolate from

Emergence is surveyed in two regimes. (1) Few-shot prompting: a frozen pretrained model is shown a few input–output examples in context and asked to complete an unseen one — no gradient updates. The ability is emergent when accuracy is flat-random until a scale, then jumps. (2) Augmented prompting and finetuning strategies: techniques like chain-of-thought, instruction tuning, or scratchpads. Here a technique counts as emergent if it gives no benefit — or even hurts — until the model is large enough, at which point it starts to help. Evidence spans five model families: GPT-3, LaMDA, Gopher, Chinchilla, and PaLM.

The authors are careful that scale is not one number: compute, parameters, and data move together but not identically — Chinchilla has one-quarter of Gopher's parameters at similar compute. They explicitly warn that the scale at which an ability emerges is not an immutable property: better data or architecture can shift it earlier. That caveat turns out to matter a great deal for how the result has aged.

// what they found

The results

The headline is a catalogue of concrete thresholds. In few-shot prompting: 3-digit addition/subtraction emerges for GPT-3 at 2.3×10²² FLOPs (13B params), with 4–5 digit arithmetic holding out until 3.1×10²³ (175B). On MMLU (57 subjects), models below ~10B params do no better than guessing; GPT-3 crosses at 175B and Gopher/Chinchilla cross at 3–5×10²³ FLOPs (70B–280B). TruthfulQA is adversarial to GPT-3 — never above random even at the largest size — while Gopher only clears chance at 280B (5×10²³ FLOPs), then jumps more than 20% above random. Word-in-Context resists GPT-3 and Chinchilla entirely (both stay random at ~5×10²³ FLOPs) and only emerges once PaLM reaches 2.5×10²⁴ FLOPs (540B).

For augmented prompting, the same threshold pattern appears in the benefit of a technique rather than in raw accuracy (Figure 3). Chain-of-thought only beats standard prompting on GSM8K past ~10²³ FLOPs (~100B params); below that it is neutral or harmful. Instruction tuning (FLAN) hurts models of 8B (7×10²¹ FLOPs) or smaller and only helps at ~100B. A scratchpad for 8-digit addition pays off only above ~9×10¹⁹ FLOPs (40M). And the P(True) self-calibration method beats letter-choice probabilities only at 2.6×10²³ FLOPs (52B).

A second finding cuts against a pure-scale reading: PaLM 62B beats LaMDA 137B and GPT-3 175B on 14 BIG-Bench tasks despite fewer parameters and FLOPs — the authors attribute this to higher-quality data and architectural choices. And once an ability is found, the threshold can fall: instruction following first worked only at 68B, but Sanh et al. induced it in an 11B encoder–decoder and Ouyang et al.'s InstructGPT made a 1.3B model preferred over much larger ones. Emergence scales are moving targets, not constants of nature.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

The phenomenon is real — downstream usefulness genuinely does jump — but the word "emergent" quietly fuses two different claims: an observation about our metrics and our sampling, and a much stronger claim about a discontinuity in the model. Most of the paper's vulnerabilities live in that gap, and the authors flag several of them themselves.

The sharpest objection is metric choice. Exact-match and multiple-choice accuracy are discontinuous, all-or-nothing scores: a multi-step answer that is 90% right earns zero until the last token lands. Section 5.1 concedes this and shows that cross-entropy loss improves smoothly below the threshold where exact-match is still flat — i.e. the model is measurably getting better the whole time; the metric just hides it. Schaeffer, Miranda & Koyejo (NeurIPS 2023, "Are Emergent Abilities a Mirage?") formalized this: swap to a continuous metric (token edit distance, Brier score) and many of these very curves straighten into smooth, predictable ramps. Emergence, on this reading, is at least partly manufactured by the scoring function.

Second, the curves confound scale with everything else. Each plot strings together heterogeneous model families — GPT-3, LaMDA, Gopher, Chinchilla, PaLM — trained on different data, tokenizers, and architectures. The paper's own PaLM-62B-beats-LaMDA-137B result is the proof: the "jump" can be driven by data quality or architecture, not parameter count. Third, the x-axis is sparsely sampled — a handful of sizes per family — so a smooth-but-steep rise is visually indistinguishable from a true step; on a log-x axis the eye is biased toward "sudden." None of this is hidden, but the framing leans on the visual reading more than the controlled evidence supports.

baselines

A survey, not controlled experiments: every curve mixes model families with different data/architecture, so "scale" is confounded — the paper's own PaLM-62B > LaMDA-137B example shows data and architecture, not just parameters, can trigger the jump.

contamination

Few-shot eval over web-scale pretraining makes benchmark leakage (BIG-Bench, MMLU) plausible and uncontrolled — but here the deeper measurement worry is the metric, not memorization.

what's missed

The "phase transition" is partly a metric artifact. Discontinuous accuracy manufactures sharp jumps; the authors' own cross-entropy analysis (§5.1) and Schaeffer et al. (2023) show continuous metrics make many curves smooth and extrapolable. Sparse size sampling under-resolves where — or whether — a true step exists.

would raise confidence

Densely sample checkpoints within one model family; report continuous metrics (per-token likelihood, edit distance, Brier) beside exact-match; pre-register an emergence definition robust to metric choice; ablate data/architecture from scale.

// bottom line

A field-defining framing that named a real and useful pattern and gave the community a shared vocabulary for unpredictable capability. But "emergent" overstates the case: the signal (capability becomes useful abruptly) is robust, while the claim (a discontinuous step in the model) is largely in the eye of the metric and the sampling grid. Read it as the paper that opened the question, not the one that settled it.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. Emergent = not-in-small, in-large. The paper's lasting contribution is a name and a definition: abilities you cannot forecast by extrapolating smaller models, observed across five model families and two regimes (few-shot prompting and augmented strategies like chain-of-thought).
  2. The jump is partly about measurement. Exact-match and multiple-choice accuracy are discontinuous; the authors show cross-entropy loss improves smoothly below threshold (§5.1), and the 2023 "mirage" rebuttal showed continuous metrics dissolve many emergent curves. Emergence is at least as much a property of the metric as of the model.
  3. Scale is not the only knob. PaLM 62B outperforms LaMDA 137B on 14 BIG-Bench tasks, and instruction-following dropped from a 68B threshold to 11B (Sanh) and 1.3B (InstructGPT). Emergence thresholds fall as data and training improve — they are moving targets, not constants.
