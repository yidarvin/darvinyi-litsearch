MathDial: A Dialogue Tutoring Dataset with Rich Pedagogical Properties Grounded in Math Reasoning Problems

A 2,861-dialogue corpus of one-to-one math tutoring, collected by pairing real teachers with an LLM that role-plays a confused student — built to train tutors that scaffold instead of tell, because a GPT model asked to tutor reveals the answer 66% of the time.

Jakub Macina, Nico Daheim, Sankalan Pal Chowdhury, Tanmay Sinha, Manu Kapur, Iryna Gurevych, Mrinmaya Sachan

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

Conversational tutoring is one of the oldest promises of education technology, but the field is starved of data. Recording real one-to-one tutoring raises serious privacy concerns, and crowdsourcing dialogues is expensive, hard to synchronize, and tends to produce pedagogically thin transcripts from untrained workers. The handful of public corpora that existed when MathDial was written are all small or compromised: CIMA (391 dialogues), TSCC (102), TalkMoves (567, classroom), and NCTE (1,660, classroom) variously lack grounding information, have low tutoring quality, or capture noisy many-to-one classroom settings rather than focused one-to-one help.

At the same time, large language models had become excellent solvers of grade-school math — but that capability did not transfer to teaching. The paper's motivating measurement is blunt: when ChatGPT is simply asked to tutor a student, it directly reveals the solution 66% of the time and gives factually incorrect feedback 59% of the time. A good tutor's hardest skill is restraint — diagnosing a student's specific confusion and guiding them toward fixing it themselves, rather than dumping the answer. The gap MathDial targets is therefore twofold: (1) a large, grounded, pedagogically annotated corpus of one-to-one tutoring, and (2) a way to measure the guide-versus-tell tradeoff that off-the-shelf LLMs fail.

// how it works

The method

MathDial's central trick is a semi-synthetic Wizard-of-Oz collection scheme. Instead of connecting two crowdworkers (the classic WoZ setup), it pairs one real expert teacher with an LLM that role-plays the student. That sidesteps both privacy (no real student data) and quality (the human in the loop is a trained teacher, not an annotator improvising both roles), while still scaling cheaply.

Manufacturing a believable confusion

Each dialogue is grounded in a multi-step word problem from GSM8K. To seed a realistic mistake, the pipeline samples N = 50 chain-of-thought solutions from ChatGPT (gpt-3.5-turbo) at temperature 0.7, groups the incorrect answers by their final numeric value, and picks one from the largest wrong-answer cluster — i.e. the model's single most common way of getting the problem wrong. The simulated student is an InstructGPT (text-davinci-003) model, prompted with that incorrect solution plus one of six misconception-based student profiles (drawn from the learning-sciences work of Booth et al., 2017, e.g. struggling to recognize the problem type).

The teacher and the move taxonomy

The human teacher — one of 91 experts recruited through Prolific, each passing a 100%-score qualification test — sees the problem, its step-by-step solution, and the student's confusion, then guides the student to the answer. Crucially, before every turn the teacher selects a pedagogical "move", which the authors find produces more diverse and deliberate teaching patterns. The moves follow a four-way taxonomy grounded in Reiser's (2004) structure-vs-problematize theory of scaffolding:

  • Focus — constrain the student toward direct progress ("Can you calculate …?").
  • Probing — generalize or perturb the problem so the student explores the underlying concept ("How would things change if they had N items instead?").
  • Telling — reveal part of the strategy or answer, used as a fallback when a student is stuck.
  • Generic — greetings and conversational glue with little pedagogical content.

Every dialogue ships with rich metadata: the word problem, the step-by-step solution, the exact step where confusion occurred, per-turn move labels, and whether the confusion was ultimately resolved. The result is MathDial: ~2.9k dialogues, ~14k utterances.

Modeling tutors

The benchmark task is tutor response generation: given the dialogue history u1T, grounding information K (the problem, step-by-step solution, and student attempt), and a target move A, generate the teacher's next turn. The models are ordinary conditional language models, optimized either by finetuning for 10 epochs or by zero-shot prompting:

pθ(uT+1 | u1T, K, A) = ∏n=1NT+1 pθ( [uT+1]n | [uT+1]1n−1, u1T, K, A ) // next teacher turn, conditioned on history u₁ᵀ, grounding K, and the chosen move A

They finetune BART, T5, Flan-T5 and OPT at several sizes, and zero-shot-prompt ChatGPT. Beyond standard per-utterance metrics (sBLEU, BERTScore, and a knowledge-overlap F1 the authors use as a rough faithfulness proxy), MathDial's signature contribution is a pair of interactive metrics computed by letting a tutor model actually teach the LLM student to the end of the conversation: Success@k (did the student reach the correct answer within k turns?) and Telling@k (did the tutor reveal the final answer before the student got there?). Together they make the guide-vs-tell tradeoff measurable rather than anecdotal.

// what they found

The results

The corpus. MathDial holds 2,861 dialogues / 14,197 utterances — the largest of the public one-to-one tutoring corpora, several times the size of CIMA (391) or TSCC (102) — while keeping high lexical diversity (bigram entropy 3.54, on par with the human-transcript datasets), an uptake score of 0.83, and 17.3 words per utterance. It is also the only dataset in the comparison with explicit grounding (confusion + answers). Teacher behavior is dominated by scaffolding: Focus is the most common move (~37% of turns), followed by Generic (~24%), Probing (~21%), and Telling is the rarest (~18%) — and Telling rises only late in a conversation, when a student is genuinely stuck. Teachers reported resolving the student's confusion in ~89% of dialogues (≈75% via scaffolding, only ≈14% by revealing the answer), and judged ~80% of the synthetic confusions to be conceptual rather than arithmetic slips.

The modeling story. Small models finetuned on MathDial beat much larger prompted ones on automatic metrics: Flan-T5-780M reaches sBLEU 9.7 / BERTScore 55.0, while zero-shot ChatGPT scores just sBLEU 2.2 / BERTScore 47.7. Two caveats the authors themselves raise: simply scaling model size does not reliably help, and there is a large generalization gap between problems seen and unseen during training. A grounding ablation shows the step-by-step solution is the single most valuable piece of context (Flan-T5-780M: 8.0 → 9.5 sBLEU when it is added).

The pedagogy story. This is where finetuning pulls clearly ahead. In human evaluation, Flan-T5-780M scores 0.89 correctness and 2.19 equitable-tutoring versus ChatGPT's 0.43 / 1.43 (human ground-truth: 0.98 / 2.42). And in the interactive simulation, the finetuned tutor matches ChatGPT's solve rate while almost never giving the answer away — Success@5 of 30% at <1% Telling@5, against ChatGPT's 29% success but 16% telling (rising to 32% telling by turn 10). No model reaches the human tutors' 59% Success@5.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

MathDial's framing is excellent and its corpus is genuinely useful — but the headline modeling claim ("small finetuned tutors beat ChatGPT") rests on an evaluation that quietly favors the finetuned model, and the most quotable number leans on a weak baseline.

Start with the student. The "student" is an LLM, not a child. Teachers knew they were talking to InstructGPT, and the authors concede they may have tutored it differently — or lost motivation — than with a real sixth-grader. More consequentially, the interactive Success@k / Telling@k metrics are scored against the same InstructGPT student family used to collect the data. So the MathDial-finetuned tutor is evaluated on exactly the student distribution it trained against, while zero-shot ChatGPT meets that simulated student cold. That circularity inflates the finetuned model's tell-vs-guide advantage.

Next, the baseline. The "tells 66% / wrong 59%" figure is un-prompted ChatGPT with no scaffolding system prompt and no few-shot pedagogical examples. And the automatic-metric gulf (sBLEU 2.2 vs 9.7) mostly punishes ChatGPT for not matching MathDial's reference style — a point the authors make themselves when they question whether these metrics are valid for tutoring at all. Low sBLEU is not the same as bad teaching.

On the data itself: the math is GSM8K, which sits in essentially every model's pretraining, and the "seen" test split shares ~60% of its problems with training — so part of the finetuning win is problem familiarity (hence the large seen→unseen drop). The confusions are LLM-generated (the most frequent wrong chain-of-thought), so they reflect model error modes, yet the "80% conceptual" characterization is a teacher judgment on those synthetic mistakes, not on observed student misconceptions. Finally, the human evaluation is thin and noisy where it matters: n = 50 per annotator with only three annotators, and inter-annotator agreement is low on the two most load-bearing axes — coherence κ = 0.29 and equitable-tutoring κ = 0.34 (correctness is fine at κ = 0.69). The move labels themselves are shaky too: validating 17 sampled conversations, agreement on the four-way taxonomy between the two independent annotators is κ = 0.60, but drops to κ = 0.49 and κ = 0.34 between either annotator and the teacher's own label — and the authors trace much of that gap specifically to Probing vs Focus confusion, since merging the two into one "scaffolding" category raises agreement to κ = 0.67 / 0.75 / 0.55.

baselines

ChatGPT runs zero-shot with no scaffolding prompt or few-shot examples; the "tells 66%" headline and the sBLEU gulf partly reflect an unfair, style-mismatched baseline rather than worse pedagogy.

contamination

Grounded in GSM8K (in everyone's pretraining); ~60% problem overlap in the "seen" split; confusions are LLM-generated, mirroring model errors rather than measured student misconceptions.

what's missed

No real students. Interactive Success@k / Telling@k are scored against the same InstructGPT student used to build the data — in-distribution for the finetuned tutor; the equitable-tutoring axis has weak agreement (κ = 0.34).

would raise confidence

A held-out human-student interactive eval, a fairly-prompted / few-shot ChatGPT tutor baseline, and fresh non-GSM8K problems with measured (not simulated) confusions.

// bottom line

Read MathDial as evidence about the data and the metric, not as a settled verdict on prompting vs finetuning for real tutoring. Its lasting contribution is operationalizing the right question — does the tutor guide or tell? — with a Success@k / Telling@k tradeoff that later tutoring benchmarks (MRBench, TutorBench) build directly on. The "780M beats ChatGPT" result is suggestive, not conclusive, precisely because the learner is an LLM and the baseline is un-coached.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. It's a method and a metric, not just a dataset. Semi-synthetic Wizard-of-Oz (real teacher × LLM student) dodges privacy and scale, and Success@k vs Telling@k turns "good tutoring" into a measurable guide-versus-tell tradeoff.
  2. Restraint, not capability, is the bottleneck. Un-prompted ChatGPT can solve the math but reveals the answer 66% of the time; a 780M Flan-T5 finetuned on MathDial matches its Success@5 (~30%) at under 1% Telling@5.
  3. Trust the corpus and the framing more than the leaderboard. Because the learner is an LLM and the ChatGPT baseline is zero-shot, the finetuning-beats-prompting result is a hypothesis — which is exactly why MathDial became a substrate for follow-on tutoring evals rather than a final answer.
