MLE-bench: Evaluating Machine Learning Agents on Machine Learning Engineering

Can an AI agent do the day-to-day job of an ML engineer? OpenAI hand-ports 75 Kaggle competitions into an offline environment and grades agent submissions against the real human leaderboards. The best setup — o1-preview with the AIDE scaffold — earns a Kaggle medal in 16.9% of competitions, but only when handed a problem solvable by a known recipe.

Chan, Chowdhury, Jaffe, Aung, Sherburn, Mays, Starace, Liu, Maksin, Patwardhan, Weng, Mądry

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

By late 2024, language models were saturating the code benchmarks that had defined progress for years — HumanEval, MBPP, APPS — and were starting to crack real software-engineering tasks on SWE-bench. But all of those measure a narrow slice of the work: write a function from a docstring, or patch a bug in an existing repository. None of them ask the harder, messier question that actually matters for AI research labs: can an agent do machine-learning engineering end-to-end? That means reading an open-ended problem, preparing a real dataset, choosing and training a model, debugging when it underperforms, managing compute and runtime, and iterating toward a competitive score — autonomously, over many hours.

The authors flag two design problems that prior benchmarks left unsolved. First, representativeness: the tasks should look like contemporary ML engineering, not toy problems with clean APIs. Second, and rarer, a human anchor — a way to say not just "the agent got 40%" but "the agent performed at the level of a competent human practitioner." Kaggle competitions solve both at once: they are real-world predictive-modeling challenges with messy multimodal data, and every one ships with a leaderboard of thousands of human attempts and a medal system that already calibrates "how good is good."

There is also a sharper reason to build this dial now. An agent that can autonomously do ML engineering is, by definition, an agent that can improve ML systems — including the models training it. MLE-bench is explicitly positioned as an autonomy / AI-R&D-acceleration measure, named as a candidate metric in OpenAI's Preparedness Framework, Anthropic's Responsible Scaling Policy, and Google DeepMind's Frontier Safety Framework. The point is to watch this capability climb before it matters, not after.

// how it works

The method

MLE-bench is an offline Kaggle competition environment. To build it, the team started from the 5,673 completed competitions in the Meta Kaggle dataset, dropped the loosely-vetted Community competitions, and hand-screened the remaining 586 for relevance to modern ML engineering and for whether the grading and a sensible train-test split could be reconstructed. The survivors: 75 competitions (plus a 7-competition development split). Each is annotated by complexity from the point of view of an ML engineer today — Low (<2 h of work, 22 comps, 30%), Medium (2–10 h, 38 comps, 50%), or High (>10 h, 15 comps, 20%). The competitions span NLP, computer vision, and signal processing, and their original prize pools total $1,948,016 ($25,974 per competition on average).

Every sample bundles four things: the competition description scraped from the Overview and Data tabs, the dataset (usually re-split, since Kaggle rarely releases the original test set — a fresh 10% holdout is carved out and checked to score similarly to the original), grading code re-implemented locally to match the competition's own metric (AUROC, domain-specific losses, etc.), and a leaderboard snapshot of the Private leaderboard (taken May–August 2024). An agent reads the description, works in the environment, and writes a single submission.csv, which is graded locally and ranked against the real humans.

Medals, and a deliberately hard headline metric

Rather than report each competition's idiosyncratic raw score, MLE-bench borrows Kaggle's own currency: medals. Bronze/silver/gold thresholds depend on the number of teams (e.g. for 1000+ teams, bronze is top 10%, gold is top 0.2%) so a medal means a comparable level of achievement everywhere. The single headline metric is the percentage of attempts that earn any medal (bronze or better). This ceiling is intentionally brutal: only nine humans in Kaggle's entire history have earned medals across 75 different competitions.

Because runs are stochastic, the team also measures performance under repeated attempts with the pass@k estimator from the Codex paper — the probability that at least one of k sampled attempts earns a medal, estimated unbiasedly from n seeds (with c medal-winning ones):

pass@k := Ecomps [ 1 − C(n − c, k)C(n, k) ] // unbiased estimate of "at least one medal in k attempts," averaged over competitions

Setup, scaffolds, and anti-cheating

Each agent runs in an Ubuntu Docker container with 36 vCPUs, 440 GB RAM, and a single 24 GB A10 GPU, with up to 24 hours per competition, repeated over 3 seeds. A local validation server checks whether a submission is well-formed (but never reveals its score), mirroring Kaggle's daily submission checks. Three open-source scaffolds are tested: AIDE (Weco AI), purpose-built to run a tree search over candidate Kaggle solutions; MLAB (the ResearchAgent from MLAgentBench); and OpenHands (its CodeActAgent). Two rules keep the test honest: the submission must be produced by a model, not hand-written from the agent's own world knowledge (no recalling memorized labels), and the agent may not look up online solutions. Compliance is enforced with a GPT-4o log analyzer for rule-breaking and the Dolos code-plagiarism detector, which disqualifies any submission >60% similar to the competition's top-50 public notebooks.

// what they found

The results

The best configuration, o1-preview with AIDE, earns a medal in 16.9% of competitions (pass@1) — almost double the next-best model. To put that in perspective, o1-preview averages 7 gold medals across the 75 competitions, and Kaggle Grandmaster status requires only 5 golds (though the authors are careful to note the caveats: not every competition grants medals, datasets and grading are slightly modified, and agents wield more recent technology than many of the original human competitors).

Two findings stand out. First, the scaffold matters as much as the model. Holding the model fixed at GPT-4o, swapping scaffolds swings the score by an order of magnitude: AIDE 8.7% vs. OpenHands 4.4% vs. MLAB 0.8%. AIDE wins because it keeps relentlessly prompting for improvements until the 24-hour clock (or its 500-node search budget) runs out, while MLAB and OpenHands frequently quit within minutes despite being told to optimize for the full day. Second, all agents struggle with the engineering, not the ML: they often fail to produce a valid submission at all, rarely budget for compute or runtime (overloading disk/RAM until the process is killed), and flounder when they need to debug and recover from a misstep.

Throwing more attempts at the problem helps a lot. Both agents roughly double their medal rate from pass@1 to pass@6, and o1-preview climbs from 16.9% (pass@1) to 34.1% (pass@8). Strikingly, GPT-4o with six attempts (17.0%) matches o1-preview with one (16.9%) — multiple cheaper rolls of the dice can buy a more expensive model's single shot.

Other resource knobs are more surprising. More compute barely moves the needle: CPU-only (9.1%), the standard single A10 (8.7%), and two A10s (10.2%) are statistically indistinguishable — and in the "Extra GPU" logs, GPT-4o never even tried to use the second card. More time helps, but with steep diminishing returns: extending the limit from 24 to 100 hours lifts GPT-4o from 8.7% to 11.8%, with most medals won in the first couple of hours.

On contamination — the central worry for a benchmark built from years-old public Kaggle data — the paper runs three checks and finds no systematic inflation. (1) Familiarity vs. performance: measuring GPT-4o's mean token probability over each competition's page and top discussion posts, the correlation with score is actually slightly negative (Pearson r = −0.24, p = 0.04) — being "familiar" doesn't help. (2) Obfuscation: manually rewriting all 75 descriptions to strip the Kaggle provenance leaves the score essentially unchanged (8.5% → 8.4%). (3) Plagiarism & rule-breaking: Dolos and the log analyzer flag no medal-winning cheats.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

MLE-bench is one of the better-constructed agent benchmarks of its era — human-anchored, honestly contamination-probed, and safety-motivated — but the headline number deserves several asterisks, and the paper, to its credit, supplies most of them itself.

Start with what "16.9%" actually measures. It is the score of the single best model paired with the single best scaffold, and the scaffold gap is enormous: on GPT-4o, AIDE (8.7%) beats MLAB (0.8%) by more than 10×. So the benchmark is partly measuring scaffold engineering, and the winning scaffold — AIDE — is purpose-built to do tree search over Kaggle solutions. That's a sensible thing to use, but it bakes Kaggle-shaped priors into the result, which makes "agent capability" and "how well-tuned your harness is to this exact task format" hard to separate. The models, meanwhile, are all 2024-vintage (o1-preview, GPT-4o, Claude 3.5 Sonnet, Llama 3.1 405B), and Gemini-1.5-Pro couldn't even be run because its outputs kept getting flagged for recitation — a telling gap in the comparison set.

The contamination story is the one to watch hardest. The dataset is entirely public Kaggle — descriptions, winning write-ups, and in some cases the actual test labels — so leakage into pretraining is near-certain (the authors note GPT-4's base model could regurgitate rows of the Titanic dataset). Their two quantitative controls are reassuring but narrow: both the familiarity correlation and the obfuscation test probe only GPT-4o's base model, and both target naive regurgitation, not the subtler failure mode they explicitly flag — a model absorbing the high-level strategies of winning solutions from discussion posts. Figure 9's temporal cliff (medals on competitions through ~2022, near-zero afterward) is read by the paper as "newer competitions are harder," and that's plausible — but it's exactly the shape you'd also expect if contamination quietly helped on older ones. The two explanations are confounded, and the benchmark can't fully tell them apart.

Finally, the metric and the comparison are both coarser than they look. "Any medal %" is a hard threshold that collapses wildly different competitions into one bit (per-category results swing from 60% on Image Regression to 0% on Image Segmentation), and the medals are awarded against a re-split test set and re-implemented grader, not the original leaderboard the humans actually faced — the team verifies distributional similarity via sample/gold submissions, but it remains an approximation. Add that today's agents enjoy years of algorithmic hindsight over the original 2013–2022 competitors, and the human anchor is more "indicative" than "exact."

baselines

Fair scaffolds (AIDE/MLAB/OpenHands) but a 10× scaffold gap means the headline is a best-model×best-scaffold figure; models are all 2024-era, and Gemini-1.5-Pro couldn't be evaluated at all.

contamination

Honestly the weak point. Public Kaggle data is almost surely in pretraining; the two controls (r=−0.24 familiarity, 8.5→8.4 obfuscation) only probe GPT-4o's base and only catch naive copying, not strategy reuse. The pre-/post-2022 cliff is genuinely confounded.

what's missed

A single thresholded medal rate hides huge per-competition and per-category variance; re-split test sets and a re-implemented grader make the human-leaderboard comparison approximate, not exact.

would raise confidence

A rolling refresh with post-cutoff competitions (the authors suggest it), contamination probes on the actually-evaluated models, and reporting a continuous leaderboard-percentile metric alongside the medal threshold.

// bottom line

A landmark, carefully-built measure of autonomous ML engineering that the major labs now treat as a real autonomy dial. Just read "16.9%" as "the best 2024 model, in a Kaggle-tuned harness, on a public dataset" — the contamination controls are honest but can't fully separate genuine engineering skill from memorized Kaggle lore on the older competitions.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. MLE-bench reframes "can AI do ML engineering?" as a Kaggle medal race: 75 hand-ported competitions, graded offline against real human leaderboards. The best agent, o1-preview + AIDE, medals in 16.9% of them.
  2. The scaffold is as decisive as the model — swapping MLAB (0.8%) for AIDE (8.7%) is a 10× swing on the same GPT-4o. More attempts and more time help (16.9%→34.1% at pass@8; 8.7%→11.8% at 100 h); extra GPUs don't.
  3. Agents shine on competitions solvable by well-known recipes and on older competitions, but stumble on the engineering: producing valid submissions, debugging, recovering from missteps, and budgeting compute — and collapse on post-2022 challenges.
  4. The work is wired into frontier labs' autonomy / AI-R&D risk frameworks, making MLE-bench a watched dial for AI accelerating AI — which is exactly why its contamination caveats matter.
