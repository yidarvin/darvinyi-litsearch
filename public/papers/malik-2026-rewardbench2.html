RewardBench 2: Advancing Reward Model Evaluation

The AI2 team's own successor to RewardBench: six domains built almost entirely from unseen human prompts, a harder best-of-4 accuracy format instead of the usual 2-way pick, two new domains (Precise Instruction Following and Ties), and — unusually for a reward-model benchmark — an explicit empirical check of whether the benchmark's scores actually predict downstream best-of-N and RLHF performance.

Saumya Malik, Valentina Pyatkin, Sander Land, Jacob Morrison, Noah A. Smith, Hannaneh Hajishirzi, Nathan Lambert

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

Reward models (RMs) sit underneath most of post-training — RLHF, best-of-N sampling, data filtering — but the tooling for evaluating them lagged the tooling for evaluating the policies they train. The original RewardBench (Lambert et al., 2024) established the field's first common accuracy-based benchmark, and follow-ups like RM-Bench and PPE extended it — but by 2025 the authors observed a specific saturation problem: RewardBench's chosen-vs-rejected pairs, largely repurposed from existing downstream evaluation sets, no longer separated strong reward models from weak ones, and "progress in evaluation has not been mirrored by the effectiveness of reward models in downstream tasks — simpler direct alignment algorithms are reported to work better in many cases."

Two more specific gaps motivate the redesign. First, contamination risk: most prior RM benchmarks reuse prompts from well-known downstream evaluations, so a high score can partly reflect memorization of those evaluations rather than genuine preference-modeling skill — "without entirely new prompts, claims of correlations to downstream benchmarks must overcome the potential of contamination with respect to the downstream evaluation target." Second, an unvalidated correlation assumption: the field had been treating RM benchmark accuracy as a proxy for downstream usefulness in best-of-N sampling and RLHF training without much direct evidence that the proxy holds, especially as prior work (Wen et al., 2024) started to question whether accuracy-based RM benchmarks predict downstream performance at all.

// how it works

The method

REWARDBENCH 2 keeps the accuracy-based classification format of its predecessor but changes two things that matter a lot in practice. First, it moves from the standard 2-completion (chosen vs. rejected) format to a best-of-4 format — one chosen completion plus three rejected distractors per prompt — which drops the random-guessing baseline from 50% to 25%, buying headroom to separate strong models from mediocre ones instead of everything clustering near the ceiling. Second, roughly 70% of its 1,865 prompts are "Human"-sourced: previously unreleased, real user queries drawn (with consent) from the WildChat pipeline, filtered with QuRater, a topic classifier, and manual inspection, then checked against twenty widely-used downstream evaluations with the Tulu 3 decontamination toolkit to ensure no overlap.

The six domains, each built with a domain-specific completion-generation and verification pipeline (1,865 prompts total, from completions generated by 20 different models):

  • Factuality (475 prompts) — natural and deliberately-corrupted completions, labeled "accurate"/"inaccurate" only when two independent LLM judges agree.
  • Precise Instruction Following (160 prompts) — a hard constraint from the IFBench taxonomy (e.g. "answer without the letter u") appended to each prompt, graded with verifier functions rather than judgment calls.
  • Math (183 prompts) — middle-school through college-level problems, candidates labeled by majority voting then manually verified given how brittle automated answer-extraction is.
  • Safety (450 prompts) — built on CoCoNot's compliance taxonomy, with subset-specific GPT-4o rubrics and full manual verification.
  • Focus (495 prompts) — following LLMBar, human prompts are LM-rewritten to induce off-topic "rejected" completions that test whether an RM notices a response has drifted from the actual question.
  • Ties (102 prompts, new) — questions with many valid answers (e.g. "name a color of the rainbow"), scoring whether an RM avoids arbitrary overconfidence between equally-correct options while still clearly preferring any correct answer over an incorrect one.

Scoring is per-domain accuracy averaged unweighted across all six domains, except Ties, which blends accuracy with a margin-based calibration check — whether the reward gap between correct and incorrect answers exceeds the gap among the correct answers themselves, a property the authors argue matters directly for the RLHF training signal.

// what they found

The results

REWARDBENCH 2 is meaningfully harder: leading models score "20 or more points lower" on it than on RewardBench 1, with Precise Instruction Following and Math the sharpest drops — leading models fall below 40% accuracy on Precise IF and below 70% on Math. The top model overall, Skywork/Skywork-Reward-V2-Llama-3.1-8B, averages 84.1% across domains (Factuality 84.6, IF 66.3, Math 77.6, Safety 96.7, Focus 98.4, Ties 81.2) — even the best available reward model is well short of the ceiling on instruction-following and math specifically.

On the correlation question, the paper reports a Pearson correlation of 0.87 between REWARDBENCH 2 average score and downstream best-of-N performance across 113 reward models, evaluated over GSM8K, MATH, IFEval, AlpacaEval 2, BBH, PopQA, and HumanEval+ with 16 sampled completions per prompt from Tulu 3 8B SFT. The Math subset in particular is a strong predictor of downstream math and coding performance. RLHF is a different story: running PPO with 17 different reward models on Tulu 3 8B SFT, downstream Tulu-suite performance saturates once the RM is "decent-to-good" (REWARDBENCH 2 scores from 49.8 to 68.5 for on-policy, in-distribution RMs), matching the comparable Tulu 3 8B DPO baseline (60.3, versus the 54.1 starting-policy score). The one factor that actually predicts a drop in PPO performance is a lineage/distribution mismatch between the reward model and the policy — an RM based on a different base model or trained on out-of-distribution preference prompts than the PPO policy can score well on REWARDBENCH 2 (e.g. 72.9) while delivering markedly weaker downstream PPO performance (54.5) than a lower-scoring, on-policy RM.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

This is an unusually self-aware benchmark paper: rather than just shipping a harder leaderboard, the authors go looking for — and find — the exact place their own metric breaks down (RLHF/PPO performance, once the RM clears a "good enough" threshold), and report it prominently instead of burying it. That's a stronger form of validation than most reward-model benchmarks attempt, and it directly informs how a practitioner should and shouldn't use the number.

baselines

Over 100 reward models are evaluated, spanning classifier RMs, generative/LM-as-judge RMs, and newly trained in-house models, which is a genuinely broad sweep. The downstream validation, though, rests on a narrower base: PPO correlation is checked with only 17 reward models and a single policy/prompt setup (Tulu 3 8B SFT, Tulu 3 preference mix), so the finding that benchmark scores saturate against PPO performance is demonstrated in one training configuration, not shown to generalize across policy sizes, algorithms (e.g. GRPO, DPO variants), or preference-data mixtures.

contamination

Handled seriously and up front: ~70% of prompts are previously-unreleased WildChat queries, cross-checked against twenty downstream evaluations with the Tulu 3 decontamination toolkit. The paper is candid that its own predecessor, RewardBench, likely suffers exactly the contamination problem this benchmark is designed to avoid — including the striking finding that RewardBench-1 and RewardBench-2 scores correlate more tightly for the authors' own trained models than for external models, which the paper itself flags as "indicating a potential of metric capture to version 1."

what's missed

The generative/LM-as-judge models (marked with *, including the Gemini and Claude entries at the top of the leaderboard) are evaluated under two different prompting strategies — picking the best of the four options, or rating each option individually — with the paper reporting whichever setting scored higher for each model. That per-model best-case selection isn't available to the classifier RMs, which makes head-to-head ranking between judge-style and classifier-style RMs on the same leaderboard less apples-to-apples than the single "Average" column implies. The Ties domain, while a genuinely novel calibration check, was "created manually with assistance from AI models" at only 102 examples — the smallest and most bespoke of the six subsets, and correlates only weakly with the downstream tasks tested (unsurprising, since none of the seven downstream evals specifically exercises multi-valid-answer calibration), which the paper acknowledges without fully resolving what would validate it externally.

would raise confidence

Extending the PPO correlation study across more policy models, RL algorithms, and preference-data mixtures beyond the single Tulu 3 8B setup; an apples-to-apples comparison protocol between classifier RMs and LM-judge RMs that doesn't let judge models cherry-pick their better prompting strategy; and a released, periodically-refreshed private split (mirroring AgentHarm's approach) to catch the same "metric capture" dynamic the paper found in its own predecessor before it repeats on REWARDBENCH 2 itself.

// bottom line

The headline "harder benchmark with unseen prompts" framing undersells the paper's real contribution, which is empirically mapping where RM-benchmark accuracy does and doesn't predict downstream training outcomes — a question the field had mostly been assuming rather than measuring. The best-of-N correlation (r=0.87) is genuinely reassuring; the PPO saturation-plus-lineage-mismatch finding is the more important and more actionable result, and it's a direct warning against the common practice of just taking the top-of-leaderboard RM for an RLHF run.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. REWARDBENCH 2 is a best-of-4 (25% random baseline), majority-unseen-human-prompt reward-model benchmark across six domains, and leading models score roughly 20+ points lower on it than on the original RewardBench — including below 40% on Precise Instruction Following and below 70% on Math for the best models tested.
  2. Benchmark score correlates strongly with downstream best-of-N sampling performance (Pearson r=0.87 across 113 RMs), but correlates with downstream PPO performance only up to a point: once an RM clears roughly 50 on the benchmark, PPO outcomes saturate near the level of a strong DPO baseline regardless of further benchmark gains.
  3. The dominant factor that actually breaks the RM-benchmark-to-PPO-outcome link is a lineage/distribution mismatch between the reward model and the policy being trained — a top-scoring but off-policy RM can underperform a lower-scoring, on-policy one in real RLHF, so simply taking the top model on the leaderboard is explicitly the wrong takeaway for RLHF use — the paper's own advice is to take the training recipe, not the checkpoint.
