2017 · NeurIPS · Post-training & RL

Deep Reinforcement Learning from Human Preferences

The paper that turned human preference into a training signal: fit a reward model to a non-expert's pairwise comparisons of one-to-two-second behavior clips, then optimize it online with ordinary deep RL. It solves Atari and simulated locomotion without ever seeing the true reward — on feedback covering under 1% of the agent's experience — and instills behaviors no one knows how to hand-code, like a backflip. The direct ancestor of RLHF.

Paul F. Christiano · Jan Leike · Tom B. Brown · Miljan Martic · Shane Legg · Dario Amodei  |  OpenAI & DeepMind

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

By 2017 deep RL had conquered every domain that came with a clean, programmatic reward — Atari scores, board games — by maximizing a number the environment hands you. But almost everything we actually want from an agent has no such number. There is no reward function for "clean the table," "scramble an egg," or "do a backflip and land upright." You can try to hand-write a proxy reward, but the agent will then optimize the proxy while trampling your intent — the misalignment worry the authors cite from Concrete Problems in AI Safety (Amodei et al., 2016).

The usual escape hatches don't reach far enough. Inverse RL and imitation learning need demonstrations, which break down for behaviors a human can't physically demonstrate — say, controlling a robot with a non-human morphology and dozens of degrees of freedom. And you could just let a human grade the agent's behavior directly, except that using human feedback as the reward is hopelessly expensive: modern deep RL burns hundreds to thousands of hours of agent experience, and no human can sit in that loop and score every step.

Preference-based RL already existed (Akrour, Wilson, Knox & Stone's TAMER, MacGlashan), but only on small or linear problems, often with synthetic feedback, or with learning confined to episodes where a trainer was present — none scaled to a state-of-the-art deep RL system. The gap this paper closes is precise: communicate a complex, hard-to-specify goal to a modern deep RL agent using a tiny, affordable amount of feedback from a non-expert. Their target spec is four properties at once — solve tasks we can recognize but not demonstrate, be teachable by non-experts, scale to large problems, and be economical with human time.

// how it works

The method

The central move is to separate the reward from the policy. The system keeps two deep networks: a policy π and a learned reward estimate r̂. They are trained by three processes running asynchronously, in a loop:

  1. The policy acts. π interacts with the environment and is improved by a standard deep RL algorithm to maximize the predicted reward r̂(o, a) — A2C for Atari, TRPO for the MuJoCo robotics tasks.
  2. A human compares. Pairs of short trajectory-segment clips (1–2 seconds of the agent's behavior) are sampled and shown to a human, who picks which clip is better, or marks them equal / incomparable.
  3. The reward model is fit. r̂ is trained by supervised learning to predict the human's comparisons collected so far.

Three design choices carry the paper. First, comparisons, not scores. Asking a human to rank two short clips turns out to be far easier and more consistent than asking for an absolute numerical rating — especially on continuous control — yet a one-bit comparison is nearly as fast to elicit as judging a single state.

Second, the reward model is a preference predictor. Treat r̂ as a latent score and assume the human's probability of preferring a segment grows exponentially in that segment's summed reward — the Bradley–Terry model (a.k.a. Luce–Shepard choice). r̂ is fit by minimizing the cross-entropy between these predicted preferences and the human's labels.

P̂ [ σ1 ≻ σ2 ] = exp Σt r̂(o1t, a1t)exp Σt r̂(o1t, a1t) + exp Σt r̂(o2t, a2t) // Eq. 1 — the probability a human prefers clip σ¹ over σ². Like Elo for chess: the gap in predicted segment-reward sets the odds of being chosen.

Third, feedback is gathered online. Comparisons are collected continuously throughout training, not in one batch up front, so the reward model keeps pace with the policy's shifting state distribution — and, crucially, the policy can't run off and exploit a frozen reward model. (The ablations show this is load-bearing, not a detail.)

Around that core sit the tricks that make it stable: an ensemble of predictors, each trained on a bootstrap resample and normalized before averaging; 1/e of the data held out for validation, with ℓ2 regularization tuned to keep validation loss between 1.1× and 1.5× the training loss (plus dropout in some domains); a baked-in assumption that the human answers uniformly at random 10% of the time, so an extreme reward gap can't blow up the loss; and reward normalized to zero mean and constant standard deviation. Queries are chosen by a cheap active-learning proxy — sample many candidate clip pairs and pick the ones the ensemble most disagrees about — which the authors flag up front as "a crude approximation."

// what they found

The results

The headline number is the efficiency: a small amount of non-expert feedback — covering less than 1% of the agent's interactions with the environment — suffices to learn most of these tasks without ever observing their reward, cutting the interaction-complexity of human oversight by roughly three orders of magnitude.

On the eight MuJoCo robotics tasks, about 700 human comparisons nearly match RL trained directly on the true reward across all eight; training from the learned reward is higher-variance but comparable in mean. More striking: with 1,400 synthetic labels the agent slightly exceeds true-reward RL, apparently because the learned reward is better-shaped. Real human feedback ranged from half as efficient to fully as efficient as a synthetic oracle — and on Ant, telling humans to prefer trajectories where the robot stayed "standing upright" produced reward shaping good enough to beat the synthetic signal.

On the seven Atari games it is harder to match RL, but the method still shows substantial learning on most. With 5,500 human comparisons (and synthetic budgets of 3,300 / 5,600 / 10,000), BeamRider and Pong reach or approach RL with as few as 3,300 synthetic labels; Seaquest and Qbert get near RL but more slowly; SpaceInvaders and Breakout never match it but improve a lot. Real human feedback often needs about 40% more labels than a clean synthetic oracle to reach the same performance — contractor noise and inconsistency cost real sample-efficiency. The clean win is Enduro: human labelers reward any incremental progress toward passing a car, shaping the reward so well that the agent outperforms A3C and rivals DQN — a goal A3C struggles to reach by random exploration. (The clean loss: on Qbert, real human feedback fails to clear level 1, because short Qbert clips are genuinely hard to judge.)

The part that made the paper famous is the novel behaviors — tasks with no reward function at all, where the only evaluation is "does it look right." Using the very same hyperparameters, about an hour of human time each teaches the Hopper a repeated backflip that lands upright (~900 comparisons), the Half-Cheetah to run forward on one leg (~800 comparisons), and an Enduro car to stay alongside other cars (~1,300 comparisons). None of these has a reward you could reasonably hand-engineer.

And it is cheap on both axes: whole experiments used between fifteen minutes and five hours of human time. For the Atari runs the authors put real numbers on it: roughly $25 of compute versus ~$36 of human labor (5 hours at US minimum wage) — meaning the human and the machine already cost about the same, and further sample-efficiency gains are hitting diminishing returns.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

As an idea this is bulletproof and historically vindicated — but the empirical case is thinner than the iconic backflip suggests, and it's worth being precise about where.

Start with the statistics. Every headline real-human-feedback curve is a single run, while the synthetic-oracle and true-reward baselines are averaged over 5 runs (MuJoCo) or 3 (Atari). The authors themselves note learned-reward training is "less stable and higher variance," so an n=1 curve that "nearly matches RL" carries wide, unquantified error bars. The much-quoted result that 1,400 synthetic labels beat the true reward is similarly a comparison against one A2C/TRPO baseline and could sit inside that variance; it's evidence of better-shaped reward, not of a raised capability ceiling.

Then who is labeling. The selling point is non-expert contractors, but several of the cleanest results aren't from naive labelers: Reacher and Cheetah feedback was provided "by an author due to time constraints," and all of the showcase novel behaviors — backflip, one-leg cheetah, Enduro pacing — were trained on author feedback. An author knows both the algorithm and the intended target, an optimistic bias that lands exactly where there is no quantitative metric to keep it honest. For those tasks "evaluation" is qualitative video plus the labeler's own judgment, with no held-out measure of how accurate the learned reward model actually is.

Finally, reward hacking is already on screen. The offline ablation ("no online queries") collapses: a reward predictor trained on a frozen batch captures only part of the true reward, and the policy promptly exploits the gap — on Pong it learns to avoid losing points without scoring them, producing infinite volleys. The method only works by keeping a human in the loop continuously, which quietly caps the "cheap, scarce oversight" promise and foreshadows the reward-over-optimization problem later quantified by Gao et al. (2022). The other heuristics — ensembling, ℓ2, the 10% noise term, disagreement-based querying — are tuned per domain, and the disagreement-based query selection the system is built around "actually impairs performance" on some tasks by the authors' own ablation.

baselines

Strong and honest: head-to-head against true-reward A2C/TRPO and a synthetic oracle at matched label budgets, aiming for parity rather than SOTA. The asymmetry is statistical — human runs are n=1 against n=3–5 baselines.

contamination

Not a pretraining-leakage setting (RL from scratch). The subtler issue: synthetic-feedback runs derive their training signal from the very true reward used to score them; only the human-feedback runs test a signal independent of the eval metric.

what's missed

No error bars on the human runs; no direct measurement of reward-model accuracy; the novel behaviors and two MuJoCo tasks use author (non-naive) labels; reward hacking is demonstrated but never bounded.

would raise confidence

Multiple human runs with reported variance; held-out evaluation of the reward model; a contractor-vs-author ablation; stress-testing the reward model under heavy optimization pressure; one non-game, non-author task.

// bottom line

A landmark proof-of-concept: a complex deep-RL goal can be communicated by a non-expert through a few thousand two-second comparisons, at under 1% feedback and roughly the cost of the compute. The rigor underneath is lighter than the result — single-run human curves, author labels on the showcase behaviors, and an already-visible reward-hacking failure mode — but the recipe (a Bradley–Terry reward model fit to pairwise human preferences, optimized online by policy-gradient RL) is exactly the one that, scaled to language, became RLHF.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. Decouple reward from policy. Fit a separate reward model to human pairwise preferences (Bradley–Terry), then optimize it with ordinary deep RL. That two-network template — preference data → reward model → RL — is the one under InstructGPT, Claude, and the entire rubric-reward line that followed.
  2. Comparisons beat scores, and clips beat frames. Non-experts give consistent one-bit comparisons where they can't give consistent numbers, and a few thousand two-second comparisons (~1 hour of human time, <1% of the agent's experience) are enough to instill behaviors with no writable reward — a backflip from ~900 queries.
  3. Keep the human online or the agent games the reward. Freezing the reward model collapses into reward hacking (Pong's infinite volleys) — the first clear demonstration of the over-optimization problem that RLHF still fights today.
