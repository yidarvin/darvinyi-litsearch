Qwen Technical Report

Alibaba's first open LLM family: 1.8B/7B/14B base models trained on up to 3 trillion tokens with a purpose-built 152K-vocabulary tokenizer, then aligned into Qwen-Chat via SFT and PPO-based RLHF — plus Code-Qwen and Math-Qwen-Chat specialists sharing the same base, and an early, explicit push into tool-use and agent behavior.

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

By September 2023 the open-weight LLM landscape — LLaMA, LLaMA 2, Falcon, MPT — was built almost entirely around English-centric tokenizers and pretraining mixes, and the alignment side of the story (SFT plus RLHF) was mostly a black box: OpenAI, Anthropic, and Google described their pipelines only in broad strokes, and truly open, reproducible RLHF recipes were rare. Meanwhile a widening gap had opened between what proprietary chat models could do — reliably call tools, drive a Python code interpreter, plan multi-step agent tasks (ReAct, AutoGPT-style loops) — and what any openly released model could do, since agentic behavior wasn't something the open pretraining corpora or SFT sets were built to elicit. On top of that, non-English (especially Chinese) capability in the open-model tier lagged badly: LLaMA's BPE tokenizer is wasteful on Chinese text, inflating both training and inference cost, and benchmarks like C-Eval and CMMLU showed a wide gap versus GPT-3.5/GPT-4.

Qwen's stated goal is to close all three gaps at once inside a single, openly released family: a bilingual (Chinese/English) base model series (1.8B, 7B, 14B) trained on a genuinely large, filtered corpus; a documented full pretrain → SFT → reward-model → PPO pipeline; and chat models explicitly trained and benchmarked on tool use, code-interpreter workflows, and Hugging Face agent tasks — while remaining competitive against much larger open baselines like LLaMA 2-70B.

// how it works

The method

Pretraining data and decontamination. The pretraining corpus mixes public web documents, encyclopedia, books, and code, deduplicated with exact-match (after normalization) and fuzzy (MinHash + LSH) methods, and quality-filtered with a combination of rule-based heuristics and learned scorers (language-model perplexity, text-quality classifiers, offensive-content detectors), plus manual sampling review. A slice of high-quality instruction data is folded directly into pretraining — following the multi-task-pretraining literature (T5, ExT5) — but only after the team removed any instruction sample with a 13-gram overlap against their evaluation test sets, the same decontamination strategy GPT-3 (Brown et al., 2020) used. The final corpus reaches up to 3 trillion tokens.

Tokenizer. Qwen builds on tiktoken's cl100k_base BPE vocabulary (the GPT-3.5/GPT-4 tokenizer) and extends it with common Chinese characters/words and vocabulary from other languages, plus LLaMA-style single-digit number splitting, landing at a final vocabulary of roughly 152K tokens. Figure 3 shows the payoff: measured compression ratio (tokens needed per unit of text, English=lower-is-more-efficient) against LLaMA-7B, Baichuan-7B, ChatGLM2-6B, and InternLM-7B across 19 languages plus code — Qwen matches or beats every competitor almost everywhere it's tested, most dramatically on Thai, Hebrew, Arabic, Korean, and Vietnamese, where LLaMA's tokenizer needs 2-3.7× more tokens for the same text.

Architecture. Qwen is a modified LLaMA-style decoder-only Transformer (Table 1: hidden sizes 2048/4096/5120 and 24/32/40 layers for the 1.8B/7B/14B models, all trained at a 4M-token batch size). The specific deviations from vanilla LLaMA: untied input-embedding and output-projection weights (traded for extra memory to gain accuracy); RoPE positional embeddings computed with the inverse-frequency matrix kept in FP32 rather than BF16/FP16 for numerical precision; biases removed everywhere except the attention QKV projection, where a bias term is kept specifically to help length extrapolation; RMSNorm in place of LayerNorm under a pre-norm scheme; and SwiGLU activations, which — because the gated unit needs an extra projection matrix — shrink the FFN's hidden dimension to 8/3 of the model width instead of the usual 4× so total FLOPs stay matched (see the box below).

dffn = 83 · dmodel // SwiGLU's extra gate projection means the FFN is sized at 8/3× the hidden dim, not the classic 4×, to hold parameter/FLOP count roughly constant

Training and context extension. Models train at a 2048-token context with Flash Attention, AdamW (β₁=0.9, β₂=0.95, ε=1e-8), a cosine schedule decaying to 10% of peak LR, and BFloat16 mixed precision. To reach past that native 2048 window at inference time without any extra fine-tuning, Qwen stacks three training-free tricks: dynamic NTK-aware interpolation (rescaling RoPE's base per-chunk rather than uniformly, unlike position interpolation), LogN-Scaling (rescaling attention logits by a factor tied to the ratio of inference length to training length, to keep attention entropy stable), and layer-wise windowed attention (shorter windows on lower layers, longer on higher ones, since the team observed lower layers are more sensitive to long-range noise). Table 3 shows the effect directly: at 16,384 tokens, Qwen-7B's perplexity falls from 2645.09 (no intervention) to 4.62 with dynamic-NTK+LogN, and to 4.32 with all three combined — versus 3.78 at the native 2048-token length, i.e. the techniques hold perplexity nearly flat across an 8× context stretch.

Alignment: SFT. Supervised finetuning uses human-style, multi-turn conversation data (not template-constrained instruction/answer pairs) formatted in ChatML, with loss masked on system/user turns. Safety-relevant data (violence, bias, explicit content) is explicitly annotated and included. Training runs 4000 steps at batch size 128, sequence length 2048, peak LR 2e-6 (linear warmup over the first 1430 steps), weight decay 0.1, dropout 0.1, gradient clipping 1.0.

Alignment: reward model and RLHF. The reward model is itself initialized from a same-sized Qwen checkpoint (with a pooling head added) and goes through the same two-stage recipe as an LLM — large-scale "preference model pretraining" (PMP) on broad comparison data, then finetuning on higher-quality, human-annotated comparison pairs drawn from a ~6,600-tag prompt taxonomy sampled for diversity and complexity. Table 4 reports pairwise test accuracy for PMP vs. the finetuned RM across six preference datasets (Qwen Helpful-base/online, Anthropic Helpful-base/online, OpenAI Summarize, Stanford SHP, OpenAI PRM800K); the finetuned RM improves most on Qwen's own in-domain sets (74.78% vs. 62.68% on Helpful-base) while PMP alone generalizes comparably well out-of-distribution. PPO then runs with four networks — policy, value, frozen reference, and reward — freezing the policy for the first 50 steps to let the value model stabilize, sampling two responses per prompt, KL coefficient 0.04, policy/value learning rates 1e-6/5e-6, value-loss clipping at 0.15, and inference top-p 0.9. A "pretrained gradient" (mixing plain pretraining loss into the PPO update, weighted so pretraining data volume dominates PPO data volume) is used explicitly to counteract alignment tax on non-code/math benchmarks.

// what they found

The results

Base models vs. open competitors (Table 2, few-shot). Against LLaMA, LLaMA 2, MPT, Falcon, Baichuan2, ChatGLM2, InternLM, and StableBeluga2, Qwen-14B posts MMLU 66.3, C-Eval 72.1, GSM8K 61.3, MATH 24.8, HumanEval 32.3, MBPP 40.8, and BBH 53.4 (5-shot MMLU/C-Eval, 8-shot GSM8K, 4-shot MATH, 0-shot HumanEval, 3-shot MBPP/BBH). Despite being 5× smaller, it beats LLaMA 2-70B (MMLU 69.8, C-Eval 50.1, GSM8K 63.3, MATH 13.5, HumanEval 29.9, MBPP 45.0, BBH 64.9) on exactly the 3 tasks the paper claims: C-Eval (72.1 vs. 50.1), MATH (24.8 vs. 13.5), and HumanEval (32.3 vs. 29.9) — it still trails LLaMA 2-70B on MMLU, GSM8K, MBPP, and BBH. Qwen-7B (58.2 MMLU / 63.5 C-Eval / 51.7 GSM8K) surpasses LLaMA 2-13B across the board and lands close to Baichuan2-13B; even Qwen-1.8B is competitive with 7B-class open models on several tasks.

Aligned chat models vs. GPT-3.5/GPT-4 (Table 5, 0-shot unless noted). Qwen-Chat-14B reaches MMLU 66.5 (5-shot), C-Eval 71.7 (5-shot), GSM8K 60.1 (0-shot) / 59.3 (8-shot), HumanEval 43.9, and BBH 58.7 (3-shot) — ahead of every other open-source chat model tested (ChatGLM2-6B, InternLM-Chat-7B, Baichuan2-Chat-7B/13B, LLaMA 2-Chat-7B/13B/70B) on all five benchmarks, including beating LLaMA 2-Chat-70B's 63.8 MMLU, 44.3 C-Eval, and 32.3 HumanEval at a fifth the parameter count. It still trails GPT-3.5 (69.1 MMLU / 78.2 GSM8K / 73.2 HumanEval) and especially GPT-4 (83.0 / 91.4 / 86.6).

Tool use and agents. On an in-house Chinese ReAct tool-selection benchmark (Table 6), Qwen-Chat-14B hits 98% tool-selection accuracy with only a 2.4% false-positive rate — actually ahead of GPT-4's 95%/15.0% and far ahead of GPT-3.5's 85%/75.0%. On a code-interpreter benchmark spanning math, data visualization, and general tasks (Tables 7-8), Qwen-Chat-14B's generated code is executable 89.2% (math) / 84.1% (visualization) / 65.5% (general) of the time, and the final answer is correct 58.4% (math) / 56.4% (visualization-all) of the time — below GPT-4 (82.8%/63.8% correctness) but clearly ahead of GPT-3.5, LLaMA 2-Chat, InternLM-Chat, and even the code-specialist Code Llama-Instruct-13B, which the paper notes tends to hallucinate nonexistent CSV columns on harder visualization tasks (illustrated by the Figure 5 side-by-side transcript). On the Hugging Face Agent benchmark (Table 9), Qwen-Chat-14B scores 93.5/94.4/87.0 (tool-selection/tool-used/code-correctness) in Run mode — ahead of the open-source Starcoder-Base/Starcoder baselines (86-87/87-88/68.9) and matching GPT-3.5 on code-correctness (87.0), though still a couple of points behind GPT-3.5 on tool-selection/tool-used (95.4/96.3) and clearly behind GPT-4 (100/100/97.4).

Specialist models. Code-Qwen-14B and Math-Qwen-14B-Chat, fine-tuned from the same base, "demonstrate significantly improved performance in comparison with open-source models" on HumanEval/MBPP and GSM8K/MATH respectively, while "slightly fall[ing] behind proprietary models" like GPT-3.5/GPT-4 — the paper's own characterization of the specialist line.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

Qwen's headline numbers are more trustworthy than most contemporaneous tech reports for one simple reason: the 1.8B/7B/14B weights were actually released, so anyone could (and later did) re-run MMLU, C-Eval, GSM8K, etc. independently — a real check the 3T-token pretraining corpus itself never gets, since its exact source mixture and per-domain proportions are never disclosed. The report is also unusually candid in places: it explicitly states its own decontamination pass (13-gram overlap removal against eval test sets, "following Brown et al. (2020)") was applied only to "the reported tasks," not swept across the entire corpus, "since it is not feasible to repeat this filtering process for all tasks" — a direct admission that unreported downstream evaluations of Qwen carry no such guarantee. The base-model table (Table 2) leans on "official results and OpenCompass" best scores for every baseline rather than a single shared harness, which is standard practice for the era but still means each baseline's number was picked (by someone else) as its best, not run fresh by Qwen under identical prompting/shot conventions.

The tool-use and agent numbers are the part that deserves the most skepticism. The ReAct tool-selection benchmark (Table 6) and the code-interpreter benchmark (Tables 7-8) are both Qwen-authored, Qwen-released evaluation sets — the same team designing the test, training the model, and grading the result. Worse, the data-visualization correctness metric in Table 8 is graded by Qwen-VL, the Qwen team's own multimodal model, checking whether a chart image satisfies the prompt — a same-family model judging same-family output, with no human or third-party verification reported for that specific metric. The human evaluation of RLHF quality (Section 3.3) is a genuinely nice touch, but it covers only 300 Chinese-language instructions rated by three in-house annotators, with no inter-annotator agreement statistic reported and no equivalent English-language human eval at all.

baselines

Base-model comparisons (LLaMA/LLaMA 2/Falcon/MPT/Baichuan2/InternLM/ChatGLM2/StableBeluga2) pull each competitor's own best-reported or OpenCompass score rather than re-running every model under one harness — a reasonable choice for 2023, but it means shot-count and prompt-format conventions weren't necessarily held constant across rows.

contamination

Decontamination (13-gram overlap vs. eval sets, GPT-3-style) is explicitly scoped to only "the reported tasks" per the paper's own text — not applied exhaustively across the full 3-trillion-token corpus — and the corpus's exact source composition is never disclosed for outside audit.

what's missed

The agent/tool-use benchmarks (ReAct tool selection, code-interpreter execution, Hugging Face agent tasks) are Qwen-designed and Qwen-released, with one correctness metric (data-visualization) graded by Qwen's own Qwen-VL model rather than a human or neutral judge. Human preference evaluation is limited to 300 Chinese instructions with no reported inter-rater agreement and no English-language counterpart.

would raise confidence

A third-party re-run of the released weights on an independent harness (which did eventually happen via the Open LLM Leaderboard and Chatbot Arena), a public accounting of the pretraining corpus's source mixture, and an agent/tool-use eval graded by a non-affiliated model or human raters rather than Qwen-VL.

// bottom line

The base and chat benchmark numbers hold up well in hindsight — the weights were public, so independent leaderboards later corroborated that Qwen-7B/14B were genuinely strong for their size. But the tool-use, code-interpreter, and agent results are effectively a vendor demo: Qwen-authored benchmarks, evaluated in part by a Qwen-authored judge model, with no outside replication reported in the paper itself. Treat the "beats GPT-4 on tool selection" headline (Table 6) as a claim to verify independently, not a settled result.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. Qwen-14B beats the 5×-larger LLaMA 2-70B on C-Eval, MATH, and HumanEval, and Qwen-Chat-14B beats LLaMA 2-Chat-70B on MMLU, C-Eval, GSM8K, and HumanEval — evidence that a bigger, cleaner, bilingual-tuned tokenizer and corpus can substitute for a large share of raw parameter count.
  2. The architecture recipe here — untied embeddings, FP32 RoPE inverse frequencies, QKV-only bias, pre-norm RMSNorm, SwiGLU with an 8/3-width FFN, plus training-free dynamic-NTK + LogN-Scaling + layer-wise windowed attention for context extension — reads like a checklist that later open LLM reports (including Qwen's own 2/2.5/3 line already in this graph) reused nearly verbatim.
  3. The tool-use and code-interpreter results (beating GPT-4 on the in-house ReAct benchmark) are the most attention-grabbing numbers in the report and also the least independently verified: same-team benchmark, same-team model, and in one metric a same-team judge (Qwen-VL) — a pattern worth watching for in every "our model uses tools better than GPT-4" claim that follows it.
