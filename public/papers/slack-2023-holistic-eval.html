A Holistic Approach for Test and Evaluation of Large Language Models

Scale AI's 2023 blueprint for testing LLMs: use an entropy-based confidence signal to let GPT-4 auto-grade the easy preference judgments, and layer automated, generalist, and expert red teamers to surface more — and more severe — safety vulnerabilities.

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

By late 2023 LLMs were being dropped into wildly different jobs — summarization, code, customer chat, open-domain Q&A — and the field had no shared way to test them that was at once broad, scalable, and cheap. The two obvious options each fail on one axis. Fixed deterministic benchmarks are cheap and reproducible, but they are confined to narrow tasks and say little about open-ended generation quality or safety. Human evaluation is the gold standard for subjective, nuanced judgments, but it is slow and expensive, and it does not scale to the volume of judgments a real deployment needs.

The paper frames this as a dual challenge: you have to assess both competence (is the answer good?) and safety (can the model be made to cause harm?), across a long tail of tasks, repeatedly, as models change. Worse, the seemingly easy fix — "just use a strong LLM like GPT-4 as the judge" — is known to be brittle: LLM graders suffer from positional bias (preferring whichever response comes first), self-bias (preferring their own outputs), and plain sampling noise. Naively trusting an LLM judge therefore launders these biases into your headline numbers.

Scale's answer is not a single benchmark but a methodology: a framework that decides, input by input, where automation is trustworthy and where a human (or a domain expert) actually adds value — and only spends the expensive resource there. The stated goal is explicitly standards-setting: to "contribute to the development of common standards and approaches around test and evaluation of LLMs."

// how it works

The method

The report defines two parallel frameworks (Figure 1). The capabilities framework combines deterministic benchmarks, automated LLM evaluation of open-ended prompts, human evaluation of static inputs, and dynamic human interaction with the live model, then synthesizes the results. The safety framework borrows the structure of a cybersecurity penetration test — adapting the Penetration Testing Execution Standard (PTES) — into a pipeline: pre-engagement → threat modeling → vulnerability analysis & exploitation → reporting, where the exploitation stage itself is layered automated → generalist → expert.

A taxonomy first

Both halves are anchored in hand-built taxonomies that guide what inputs to construct. Capabilities are split into ten top-level categories — Classification, Information Retrieval, Rewrite, Generation, Open QA, Reasoning, Conversation, Illogical, Coding, and Math — each with subcategories (math and coding are deliberately separated because they have their own "language" and their own evaluation criteria). Safety is split two ways that the authors are careful not to conflate: a risk taxonomy (five areas — harmful information, harms against groups, distribution of sensitive content, enabling malicious actors, and LLM misalignment — organized by user intent × party harmed) and a vulnerability taxonomy (unreliability, susceptibility to adversarial prompts, misaligned agency).

Capabilities: an entropy confidence gate

The core capabilities contribution is a way to know when to trust the LLM judge. In the most common setting — pairwise comparison, "which of these two responses is better?" — the method estimates the grader's confidence with a Monte Carlo procedure: fix the grader's temperature to 1.0 and sample its verdict many times, presenting both response orderings equally often so positional bias averages out. From the vote distribution it computes the entropy of the judgment: let R1i and R2i be the fraction of samples voting for response 1 and 2 on pair i, and N the fraction voting for neither (GPT-4 sometimes declines to pick).

Wi = − Σ r ∈ {R1i, R2i, N}  r · log r // entropy of the Monte-Carlo vote = the grader's uncertainty on pair i. Low W ⇒ confident ⇒ trust the auto-label.

The final auto-prediction is the majority vote across samples (ties broken randomly). Low-entropy pairs are confidently judged and kept; any pair whose entropy exceeds a threshold C is routed to a human annotator. The threshold is the dial that trades automation against quality, and the experiments tune it empirically.

Safety: a layered red team

For safety, automated probes (rule-based scanners such as garak, optionally fine-tuned adversarial models and learned trigger strings) cast a wide, cheap net. Generalist red teamers — trained adversarial-prompting specialists who are not domain experts — then hunt for novel exploits and decide which findings need escalation. Expert red teamers (biology, medicine, cyber, nuclear, …) are the scarce resource, so they are spent only on the highest-value work: validating and scoring escalated attacks, deepening successful ones, and generating domain-specific novel attacks. Crucially, experts can be "seeded" — handed successful attacks from the automated probes and generalists as inspiration — which is itself one of the hypotheses the experiments test.

// what they found

The results

Capabilities — entropy is a usable trust signal

On a dataset of 500 prompts (Llama V2 70b Chat vs. Falcon 7b responses, paired with a GPT-4 response and a human-expert response) and a separate 900-prompt set (GPT-4 responses vs. human "rewrites" of those responses), with ground truth from the consensus of three independent annotators, the entropy gate behaves as hoped. Computing entropy from 10 grader samples (each response placed first 5 times), GPT-4 is roughly 78% accurate on the ~300 lowest-entropy points in the GPT-4-vs-rewrite setting, and accuracy falls to ~65% as the threshold is relaxed — a 13-point absolute drop that confirms low entropy ≈ higher accuracy. The authors recommend a cutoff of C ≈ 0.3. Headline framing: the hybrid approach scores human preference at about 86% accuracy while offloading up to ~20% of inputs to the model.

Translated into cost, the gate is attractive (Figure 4). Auto-labeling everything below C and sending the rest to humans, the GPT-4-vs-Human-Rewrites comparison reaches ~90% human agreement at ~65% of the total annotation time; the easier Llama V2-vs-Falcon comparison reaches close to 100% agreement at just ~10% of the data annotated.

But the method also exposes its own failure mode: self-bias. Splitting the GPT-4 grader's agreement by which response ground-truth humans actually preferred (the paper's Table 2, reproduced below) shows GPT-4 agreeing only 53.8% of the time when humans favored the GPT-4 response, versus 76.1% when humans favored the human rewrite — i.e. GPT-4 over-rates its own writing. Even so, it beats the Claude 2.0 grader on both slices.

A qualitative error pass found two recurring ways the confident grader goes wrong: it favored a model that refused a prompt over one that answered (when humans preferred the answer), and it confidently mis-scored factual prompts about events after its training cutoff (e.g. "top 5 fast food restaurants as of 2022", "production budget for the Super Mario movie").

Safety — layers beat any single source

The red-teaming study scoped to malware and ran on GPT-4, with 14 generalist and 27 expert red teamers (experts split into seeded and unseeded groups), plus garak's malware probe (240 prompts × 10 samples). Human success was self-reported on a 1–5 Likert scale, "success" = 3 or higher. Generalists scored the highest success rate (~80%); garak's automated probe was lowest (≈41%) despite a generous "contains code" success criterion. Seeding helped experts: within the seeded project, experts scored 52% on unseeded tasks versus 46% in the all-unseeded project — exposure to prior attacks raised their hit rate even on fresh tasks. And although experts had lower success rates, their successful attacks were rated more harmful on average than generalists' (Figure 5, right).

Did seeding hurt diversity? Apparently not: seeded conversations diverged from the originals they were shown (average cosine similarity 0.52), and embedding all conversations (Figure 6) shows the automated attacks clustering tightly while human — and especially seeded-expert — attacks spread across the space, covering clusters the automated probes rarely reach. Tellingly, 59% of successful generalist attacks were flagged as needing domain expertise to even evaluate — the strongest argument for keeping experts in the loop. Success rate also generally rose with conversation turn (≈0.21 at turn 1 up to ≈0.44 at turn 4), underscoring that multi-turn probing finds failures single prompts miss.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

Read as engineering, this is a sensible, honest playbook — and the authors are admirably candid about its limits ("we cannot fully automate LLM evaluation"). Read as a set of measurements, almost every headline number is an operating point on one 2023 dataset, with one model family, judged by labels the participants reported about themselves. The numbers are best treated as existence proofs that the workflow works, not as transferable accuracy figures. They do not even line up internally: the abstract's ~86% agreement sits above the detailed threshold curve (≈78% at low entropy, falling to ≈65%), and the report never reconciles the two — the headline likely blends in the easy Llama-vs-Falcon comparison where the grader is near-perfect.

The sharpest tension is structural: the capabilities study lets GPT-4 grade a pool that GPT-4 half-wrote, and the paper's own self-bias breakdown (its Table 2) quantifies the contamination — 53.8% agreement when its own output was the right pick versus 76.1% otherwise. That is the headline confound, not a footnote, and the "rewrite" design (humans editing GPT-4's draft) makes the two candidates textually similar, which can inflate raw "agreement" through shared content while masking the self-preference. Meanwhile the entropy gate's promise — auto-label the confident cases — collides with the finding that GPT-4 is confidently wrong on exactly the safety-adjacent cases (refusal-vs-answer, post-cutoff facts) you would most want a human to see. A low-entropy verdict on "is the refusal better than the answer?" is precisely where the gate misfires.

On safety, "success" and "harm severity" are self-reported on a 1–5 scale by two populations that differ sharply (degree, age, gender), so source effects and rater-calibration effects are entangled — and the paper's own statistic that 59% of generalist "successes" needed an expert to adjudicate undercuts taking the generalists' 80% at face value. There is also no human–human agreement ceiling or inter-annotator-reliability figure, so "86% agreement" floats without a reference point, and tie handling is doing a lot of silent work (250 of 250 GPT-4 ties resolve at 8% agreement). The red-team population is also tiny and uneven — 14 generalists vs. 27 experts, with only ~50% answering the demographic survey — and the paper itself cautions the data "may be skewed due to the small sample sizes," which leaves the seeding effect (52% vs. 46%) and the harm-severity gap badly underpowered. And it is all a single model (GPT-4), a single threat domain (malware, where "contains code" is a convenient success proxy), one snapshot of grader models (GPT-4, Claude 2.0), and a non-arXiv report with proprietary prompts — none of it independently reproducible.

baselines

Automated baseline is just garak's rule-based malware probe; graders are circa-2023 GPT-4 and Claude 2.0 on one snapshot. No human–human agreement ceiling and no inter-annotator reliability, so the agreement metrics lack a reference point.

contamination

The GPT-4 grader judges a pool it co-authored — self-bias is measured at 53.8% vs 76.1% agreement split by who ground-truth favored. Factual prompts after GPT-4's training cutoff are graded confidently wrong.

what's missed

Confidence ≠ correctness on the cases that matter: the entropy gate would auto-label refusal-vs-answer and safety-adjacent pairs that GPT-4 gets confidently wrong. Red-team success/harm are self-reported by demographically different pools; 59% of generalist "successes" needed an expert to even evaluate.

would raise confidence

Report IRR and a human–human ceiling; grade with a model that generated none of the responses; independently adjudicate red-team success and harm; test more than one model and one threat domain; release the prompts.

// bottom line

A pragmatic operations manual rather than a benchmark. Its durable contributions are two reusable primitives — an entropy-offloading knob for LLM-as-judge and an automated→generalist→expert escalation for red teaming — both of which have since become standard moves in LLM-as-a-judge evaluation and structured red teaming. Trust the design; discount the specific percentages.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. An LLM judge's confidence — estimated as the entropy of repeated, order-swapped Monte-Carlo votes — is a usable triage signal: auto-label the low-entropy ~20% at ~86% human agreement and route the rest to people. The recommended cut is C ≈ 0.3.
  2. Never let a model grade its own outputs. GPT-4 over-rated its own writing (53.8% vs 76.1% agreement split by who ground-truth favored), a self-bias that silently inflates any eval where the judge is also a contestant.
  3. Layered red teaming beats any single source. Automated probes are cheap but low-yield and tightly clustered; generalists land the most successes (~80%); experts find the most severe harms; seeding experts with prior attacks raises both their success (52% vs 46%) and coverage — and multi-turn conversations surface failures single prompts miss.
