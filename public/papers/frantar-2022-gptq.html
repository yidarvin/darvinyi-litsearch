GPTQ: Accurate Post-Training Quantization for Generative Pre-trained Transformers

A one-shot, layer-wise weight quantization method built on approximate second-order (Hessian) information compresses GPT-scale models — up to 175 billion parameters — to 3 or 4 bits per weight in about four GPU-hours, losing just 0.03 WikiText2 perplexity points on OPT-175B relative to full FP16.

Elias Frantar, Saleh Ashkboos, Torsten Hoefler, Dan Alistarh

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

By late 2022, the best generative language models — GPT-175B-class OPT and BLOOM — had grown too large to serve on ordinary hardware: GPT3-175B's parameters alone occupy 326GB in float16, "exceed[ing] the capacity of even the highest-end single GPUs," which forces multi-GPU deployments just for inference. Model compression is the obvious lever, but the accurate post-training quantization (PTQ) methods that existed — AdaRound, BRECQ, AdaQuant, and Optimal Brain Quantization (OBQ, the same authors' own prior method) — simply don't scale: OBQ's per-weight update has cubic cost in the layer's column dimension, so it takes roughly an hour to quantize a 25-million-parameter ResNet-50 on a single GPU, an eternity when the target has 175 billion parameters. The PTQ methods that had been applied at GPT-175B scale — ZeroQuant, LLM.int8(), nuQmm — all fall back to simple round-to-nearest (RTN) weight rounding, which holds up for a modest 8-bit target but, in the paper's words, "fail[s] to preserve accuracy at higher rates." That left an open question the paper poses directly: is one-shot post-training quantization to 3 or 4 bits per weight even feasible at hundred-billion-parameter scale, in a practical amount of compute — hours, not weeks?

// how it works

The method

GPTQ takes the authors' own Optimal Brain Quantization (OBQ) algorithm — itself a generalization of the classic Optimal Brain Surgeon second-order pruning framework to quantization — and applies three modifications that turn a cubic-time, single-layer-at-a-time procedure into one that finishes an entire 175-billion-parameter model in a few GPU-hours. Like OBQ, GPTQ quantizes one Transformer layer ℓ at a time, solving a per-layer reconstruction problem: find quantized weights Ŵ that minimize the squared error ||WX − ŴX||² against the layer's actual inputs X, where X comes from just 128 random 2048-token segments of the C4 corpus — so the whole procedure never sees any task-specific or labeled data and stays genuinely "zero-shot." OBQ solves this by quantizing one weight at a time, in greedy order of least additional error, always adjusting the remaining full-precision weights in that row to compensate, using an inverse-Hessian matrix HF−1 computed over the layer's inputs.

wq = argminwq (quant(wq) − wq)²[HF−1]qq // OBQ's per-weight rounding objective, solved greedily row-by-row — GPTQ's changes make this scale to hundred-billion-parameter layers

The three modifications: (1) Arbitrary Order Insight — GPTQ finds that quantizing every row of a layer's weight matrix in the same, fixed column order gives final error nearly identical to OBQ's per-row-optimal greedy order, especially on large layers. Because HF depends only on the layer's shared inputs and not on which weights are already quantized, fixing the order means the same HF−1 update serves every row, cutting the per-layer cost from O(drow·dcol³) to O(max{drow·dcol², dcol³}) — several orders of magnitude at LLM scale. (2) Lazy Batch Updates — a naive implementation is still memory-bandwidth-bound, since each Hessian update touches a huge matrix for only a handful of FLOPs per entry; GPTQ instead processes B=128 columns at a time, keeping updates local to that block and only propagating the full H−1/W update once the block is done, which the paper reports as "an order of magnitude speedup for very large models in practice." (3) Cholesky Reformulation — at multi-billion-parameter scale, the repeated matrix inversions needed to remove rows/columns from H−1 accumulate numerical error and can make it indefinite, silently corrupting an entire layer; GPTQ instead precomputes everything it needs from H−1 upfront via a numerically-stable Cholesky decomposition (plus mild 1%-of-diagonal dampening), which the paper found necessary to run reliably on the largest models.

The resulting algorithm is compatible with any quantization grid choice, and the paper shows it composes well with grouping — applying independent scale/zero-point parameters to blocks of g consecutive weights — which extends accurate quantization down to 2-bit and even ternary (−1, 0, +1) weights for the largest models.

// what they found

The results

Speed. On a single NVIDIA A100, GPTQ quantizes OPT-13B in 20.9 minutes, OPT-30B in 44.9 minutes, OPT-66B in 1.6 hours, and the full 175-billion-parameter OPT-175B in 4.2 hours; BLOOM-176B takes 3.8 hours. For comparison, the straight-through ZeroQuant-LKD method needs roughly 3 hours just for a 1.3-billion-parameter model — a runtime that would linearly extrapolate to "several hundred hours (a few weeks)" at 175B scale.

Accuracy. On WikiText2, 4-bit GPTQ loses only 0.03 perplexity points on OPT-175B (8.37 vs. the FP16 baseline's 8.34), while 4-bit RTN drops 2.2 points to 10.54 — worse than the ten-times-smaller full-precision OPT-13B (10.13). At 3-bit, RTN collapses outright (7.3×10³ on OPT-175B, 571 on BLOOM-176B) while GPTQ still holds reasonable perplexity: 8.68 on OPT-175B and 8.64 on BLOOM-176B, a loss of only 0.3–0.6 points for more than 5× compression. Finer-granularity grouping recovers still more: group-size-1024 improves perplexity by about 0.2 on average and group-size-128 by another 0.1, landing within 0.1–0.3 of the uncompressed model; pushing to group-size-8 enables ternary weights at 9.20 WikiText2 perplexity on OPT-175B, under a 1-point drop from 16-bit.

Deployment payoff. Quantized to 3-bit, OPT-175B needs about 63GB of memory (embeddings and the output layer stay FP16) plus roughly 9GB for the KV-cache at 2048 tokens — small enough to fit on a single 80GB A100, versus 5×80GB GPUs for FP16 and 3 such GPUs for 8-bit LLM.int8(). Custom kernels that dequantize weights on the fly during matrix–vector multiplication turn that memory reduction into real latency wins: average per-token generation latency (batch size 1, sequence length 128) drops from 230ms to 71ms on a single A100 (3.24×, replacing 5 GPUs with 1) and from 589ms to 130ms on two A6000 GPUs versus eight for FP16 (4.53×) — achieved purely by moving less data per token, since the underlying matrix multiplications still execute in FP16.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

The paper's accuracy claims lean almost entirely on perplexity — WikiText2, Penn Treebank, and C4 — and it says so explicitly, choosing these "as they are known to be particularly sensitive to model quantization." That's a reasonable choice for measuring degradation in raw next-token prediction, but perplexity on generic web/news text doesn't directly tell you whether a compressed 175B model still reasons well, follows instructions, or holds up on the tasks people actually deploy such models for. The paper does supplement this with zero-shot accuracy on LAMBADA, ARC (Easy/Challenge), and PIQA (Table 5, Figure 3, Appendix A.4), and those numbers corroborate rather than complicate the perplexity story: at 4-bit even RTN performs reasonably, and at 3-bit RTN collapses (LAMBADA accuracy falls to 0.0% on OPT-175B and 0.17% on BLOOM-176B) while GPTQ holds at 76.19% and 65.10% respectively — GPTQ's 4-bit LAMBADA accuracy (76.80%) even edges above the FP16 baseline (75.59%), a gap small enough to read as calibration noise rather than a real improvement from quantization. Still, perplexity plus four next-word/cloze-style zero-shot tasks is a narrow slice of what "175-billion-parameter model quality" means: there is no open-ended generation, instruction-following, multi-step reasoning, or code evaluation anywhere in the paper, and nothing resembling the harder benchmarks (MMLU, GSM8K) that arrived shortly after and became standard for judging LLM quality. Notably, the paper's own Ethics Statement concedes the gap directly: it says the study "focused on 'leading accuracy' metrics that are standard in the literature, such as perplexity," and calls for "a thorough study of the impact of compression upon secondary measures, and in particular bias effects" as future work — an unusually candid admission that the evaluation, while internally consistent, doesn't cover what compression does beyond the accuracy axis. It's also worth noting every perplexity and zero-shot number in Tables 3–5 is a single point estimate from one calibration draw (128 random C4 segments) and one run per configuration; no variance across seeds or calibration samples is reported anywhere, so "0.03-point loss" on OPT-175B is a single measurement, not a characterized distribution.

baselines

Round-to-nearest (RTN) on the identical asymmetric per-row grid is real and current — the paper notes it "corresponds precisely to the state-of-the-art weight quantization of LLM.int8()," the method actually deployed at this scale at the time. But the more accurate small-model PTQ methods (AdaRound, BRECQ, the authors' own OBQ) are explicitly acknowledged as "too slow" to run at billion-parameter scale, so they only appear on ResNet-18/50 and small language models (BERT-base, OPT-125M) — never validated head-to-head against GPTQ at the 175B scale the headline claims are actually about.

contamination

Not the usual sense of the term — GPTQ calibrates on 128 unlabeled C4 excerpts and touches no labeled task data, so there's no finetuning-style train/test leakage to check. But the paper never audits whether WikiText2/PTB/C4 or the zero-shot eval sets (LAMBADA/ARC/PIQA) overlap with OPT's or BLOOM's own undisclosed pretraining corpora — a property it inherits from those base models rather than introduces itself.

what's missed

No downstream generation-quality evaluation (chat, code, long-form reasoning), no variance/error-bar reporting across calibration draws or seeds, and no activation quantization at all — every reported speedup comes from a kernel that only dequantizes weights on the fly, so none of the accuracy numbers reflect what a fully int-weight-and-activation deployment would look like.

would raise confidence

Repeated calibration draws with reported variance at the 175B scale; a broader zero-shot suite including the reasoning-heavy benchmarks (MMLU, GSM8K) that postdate this paper; the bias/secondary-measures study the authors' own Ethics Statement calls for but doesn't run; and a joint weight+activation quantized variant, since production serving typically wants both.

// bottom line

GPTQ's headline claim — 3–4 bit weight-only quantization of 175B-parameter models with negligible accuracy loss — is well-supported on its own terms: the perplexity story (0.03pt loss at 4-bit, 0.3–0.6pt at 3-bit on OPT-175B/BLOOM-176B) is corroborated by four zero-shot benchmarks showing the identical RTN-collapses/GPTQ-holds pattern, and unlike many contemporaneous compression papers it backs this with a measured, real end-to-end latency win (3.24×–4.53×) rather than just a theoretical memory number. The gap is scope, not internal rigor: perplexity plus LAMBADA/ARC/PIQA is a real but narrow window into "quality," there's no variance reporting across calibration draws, and — as the paper's own Ethics Statement flags — no study yet of what quantization does to bias or other properties beyond accuracy. Read "negligible accuracy degradation" as well-demonstrated for language-modeling perplexity and simple zero-shot cloze/QA tasks specifically, not yet as a guarantee across every downstream use a 175B model gets put to.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. GPTQ modifies the authors' own Optimal Brain Quantization (OBQ) method with three changes — a fixed (arbitrary) column visitation order, lazy batch updates in blocks of 128 columns, and a numerically-stable Cholesky reformulation — cutting per-layer cost from OBQ's cubic O(drow·dcol³) down to quantizing OPT-175B or BLOOM-176B in under 4.5 hours on a single A100 GPU (versus roughly 1 hour for OBQ on a 25-million-parameter ResNet-50).
  2. At 4-bit, GPTQ loses just 0.03 WikiText2 perplexity points on OPT-175B (8.37 vs. FP16's 8.34); at 3-bit it holds 8.68 while round-to-nearest quantization collapses to 7,300 — and the same pattern replicates on zero-shot LAMBADA accuracy (RTN falls to 0.0%, GPTQ stays at 76.19%).
  3. Weight-only 3-bit quantization shrinks OPT-175B to about 63GB, fitting inference on a single 80GB GPU instead of five FP16 GPUs or three 8-bit LLM.int8() GPUs — and dequantize-on-the-fly kernels turn that into a measured 3.24×–4.53× latency speedup, without changing the underlying FP16 compute.
  4. The evaluation is calibration-data-free (128 random C4 excerpts, no labeled or task-specific data) but relies almost entirely on perplexity plus four zero-shot cloze/QA benchmarks — the paper's own Ethics Statement concedes that quantization's effect on bias and other "secondary measures" beyond accuracy remains unstudied.
