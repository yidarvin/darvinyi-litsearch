Equality of Opportunity in Supervised Learning

A post-processing recipe that turns any trained score into a classifier with equal error rates across a protected group — plus a proof that no such oblivious audit, including this one, can fully certify that a predictor isn't discriminating.

Moritz Hardt, Eric Price, Nathan Srebro
venue NeurIPS citations 5,251 topic Benchmarks & Evals source ↗

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

By 2016 the default answer to "is this classifier discriminatory?" was demographic parity: require the accept rate to be equal across a protected attribute A, Pr{Ŷ=1 | A=0} = Pr{Ŷ=1 | A=1}. Hardt, Price, and Srebro open by cataloguing exactly why that's the wrong yardstick, building on Dwork et al.'s 2012 critique. First, it doesn't ensure fairness: it happily accepts qualified applicants in one group and unqualified applicants in another, so long as the percentages match — a failure mode that shows up naturally whenever training data is thin for one group. Second, and more damning, it can forbid the perfectly accurate predictor. Whenever the true outcome Y — actually defaulting, actually graduating — is itself statistically correlated with A (the ordinary case in credit, admissions, or hiring data), demographic parity rules out Ŷ = Y as "unfair," even though it represents ground truth. Enforcing parity in that setting can be expensive and, the authors argue, incoherent as a notion of non-discrimination.

What was missing was a criterion that (1) can be checked directly from data — the joint distribution of prediction, protected attribute, and outcome — without interpreting individual features or making causal assumptions about them (an "oblivious" test, in the paper's terminology); (2) never brands the ideal, fully accurate classifier as discriminatory; and (3) comes with a constructive way to fix an already-trained, possibly biased score without re-engineering the training pipeline. No such criterion existed with a matching algorithm and optimality guarantee. This paper supplies both — and it is the direct formal ancestor of the disaggregated, per-subgroup error-rate reporting that empirical audits like Gender Shades would later use to expose real-world classifiers.

// how it works

The method

Definition 2.1 gives the paper's core criterion: a predictor Ŷ satisfies equalized odds with respect to protected attribute A and outcome Y if Ŷ and A are independent conditional on Y. In the binary case this unpacks to equal true-positive rates and equal false-positive rates across the two groups. A relaxation, equal opportunity, asks only for equal true-positive rates within the "advantaged" outcome (paying back the loan, being admitted) — leaving the false-positive side unconstrained. Both are oblivious: they depend only on the joint distribution of (Ŷ, A, Y), never on the raw features or how the score was produced, so they can be audited from aggregate statistics alone.

Pr{ Ŷ=1 | A=0, Y=y }  =  Pr{ Ŷ=1 | A=1, Y=y },   y ∈ {0,1} // equalized odds — equal TPR (y=1) and equal FPR (y=0) across groups

Pr{ Ŷ=1 | A=0, Y=1 }  =  Pr{ Ŷ=1 | A=1, Y=1 } // equal opportunity — equal TPR only, unconstrained FPR

The constructive half of the paper is a post-processing step: given any existing (possibly discriminatory) binary predictor Ŷ or real-valued score R, derive a new predictor Ỹ that is a (possibly randomized) function of R and A alone — never touching the raw features X or the original training pipeline — such that Ỹ satisfies equalized odds or equal opportunity while minimizing expected loss. For a fixed group a, every achievable trade-off between false-positive rate and true-positive rate forms a convex region (the convex hull of (0,0), the base classifier's own operating point, its mirror image, and (1,1)); Figure 1 draws these two achievable regions, one per group, in ROC space. An equalized-odds predictor must land in their overlap — the same (FPR, TPR) point for both groups — while equal opportunity only requires the two points to share the same TPR (the same horizontal line), leaving each group free to sit on a different point of its own ROC curve. Finding the loss-minimizing feasible point is a linear program in four variables (Proposition 4.4), solvable exactly and efficiently from the joint distribution of (Ŷ, A, Y) alone — no retraining required, and (as the paper notes) computable in a privacy-preserving, aggregate-statistics-only way.

A stronger guarantee (Theorem 5.6) shows this isn't just convenient — it's close to optimal. If Ŷ or R is exactly the Bayes-optimal (accuracy-maximizing) predictor, then deriving an equalized-odds/equal-opportunity classifier from it via this post-processing step yields the Bayes-optimal predictor among all classifiers satisfying the constraint. And if the input score merely approximates Bayes-optimality (within Kolmogorov distance d_K of the true optimum), the resulting fair classifier's excess loss is bounded by 2√2 · d_K — a quantified, no-surprises degradation. Practically: you don't need a bespoke "fair training algorithm"; a good ordinary classifier plus this post-processing step gets you most of the way.

// what they found

The results

The case study runs on 301,536 TransUnion TransRisk (FICO) scores from 2003, ranging 300–850, with race — Asian, white non-Hispanic, Hispanic, and black — as the protected attribute. The outcome Y is default: failing to pay a debt for at least 90 days on at least one account within the following 18–24 months. A commonly used cutoff of 620 corresponds to an aggregate non-default rate of 82%, and the paper studies a lender who is indifferent at that rate (false positives cost 82/18× as much as false negatives). Against that lender's objective, five policies are compared: max profit (an unconstrained, per-group optimal threshold), race-blind / single threshold (one cutoff for everyone), demographic parity, equal opportunity, and equalized odds — the last of which cannot generally be achieved with a single deterministic per-group cutoff at all, since equalizing both TPR and FPR simultaneously requires randomizing between two thresholds per group.

Figure 9 shows where each policy lands: equal opportunity and equalized-odds thresholds consistently fall between the max-profit/race-blind thresholds and the demographic-parity thresholds, for every racial group. The ROC curves in Figure 10 add an uncomfortable nuance the paper is careful to flag explicitly: FICO scores are measurably less predictive for black and Hispanic applicants than for white and Asian ones (a lower ROC curve within a group), which the authors attribute to prediction-accuracy differences in the underlying score, not to differences in actual default behavior.

The profit cost of fairness is the headline quantitative result. At the 82% non-default operating point:

Policy% of max profit
Race-blind (single threshold)99.3%
Equal opportunity92.8%
Equalized odds80.2%
Demographic parity69.8%

As the paper puts it, "equal opportunity fairness costs less than a quarter what demographic parity costs" in forgone profit at this operating point — and the gap would shrink further with a more accurate underlying classifier, since both criteria approach the unconstrained optimum as prediction quality improves.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

The most striking thing about this paper's rigor is that it builds its own strongest counterargument into Section 6. The authors construct two "scenarios" — one where a feature is used as an explicit proxy for the protected attribute (arguably illegitimate "profiling"), another where a legitimately predictive feature happens to correlate with the protected attribute and gets corrected via a compensating shift (a form of "affirmative action") — and prove that these can produce identical joint distributions over (Y, A, R*, R̃). Proposition 6.1's conclusion is blunt: no oblivious test — including equalized odds itself — can tell the two scenarios apart. Passing (or failing) this audit is evidence, not proof, of non-discrimination; the paper says so explicitly in its own conclusion ("measuring unfairness, rather than proving fairness"). That's an unusually honest admission for a paper whose whole contribution is a checkable criterion, and it should temper how the criterion gets used in practice — as a screening signal, not a certificate.

The FICO case study's own numbers double as a critique of equalized odds itself. Figure 11's left panel plots the fraction of non-defaulters who'd actually get a loan under each policy, by group: because equalized odds only equalizes true/false-positive rates, not overall approval volume, it does nothing to equalize how many people from each group are approved in aggregate — that's precisely the demographic-parity axis the paper spends its introduction arguing against. A regulator worried about disparate access to credit (rather than disparate accuracy) would find equalized odds silent on exactly the number they care about. And because equalized-odds classifiers must match the worst-performing group's ROC curve (Figure 10's black/Hispanic curves sit measurably below white/Asian), the criterion effectively drags every group's classification down to the least-predictive group's ceiling — which is why it costs 2–3× more profit than the weaker equal-opportunity relaxation.

The result is best read alongside two papers published within weeks of this one: Kleinberg, Mullainathan & Raghavan's Inherent Trade-offs in the Fair Determination of Risk Scores and Chouldechova's Fair Prediction with Disparate Impact (the COMPAS/ProPublica analysis). Together they show that equalized odds, calibration within groups, and equal predictive value cannot all hold simultaneously unless the classifier is perfect or the base rates of Y already match across groups — an impossibility result this paper doesn't state itself, though its own machinery (the calibration remark citing concurrent work [KMR16]) gestures at it. Read in isolation, equalized odds can look like "the" answer to non-discrimination; read against its contemporaries, it's one point on a Pareto frontier of incompatible fairness notions, and the paper is candid that it doesn't resolve which point a practitioner should pick.

baselines

Compared against demographic parity and an unconstrained race-blind classifier — the two standard reference points of 2016 — on a single, real (if dated) FICO dataset. No comparison against Dwork et al.'s individual-fairness notion, which the paper acknowledges requires a similarity metric it explicitly avoids constructing.

contamination

Not a benchmark-leaderboard paper, so no leakage risk in the usual sense — but the FICO case study inherits the "selective labels" problem endemic to credit data: default status Y is only observed for people who historically received a loan, and the analysis implicitly treats Y as known/reliable for the full population, a caveat the paper raises in the abstract ("Choose reliable target variables") but does not resolve empirically.

what's missed

Equalized odds says nothing about aggregate approval-rate disparities (Figure 11), does not by itself resolve whether a score is discriminatory (Section 6's unidentifiability result), and is evaluated on one dataset with one protected attribute — no intersectional analysis of race × another axis, the kind Gender Shades would popularize two years later.

would raise confidence

An explicit statement of the calibration/equalized-odds incompatibility (spelled out fully by Kleinberg et al. and Chouldechova within the same year), a second dataset/domain beyond consumer credit, and empirical engagement with the selective-labels problem rather than a target-variable caveat in the conclusion.

// bottom line

A landmark, technically airtight contribution: a checkable, oblivious, Bayes-optimality-preserving criterion with an efficient post-processing algorithm, and a refreshingly self-aware limitations section that most papers introducing a new metric never bother to write. But it is one leg of a three-paper impossibility result that arrived almost simultaneously (with Kleinberg et al. and Chouldechova), and on its own it can read as more conclusive than the authors intend — "equalized odds" measures one specific notion of accuracy-parity, it does not certify an absence of discrimination, and the paper says so, in writing, in its own final section.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. Equalized odds and equal opportunity tie non-discrimination to the target Y itself (Ŷ ⊥ A | Y), so unlike demographic parity they never rule out the ideal predictor Ŷ = Y — the criterion gets easier to satisfy, not harder, as the classifier gets more accurate.
  2. It's a post-processing step, not a training method. Given any existing score and the joint distribution of (score, protected attribute, outcome), the optimal fair derived classifier is the solution to a four-variable linear program — no access to raw features, no retraining, and (Theorem 5.6) provably close to Bayes-optimal if the underlying score was too.
  3. Passing this audit isn't proof of fairness. Section 6 constructs two structurally opposite data-generating processes — one "profiling" on the protected attribute, one legitimately correcting for it — that are statistically indistinguishable to any oblivious test, equalized odds included. On FICO data, equal opportunity costs under a quarter of what demographic parity costs in profit, but read alongside Kleinberg et al. and Chouldechova (published within weeks), equalized odds is one incompatible fairness notion among several, not a final answer.
