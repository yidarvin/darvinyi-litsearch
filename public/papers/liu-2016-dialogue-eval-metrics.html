How NOT To Evaluate Your Dialogue System: An Empirical Study of Unsupervised Evaluation Metrics for Dialogue Response Generation

The first systematic human-correlation check on the metrics the dialogue-generation field had been quietly borrowing from machine translation and summarization — BLEU, METEOR, ROUGE, and three word-embedding heuristics all correlate weakly with human judgment on chit-chat, and several of them show no significant correlation at all on technical dialogue.

Chia-Wei Liu, Ryan Lowe, Iulian V. Serban, Michael Noseworthy, Laurent Charlin, Joelle Pineau · McGill University & Université de Montréal

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

By 2016, end-to-end neural dialogue models — the Neural Conversational Model, HRED, persona-based generation, diversity-promoting objectives — had no task-completion signal to train or evaluate against, so paper after paper reached for the nearest off-the-shelf metric: BLEU from machine translation, METEOR and ROUGE alongside it, sometimes a word-embedding cosine similarity on top. Ritter et al. (2011), Sordoni et al. (2015), Li et al. (2015), and Wen et al. (2015) all reported BLEU scores for unsupervised dialogue systems. None of them first checked whether BLEU actually tracks what a human thinks of a chatbot's reply.

The mismatch is structural, not incidental. BLEU and its relatives assume that a good response looks like the reference response — high word overlap implies high quality. That assumption is reasonable in translation, where there's a comparatively narrow band of correct target sentences. It is not reasonable in open-domain dialogue, where the space of valid replies to a given context is enormous and semantically diverse. The paper's own illustration makes this concrete:

// Table 1 — the intrinsic-diversity problem

Context — A: "Hey John, what do you want to do tonight?" B: "Why don't we go see a movie?"
Ground truth — "Nah, I hate that stuff, let's do something active."
Model response — "Oh sure! Heard the film about Turing is out!"

Both responses are perfectly reasonable, contextually appropriate replies. They share zero words. BLEU scores the model response 0.

Nobody had actually measured, at the level of individual responses, whether these borrowed metrics correlate with human adequacy judgments in dialogue — as opposed to just being different numbers papers could report going up. That is the entire empirical contribution of this paper: run the metrics and a human survey side by side on the same responses, and see if they agree.

// how it works

The method

The authors test ten metrics in two families. Word-overlap metrics — BLEU-1 through BLEU-4, METEOR, and ROUGE-L — score a proposed response r̂ against a single ground-truth response r by counting shared n-grams (BLEU), aligned tokens with WordNet/stem/paraphrase matching (METEOR), or longest common subsequence (ROUGE-L). Embedding-based metrics — Greedy Matching, Embedding Average, and Vector Extrema — instead compare Word2Vec vector representations of the two responses via cosine similarity, so a response can score well even with no lexical overlap, as long as its words are semantically close to the reference's. The embeddings are deliberately trained on a corpus disjoint from the task data, to keep the metric statistically independent of what it's evaluating.

BLEU-N := b(r,r̂) · exp(Σn=1N βn log Pn(r,r̂)),  Pn(r,r̂) = Σk min(h(k,r), h(k,r̂))Σk h(k,r) // n-gram precision P_n, geometrically averaged across n and discounted by a brevity penalty b(·)

To generate the responses being judged, the authors run five systems per domain spanning a deliberately wide quality range: two TF-IDF retrieval baselines (context-based C-TFIDF and response-based R-TFIDF), a neural Dual Encoder (DE) retrieval model, a plain LSTM language-model generator, and HRED, the hierarchical recurrent encoder-decoder. They evaluate on two contrasting corpora — a casual, non-technical Twitter corpus and the technical, troubleshooting-oriented Ubuntu Dialogue Corpus — chosen precisely because they sit at opposite ends of the diversity/specificity spectrum.

The human side of the comparison is a controlled survey: 25 volunteers from the authors' own CS department each rated 100 responses per domain (20 contexts × 5 candidate responses — one random utterance, one from each of TF-IDF/DE/HRED, and one written by a human annotator) on a 1–5 adequacy scale. Two raters were dropped for weak inter-rater agreement (Cohen's κ < 0.2); the remaining 23 had a median κ ≈ 0.55, and 88.9% of all rater pairs exceeded κ = 0.4. The paper then computes both Pearson (linear) and Spearman (monotonic-rank) correlation between each metric's score and the averaged human rating, response by response, and compares that to the correlation between two randomly split halves of the human raters themselves — an empirical ceiling for how well any metric could possibly agree with "the" human judgment.

BLEU-2 is the best-performing word-overlap metric on Twitter (Spearman 0.3576, Pearson 0.3874, both p < 0.01) — but even that is under 40% of the ~0.95 correlation two independent groups of humans achieve with each other. On Ubuntu, every word-overlap metric is statistically indistinguishable from zero (all p ≥ 0.16). BLEU-3 and BLEU-4 track BLEU-2 closely on Twitter despite being near-zero for most responses — the paper found that for BLEU-4, only four response pairs scored above 10⁻⁹ — because the smoothing constant assigns most of the score to unigram/bigram overlap regardless. The embedding metrics fare no better: Embedding Average is actually negatively correlated with humans on Ubuntu (Pearson −0.1631, Spearman −0.1387).

Yet the same embedding metrics do separate model quality when averaged over many responses (Table 2): the neural Dual Encoder significantly outperforms both TF-IDF baselines, and HRED significantly outperforms the plain LSTM, on every metric in both domains. The paper calls this out explicitly as a trap — "Conclusions from an Incomplete Analysis" — because a metric that correctly ranks systems in aggregate can still be nearly uncorrelated with human judgment at the response level, which is exactly the level most papers' example tables and per-response claims operate at.

Two further experiments (Tables 4–5) probe why the metrics fail. Stripping stopwords and punctuation from BLEU actually weakens its correlation on Twitter (BLEU-2 Spearman drops from 0.3576 to 0.2030), implying BLEU's raw correlation was partly riding on non-semantic surface features. And BLEU-1, BLEU-2, and METEOR all show a large, significant (p < 0.01) score gap between response pairs with small vs. large word-count differences — even though mean human ratings barely move between those same groups (2.66 vs. 2.57, p = 0.73) — meaning these metrics are sensitive to response length in a way that has nothing to do with how humans judge quality.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

This is a methodology paper whose entire job is to be a rigorous negative result, and mostly it holds up well — but its own numbers come from a small, narrow instrument, which is worth sitting with rather than glossing over.

The human survey is 25 raters (23 kept), all drawn from the authors' own computer-science department, judging 100 responses per domain — 20 unique contexts × 5 responses. That is n = 100 data points per domain feeding each correlation coefficient. Several of the paper's "significant" findings sit right at the edge: METEOR on Twitter is p = 0.06 (not significant at the conventional 0.05 threshold), Vector Extrema's Pearson is p = 0.067. With a rater pool this small and homogeneous — CS graduate students, not a demographically broad user population — both the point estimates and which comparisons clear significance are more fragile than a single clean table suggests. The paper's own recommendation (a larger test corpus would likely raise BLEU-3/4's correlation, since so few responses have non-zero scores at n=100) is itself an admission that these numbers could shift with more data.

The metric roster is also a snapshot of exactly what the field was using in 2016 — BLEU/METEOR/ROUGE plus three word-embedding compositions — and doesn't include the multi-reference variants (deltaBLEU) or context-aware/learned metrics that the paper itself gestures at in its discussion as the likely future direction. That's a fair scoping choice for a "how not to" paper about current practice, not a flaw, but it does mean the paper diagnoses a problem without benchmarking any candidate fix.

baselines

Comprehensive for its moment — every word-overlap and word-embedding metric actually in use in 2016 dialogue papers is tested, matching precisely what Ritter et al., Sordoni et al., Li et al., and Wen et al. had been reporting. No learned or context-aware metrics are included, but none existed yet for this task.

sample size

n = 100 human-judged responses per domain (20 contexts × 5 responses), 23 raters after filtering. Several reported effects sit at p ≈ 0.05–0.07, meaning statistical significance is borderline for a handful of the weaker metrics.

what's missed

Single-reference, single-turn, single "adequacy" dimension only — no multi-reference scoring (deltaBLEU is discussed but not run here), no fluency/informativeness axes, no multi-turn coherence, and raters are all CS-department volunteers rather than a broad population of chat users.

would raise confidence

A larger, more demographically diverse rater pool; a bigger context/response sample to firm up the borderline p-values; and a direct comparison against the multi-reference and learned metrics (BLEURT, COMET, ADEM) that the field adopted in the years after this paper, to see whether they actually closed the gap this paper measures.

// bottom line

The paper's central number is damning regardless of these caveats: the metrics the field was using achieve at best about a third of the correlation with human judgment that two independent human raters achieve with each other (Spearman 0.36 vs. 0.95 on Twitter), and on a technical domain several drop to statistically indistinguishable from zero. Whatever the exact confidence interval on any single coefficient, the qualitative conclusion — don't trust an unvalidated word-overlap or embedding-average score to tell you a dialogue system got better — is well supported and the reason this paper is still cited a decade later, including by BLEURT (Sellam et al., 2020), which opens by citing exactly this kind of correlation failure as its motivation.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. Word-overlap metrics ported wholesale from machine translation and summarization — BLEU, METEOR, ROUGE — correlate weakly with human judgment of dialogue quality at best (BLEU-2 Spearman 0.36 on Twitter) and are statistically indistinguishable from zero on a more technical domain (Ubuntu, all p ≥ 0.16).
  2. Word-embedding metrics (Greedy Matching, Embedding Average, Vector Extrema) don't fix the problem — Embedding Average is actually negatively correlated with human ratings on Ubuntu (Pearson −0.16).
  3. A metric can rank systems sensibly in aggregate (DE beats TF-IDF, HRED beats LSTM) while being nearly uncorrelated with human judgment at the individual-response level — the level most papers' example tables and headline numbers actually operate at.
  4. The human-vs-human correlation ceiling (Spearman ≈ 0.95) is the yardstick: no metric tested here gets remotely close, which is the empirical case this paper makes for treating "our BLEU score improved" as weak evidence about dialogue quality without independent human validation.
