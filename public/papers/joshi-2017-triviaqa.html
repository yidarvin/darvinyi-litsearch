TriviaQA: A Large Scale Distantly Supervised Challenge Dataset for Reading Comprehension

A reading-comprehension dataset built backwards: 95,956 questions authored independently by trivia enthusiasts, with 650K+ question-answer-evidence triples retrieved afterward from Wikipedia and the open web — decoupling question-writing from evidence-gathering to force real cross-sentence reasoning instead of the lexical overlap crowdsourced datasets bake in.

Mandar Joshi, Eunsol Choi, Daniel S. Weld, Luke Zettlemoyer

What problem is this solving?

By 2017, large-scale reading-comprehension (RC) datasets came in two flavors, and both smuggled in a shortcut. Cloze-style datasets — CNN/Daily Mail (Hermann et al., 2015), the Children’s Book Test, LAMBADA — scale automatically because a script blanks out a word or entity in real text and calls that the “question,” but the result isn’t a natural-language question at all. Crowdsourced datasets like SQuAD (Rajpurkar et al., 2016) fixed that by paying annotators to write real questions, but the annotator reads the passage first and writes the question against it — which means question and answer sentence inevitably share vocabulary and structure, and a model can often win by string-matching rather than reasoning.

TriviaQA's authors argue that coupling question authorship to a specific evidence source is the actual problem: it biases both question style and the vocabulary overlap between question and answer. Their fix is to decouple the two entirely. Questions come from 14 trivia and quiz-league websites — written by trivia enthusiasts for other trivia enthusiasts, with no evidence document in view and no NLP task in mind. Evidence is gathered afterward, automatically, from Wikipedia and the open web. Because nobody who wrote a question ever saw the text that would later be used to answer it, there's no built-in reason for the two to share phrasing — forcing any system that wants to do well to handle real compositional, syntactically divergent questions instead of pattern-matching a paraphrase.

// how it works

The method

Collection starts with question–answer pairs scraped from 14 trivia and quiz-league websites, dropping anything under four tokens (too simple or too vague to be interesting). Evidence is then gathered from two independent sources. For the web domain, each question — deliberately posed without its answer, so the search results aren't biased toward it — is sent to the Bing Web Search API; the top 50 URLs are filtered to drop the original trivia sites and any URL containing “trivia,” “question,” or “answer,” and the top 10 remaining pages are crawled (PDFs and malformed pages pruned). For the Wikipedia domain, the TAGME entity linker (Ferragina and Scaiella, 2010) finds Wikipedia entities mentioned in the question text and pulls in their pages as evidence.

This is distant supervision: the working assumption is that if the answer string appears in a retrieved document, that document probably explains the answer — not that it's been human-verified. Documents that don't contain the answer string are simply discarded. The two domains are treated differently downstream: web evidence is highly redundant (about six documents per question on average), so each question–document pair becomes an independent training instance; Wikipedia evidence is sparser (about 1.8 documents per question) and rarely restates a fact twice, so all of a question's Wikipedia documents are pooled into one example. After filtering, this yields 95,956 question–answer pairs, 40,478 unique answers, and 662,659 evidence documents (avg. question length 14 words, avg. document length 2,895 words) — over 650K question-answer-evidence triples in total. An unfiltered release goes further still: 110,495 QA pairs and 740K evidence documents, for downstream IR-style QA work the paper doesn't itself explore.

Because distant supervision is noisy, the authors also built a small human-verified subset: an annotator read 986 Wikipedia and 1,345 Web dev/test questions and their evidence, and confirmed whether the minimal facts needed to answer were actually present. Accuracy on this check — effectively the fraction of automatically-retrieved evidence that really does support its answer — came out to 79.6% (Wikipedia) and 75.3% (Web). Only the correctly-answered subset (1,975 question-document-answer triples across both domains) becomes the “verified” gold evaluation set used alongside the full noisy distant-supervision set.

Three baselines probe the difficulty: a random-entity heuristic (Wikipedia only, picks a random candidate entity off the page that doesn't already appear in the question); a feature-based classifier that ranks candidate answer n-grams (any Wikipedia-title n-gram, n∈[1,5], from sentences sharing a word with the question) using LambdaMART (a boosted-tree ranker, via RankLib) over context and Wikipedia-catalog features; and a neural model — a modified BiDAF (Seo et al., 2017), the strongest SQuAD model of the day. BiDAF was built for ~122-word SQuAD paragraphs, not TriviaQA's ~2,895-word documents, so documents are truncated to the first 800 words before it runs (a cap the paper notes already puts a ceiling below 100% on the achievable oracle score). Since Wikipedia questions can have several evidence documents, BiDAF scores each document separately and the candidate answer's final score is just the sum of its per-document confidences:

score(Ai) = Σk ci,k  // confidence of candidate answer Aᵢ, summed across its n retrieved documents Dᵢ,₁ … Dᵢ,ₙ
// what they found

The results

The headline number is the gap to humans. On the Wikipedia dev set, the random baseline scores 12.72 EM / 22.91 F1; the feature classifier reaches 23.42 EM / 27.68 F1; BiDAF does best at 40.26 EM / 45.74 F1 (40.32/45.91 on test) — and the paper's separate human evaluation puts people at 79.7% accuracy on Wikipedia and 75.4% on the Web domain. As the abstract summarizes it: the classifier and neural model land at roughly 23% and 40%, against a human baseline near 80% — “neither approach comes close.” On the smaller human-verified subset, scores rise across the board (BiDAF reaches 47.47 EM / 53.70 F1 on verified Wikipedia dev, 51.38 EM / 55.47 F1 on verified Web dev), but the gap to human performance persists throughout.

Oracle scores (the best a model could do if it always picked from the truncated evidence it was given) reveal how much headroom is real reasoning difficulty versus retrieval/truncation noise: BiDAF's Wikipedia dev oracle is 82.55% and its classifier counterpart is 71.41% — both well under 100%, because the 800-word truncation and imperfect retrieval sometimes cut the answer out entirely. Accuracy also degrades cleanly with question complexity: BiDAF's Wikipedia-dev accuracy falls from 50% on questions of 5 words or fewer to 32% on questions of 20 words or more. A 100-example manual error analysis of incorrect BiDAF predictions found the largest failure modes were paraphrasing (29 examples), insufficient evidence in any retrieved document (19), reasoning over multiple sentences (18), the answer being truncated out of the clipped document (15), and distractor entities confusing the model (11). Separately, comparative analysis found TriviaQA's Wikipedia questions require multi-sentence reasoning about three times as often as SQuAD's, with 69% showing syntactic divergence and 41% lexical (synonym) divergence between question and evidence sentence.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

The decoupling trick genuinely works — TriviaQA's questions are more compositional and syntactically divergent from their evidence than SQuAD's, and it took the era's best model (BiDAF) from ~90% F1 territory on SQuAD-length passages down to 40–47% here. But the same automation that buys scale and naturalness reintroduces exactly the noise the paper is trying to avoid, and the paper's own numbers show it. Its own human check found only 79.6% (Wikipedia) and 75.3% (Web) of automatically-retrieved evidence documents actually contain the facts needed to answer — meaning roughly one in four training and evaluation examples in the un-verified majority of the dataset may be mislabeled: the answer string appears by coincidence, not because that document explains it. That is a materially higher noise floor than a fully human-authored benchmark like SQuAD, and it is why a separate 1,975-triple “verified” gold subset had to be carved out at all.

That noise complicates the paper's own headline claim. The oracle scores — 82.55% for BiDAF and 71.41% for the classifier on Wikipedia dev, both far short of 100% — show that even a hypothetically perfect answer-selector couldn't close the gap to the 79.7% human number, because the 800-word truncation and imperfect retrieval sometimes remove the gold answer from the evidence entirely. So part of the celebrated 40-point gap between BiDAF and humans is real reading-comprehension difficulty, and part of it is simply data engineering (truncation, retrieval noise) that a smarter pipeline — not a smarter reader — would fix. The paper doesn't disentangle the two.

The web domain's redundancy cuts the other way: with an average of six evidence documents per question and scoring done at the document level rather than the question level, a system doesn't need to comprehend any single hard document if one of the other five states the fact in easy, lexically-overlapping language. That's implicitly a retrieval-friendly setup, in tension with the paper's stated goal of testing genuine cross-sentence reasoning — the sparser, non-redundant Wikipedia setting (1.8 docs/question, question-level scoring) is the more honest reading-comprehension test, and it's not a coincidence that's also where BiDAF's oracle-to-actual gap is largest.

baselines

BiDAF (2017's strongest SQuAD reader) and a LambdaMART feature classifier — fair, current baselines at launch, but pre-transformer and pre-retrieval-augmentation; TriviaQA was effectively saturated within a few years and today functions mainly as an easy closed-book factual-recall check for LLMs rather than a frontier RC benchmark.

contamination

Structurally high: evidence is scraped live from the open Wikipedia/web rather than a held-out curated corpus, and the trivia questions themselves are scraped from public quiz sites — both plausibly sit inside modern LLM pretraining data, which is exactly why later work (SimpleQA, 2024) calls TriviaQA “now saturated” when picking a harder comparison benchmark.

what's missed

Distant-supervision noise is measured (79.6%/75.3% evidence-support accuracy) but not corrected for in the main results; oracle scores below 100% show truncation/retrieval noise inflates the apparent model-vs-human gap; and document-level scoring over 6×-redundant web evidence rewards retrieval as much as comprehension.

would raise confidence

Reporting the verified 1,975-triple subset as the primary metric rather than a secondary one, question-level (not document-level) scoring even in the redundant web domain, and a contamination-audited held-out split now that the source text is over eight years old.

// bottom line

TriviaQA's lasting contribution is the decoupling idea itself: collect questions from people who never see the evidence, then retrieve evidence afterward, and you get more naturally compositional, lexically divergent questions than any single-annotator crowdsourcing pipeline produces. But buying that naturalness through automatic, unaudited retrieval reintroduces the very noise the design was meant to avoid — which is exactly the tension Natural Questions and HotpotQA were built to resolve next, and why TriviaQA today survives less as a reading-comprehension frontier and more as a large, fast, well-known closed-book QA sanity check baked into nearly every LLM eval suite.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. TriviaQA's core idea is decoupling: 95,956 questions are authored by trivia enthusiasts who never see the evidence, and 650K+ evidence documents are retrieved afterward from Wikipedia and the web — producing more naturally compositional, lexically and syntactically divergent questions (69% syntactic divergence, three times SQuAD's rate of multi-sentence reasoning) than datasets where the same annotator writes both the passage-grounded question and reads the answer.
  2. Scale came from automating evidence collection via distant supervision, but that's also the paper's own admitted weak spot: a human check found only 79.6% (Wikipedia) and 75.3% (Web) of retrieved evidence documents actually support their answer, which is why a small 1,975-triple human-verified gold subset ships alongside the 650K+ noisy triples.
  3. The 2017 baselines — a LambdaMART feature classifier at 23% EM and BiDAF at 40% EM on Wikipedia — trailed the 79.7% human baseline by a wide margin, but that gap closed within a few years of pretrained readers and retrieval augmentation; today TriviaQA is used less as a comprehension frontier and more as a fast, ubiquitous closed-book factual-recall check for LLMs (GPT-3's few-shot QA table, SimpleQA and BrowseComp's “now-easy” comparison points, LLM-as-judge studies).
