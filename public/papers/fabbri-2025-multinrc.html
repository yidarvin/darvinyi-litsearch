MultiNRC: A Challenging and Native Multilingual Reasoning Evaluation Benchmark for LLMs

1,055 reasoning questions written from scratch by native French, Spanish, and Chinese speakers — not translated from English. Each one was kept only because frontier models already fail it. The best model, o3-pro, scores just 49%.

Alexander R. Fabbri, Diego Mares, Jorge Flores, Meher Mankikar, Ernesto Hernandez, Dean Lee, Bing Liu, Chen Xing · Scale AI

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

English reasoning evaluation is rich — GSM8K, MATH, MMLU-Pro, GPQA — but the multilingual landscape is mostly a translation of it. MGSM is GSM8K translated into ten languages; MMLU-ProX is MMLU-Pro machine-translated and reviewed across 28 languages; BenchMAX renders GPQA into other tongues. Useful as these are, they inherit English's cultural framing: the entities, the units, the assumptions all stay Anglophone. A question's reasoning never actually depends on knowing how French gender agreement works, what a Chengyu is, or when Día de la Candelaria falls.

The other common route — repurposing real exams in other languages (e.g. M3Exam) — tends to produce multiple-choice knowledge recall rather than reasoning, and those questions are already easy for frontier models. The result is a blind spot: we genuinely did not know how state-of-the-art LLMs handle reasoning that is natively grounded in a language's grammar, wordplay, traditions, and number systems. MultiNRC is built to measure exactly that — and to make the comparison "does the model reason worse in French than in English?" precise by shipping human English equivalents of the translatable questions.

// how it works

The method

MultiNRC (the Multilingual Native Reasoning Challenge) is 1,055 questions authored from scratch by native speakers of French, Spanish, and Chinese, each with a short, objective ground-truth final answer (a word or a phrase). Every item belongs to one of four reasoning categories chosen to demand local knowledge, not translated context:

  • Language-specific linguistic reasoning — grammar, honorifics, word-formation rules that exist only in the target language (e.g. délice is masculine singular but feminine plural in French).
  • Wordplay & riddles — puns, homophones, idioms that cannot survive translation (a Chinese riddle answered with a homophonic Chengyu).
  • Cultural / tradition reasoning — multi-step reasoning over local holidays, customs, and timelines (planning flights around Día de la Candelaria with two full weekends at the beach).
  • Math with cultural relevance — calculations that hinge on a culture-specific calendar, currency, counting system, or institution (the French viager property scheme, dated against the Cannes Film Festival).

The defining design choice is an adversarial difficulty filter: a candidate question is retained only if at least three of five then-current frontier models fail it. The filter panel was Gemini-2.5-pro, o4-mini, Claude-3.7-Sonnet, DeepSeek-R1, and Llama-4-Maverick.

keep question q  ⟺  #{ m ∈ panel : m fails q }5 ≥ 35 // inclusion rule — panel = {Gemini-2.5-pro, o4-mini, Claude-3.7-Sonnet, DeepSeek-R1, Llama-4-Maverick}

Two native-speaker review passes follow: the first checks that each prompt and gold answer are correct and on-category; the second re-verifies that three of five models really do fail. Because every answer is short and objective, grading is automated with an LLM judge (GPT-4.1), which reaches over 95% agreement with human judgments and a Scott's Pi of 0.88. For the Cultural and Math categories, annotators also wrote English-equivalent prompts (translating the logic, not the words) so a model's native-language reasoning can be compared head-to-head with English on the same problem. Linguistic and wordplay items have no English equivalents — they would stop making sense. All results are pass@1, 32k max completion tokens, 16k thinking budget where applicable.

The results

Across 14 frontier models, none clears 50%. o3-pro leads at 49.00%, with o3-high (45.50%), Gemini-2.5-Pro (45.12%), and o3-medium (44.45%) close behind; everything else trails badly, down to Llama-4-Maverick at 8.44%. The reasoning-effort and reasoning-model story is clean: thinking variants beat their non-thinking siblings (Claude-4-Opus-thinking 33.93% vs 29.00%), DeepSeek-R1-0528 improves on the original R1, and GPT-4.1 beats GPT-4o.

A single average hides the real structure

Broken down by category and language, the difficulty is uneven. Math with cultural relevance is hardest (23.3% average across models), with Spanish math the single worst cell (18.7%). Linguistic (34.8%) and cultural (31.1%) reasoning score higher; wordplay stays hard everywhere but is easiest in French. Spanish is the most challenging language overall. Crucially, models re-rank across categories: Gemini-2.5-Pro is only third on average but first in math, while o3-pro spikes on French wordplay (75.9%). A leaderboard number, the authors argue, is less informative than the taxonomy.

English is a crutch for math, not for culture

The English-equivalent set isolates the language effect. For math, translating into English lifts accuracy by ~10 points on average — and far more for Spanish (+19.1) and Chinese (+12.5). The case studies attribute this to retrieval: models surface the right background facts and values more reliably in English. But for cultural reasoning the gain is essentially zero on average (Spanish +3.5, Chinese +1.4, French −5.0) — the specific cultural knowledge is simply missing from the model regardless of the query language. The aggregate "Mother Tongue Effect" is −5.36 (models are slightly worse in the native language), within-model consistency between settings averages just ~35%, and the gap between a model's best and worst language averages 13.9 points (DeepSeek-R1 swings 29.1 points between Chinese and Spanish).

// does it hold up?

The eval read

This is a genuinely hard, hard-to-game benchmark, and the native-authoring premise is the right one. But the two headline claims — "no model passes 50%" and the leaderboard ordering — are entangled with the construction process in a way that deserves care.

The difficulty filter keeps a question only if ≥3 of 5 specific models fail it. That guarantees the surviving set is adversarially selected against those five — so a low absolute ceiling is partly built in, not purely discovered. More pointedly, the filter panel was {Gemini-2.5-pro, o4-mini, Claude-3.7-Sonnet, DeepSeek-R1, Llama-4-Maverick} — and the o3 family (o3, o3-pro) was never in it. The models that top the leaderboard were exempt from the very filter that defines the dataset, while o4-mini, DeepSeek-R1, and Llama-4 were filtered against directly. That asymmetry plausibly inflates the o3 family's relative standing and deflates the panel members'; the ranking should be read as directional, not a clean capability ladder.

The per-language/category claims — the most interesting part — ride on small cells. The 1,055 items split into 12 language×category buckets of roughly 70–120 each, then again per model. Eye-catching results like o3-pro's 75.9% on French wordplay (79 items) or DeepSeek-R1's Chinese edge sit on samples where the binomial confidence interval is wide, and there is no run-to-run variance reported at all (everything is single-sample pass@1).

baselines

14 current frontier models, well chosen (o3-pro, Gemini-2.5-Pro, Claude-4, DeepSeek-R1-0528). But the difficulty filter used a different five-model panel that excluded the o3 family — so the leaderboard's winners were never filtered against, an asymmetry that biases the ranking.

contamination

Low for now — questions are newly authored in-language, not scraped. But the set is public on Hugging Face, so future training-data leakage is a real risk, and "3-of-5 fail" pins difficulty to mid-2025 model versions that will decay as models improve.

what's missed

Only three high-resource languages, chosen by author familiarity; no low-resource languages and no dialectal variation (Iberian vs Latin-American Spanish, etc.). The English-equivalent control covers only Cultural and Math — the two hardest categories (wordplay, linguistic) have no cross-lingual comparison, and the +10% math gain could partly reflect translation simplification rather than pure knowledge retrieval.

would raise confidence

A held-out filter panel that includes the top models (symmetric difficulty); pass@k or multi-sample variance, not just binomial CIs; per-cell sample sizes on the breakdowns; extending the English-equivalent control to all translatable items with a translation-difficulty ablation; and adding low-resource languages and dialect splits.

// bottom line

Strong evidence that native, culturally grounded reasoning is unsolved — frontier models top out at 49% and lean on English to do math. Treat the specific "<50% for everyone" figure and the exact model ordering as artifacts of an asymmetric, version-pinned difficulty filter, and the per-language/category spikes as suggestive rather than settled.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. Native authoring beats translation. Writing questions from scratch in-language surfaces linguistic and cultural reasoning that translated benchmarks erase — and on it, the best frontier model (o3-pro) reaches only 49%, with most models far lower.
  2. English helps math, not culture. Translating questions into English raises math accuracy ~10 points (Spanish +19.1, Chinese +12.5) because models retrieve quantitative background better in English — but cultural reasoning barely moves, because the specific knowledge is absent in any language.
  3. The taxonomy outranks the leaderboard. Models re-rank by category and language (Gemini is 3rd overall yet 1st in math; Spanish is the hardest language; within-model language gaps average 13.9 points) — and the headline "<50%" is partly a property of an adversarial filter that exempted the very models that lead.
