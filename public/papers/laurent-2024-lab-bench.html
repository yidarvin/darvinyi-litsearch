LAB-Bench: Measuring Capabilities of Language Models for Biology Research

Most science benchmarks quiz a model on textbook facts. LAB-Bench instead asks whether it can do the work of research — search the literature, read a figure, query a database, fix a broken protocol, design a clone — across 2,457 multiple-choice questions, scored against PhD biologists.

Jon M. Laurent, Joseph D. Janizek, Michael Ruzo, Michaela M. Hinks, Michael J. Hammerling, Siddharth Narayanan, Manvitha Ponnapati, Andrew D. White, Samuel G. Rodriques · FutureHouse

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

There is no shortage of optimism that LLMs will accelerate science — but very little of the evidence comes from tasks scientists actually do. The benchmarks that dominate the science-LLM conversation, MMLU and GPQA, are essentially exams: graduate-level, "Google-proof," but still recall-and-reasoning over textbook-style questions. A model can ace them and still be useless in a wet lab, because real biology research is a workflow, not a quiz: you search the literature for a result attested once in some 2023 paper, you read an unlabeled figure, you pull a number out of a specific database, you notice that a protocol step is broken, you plan a multi-enzyme cloning reaction.

LAB-Bench targets exactly that gap — practical research capability rather than rote knowledge. The authors (FutureHouse) frame strong performance here as a necessary-but-not-sufficient condition for a useful AI research assistant: a system that can't do these things won't help a biologist, even if it can recite a textbook. They go further on the hardest slice, the 41 "Cloning Scenarios": being able to answer those — multi-step molecular-cloning problems that take a trained biologist 10–60 minutes — may be close to necessary and sufficient for a model to add real value as a molecular-biology collaborator.

// how it works

The method

LAB-Bench is 2,457 multiple-choice questions spread across eight categories chosen to be largely universal across biology research. Three are tool-free probes of raw reasoning (FigQA, TableQA, ProtocolQA); the rest are deliberately tool-dependent — they're built so that a model should need retrieval or sequence software to answer, making them stand-ins for science-specific RAG and agentic tool use.

  • LitQA2 — retrieve an esoteric finding usually attested once in a recent paper, and not recoverable from the abstract. A scientific-RAG probe.
  • SuppQA — answer from a paper's supplemental material, given only its title and DOI.
  • FigQA — reason over a figure presented as an image with no caption; multi-modal, often multi-hop.
  • TableQA — look up and compute over a table, again as a caption-less image.
  • DbQA — return facts attributable to specific biology databases (miRDB, GTRD, …), designed so no single API answers them all.
  • ProtocolQA — published protocols are deliberately broken; the model must say what to fix.
  • SeqQA — 15 subtasks on DNA/RNA/protein sequences: PCR primer design, restriction digests, ORF translation.
  • CloningScenarios — 41 "human-hard" multi-plasmid, multi-step cloning problems.

The metric that does the real work

The benchmark's most important design choice isn't a category — it's the scoring. Every question includes an explicit "insufficient information / decline to answer" option. That lets the authors separate what a model knows from what it's willing to guess, and report two numbers instead of one, plus coverage:

accuracy = correctall     precision = correctattempted     coverage = attemptedall // a model that declines often has high precision but low accuracy — exactly what you want to detect in a science tool

This matters because in research a confidently wrong answer is worse than "I don't know." A model can post respectable precision while quietly refusing most of the questions (low coverage), which tanks its accuracy — and the gap between those two numbers is itself a finding.

How the questions were built

Two pipelines (Figure 2). The scalable, structured categories — SeqQA and DbQA — were generated programmatically. The categories needing judgment — LitQA2, SuppQA, FigQA, TableQA, ProtocolQA, CloningScenarios — were written manually by the authors and contracted biology experts. A recurring trick: ask a frontier model to propose candidate questions, watch where it produces nonsensical or unanswerable ones, and revise those — a cheap way to find the "null space" of a model's reasoning and harvest questions it can't solve.

Evaluation setup. Models are run 0-shot with chain-of-thought, no tools at all (no internet, no RAG, no sequence software), with answers parsed straight from the text completion and metrics averaged over three runs. Seven frontier models were tested — claude-3-5-sonnet-20240620, claude-3-opus, gemini-1.5-pro, gpt-4o, gpt-4-turbo, claude-3-haiku, and llama-3-70B-Instruct (not multimodal, so it skips FigQA/TableQA). The human baseline is a panel of PhD-level biologists who, unlike the models, were allowed internet and a DNA-manipulation tool (and asked, on the honor system, not to use AI). About 80% of each subtask is public (on Hugging Face); 20% is held private for contamination monitoring, and a BIG-bench-superset canary string is embedded in the data.

// what they found

The results

The headline: human experts greatly outperform every model on both accuracy and precision across most categories, while also answering more questions (higher coverage). But the per-category texture is where the benchmark earns its keep (Figure 3).

  • FigQA is the wall. Almost every model sits near random precision — except claude-3-5-sonnet, which is dramatically better at reading technical images.
  • TableQA is the easiest for models, even though tables are images. Claude 3.5 Sonnet narrowly surpassed human precision and equaled human accuracy here — the one category where a model genuinely closes the gap.
  • LitQA2 exposes refusal behavior. All models clear ~40% precision (above random), yet the big frontier models often attempt well under 20% of questions, pushing their accuracy below random. They know they should look something up, and decline.
  • SuppQA has the lowest coverage of all — models simply refuse, consistent with supplemental text being under-represented in training data.
  • SeqQA lands around 40–50% precision overall but swings wildly by subtask: above 90% precision on the easiest primer task (PCR-seq-enzprimers), and poor on anything needing long-sequence manipulation (amplicon length from a template, restriction-fragment counts/lengths). On one translation-efficiency subtask Claude 3.5 Sonnet beat humans 0.88 to 0.75 precision — partly because the human experts found the question genuinely confusing.
  • ProtocolQA clusters at 50–60% precision; Cloning Scenarios sit well below human performance.

// the result that should change how you read MCQ benchmarks

On hard questions, models reach above-random precision by eliminating implausible distractors and guessing, not by reasoning. To prove it, the authors re-ran subsets of CloningScenarios, ProtocolQA and FigQA in open-response form against Claude 3.5 Sonnet and GPT-4o. Scores collapsed: both models hit only 0.20 accuracy on Cloning Scenarios and 0.30 on FigQA (Claude 3.5 beat GPT-4o 0.30 to 0.20 on ProtocolQA). On Cloning Scenarios, both models got the same 2 of 10 right — and both arrived at them by guessing the most common enzyme (BsaI) or antibiotic (Carbenicillin/Ampicillin). The multiple-choice numbers were measuring test-taking, not molecular biology.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

LAB-Bench is unusually honest about its own weaknesses, and that honesty is what makes it worth reading carefully — because the honest weaknesses materially change how you should read the leaderboard.

The first is baseline fairness. The models ran with no tools; the human experts had the internet and DNA software. The paper concedes the comparison is "not entirely equitable" — and it bites hardest exactly where the gap looks largest. LitQA2, SuppQA, DbQA and much of SeqQA are built to require retrieval, then handed to a model that's forbidden from retrieving. So "humans crush models" on those categories is partly an artifact of handicapping the models; the fair test — tool-augmented agents — is explicitly deferred to "the community." Tellingly, on the genuinely tool-free categories (FigQA, TableQA, ProtocolQA) the gap is smallest, and on TableQA it vanishes.

The second is the open-response collapse (above): MCQ precision systematically overstates capability, because distractor quality — not question difficulty — sets the ceiling. The authors deserve credit for surfacing this rather than burying it, but it means every multiple-choice number on this benchmark should be read as an upper bound, with the open-response figures as the real signal. The third is the human baseline itself: on Cloning Scenarios the experts often didn't think the 10–60-minute questions were worth their time, so the authors admit the human baseline there may be "unreasonably low." Any "model approaches human" reading on the hardest tasks is therefore unstable in both directions — models guess up, humans under-try down.

On contamination they did the right things — a 20% private hold-out, a canary string, and a public-vs-private parity check (Figure 6) showing every model scores within a 5% tolerance band on the two splits, so the public set isn't obviously memorized yet. The caveat is durability: 80% of the data lives on Hugging Face and the headline numbers are reported on the full set, so as that public split leaks into training corpora, full-set scores will drift upward artifactually and only the private 20% will stay trustworthy.

baselines

Models run tool-free; humans had internet + sequence software. The paper concedes the comparison is "not entirely equitable" — so the human-vs-model gap on the tool-dependent categories (LitQA2, SuppQA, DbQA, SeqQA) is partly a handicap artifact. The fair, agentic rematch is left to others.

contamination

Handled carefully: 20% private hold-out, a BIG-bench-superset canary, and a public/private parity check (<5% gap, Fig 6). But 80% is public on Hugging Face and headline numbers are on the full set, so only the private split stays meaningful as the public one leaks.

what's missed

MCQ precision overstates reasoning. Open-response reruns drop Cloning Scenarios to 0.20 accuracy, with the only correct answers being base-rate guesses (BsaI, Ampicillin). Distractor quality is the silent confound, and accuracy is partly a measure of prompt-induced refusal, not knowledge.

would raise confidence

Tool-augmented / agentic runs (the real use case); full human coverage (it's only 69%, and 35% on DbQA) with multiple raters; open-response grading on the hard categories as the default; and reporting on the private split once the public one saturates.

// bottom line

LAB-Bench's lasting contribution isn't the leaderboard — it's the methodology: practical research tasks over textbook recall, an explicit "decline to answer" option that separates knowing from guessing, and an open-response check that exposes how much multiple-choice flatters models. Read it as a careful, self-aware first map of where tool-free, mid-2024 frontier models fall short on real biology work — and treat its human-vs-model gaps as upper bounds awaiting the tool-augmented rematch the authors deliberately left open.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. LAB-Bench reframes science evals from textbook recall (MMLU, GPQA) to practical research work: 2,457 multiple-choice questions across literature search, figure/table reading, database lookup, protocol troubleshooting, and DNA/protein sequence and cloning tasks.
  2. Its core methodological move is the accuracy / precision / coverage split, enabled by an explicit "insufficient information" option — separating what a model knows from what it's willing to guess. Frontier models often refuse tool-dependent questions, tanking accuracy while precision stays above random.
  3. Multiple choice flatters models. The open-response rerun shows above-random precision comes from eliminating distractors, not reasoning — Cloning Scenarios fell to 0.20 accuracy, with "correct" answers being base-rate guesses. Distractor quality, not question difficulty, often sets the ceiling.
  4. It's a tool-free, mid-2024 snapshot: models ran without web/RAG while humans had tools, so the human-vs-model gap is an upper bound and the real test — tool-augmented agents — was left to the community. Humans still won most categories, but Claude 3.5 Sonnet matched or edged them on TableQA.
