Qwen2 Technical Report

Alibaba's second open LLM generation: five models spanning 0.5B to 72B parameters — four dense sizes plus a 57B-A14B Mixture-of-Experts — pre-trained on over 7 trillion tokens, extended to a 128K-token usable context via Grouped Query Attention, Dual Chunk Attention, and YARN, then aligned with SFT and a two-stage offline+online DPO pipeline. Qwen2-72B-Instruct beats Llama-3-70B-Instruct and Mixtral-8x22B-Instruct on MMLU-Pro, HumanEval, and Arena-Hard alike.

Qwen Team — An Yang, Baosong Yang, Binyuan Hui, Bo Zheng, Bowen Yu, Chang Zhou, Chengpeng Li, Chengyuan Li, Dayiheng Liu, Fei Huang, et al. (Alibaba)

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

By mid-2024 the open-weight race had accelerated past where Qwen (Bai et al., 2023a) left it ten months earlier. Llama 3 had "narrowed the performance gap with leading proprietary models" and was "widely acknowledged as GPT-4-level"; Claude 3 Opus and then GPT-4o traded the top spot on Chatbot Arena in quick succession; and Mistral's Mixtral-8x22B pushed sparse Mixture-of-Experts into the open-weight mainstream. Qwen1 had shown that a large, cleaned, bilingual corpus and a purpose-built 152K-vocabulary tokenizer could let a 14B model beat a 5×-larger LLaMA 2-70B on several benchmarks — but it trained on only 3 trillion tokens, natively handled just 2,048 tokens of context, and its interim successor Qwen1.5 had sprawled into an unwieldy eight-point size ladder (0.5B up to 110B, plus a 2.7B-active MoE) without a matching jump in pretraining scale.

Qwen2 is framed as the next full-scale iteration on three axes at once. Data: more than double the pretraining budget (3T → 7T tokens, with a 12T variant tested and explicitly rejected — see below), with a heavier skew toward code, mathematics, and ~30-language multilingual text. Context: stretch usable context 32× (4,096 → 131,072 tokens) using architecture-level tricks — Grouped Query Attention, Dual Chunk Attention, YARN — rather than simply training longer at high memory cost. Lineup discipline: rather than Qwen1.5's eight sizes, Qwen2 ships five deliberately chosen ones (0.5B / 1.5B / 7B / 72B dense, plus a fresh 57B-A14B MoE), dropping the 4B/14B/32B/110B middle tiers entirely — a gap Qwen2.5 would fill five months later. On alignment, the report's stated goal is scalable alignment with minimal human annotation: a recipe that has to align every point on that ladder, down to a 0.5B model meant to run on smartphones and earphones, without a proportional blow-up in human-labeled preference data the way Qwen1's PPO-based RLHF required.

// how it works

The method

Architecture. Qwen2 is four dense Transformer-decoder scales (0.5B, 1.5B, 7B, 72B) plus a 57B-A14B Mixture-of-Experts model (57B total parameters, 14B active per token), all sharing Qwen1's 152K-token byte-level BPE vocabulary (151,646 regular tokens after distributed-training padding). Table 1's hyperparameters run 24 layers/896 hidden at 0.5B up to 80 layers/8,192 hidden at 72B. Relative to Qwen1, the dense models swap standard multi-head attention for Grouped Query Attention (GQA) — 2–8 KV heads instead of one-per-query-head — which cuts KV-cache size and lifts inference throughput; SwiGLU activations, RoPE, QKV bias, and pre-norm RMSNorm all carry over unchanged from Qwen1. The MoE model is upcycled from the dense 7B checkpoint (Komatsuzaki et al., 2023-style): each fine-grained expert is built by replicating and reshuffling the dense FFN's parameters along the intermediate dimension, then randomly reinitializing 50% of each expert's weights for diversity, following DeepSeekMoE's shared+routed expert split rather than the coarse-grained Mixtral-style "split one FFN into 8" approach.

y = Σi∈topk(p) pi·Ei(x)p = softmax(G(x)) // Qwen2's MoE FFN: a gating network G scores every expert, the top-k (by probability p) are weighted-summed — plus always-on shared experts layered on top, per DeepSeekMoE's routing split

Pretraining data — a rare public "more data didn't help" result. The corpus grew to over 7 trillion tokens (up from Qwen1's 3T), filtered and partly synthesized using Qwen models themselves, with a heavier skew toward code, math, and ~30-language multilingual text. The team also built a 12-trillion-token variant by relaxing the quality filter further — and found "the model trained on this dataset did not show a significant performance improvement over the 7 trillion token model," concluding that "increasing the volume of data does not necessarily benefit model pre-training." They shipped the 7T corpus for every model except Qwen2-0.5B, which trains on the full 12T set (Table 1); the MoE model receives an additional 4.5T tokens of upcycling-stage pretraining on top of the 7B base's 7T.

Long-context extension. Pretraining runs mostly at a 4,096-token context, extended to 32,768 tokens only in the concluding phase, with the RoPE base frequency raised from 10,000 to 1,000,000 to suit the longer window. At inference time, stacking YARN (rescaling attention weights for length extrapolation) with Dual Chunk Attention (segmenting long sequences into chunks and modeling within/across-chunk position separately) pushes the usable context to 131,072 tokens — a 32× extrapolation over the extended training length — with "minimal perplexity degradation" reported in preliminary tests.

Post-training — SFT, then two-stage DPO. Supervised fine-tuning uses more than 500,000 examples across instruction-following, coding, math, logical reasoning, role-play, multilingual, and safety data, trained for two epochs at a 32,768-token sequence length with the learning rate decayed from 7×10⁻⁶ to 7×10⁻⁷. The instruction data itself comes from two pipelines: collaborative data annotation (InsTag extracts a fine-grained instruction ontology; instructions are then selected for tag diversity and complexity, "evolved" into harder variants via self-prompting, and multiple Qwen-scale responses are human-ranked into demonstration + preference pairs) and automated data synthesis (rejection sampling keeps only correct math/reasoning chains; coding and instruction-following data is validated by actually executing generated code and constraint-checking Python functions against the response; literary/role-play data is repurposed from public-domain works and Wikipedia character profiles; a "constitution" dataset drives safety-aligned vs. safety-violating response pairs). Reinforcement learning then runs in two stages: offline DPO on the pre-compiled preference set, then online DPO, where the current policy samples multiple responses per prompt and a reward model picks the best/worst to form fresh preference pairs each episode — with an Online Merging Optimizer folded in specifically to offset the "alignment tax" (capability loss from RLHF) on non-code/math tasks.

// what they found

The results

Base model, 70B+ tier (Table 2). Qwen2-72B posts 84.2 MMLU, 55.6 MMLU-Pro, 37.9 GPQA, 43.1 TheoremQA, 82.4 BBH, 64.6 HumanEval, 76.9 MBPP, 89.5 GSM8K, and 51.1 MATH — beating Llama-3-70B on MMLU (84.2 vs. 79.5) and MMLU-Pro (55.6 vs. 52.8), and beating its own predecessor Qwen1.5-72B by 18.3 points on HumanEval (64.6 vs. 46.3), 10.0 on GSM8K (89.5 vs. 79.5), and 17.0 on MATH (51.1 vs. 34.1). On Chinese benchmarks it reaches 91.0 C-Eval / 90.1 CMMLU, ahead of Mixtral-8x22B (54.6 / 53.4) and Llama-3-70B (65.2 / 67.2) by a wide margin.

Instruction-tuned, 70B+ tier (Table 6). Qwen2-72B-Instruct reaches 82.3 MMLU, 64.4 MMLU-Pro, 86.0 HumanEval, 69.0 MATH, 35.7 LiveCodeBench v1, 9.12 MT-Bench, 48.1 Arena-Hard, and 77.6 IFEval (strict-prompt) — ahead of Llama-3-70B-Instruct on MMLU-Pro (64.4 vs. 56.2), HumanEval (86.0 vs. 81.7), MATH (69.0 vs. 50.4), Arena-Hard (48.1 vs. 41.1), and IFEval (77.6 vs. 77.3), and ahead of Mixtral-8x22B-Instruct on every listed metric. Smaller sizes carry the pattern down: Qwen2-7B (base) hits 70.3 MMLU / 40.0 MMLU-Pro / 31.8 GPQA, ahead of Llama-3-8B (66.6 / 35.4 / 25.8) and Mistral-7B (64.2 / 30.9 / 24.7) on every one of those three.

Long context (Figure 1, NeedleBench). On the Needle-in-a-Haystack retrieval test, Qwen2-72B-Instruct and Qwen2-7B-Instruct hold near-100% retrieval accuracy across the full 8K–128K range, with only isolated yellow (partial-accuracy) cells at specific depth/length combinations for the smaller 57B-A14B, 1.5B, and 0.5B models — direct evidence the YARN+DCA extrapolation actually delivers usable long context, not just a nominal context-length number.

Safety (Table 14) and contamination (Table 15). On an in-house multilingual jailbreak-prompt safety eval (lower = better rate of harmful responses), Qwen2-72B-Instruct scores 0.00 illegal / 2.41 fraud / 22.91 pornography / 2.47 privacy — tying GPT-4 (0.00) on illegal and beating it on the other three (GPT-4: 3.40 / 23.63 / 3.37), and beating Mixtral-8x22B-Instruct (6.87 / 8.49 / 33.82 / 15.03) on all four. The contamination table is the paper's most striking self-disclosure: under a strict rule (any test sample sharing a 13-gram with the training data is "contaminated"), 75.0% of HumanEval, 37.7% of MultiPL-E, 31.7% of MATH, and 29.6% of MBPP test samples are flagged — versus under 12% for MMLU/MMLU-Pro and under 1% for GPQA/GSM8K/IFEval. Re-scoring on the non-contaminated subset only, the deltas are mostly small (≤2 points) except MATH (+5.6 for 72B-Instruct, +4.7 for 7B-Instruct) and HumanEval for the 7B model (79.9→87.8, +7.9) — the paper reads this as evidence that "the potential issue of data contamination does not significantly impact the model's performance," attributing most flagged overlaps to boilerplate code/math expressions rather than genuine leakage.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

Table 15 is the section worth reading most carefully, and it cuts both ways. It's genuinely more transparent than most contemporaneous model reports — an explicit, quantified contamination table is still rare in 2024 releases — but the numbers it discloses are, on their face, alarming: a strict 13-gram overlap check flags three-quarters of the HumanEval test set as present somewhere in Qwen2's pretraining or post-training data, along with over a third of MultiPL-E and nearly a third of MATH. The paper's defense is that "most of the identified contaminated samples are false positives," because short code idioms and common mathematical expressions trip a 13-gram matcher without conferring any real advantage — a plausible mechanism, but one asserted rather than demonstrated: there is no reported manual audit distinguishing genuine solution-level leakage from harmless boilerplate overlap within that 75%, and no external party has reviewed the flagged samples. The headline evidence offered instead is that the delta between "original" and "non-contaminated" scores is small — but by the report's own numbers that isn't uniformly true (MATH gains 4.7–5.6 points and 7B HumanEval gains 7.9 points after removing "contaminated" samples), and a positive delta after removing supposedly-leaked data is at least as odd as a negative one: real leakage should make the contaminated subset look artificially easy, not make removing it raise the average, which instead suggests the flagged/unflagged split correlates with something other than genuine test exposure (e.g. item difficulty) — exactly the kind of pattern that would benefit from the independent decontamination tooling (Golchin & Surdeanu's embedding-based method, cited in Qwen2's own bibliography, is one such alternative) the report doesn't apply here.

Elsewhere the evaluation follows 2024-era convention rather than pushing past it. The base-model comparison tables (2–5) mix "official results and OpenCompass" best-reported scores per baseline rather than a single re-run harness — standard practice, but it means each competitor's number was picked as its own best rather than measured under identical shot-count and prompting conventions. The safety table (14) and the multilingual human evaluation (13) are both in-house: the jailbreak prompt set, risk taxonomy, and response grading are Qwen-designed and (implicitly) Qwen-graded, with no inter-rater agreement statistic reported and no external red-team benchmark (HarmBench, AdvBench) run alongside it for comparison — the same "same team designs the test and grades the result" pattern flagged in Qwen1's tool-use benchmarks, just moved to the safety domain this time.

baselines

Current and reasonable (Llama-3-70B, Mixtral-8x22B, Qwen1.5-72B/110B for the 70B+ tier; Mistral-7B, Gemma-7B, Llama-3-8B at 7B), but pulled from each model's own best-reported or OpenCompass score rather than a single shared harness — shot-count and prompt-format conventions aren't guaranteed identical across rows.

contamination

An unusually explicit, quantified contamination table (13) — rare for the era — but it self-reports startlingly high overlap rates (75.0% HumanEval, 37.7% MultiPL-E, 31.7% MATH) that are waved off as "mostly false positives" without a disclosed manual audit, and the accompanying performance deltas are inconsistent with a clean "no impact" story (some rise by 5–8 points on the non-contaminated subset).

what's missed

No independent contamination-detection method is cross-checked against the in-house 13-gram/LCS rule; the safety eval (Table 14) and multilingual human eval (Table 13) are both self-collected and self-graded with no inter-rater agreement statistic or external benchmark comparison; and the exact per-domain composition of the 7T/12T pretraining corpus is never disclosed for outside audit.

would raise confidence

A manual (or third-party) audit of a sample of the flagged-contaminated HumanEval/MATH items, distinguishing genuine solution leakage from boilerplate n-gram matches; cross-checking the in-house decontamination against an independent method; and an external red-team safety benchmark run alongside Table 14's in-house jailbreak set.

// bottom line

The capability gains over Llama-3-70B and Mixtral-8x22B are real and consistent across coding, math, and Chinese-language benchmarks, and the pretraining-scale negative result (12T tokens not beating 7T) is a genuinely useful public data point most labs never publish. But treat the contamination story as unresolved rather than closed: a self-administered 13-gram check flagging 75% of HumanEval as "contaminated," explained away as mostly boilerplate without an independent audit, is a claim to verify — not a settled fact — before leaning on Qwen2's HumanEval/MATH numbers as clean measures of held-out coding and math ability.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. A public "more data didn't help" result. Qwen2's team built a looser-filtered 12-trillion-token corpus specifically to test whether more data would help, found it didn't beat the tighter 7T corpus at the target scale, and shipped 7T for every model except the 0.5B — a negative pretraining-scale finding most labs never disclose.
  2. GQA + DCA/YARN is the architecture checklist that stuck. Grouped Query Attention for cheap KV-cache inference, plus Dual Chunk Attention and YARN for training-cheap 128K-token context, is exactly the recipe Qwen2.5 (already in this graph) reuses five months later — this is the release where it was introduced into the Qwen line.
  3. The contamination table is candid in format, alarming in content. A self-published 13-gram overlap check flags 75.0% of HumanEval and 31.7% of MATH as "contaminated," dismissed as mostly boilerplate without an independent audit — read the HumanEval/MATH numbers as a claim to verify, not a settled measurement of held-out ability.
