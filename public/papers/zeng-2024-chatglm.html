ChatGLM: A Family of Large Language Models from GLM-130B to GLM-4 All Tools

Zhipu AI and Tsinghua's four-generation LLM family — GLM-130B through ChatGLM-6B/2/3 to GLM-4, GLM-4-Air, and GLM-4-9B — with the flagship GLM-4 (0520) beating GPT-4 (0613) on five of six English academic benchmarks, tying GPT-4 Turbo for the top AlignBench (Chinese) score, and a GLM-4 All Tools variant that autonomously plans, browses, and runs Python/CogView calls.

Team GLM — Aohan Zeng, Bin Xu, Bowen Wang, Chenhui Zhang, Da Yin, et al. (Zhipu AI / Tsinghua University)

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

By mid-2024 the "how we built a frontier LLM" genre was dominated by Western labs — OpenAI, Google, Meta, Anthropic — training and aligning primarily in English, with a fully agentic, tool-using flagship (GPT-4 All Tools / ChatGPT with plugins) available only behind a closed API. Outside China, there was no similarly detailed, similarly evolving public record of a lab building a competitive bilingual Chinese/English model line end-to-end: the base architecture, the pre-training data pipeline, the multi-stage alignment recipe, and the agentic tool-use layer on top. Zhipu AI and Tsinghua's own GLM line had been running in parallel since 2021 — GLM-10B, then the 130-billion-parameter GLM-130B research model in 2022 — but each generation's report was scattered across separate papers, leaving no single account of what actually changed release to release or how much of the gain came from architecture versus data versus alignment. This report consolidates that lineage — GLM-130B → ChatGLM-6B (Mar. 2023) → ChatGLM2-6B (Jun. 2023) → ChatGLM3-6B (Oct. 2023) → GLM-4 / GLM-4-Air / GLM-4-9B / GLM-4 All Tools (2024) — into one document, quantifying the compounding gains (MMLU climbs from 25.2 on ChatGLM-6B to 74.7 on the fully open GLM-4-9B) and positioning the closed flagship GLM-4 (0520) as a genuine contender against GPT-4-class models, with a specific claim to leadership on Chinese-language alignment (AlignBench) — a dimension Western frontier labs report on rarely, if at all.

// how it works

The method

The whole line is built on the GLM architecture (blank-infilling pretraining objective) the team introduced back in 2021, but GLM-4 itself is a fairly conventional decoder-only Transformer with four specific departures from the vanilla recipe: no bias terms except in the QKV projections (a small speed win that also slightly improved length extrapolation); RMSNorm and SwiGLU in place of LayerNorm and ReLU; 2D Rotary Position Embeddings extended to fit GLM's 2D positional scheme; and Group Query Attention (GQA) in place of multi-head attention to shrink the KV cache, with the freed parameter budget redirected into a larger FFN (dffn set to 10/3 of the hidden size) so total parameter count stays roughly constant. Context length was pushed in three jumps — 2K (ChatGLM) → 32K (ChatGLM2/3, via FlashAttention plus position-encoding extension) → 128K and an experimental 1M (GLM-4), the last achieved through the team's own LongAlign long-context alignment recipe rather than architecture alone.

dffn = 103 × dhidden // GQA frees attention parameters by sharing K/V heads; GLM-4 reinvests the savings into a wider FFN to hold total parameter count roughly fixed

Pre-training runs on roughly ten trillion tokens, mostly Chinese and English plus a smaller slice from 24 other languages, cleaned through exact-and-fuzzy deduplication and quality filtering, then tokenized with a byte-level BPE vocabulary of 150,000 that merges separately-learned Chinese and multilingual tokens with OpenAI's cl100k_base tokenizer. Post-training runs SFT followed by RLHF, and the team is explicit that the SFT data is authentic human-written prompt/response pairs rather than template- or model-generated ones — annotators score every response on safety, factuality, relevance, helpfulness, and human preference. A cluster of companion techniques feeds into this pipeline and is name-checked rather than re-derived here: ChatGLM-RLHF (their PPO/DPO alignment practice), Self-Contrast (a feedback-free alignment method that has the model generate its own negative samples instead of collecting human preference pairs), ChatGLM-Math (self-critique-driven math data selection), and AgentTuning/AgentInstruct (agent-trajectory instruction tuning).

On top of the aligned GLM-4 checkpoint sits a separate GLM-4 All Tools alignment stage that trains the model to autonomously decide when a request needs an external tool — a web browser, a Python interpreter, the CogView3 text-to-image model, or a user-defined function — plan a sequence of calls, execute them, and fold the results back into its response loop (Figure 4). The same all-tools substrate powers "GLMs," a user-facing platform for building custom agents on top of GLM-4 with their own knowledge bases and functions.

// what they found

The results

Across the report's own four generations, the gains compound: from ChatGLM-6B (2023-03-14) to ChatGLM2-6B (2023-06-25) to ChatGLM3-6B-Base (2023-10-27) to the fully open GLM-4-9B (2024-06-05), GSM8K climbs 1.5 → 25.9 → 72.3 → 84.0, MMLU climbs 25.2 → 45.2 → 61.4 → 74.7, HumanEval climbs 0.0 → 9.8 → 58.5 → 70.1, and the Chinese C-Eval benchmark climbs 23.7 → 51.7 → 69.0 → 77.1.

The headline comparison, though, is the closed flagship GLM-4 (0520) against GPT-4. Against the original GPT-4 (0613), GLM-4 actually comes out ahead on five of six academic benchmarks — GSM8K 93.3 vs. 92.0, MATH 61.3 vs. 52.9, BBH 84.7 vs. 83.1, GPQA 39.9 vs. 35.7, HumanEval 78.5 vs. 67.0 — and trails only on MMLU (83.3 vs. 86.4, which the paper itself frames as "96.3% of GPT-4's accuracy"). Against the more current GPT-4 Turbo (2024-04-09), the picture flips: GLM-4 trails on every one of the same six benchmarks (e.g. MATH 61.3 vs. 73.4, HumanEval 78.5 vs. 88.2). On IFEval instruction-following, GLM-4 reaches 99.0% and 98.6% of GPT-4 Turbo's instruction-level accuracy in English and Chinese respectively. On AlignBench — an LLM-as-judge benchmark for Chinese alignment scored by GPT-4 itself — GLM-4 (0520) posts an overall 8.00, tying GPT-4 Turbo (2024-04-09)'s 8.00 for the top score among all baselines tested (GPT-4 0613: 7.46; Claude 3 Opus: 7.53; Gemini 1.5 Pro: 7.47), with a particularly wide lead on the Logic dimension. On LongBench-Chat, GLM-4's English score of 87.3 sits between GPT-4 Turbo (1106)'s 87.2 and Claude 3 Opus's 87.7 — "matches," in the paper's own words.

On NaturalCodeBench — a real-user-prompt coding benchmark the team built specifically because HumanEval is known to be contamination-prone — GLM-4 (0520) posts an overall 47.1, behind Claude 3 Opus (48.3) and well behind both GPT-4 variants (52.8, 53.8). On the Berkeley Function Call Leaderboard, GLM-4 (0520) posts 81.76 overall, essentially matching GPT-4 Turbo (2024-04-09)'s 81.24. On AgentBench — a 7-environment agent benchmark the GLM team itself created — GLM-4 (0520) posts the highest overall score of any model tested, 3.79, versus GPT-4 (0613)'s 3.69, GPT-4 Turbo (1106)'s 3.77, and Claude 3 Opus's 3.62. And in the All Tools regime (Figure 2 shows a worked example), GLM-4 All Tools roughly matches GPT-4 All Tools on Python-interpreter math tasks (GSM8K 91.59 vs. 92.72, MATH 63.60 vs. 65.00) and clearly beats it on browser-based information seeking, 78.08 vs. 67.12.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

The report's most consequential fact is stated almost in passing: the model behind every headline table — GLM-4 (0520), the checkpoint compared against GPT-4, Claude 3 Opus, and Gemini 1.5 Pro — is not open-weight. Only ChatGLM-6B (three generations), GLM-4-9B (128K/1M), GLM-4V-9B, WebGLM, and CodeGeeX are released; GLM-4, GLM-4-Air, and GLM-4 All Tools are reachable solely through Zhipu's own API (bigmodel.cn) or web app (chatglm.cn). That means none of Tables 2–10 — the entire GPT-4/Claude/Gemini comparison — can be independently reproduced: an outside party cannot download the exact weights, re-run the exact prompts, or audit the exact decoding settings behind any of the GLM-4 (0520) numbers. The open GLM-4-9B is a materially weaker model (MMLU 74.7 vs. GLM-4's 83.3), so its openness doesn't transfer credibility to the flagship's head-to-head claims.

The baseline sourcing compounds this: the paper states competitor numbers were "extracted from the corresponding technical reports or tested through their public APIs" — a mix of self-reported figures the competitors chose to publish and numbers Zhipu generated itself, with no per-cell indication of which is which, and no shared prompting/decoding harness guaranteed across the two sources. The AlignBench comparison has a sharper version of the same problem: it's an LLM-as-judge benchmark scored by GPT-4, meaning the model GLM-4 is claimed to tie (GPT-4 Turbo, 8.00 vs. 8.00) is also the judge grading both submissions — a self-referential setup the report doesn't discuss or control for (e.g. via a second, non-GPT-4 judge). And GLM-4's single clearest win — leading every baseline on AgentBench's overall score (3.79 vs. GPT-4 0613's 3.69 and Claude 3 Opus's 3.62) — is a benchmark the GLM team designed and published itself (ref. [25]); its clearest loss, NaturalCodeBench, is a benchmark built specifically to resist the contamination that HumanEval is known to suffer from, and there GLM-4 (47.1) trails both GPT-4 variants (52.8, 53.8) by a wide margin.

On contamination specifically, the report is candid about the field's problem — it cites prior work finding "HumanEval-contaminated training data" in GPT-4 and other models as its explicit reason for introducing NaturalCodeBench — but never turns that lens on its own ~10-trillion-token pretraining corpus. There is no n-gram, embedding, or rephrased-sample decontamination table anywhere in the document for GLM-4's own MMLU/GSM8K/MATH/BBH/GPQA/IFEval/AlignBench numbers, which is a lower bar of transparency than sibling reports in this same graph (Qwen2, for instance, at least publishes a contested contamination table). Finally, the safety evaluation is run only on "the Chinese subset of SafetyBench, which is created by removing highly sensitive questions that tend to be censored" — a filtering choice applied identically to every model, so it isn't an unfair advantage, but it does mean the published 87.2 safety score describes a curated subset rather than the full official benchmark.

baselines

GPT-4/Claude/Gemini numbers are a mix of "extracted from the corresponding technical reports" and "tested through their public APIs," with no per-number attribution and no confirmation of a shared prompting/decoding harness across the two sources.

contamination

None disclosed for GLM-4's own ~10T-token training corpus, despite the report explicitly citing HumanEval contamination in other LLMs as its own motivation for building NaturalCodeBench — the lens is never turned inward.

what's missed

The flagship GLM-4 (0520) is API/web-only, not open-weight, so none of the GPT-4/Claude/Gemini comparison tables are independently reproducible; AlignBench is graded by GPT-4 itself, one of the models being compared; and GLM-4's best relative result (AgentBench) is on a benchmark its own team authored, while its worst (NaturalCodeBench) is on a competitor-built, contamination-resistant one.

would raise confidence

Independent (even research-only) access to run GLM-4 (0520) directly; a disclosed n-gram/embedding contamination audit of the training corpus against every eval set used; a non-GPT-4 judge cross-checking the AlignBench scores; and a third-party agent benchmark alongside AgentBench as a tiebreaker.

// bottom line

The open, independently-checkable part of the story — ChatGLM-6B → ChatGLM2-6B → ChatGLM3-6B → GLM-4-9B climbing from 25.2 to 74.7 MMLU and 1.5 to 84.0 GSM8K — is real and verifiable by anyone with the released weights. The "GLM-4 matches/beats GPT-4" headline is a different claim entirely: it rests on a closed model nobody outside Zhipu can rerun, baseline numbers pooled from two different sourcing methods, a tied Chinese-alignment score graded by one of the two models being compared, and a best-case win on the team's own benchmark — treat it as a vendor's technical-report presentation, not an independently reproducible result.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. A four-generation lineage compressed into one report — and the open half of it is independently verifiable. ChatGLM-6B → ChatGLM2-6B → ChatGLM3-6B-Base → GLM-4-9B is all open-weight, and its compounding gain is real: MMLU 25.2 → 45.2 → 61.4 → 74.7, GSM8K 1.5 → 25.9 → 72.3 → 84.0, HumanEval 0.0 → 9.8 → 58.5 → 70.1.
  2. GLM-4 All Tools is an early, clean template for the "agentic LLM." Plan → decide which tool(s) → recursively call and fold in feedback (Figure 4) — with a Python-interpreter/web-browser worked example (Figure 2) shipped alongside a user-facing "build your own agent" platform (GLMs) — anticipated a pattern every major lab has since converged on.
  3. The GPT-4-class comparison is not reproducible outside Zhipu. GLM-4 (0520), the model behind every headline number, is API/web-only — never open-weight — so the AgentBench win (a benchmark the GLM team built itself), the AlignBench tie (graded by GPT-4, one of the models being compared), and the total absence of a training-data contamination audit all have to be taken on trust rather than checked.
