The China Syndrome: Local Labor Market Effects of Import Competition in the United States

The reduced-form ancestor of the commuting-zone Bartik-IV design that later regional-shock papers — including Acemoglu & Restrepo's robots-and-jobs — would borrow wholesale: relating 1990–2007 changes across 722 U.S. commuting zones to their exposure to rising Chinese import competition, instrumented with the same industries' import growth into eight other high-income countries. A $1,000-per-worker rise in exposure cuts manufacturing's share of the working-age population by 0.60 percentage points, with no offsetting job growth anywhere else in the local economy; the authors estimate Chinese import competition explains up to 44% of the aggregate U.S. manufacturing employment decline between 1990 and 2007 (a more conservative 21% once isolating the supply-driven component) — on the order of 1.53 million manufacturing jobs.

David H. Autor (MIT) · David Dorn (CEMFI) · Gordon H. Hanson (UC San Diego)

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

Through the 1980s and 1990s, the trade-and-labor-markets literature was mostly a debate about U.S. wage inequality, and imports from low-wage countries were a minor character in it: in 1991, low-income countries accounted for just 2.9% of U.S. manufacturing imports, and the consensus explanation for rising wage dispersion pointed to skill-biased technical change, not trade (Katz and Autor, 1999). That changed with China. The low-income-country share of U.S. imports climbed from 15% in 2000 to 28% by 2007, with China responsible for 89% of the increase; the U.S. spending share on Chinese goods rose from 0.6% (1991) to 4.6% (2007), with a visible inflection at China's 2001 WTO accession. Over the same span, the fraction of the U.S. working-age population employed in manufacturing fell by a third, from 12.6% to 8.4%. Compounding the scale of the shock, China ran a current-account surplus averaging 5% of GDP through the 2000s — almost exactly matching the average U.S. current-account deficit — so U.S. industries faced a sustained rise in import competition with no offsetting rise in demand for U.S. exports to absorb it.

The trouble was that no existing empirical design was built to measure this at the right scale. National time series offer far too few observations to separate a trade shock from every other national-level confounder in the same decade. Industry- or occupation-level studies (Bernard, Jensen & Schott 2006; Liu & Trefler 2008) can show that import-exposed plants shrink, but they are silent on where those workers and wages end up once local labor and product markets adjust. And the dominant trade-theory approach — mapping industry product-price changes into "mandated" factor-price changes via a Heckscher-Ohlin model (Leamer 1993; Feenstra & Hanson 1999) — speaks only to wages, saying nothing about employment status, unemployment, or the public transfer programs (disability, unemployment insurance) that a labor-market shock this size would plausibly touch.

The paper's answer is to make the regional economy the unit of analysis: 722 U.S. commuting zones (CZs), covering the entire mainland, that differ enormously in how exposed they are to Chinese import growth because of differences in their 1990 industry mix — not primarily how much of a CZ's employment was in manufacturing overall (that explains under a quarter of the variation), but which manufacturing industries it specialized in, since China's comparative advantage is concentrated in specific labor-intensive lines (by 2007 China supplied over 40% of U.S. imports in four four-digit SIC industries and over 30% in 28 more, including apparel, textiles, furniture, and electrical appliances). That regional design solves the degrees-of-freedom problem — but only if the resulting cross-CZ variation in import exposure can be treated as exogenous to local labor demand, which is exactly what the paper's instrument is built to establish.

// how it works

The method

The paper starts from a small open-economy trade model (monopolistic competition, à la Helpman-Krugman) in which a U.S. region's wage and employment respond to two Chinese shocks: growth in China's export-supply capability in an industry (rising comparative advantage or falling trade costs) and growth in Chinese import demand for that industry's goods. Crucially, because the U.S. runs a large and persistent trade deficit with China, the model implies that these shocks can move the absolute size of a region's traded sector, not just reallocate workers between industries — the imbalance is what lets a regional import shock show up as a net regional employment effect rather than a wash.

The main measure of local exposure apportions realized U.S. imports from China to each commuting zone by its share of national industry employment:

ΔIPWuit = Σj LijtLujt · ΔMucjtLit // change in Chinese import exposure per worker in CZ i: industry j's share of CZ i's employment (Lijt/Lujt), weighted by industry j's realized U.S. import growth, normalized by CZ i's total employment

The obvious threat to this measure is that realized U.S. imports from China may themselves respond to U.S. industry-level demand or labor-supply shocks — a CZ specialized in an industry with rising local demand could show both rising "Chinese import exposure" and independently rising employment, biasing a naive regression toward zero, or the reverse. To purge this, the paper instruments realized U.S. import exposure with import exposure built the same way but using each industry's Chinese export growth to eight other high-income countries — Australia, Denmark, Finland, Germany, Japan, New Zealand, Spain, and Switzerland — apportioned using each CZ's industry employment shares from a decade earlier (to avoid contemporaneous simultaneity):

ΔIPWoit = Σj Lij,t−1Luj,t−1 · ΔMocjtLit−1 // the instrument: growth in industry j's Chinese imports into 8 other rich countries, apportioned by CZ i's lagged (10-year-earlier) industry employment shares

The identifying logic: the component of rising Chinese imports common to the U.S. and these eight other high-income economies is plausibly driven by China's own falling trade costs and rising productivity (its transition off central planning, WTO accession, opening to multinationals) rather than by anything happening inside U.S. labor markets. Correlated demand shocks across countries would bias the design toward understating the effect (attenuation); correlated technology shocks are harder to fully rule out, though the paper shows the estimate is stable to dropping housing-boom-correlated industries (steel, glass, cement: coefficient moves from −0.60 to −0.57), computers (moves to −0.68), and China's signature consumer-goods categories (apparel, footwear, textiles: moves to −0.51).

// what they found

The results

The fully-controlled 2SLS specification (Census division dummies plus demographic and occupational controls) puts the headline coefficient at −0.596 percentage points of manufacturing employment share per $1,000-per-worker rise in decadal import exposure (se 0.087) — roughly double the corresponding OLS estimate of −0.171 to −0.273, consistent with OLS being attenuated by both CZ-employment measurement error and demand-side simultaneity.

Benchmarked against the actual 1990–2007 decline: Chinese import exposure rose by $1,140 per worker in the 1990s and by a further $1,839 per worker (decadal terms) in 2000–2007, predicting manufacturing employment/population declines of 0.68 and 1.10 percentage points against the observed declines of 2.07 and 2.00 points in those decades. That implies Chinese import competition explains 33% of the 1990s decline, 55% of the 2000s decline, and 44% of the full-period decline — gross figures that attribute all of the variation in realized Chinese imports to supply-side (Chinese productivity/trade-cost) forces. Isolating just the ~48% of that variation the authors' OLS/2SLS decomposition attributes to the supply-driven component yields a more conservative 16% / 26% / 21% — in levels, a net reduction of roughly 548,000 manufacturing jobs in the 1990s and a further 982,000 in 2000–2007, on the order of 1.53 million manufacturing jobs total.

Strikingly, none of this shows up as measurable outmigration: in the full-controls specification, CZ working-age population shows no significant response to import exposure (a statistically insignificant −0.05 log points). With workers staying put, the manufacturing losses translate almost one-for-one into non-employment rather than being absorbed elsewhere in the local economy: a $1,000/worker shock cuts manufacturing employment by 4.2% (t=4.04) with no significant offsetting rise in non-manufacturing employment (a statistically insignificant −0.27%), while raising the numbers of unemployed and non-participating adults by 4.9% and 2.1% respectively.

Of the 0.77-point employment/population decline, roughly three-quarters is the direct manufacturing loss and the remainder is a (statistically insignificant) decline in non-manufacturing employment. Of the corresponding rise in non-employment, one-quarter shows up as measured unemployment and three-quarters as labor-force exit — with 9.9% of the workers who leave employment (0.076/0.77) landing on federal disability insurance (SSDI). The dollar increase in per-capita SSDI payments in exposed CZs runs more than 30 times the dollar increase in Trade Adjustment Assistance, the program explicitly designed to compensate trade-displaced workers — TAA is temporary, SSDI is (functionally) for life.

On wages, average CZ log weekly earnings fall 0.76 log points per $1,000/worker exposure, but the effect is concentrated entirely outside manufacturing: mean manufacturing wages show no significant effect at all (plausibly because lower-productivity workers are the ones who lose their manufacturing jobs, leaving a more durable, better-paid residual), while non-manufacturing wages fall a nearly identical 0.76 log points for both college and non-college workers — evidence the wage damage is a spillover onto local non-traded services (retail, construction, personal services) rather than a within-sector effect. Comparing a CZ at the 75th percentile of 2000–2007 import-growth exposure against one at the 25th: the more-exposed CZ would be expected to see a differential 4.5% fall in manufacturing employees, a 0.8-point fall in employment/population, a 0.8% fall in mean log weekly earnings, and 2–3.5% increases in per-capita unemployment, disability, and income-assistance transfers.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

This is the paper the graph's own Acemoglu & Restrepo robots-and-jobs page calls out by name: "the same commuting-zone design applied to Chinese import competition… implies a 2.5× larger local employment decline than robots produced over the same window." That's a testament to how completely this design became the template for regional-shock papers — and it means the identification concerns later raised about that instrument apply, in the first instance, here.

baselines

OLS and 2SLS point the same direction and 2SLS is larger, which is the opposite of upward-bias contamination — but the "control" side of the comparison (unexposed CZs) isn't a clean counterfactual either, since export-driven demand gains from the same China trade expansion can land in different CZs than the import losses, invisible to a design that only compares relative exposure.

contamination

Not leakage in the ML sense, but the econometric analogue: Goldsmith-Pinkham, Sorkin & Swift's (2020, AER) Rotemberg-weight diagnostic — the same tool later applied to Acemoglu-Restrepo's robot-adoption instrument — was run on this exact other-countries' import-growth instrument, and finds the growth-rate component explains only about 20% of the variance in Rotemberg weights. In their framing, the identifying assumption is better understood as resting on industry shares (which industries a CZ specializes in) than on the growth rates themselves — a subtler version of the single-industry concentration problem later diagnosed in the robots-and-jobs instrument.

what's missed

General-equilibrium channels a CZ-differencing design can't see by construction. Caliendo, Dvorkin & Parro (2019, Econometrica) build a full dynamic multi-region trade-and-migration model and, for 2000–2007, attribute about 0.55 million manufacturing job losses to the China shock — 16% of the observed decline, roughly half this paper's own supply-driven estimate of 982,000 jobs (26%) for the identical window — while finding the U.S. gains in the aggregate once consumer welfare is counted. Feenstra & Sasahara (2018) go further: using global input-output linkages over 1995–2011, they estimate Chinese import competition cost about 2.0 million jobs but U.S. export growth (driven substantially by services) created about 6.6 million, for a net gain of roughly 1.7 million jobs nationally — an offsetting channel a relative, cross-CZ comparison is not built to detect.

would raise confidence

The authors' own 2021 follow-up ("On the Persistence of the China Shock," Brookings Papers on Economic Activity) extends the sample to 2019 and finds the adverse CZ-level effects on manufacturing employment, employment/population, and income persisted rather than reversed — real evidence against a story where locally-measured effects are just transient reallocation. What would help further: a version of the Caliendo-Dvorkin-Parro GE reconciliation run on the exact 1990–2007 sample and specification here (not just 2000–2007), and an instrument whose Rotemberg-weight concentration has been independently re-audited since GPSS.

// bottom line

The local, relative finding — CZs more exposed to Chinese imports lost more manufacturing jobs, with the losses landing on non-employment and disability rolls rather than migration or wage adjustment — is well identified and has held up under a decade of scrutiny and a decade more of data. The national headline numbers built on top of it (one-quarter to 44% of the manufacturing decline, 1.53 million jobs) are a partial-equilibrium extrapolation from a relative-effects design, and general-equilibrium reassessments that model interregional and export-side offsets consistently land at smaller — sometimes much smaller, even net-positive — aggregate magnitudes. Treat the CZ coefficient as solid ground and the national jobs-lost number as the load-bearing assumption to interrogate, exactly the caution the graph's own robots-and-jobs page raises about the aggregate number built on top of its (descendant) design.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. Differential CZ exposure to Chinese imports — instrumented with the same industries' import growth into eight other high-income countries — cuts manufacturing employment share by 0.596 percentage points per $1,000/worker exposure, with no compensating job growth elsewhere locally; benchmarked against the U.S. manufacturing decline, that's 21–44% of it (1990–2007), on the order of 1.53 million jobs on the conservative, supply-driven cut. This is the exact commuting-zone Bartik-IV template Acemoglu & Restrepo's robots-and-jobs design later reused, finding a ~2.5× smaller local effect over the same window.
  2. Only about a quarter of the non-employment response is measured unemployment; three-quarters is labor-force exit, a meaningful share of it onto SSDI disability rolls (dollar increases over 30× the size of Trade Adjustment Assistance) — arguably the paper's most durable and most-cited non-wage finding.
  3. The instrument that once read as a clean natural experiment has since been formally re-audited (Goldsmith-Pinkham, Sorkin & Swift 2020) and embedded in full general-equilibrium trade models (Caliendo, Dvorkin & Parro 2019; Feenstra & Sasahara 2018) that roughly halve — or, once export-side job creation is counted, potentially reverse the sign of — the implied national jobs number, even as the underlying local CZ effect (re-confirmed by the authors themselves through 2019) has persisted.
