tinyBenchmarks: evaluating LLMs with fewer examples

MMLU has roughly 14,000 questions. tinyBenchmarks shows that 100 of them, chosen with item response theory, predict a model's full-MMLU accuracy within about 2% error — a 140x cut in evaluation cost, extended to the Open LLM Leaderboard, HELM, and AlpacaEval 2.0.

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

Modern LLM benchmarks are large by design — MMLU spans 57 subjects across roughly 14,000 questions, the Open LLM Leaderboard aggregates six such scenarios into roughly 29,000 examples, and HELM Lite runs 10 core scenarios over roughly 10,000 examples. Liang et al. (2022) report that evaluating a single LLM on the full HELM suite costs over 4,000 GPU-hours, or more than $10,000 in API fees; AlpacaEval needs a commercial LLM (GPT-4) as judge for every one of its 805 head-to-head comparisons. That cost isn't paid once — it's paid every time a lab checkpoints a model during pretraining, sweeps a prompt template, or evaluates yet another fine-tune.

Prior attempts to cut this cost existed but were narrow: Perlitz et al. (2023) showed HELM could be stratified-random-subsampled without losing much ranking fidelity, and Vivek et al.'s (2023) Anchor Points clustered examples by which models get them right, but only for classification-style tasks. Meanwhile, item response theory (IRT) — the statistical toolkit behind standardized tests like the GRE and SAT, which estimates a test-taker's latent ability from a carefully chosen subset of questions — had been used in NLP to rank models or study benchmark saturation, but, per the authors, "IRT has not been used for performance estimation in the context of efficient benchmarking of LLMs" before this work. No one had built a general, principled, cross-benchmark answer to "how few examples do I actually need, and how do I turn that small sample into an accurate performance estimate?"

// how it works

The method

The paper frames each benchmark as a set of scenarios (e.g., MMLU, HellaSwag), each scenario a set of examples Ij. The goal is to choose a small subset of examples Îj ⊂ Ij — plus per-example weights — such that a weighted average of a new model's correctness on just those items closely approximates its true average score over the entire scenario. Three strategies for choosing Îj are compared: stratified random sampling within sub-scenarios (adopting Perlitz et al.'s HELM approach); correctness clustering, which groups examples by the historical pattern of which "training" LLMs get each one right (a generalization of Vivek et al.'s Anchor Points beyond classification tasks); and IRT clustering, which fits a two-parameter multidimensional item response model — each example gets a difficulty term βi and a discrimination vector αi (dimension ≤16 in the experiments), each model gets a latent ability vector θl — and clusters on the fitted (αi, βi) embeddings.

Beyond just picking which examples to run, the authors use the fitted IRT model to sharpen the resulting estimate. The performance-IRT (p-IRT) estimator blends the raw observed correctness on the sampled anchor items with the IRT model's predicted correctness probabilities on every item the new model was not run on:

Ẑp-IRTjl = λ̂ · Σi∈Îj Yil|Îj| + (1−λ̂) · Σi∈Ij\Îj p̂il|Ij\Îj| // weighted blend of observed correctness on the sampled anchor items (Y) and IRT-predicted correctness on the unseen rest (p̂); λ̂ = |Îj|/|Ij|

A final generalized p-IRT (gp-IRT) estimator takes a convex combination of the raw weighted-average estimate and the p-IRT estimate, with a mixing weight chosen via a bias-variance heuristic (derived from a result in Song, 1988) that grows as the sampled subset grows — leaning on the low-variance raw estimate once enough real data is available, and on the IRT model's extrapolation when the sample is very small. The whole IRT model is fit once via variational inference over a hierarchical Bayesian specification, using point estimates (posterior means) for every model's and item's parameters; benchmarks with continuous scores (like AlpacaEval's preference rate) are binarized per-scenario via a threshold chosen to match the empirical mean, so the same machinery applies uniformly.

The strategies are evaluated on four benchmarks and their existing correctness records: the Open LLM Leaderboard (395 LLMs, 6 scenarios, ~29K examples, split 75/25 for training/testing anchor selection), MMLU (the same 395 LLMs and split, 57 subjects, ~14K examples), HELM Lite v1.0.0 (30 models, 10 core scenarios, ~10K examples, split by the organization that trained each model so, e.g., all OpenAI models land in the same fold), and AlpacaEval 2.0 (100 LLMs, 805 examples judged by GPT-4). Both a random train/test split and a "by date" split (testing only on the most recent models) are tried, the latter mimicking the realistic case of evaluating tomorrow's models with today's calibration.

// what they found

The results

The headline demonstration is on MMLU: evaluating a model on just 100 curated examples — under 1% of MMLU's ~14,000 — and applying gp-IRT ("IRT++") predicts that model's true full-MMLU accuracy within 1.9% average error for LLMs released between December 30 and January 18 (a genuinely held-out, later time window), with a Spearman rank correlation of rS=0.92 between predicted and true accuracy.

That result generalizes across all four benchmarks and both train/test splits: at 100 examples per scenario, every benchmark reaches roughly 2% average estimation error. For the Open LLM Leaderboard even 30 examples per scenario is enough — a 160x reduction in evaluation cost (29K → 180 examples across 6 scenarios); for MMLU alone the reduction is 140x (14K → 100). IRT and IRT++ (gp-IRT) are consistently the best or tied-best strategy across every benchmark and split tested, and gp-IRT "always improves or matches" plain IRT while adding only a few seconds of extra compute.

The method is also robust to a real distribution shift: tested on a hand-picked set of 40 domain-specialized LLMs (fine-tuned for code, biology, or finance) evaluated on MMLU, the correctness-clustering strategy's error inflates noticeably, while the IRT-based strategy is "only slightly affected" — evidence for the authors' choice to build the released tiny benchmarks on IRT anchors rather than correctness clustering. Across the 99 test LLMs evaluated with IRT++ on MMLU at 100 examples, per-model estimation error never exceeds 4%, except for one model with extremely low performance. The authors release tiny (100-example) versions of the Open LLM Leaderboard, MMLU, HELM, and AlpacaEval 2.0, plus the fitted gp-IRT tool, all runnable "on a CPU in a few seconds" — via a public GitHub repository, HuggingFace datasets (including tinyMMLU, tinyHellaswag, tinyWinogrande, tinyGSM8k, tinyAI2-ARC, and tinyAlpacaEval), and a Colab demo.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

The core empirical claim is well-supported: IRT-based anchor selection is tested against two real prior baselines (Perlitz et al.'s stratified random sampling and an adaptation of Vivek et al.'s Anchor Points), not strawmen, across four widely-used benchmarks and two train/test split regimes, and it wins or ties in essentially every setting. That's a meaningfully higher bar than most efficient-evaluation papers clear. Still, a few things temper how much a practitioner should trust the released artifacts going forward.

The released tinyBenchmarks are a frozen product of a fixed training pool — 395 pre-2024 LLMs for the Open LLM Leaderboard and MMLU, 30 models for HELM, 100 for AlpacaEval 2.0. Every future model scored against tinyMMLU is implicitly being measured against an IRT ability scale and a set of 100 anchor items calibrated on that pool. The authors' own Limitations section is candid about this: "a rapid increase in LLM capabilities may also cause extrapolation errors," and they recommend "periodically updating the curated examples and IRT parameter estimates using data from more modern LLMs" — but the paper describes no versioning or refresh mechanism, so a user downloading tinyMMLU today has no signal for how stale the calibration has become. Related but distinct: because the tiny subsets are small, fixed, and public, they're also a much more memorizable target than the full 14K-question MMLU — the paper never discusses the risk that a lab could (deliberately or not) curate training data that specifically covers these 100 items, inflating tiny-benchmark accuracy without the same effect on full-benchmark accuracy.

The only out-of-distribution stress test — the 40 specialized (code/biology/finance) LLMs — is run solely on MMLU. Whether IRT-based anchors hold up equally well under a comparable shift on HELM or, especially, AlpacaEval 2.0 isn't shown; AlpacaEval is judged by GPT-4's pairwise preferences, and a growing body of work on LLM-as-judge bias (e.g., judges rewarding answer length or position over substance) raises the question of whether a 100-example anchor subset preserves the judge's own bias profile as faithfully as it preserves raw accuracy — a question this paper doesn't ask. Finally, the headline "≈2% average error" is exactly that — an average with visibly wide error bars at low sample counts in Figure 2 (up to roughly 0.10 at N≈5–10) — and no per-model confidence interval is offered, so a practitioner evaluating one specific new model has only the method's aggregate historical track record to lean on, not a guarantee for that model.

baselines

Real and appropriately chosen: stratified random sampling (Perlitz et al., 2023) and a correctness-clustering adaptation of Anchor Points (Vivek et al., 2023) are both actually run as competing strategies, and IRT/IRT++ beats or ties them across all four benchmarks and both split regimes.

contamination

Not addressed. The tiny benchmarks are fixed, public 100-item subsets of already-public benchmarks — a smaller, more memorizable target than the full benchmark — and the paper doesn't discuss the risk of models being tuned or curated against exactly those items.

what's missed

Whether the frozen 2023-2024 IRT calibration still holds for today's frontier models beyond the one narrow by-date test window; whether anchor items preserve judge-side biases on AlpacaEval-style pairwise evaluation, not just raw accuracy; and per-model confidence intervals rather than aggregate averages.

would raise confidence

A periodic-refresh study showing tinyBenchmarks estimates stay accurate for models released well beyond the tested window, plus a specialized-LLM-style distribution-shift stress test on HELM and AlpacaEval 2.0, not only MMLU.

// bottom line

A methodologically serious result, validated against real baselines across four benchmarks: IRT-based anchor selection cuts MMLU evaluation cost roughly 140x for about 2% average error. But the released tinyBenchmarks are a frozen artifact calibrated on a fixed pre-2024 model pool, the paper's own robustness check covers only MMLU, and it never examines the elevated memorization risk of its own much-smaller, much more memorizable item sets.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. 100 curated examples (of MMLU's ~14,000) predict a model's full-MMLU accuracy within 1.9% average error on a genuinely held-out, later time split — a 140x reduction in evaluation cost.
  2. IRT-based anchor selection ("IRT"/"IRT++") consistently matches or beats both a stratified-random-sampling baseline and a correctness-clustering baseline across all four benchmarks tested: Open LLM Leaderboard, MMLU, HELM, and AlpacaEval 2.0.
  3. The method survives a real distribution shift better than its main competitor: on 40 domain-specialized LLMs evaluated on MMLU, IRT-based anchors are "only slightly affected" while correctness-clustering degrades noticeably.
  4. The released tools (tiny 100-example versions of the Open LLM Leaderboard, MMLU, HELM, and AlpacaEval 2.0, plus the gp-IRT estimator) are calibrated on a fixed, frozen pool of pre-2024 models — the authors themselves warn that rapid capability growth "may... cause extrapolation errors," so periodic recalibration matters for anyone applying these tools to today's models.
