Assessing Robustness to Spurious Correlations in Post-Training Language Models

When training data is laced with shortcut features, does the alignment objective matter? A controlled study injects spurious cues into SFT, DPO, and KTO training sets and finds no universal winner — preference methods resist shortcuts on math reasoning, plain SFT holds up better on context-heavy QA, and 90% contamination doesn't always hurt.

Julia Shuieh, Prasann Singhal, Apaar Shanker, John Heyer, George Pu, Samuel Denton · Scale AI
venue arXiv (ICLR 2025 SCSL Workshop) citations 6 topic Post-training & RL source ↗

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

NLP datasets are full of shortcuts — surface features that correlate with the right answer in the training set but have nothing to do with why it's right. The classic demonstration is HANS (McCoy et al., 2019), where NLI models that looked strong turned out to be keying on lexical overlap rather than entailment. Post-training data is no exception: a curated preference set can quietly encode that "longer answers win," "answers with a date are correct," or "the gold response always uses a certain phrase." When a model latches onto these, it scores well on the contaminated distribution and quietly fails the moment the cue disappears.

The alignment literature has documented one flavor of this in detail — length over-optimization, where RLHF and DPO inflate response length as a proxy for quality (Singhal et al., 2024; Park et al., 2024) — and has built debiased evaluators (length-controlled AlpacaEval) and reward-model probes (RewardBench) in response. But a more basic question went unasked: given the same contaminated data, does the choice of post-training objective change how badly the model gets fooled? Supervised fine-tuning imitates a gold response token-by-token; preference methods like DPO and KTO instead learn from a contrast between a chosen and a rejected answer. Those are very different ways to absorb a shortcut, yet they're rarely compared head-to-head under controlled contamination.

This paper builds exactly that controlled comparison. It injects known shortcuts at known rates into three task families, trains SFT / DPO / KTO on each, and measures how much each objective's out-of-distribution accuracy decays. The deliverable is not a new method but a map: which objective is more shortcut-robust, and under what kind of contamination.

// how it works

The method

The design is a controlled factorial sweep. Three task families stand in for different alignment regimes: document-grounded QA (docQA, built from passages and questions in QuAC), mathematical reasoning (math, from GSM8K), and constrained instruction-following (instruction, inspired by the CoLLIE constraint-generation benchmark). Into each, the authors inject spurious features two ways:

  • Feature Ambiguity (FA) — every chosen response carries an extra, unnecessary token or pattern (an irrelevant date, a keyword like "the"/"in", a fixed final word) that no rejected response has. The correlation "token ⇒ correct" is perfect, tempting the model to treat the cue as the signal.
  • Distributional Narrowness (DN) — the correct answers are squeezed into an artificially narrow slice of the real distribution (all math answers forced into the range 1–5; all docQA answers set to "no answer"; instruction outputs limited to a 3-word vocabulary). The model can win on the narrow training set by collapsing onto it.

Each spurious pattern is mixed in at two contamination ratios — 10% vs. 90% — to contrast mild and near-total contamination. Nine patterns in all (four docQA, two math, three instruction). Models are the open-weight Llama-3.1-70B, Llama-3.1-8B, and Llama-3.2-3B "Instruct" variants, each post-trained with one of three objectives. Crucially, all evaluation is on a wider distribution than training, so a model that has overfit a shortcut is exposed.

The three objectives

SFT simply maximizes the likelihood of the gold (chosen) response — it never sees the rejected one, so any pattern in the chosen set is imitated directly. DPO and KTO instead optimize a contrast. DPO turns a reward model into a closed-form classification loss over preference pairs:

ℒDPO = −log σ( β·log πθ(yw|x)πref(yw|x) − β·log πθ(yl|x)πref(yl|x) ) // yw = chosen, yl = rejected; β controls divergence from the reference policy πref

KTO (Kahneman–Tversky Optimization) drops the need for paired data, scoring each example by a prospect-theory value of how its implied reward compares to a reference point — gains and losses weighted asymmetrically. Both preference objectives therefore learn from the gap between chosen and rejected, which is where the difference in shortcut behavior comes from: when the shortcut lives only in the chosen set, the contrast can amplify it.

Training budgets are not matched across objectives — a detail that matters later. SFT runs 3 epochs at learning rate 2e-5; DPO runs 1 epoch at 3e-6 with β = 0.1 and an auxiliary NLL term (coefficient 0.2); KTO runs 1 epoch at 5e-6 with β = 0.1. All use a global batch size of 512 and max length 1024.

// what they found

The results

The headline is a clean dissociation by task family, and it cuts the opposite way in math vs. docQA.

Preference methods win on math

On the two math tasks, DPO and KTO dominate SFT by a wide margin. On word-inclusion bias, KTO averages 0.802 → 0.807 (10% → 90%) and DPO 0.775 → 0.781, while SFT sits at 0.551 → 0.567 — roughly a 24-point gap that barely moves with contamination. On restricted-range, KTO is 0.800 → 0.751 and DPO 0.787 → 0.779 against SFT's 0.538 → 0.643. Note the direction: here SFT actually improves as contamination rises, and the preference methods dip slightly — the opposite of the naive "more shortcut ⇒ worse" intuition.

SFT wins on context-heavy QA

On docQA the ranking flips: SFT preserves correctness better, scoring ≈0.40–0.48 against ≈0.30–0.34 for DPO/KTO on word-inclusion (SFT 0.466 → 0.469 vs DPO 0.305 → 0.307, KTO 0.311 → 0.306), date (SFT 0.399 → 0.423), and late-spurious (SFT 0.439 → 0.320). The authors read this as cross-entropy on the full gold answer forcing the model to attend to context, where the pairwise objectives over-weight whatever token happens to separate chosen from rejected.

The dramatic exception is the omission (DN) task, where all chosen answers are "no answer." At 90% contamination SFT collapses from 0.465 to 0.108 — with the 70B model worst-hit (0.472 → 0.070). Here DPO is the most robust (0.309 → 0.284), inverting the docQA story: when the shortcut is "always abstain," imitation learning swallows it whole while the preference contrast resists it.

Instruction-following is on the floor

Two of the three instruction tasks are barely learnable at all: on max-words-vs-all-ends and not-in-vs-all-ends, almost every method scores ≈0.00 at both ratios, the lone exception being DPO (driven by the 3B model reaching 0.340 on not-in at 10%). Only tiny-constraints is in a meaningful range (SFT 0.385 → 0.329, DPO 0.401 → 0.371, KTO 0.384 → 0.399), and there contamination has little consistent effect.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

This is an honest, exploratory workshop study (ICLR 2025 SCSL workshop), and it reads like one — its value is mapping the question, not settling it. The central claim ("no objective universally wins; it depends on task and shortcut type") is genuinely supported by the dissociation in Figures 1–2. But several design choices make the magnitudes — and some of the secondary claims — hard to bank.

The biggest gap is statistical: every cell is a single run. There are 162 checkpoints but no seeds, no error bars, no variance estimate. Many shifts the paper narrates as signal — SFT math "rising" 0.538 → 0.643, or docQA word-inclusion "stable" at 0.466 → 0.469 — are within plausible single-run noise, yet read as effects. With one run per setting you cannot separate a real 90%-helps result from variance, and the paper's own hedged "larger models may be more robust (although inconclusive)" is contradicted by its data (omission 90%: the 70B SFT model is worst at 0.070, below the 3B's 0.122).

The second is that the headline SFT-vs-preference comparison is confounded by training budget. SFT gets 3 epochs at lr 2e-5; DPO/KTO get 1 epoch at ~4–7× lower learning rates, and DPO carries an extra NLL term that KTO doesn't. A single β = 0.1 is used for every preference run with no per-task tuning. So "SFT wins docQA / preference wins math" partly reflects unequal optimization, not just the objective. The cleaner experiment would match compute or ablate epochs/LR — and would add a 0% (clean) control plus intermediate ratios so "degrades from 10→90" sits on a dose–response curve rather than two contaminated points.

Third, the docQA numbers — the cleanest "SFT wins" story — rest on the least trustworthy metric: GPT-4o-mini as correctness judge, with no human-agreement check. A judge can itself be swayed by the very surface features (dates, keywords) being manipulated, which would inflate exactly the FA conditions. And because math is evaluated on a transformed GSM8K (e.g., "divide by 20"), the ~0.54 SFT accuracy isn't comparable to standard GSM8K leaderboards — and with no no-finetune baseline, we can't tell "spurious data hurt" from "fine-tuning on a tiny synthetic set hurt regardless."

baselines

No clean-data (0% spurious) control and no no-finetune baseline, so degradation can't be referenced to an uncontaminated point. Objectives compared at unequal epochs/LR (SFT 3ep@2e-5 vs DPO/KTO 1ep@3–5e-6), confounding objective with budget.

contamination

Shortcuts are hand-injected and extreme (90% = near-perfect correlation), far more salient than real dataset artifacts; only 10% and 90% are tested, with no intermediate dose–response.

what's missed

Single run per cell — no seeds or CIs, so small shifts may be noise. docQA "accuracy" is an unvalidated GPT-4o-mini judgment. Accuracy is the only readout; the paper never directly measures how often the spurious token appears in outputs, the most direct evidence of shortcut reliance. Two of three instruction tasks sit at ~0, carrying no signal.

would raise confidence

Multiple seeds + error bars; a 0% control and a ratio sweep; budget-matched objectives (or an epoch/LR ablation); human validation of the judge; at least one non-Llama family; and a direct shortcut-usage metric alongside end-task accuracy.

// bottom line

A useful, well-scoped negative result: the post-training objective does interact with shortcut robustness, and the interaction is task-dependent rather than monotone — preference methods are sturdier on structured math, SFT on context-heavy QA, except when the shortcut is "always abstain," which flips the order. Treat it as a hypothesis-generating map. The specific numbers are single-run and budget-confounded, so the qualitative dissociation is the part to carry forward, not the decimals.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. The alignment objective is a robustness knob. Given identically contaminated data, SFT and preference methods (DPO/KTO) absorb shortcuts differently — so "which post-training method" is also a question about how the model will fail under biased data, not just how well it does on clean data.
  2. The robustness ranking is task-dependent and can invert. DPO/KTO beat SFT by ~24 points on math reasoning; SFT beats them on context-heavy docQA — except the "always abstain" omission shortcut, where SFT collapses (0.465 → 0.108) and DPO is steadiest. No single objective dominates.
  3. More contamination isn't always worse. Several settings are flat or improve from 10% → 90% (SFT math restricted-range 0.538 → 0.643), so real-world data bias doesn't guarantee catastrophic failure — but the single-run, judge-graded, budget-unmatched setup means treat the magnitudes as directional, not definitive.
