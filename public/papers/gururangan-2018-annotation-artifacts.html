Annotation Artifacts in Natural Language Inference Data

A classifier that never sees the premise — only the hypothesis sentence a crowdworker wrote — correctly labels 67% of SNLI and about 53% of MultiNLI, exposing lexical giveaways (negation words scream “contradiction”) baked into the annotation protocol itself.

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

By early 2018, SNLI and MultiNLI had become the field's default proving ground for natural language inference: give a model a premise and a hypothesis, ask whether the premise entails, contradicts, or is neutral toward the hypothesis, and a growing zoo of neural architectures (decomposable attention, ESIM, DIIN) was steadily closing in on human-level accuracy. Both datasets were built the same clever way: crowdworkers see a premise (an image caption, in SNLI's case) and are asked to write three new sentences from it — one that must be true given the premise (entailment), one that might be true (neutral), and one that must be false (contradiction). This elicitation protocol is what let Bowman et al. collect 570k labeled pairs cheaply, and it is exactly the protocol this paper puts under the microscope.

The authors' question is blunt: is a state-of-the-art NLI model actually reasoning about the relationship between two sentences, or has it learned to detect surface patterns that crowdworkers unintentionally leave behind when asked to invent three different sentences from one premise, under time pressure, over and over? If humans generating hypotheses gravitate toward the same shortcuts — deleting a noun to make an entailment, adding a modifier to make something neutral, inserting a negation to make a contradiction — then a label can become predictable from the hypothesis alone, with the premise contributing nothing. That would mean a big chunk of an NLI leaderboard score is measuring pattern-matching against annotator habits rather than the inference task the benchmark claims to test, and no standard accuracy number can tell the difference.

// how it works

The method

The diagnostic is deliberately simple: train a text classifier to predict the entailment label using only the hypothesis sentence, with the premise thrown away entirely. The classifier is fastText (Joulin et al., 2017) — an off-the-shelf, linear bag-of-words-and-bigrams model, with character 4-grams and a min-frequency-10 vocabulary filter added for MultiNLI. If this premise-oblivious model scores meaningfully above the most-frequent-class baseline, that gap is not modeling inference — a model with zero access to the premise cannot, by construction, reason about entailment — it is evidence that the hypothesis alone leaks the label.

To characterize why the leak exists, the authors run a shallow statistical audit of the training data along two axes. First, lexical choice: for every word and every class they compute pointwise mutual information, PMI(word, class), with aggressive add-100 smoothing so the ranking foregrounds words that are both frequent and disproportionately class-specific rather than rare one-off co-occurrences.

PMI(word, class)  =  log p(word, class)p(word, ·) · p(·, class) // ranked per class after add-100 smoothing; Table 4 shows each class's top-5 words

Second, sentence length: they compare the token-count distribution of hypotheses across the three classes, on the theory that crowdworkers' generation strategies (deleting information vs. adding a purpose clause vs. inserting a negation) don't just change word choice, they change how long the resulting sentence tends to be.

Finally, to see whether full NLI models actually exploit this leak rather than just co-existing with it, the paper partitions each test set in two: examples the premise-oblivious fastText classifier gets right are labeled Easy, and everything it misses is labeled Hard. Three published, high-performing NLI architectures — the Decomposable Attention Model (DAM), ESIM, and DIIN — are then retrained out of the box on the ordinary training sets and evaluated separately on the Full test set, the Hard subset, and the Easy subset. If a model's accuracy gap between Easy and Hard is large, its aggregate leaderboard score is substantially propped up by artifact-exploitable examples rather than uniform inference skill.

// what they found

The results

The headline number: a hypothesis-only fastText classifier reaches 67.0% accuracy on SNLI, far above the 34.3% majority-class floor, and 53.9% / 52.3% on MultiNLI's matched and mismatched test sets, against a roughly 35% floor on each — a footnote reports that swapping in a logistic-regression n-gram classifier or a premise-oblivious decomposable-attention model gives similar numbers, so the effect isn't an artifact of picking a weak model. That is, more than half of MultiNLI and roughly two-thirds of SNLI can be labeled correctly by a linear classifier that has never seen the premise sentence at all.

The PMI and length analyses (figure above) trace the leak to specific crowdworker heuristics: negation words (nobody, no, never, nothing, none) are strong contradiction tells. Modifiers and superlatives (tall, sad, first, favorite, most) mark neutral, often via added purpose/cause clauses (because) — the paper traces this directly to its own SNLI annotation-guideline example (Table 3), whose entailment sentence generalizes specific words into generic ones and whose neutral sentence adds a purpose clause; that same guideline example's contradiction sentence, notably, is built without any negation at all. Length is discriminative on its own: the median neutral hypothesis in SNLI runs 9 tokens, while 60% of entailed hypotheses have 7 tokens or fewer, and half of all hypotheses of 12+ tokens are neutral.

The Hard/Easy split is where the artifact leak is shown to actually distort model evaluation, not just co-exist with it. Every one of the three retrained NLI models loses 20–30 accuracy points moving from Easy to Hard, on every test set:

The same pattern holds on both MultiNLI splits. The authors release the Hard SNLI and MultiNLI (matched + mismatched) test subsets publicly, explicitly so the community can evaluate future models on the artifact-resistant slice rather than the full, artifact-inflated one.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

This paper belongs to the same small, load-bearing family as Torralba & Efros's dataset-bias classifier and Jia & Liang's adversarial SQuAD attack: it doesn't propose a new model, it proposes a diagnostic for whether a benchmark's headline number means what everyone assumes it means. Its own methodology is disarmingly cheap — a linear bag-of-words classifier and a PMI table — and that is exactly the point: if a shortcut this simple can find 67% of the signal, no fancy neural model is required to exploit it either, which is precisely what the DAM/ESIM/DIIN Hard-vs-Easy gap goes on to confirm.

The Hard/Easy partitioning is the paper's strongest piece of evidence, because it moves past correlation (these words appear disproportionately often) to a causal-flavored claim about existing models (these three independently-published, out-of-the-box architectures all do 20–30 points worse specifically on the examples the artifact detector flags as hard). That's a meaningfully stronger result than the PMI table alone, and it's presented for three different architectures rather than one, which guards against the result being an accident of DIIN's or ESIM's particular inductive bias.

The paper is also unusually honest about the limits of its own fix. In the Discussion, the authors note that simply filtering Easy examples out of a dataset doesn't eliminate artifacts, it relocates them: strip every contradiction containing “no” and the remaining Hard set now has “no” skewed toward neutral and entailment instead, a fresh artifact created by the very act of artifact removal. That whack-a-mole observation, made in 2018, anticipated a problem the field would spend years rediscovering — that curating a benchmark against one known shortcut tends to encode a new, subtler one, which is the core argument later made for adversarial-filtering benchmarks (this graph's HellaSwag runs exactly that loop: a discriminator model iteratively removes exploitable endings rather than relying on one static Hard/Easy split) and for dynamic, continuously-refreshed evaluation.

baselines

Deliberately weak by design (linear fastText bag-of-words/bigrams) so the result reads as a floor, not a ceiling — and the paper reports (without full numbers, in a footnote) that a logistic-regression n-gram model and a premise-oblivious decomposable-attention model land in a similar range, guarding against "we just picked a lucky classifier." The Hard/Easy re-evaluation then checks the finding against three genuinely strong, independently-authored NLI architectures (DAM, ESIM, DIIN) rather than a toy model.

contamination

Not test-set leakage in the modern sense — this is annotator-protocol leakage, baked into training and test data alike by the same elicitation instructions. The paper's own Discussion flags the closest thing to a mitigation trap: removing the Easy/artifact-laden examples from a dataset doesn't remove the artifacts, it just concentrates a new, subtler one in what's left (e.g., "no" flips from a contradiction tell to a neutral/entailment tell once the obvious contradiction cases are filtered out).

what's missed

Everything here is pre-BERT (this paper predates BERT by about seven months) and pre-contextual-embedding, so it cannot speak to whether large-scale pretraining shrinks, relocates, or has no effect on hypothesis-only exploitability. The paper also doesn't quantify how much of DAM/ESIM/DIIN's Full-test accuracy gain over a hypothesis-only baseline is genuine compositional reasoning versus a second-order artifact the deeper models learned that fastText simply can't represent (e.g. syntactic patterns, not just unigrams/bigrams).

would raise confidence

Re-running the identical hypothesis-only + Hard/Easy protocol against contextual and pretrained models (BERT-era and later) to see whether the ~67%/~53% ceiling moved, and whether the Hard-set gap shrank; concurrent work by Poliak et al. (2018, already in this queue) ran the same hypothesis-only diagnostic across a wider set of NLI datasets and is the natural companion reading here.

// bottom line

A hypothesis-only classifier that never sees the premise correctly labels roughly two-thirds of SNLI and half of MultiNLI, and the field's best 2018 NLI models lose 20–30 accuracy points specifically on the subset that classifier can't already solve — direct evidence that a chunk of the leaderboard was measuring pattern-matching against crowdworker habits, not entailment. The diagnostic (train on the incomplete input, see how far above chance you get) became a standard eval-rigor move applied well beyond NLI, and its own Discussion section already previewed the harder lesson that later adversarial-filtering and dynamic benchmarks (HellaSwag among them) were built to address: removing one artifact tends to just relocate the next one.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. The premise-oblivious baseline. Train a classifier on only part of the input (here, the hypothesis with no premise) and see how far above the majority-class floor it scores. A linear fastText model hits 67.0% on SNLI and 53.9%/52.3% on MultiNLI matched/mismatched — against a ~35% floor — despite having no access to the sentence pair relationship the task is supposedly about.
  2. Crowdsourcing leaves a fingerprint. Negation words (no, never, nothing, nobody, none) are strong contradiction tells; modifiers and superlatives (tall, sad, favorite, most) mark neutral; hypothesis length itself is discriminative (SNLI neutral median 9 tokens vs. 60% of entailments at 7 tokens or fewer). These are traceable to specific crowdworker generation heuristics, not random noise.
  3. The artifact isn't just detected, it's exploited. Splitting each test set into Hard (the artifact-detector gets it wrong) and Easy (it gets it right) subsets, three independently-built, high-performing NLI models (DAM, ESIM, DIIN) all lose 20–30 accuracy points on Hard versus Easy — a gap invisible in the single Full-test-set number every leaderboard reports, and the paper's own warning that filtering out Easy examples just relocates the artifact rather than removing it anticipated the case for adversarial-filtering and dynamic benchmarks that followed.
