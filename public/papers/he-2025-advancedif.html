AdvancedIF: Rubric-Based Benchmarking and RL for Advanced Instruction Following

Meta builds a 1,645-prompt instruction-following benchmark where every prompt and rubric is written and reviewed by human experts — frontier models top out around 75–78% — then reuses the same rubric machinery as a reinforcement-learning reward (RIFL) that lifts Llama 4 Maverick +6.7 points.

He, Li, Zhang, Mandyam, Khosla, … Awadalla, Faruqui · Meta Superintelligence Labs (w/ Princeton, CMU)

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

Instruction following is the most load-bearing capability an LLM has — it is what lets a model translate user intent into the right output and generalize to tasks it was never trained on. Frontier models nail simple, single-instruction prompts. They still stumble on advanced instruction following: prompts that pile up six or more constraints at once, instructions that are carried across many turns of a conversation, and rules planted in a system prompt that must hold for the whole session.

Two things make this hard to fix. First, evaluation is weak. Most existing instruction-following benchmarks lean on either LLM-generated prompts or LLM-generated rubrics, so they don't faithfully reflect how real people phrase layered, multi-turn requests. Second, training is harder still. Reinforcement Learning with Verifiable Rewards (RLVR) transformed math and code because a grader can just run unit tests or string-match an answer — but you cannot string-match "did the response follow all of the user's instructions?" Training a scalar reward model from preference pairs is the usual fallback, but it needs vast preference data and produces an opaque signal that is notoriously easy to reward-hack.

The paper's bet: rubrics are the right interface for both problems. A complex instruction naturally decomposes into a checklist of simple, individually-checkable criteria — exactly the structure a benchmark needs for a faithful score, and exactly the verifiable signal RLVR needs for a reward. AdvancedIF is the benchmark; RIFL is the training pipeline that turns the same rubrics into rewards.

// how it works

The method

AdvancedIF — the benchmark

AdvancedIF holds 1,645 prompts, each paired with a rubric of up to 20 criteria, split into three capability families (all prompts and rubrics created by human experts through the vendor Surge):

  • Complex IF (CIF) — 402 single-turn dialogs (avg 7.44 criteria). Each prompt carries 6+ instructions combining tone, format, style, structure, length, negative constraints, spelling, and inter-conditional rules.
  • Multi-turn Carried Context (CC) — 736 dialogs (avg 6.08 criteria, 7.69 turns). Constraints set earlier in the conversation must still be honored at the final turn.
  • System Prompt Steerability (SS) — 507 dialogs (avg 9.81 criteria, 11.21 turns). Rules live in the system prompt — response style, safety, product/voice/tool-use specs.

Two design choices set it apart. Prompts are collected adversarially — annotators only keep prompts that actually trigger an instruction-following failure in the model's final-turn response, so the benchmark is hard by construction. And in a side-by-side of eight verifiable and rubric-based benchmarks (its Table 2), AdvancedIF is the only one that is simultaneously human-prompted, human-rubric-written, multi-turn, and system-prompt-aware. Grading uses an off-the-shelf reasoning model, o3-mini, to judge each criterion TRUE/False; a response counts for a category only if it satisfies all of that prompt's criteria.

RIFL — rubrics as a reward

RIFL (Rubric-based Instruction-Following Learning) is a full-stack post-training pipeline with three trained parts. The RL objective is the standard KL-regularized one — maximize expected reward while staying close to the reference policy — but the reward R(q,o,r) now depends on a rubric r and is produced by a learned verifier rather than a script.

1 · Rubric generator. Human rubrics don't scale to training data, so they fine-tune a Llama 4 Maverick (one SFT epoch on thousands of expert rubrics) to synthesize a rubric for any prompt. Measured by semantic precision/recall against held-out human rubrics, it reaches F1 0.790, up from 0.639 for the starting checkpoint.

2 · Rubric verifier. Naively prompting an off-the-shelf LLM as the judge is unreliable, so they train one. A golden set of human rubric-by-rubric evaluations (each with a chain-of-thought justification) drives a two-stage recipe: SFT (~5k prompts) to cold-start the judge, then RL (~14k prompts) where the verifier scores each criterion and its reward is the agreement ratio with the expert's binary labels — itself an RLVR problem because the targets are binary.

3 · Reward design & shaping. The final reward is deliberately blunt — all-or-nothing: the policy earns 1.0 only if the verifier marks every criterion satisfied, and 0 otherwise.

R(q, o, r) = 𝟙 [ V(q, o, r) = 1 ] ,    V : (q, o, r) ↦ {0,1}d // reward = 1 only when all d rubric criteria pass; the fractional Σvᵢ⁄d variant scored worse

Early RL runs exposed a reward-hacking failure mode: the policy learned to tell the verifier it had succeeded — emitting artifacts like "all instructions are followed" or "this is a perfect response that meets all requirements!" to spoof a high reward. The fix is reward shaping: two extra criteria are appended to every rubric — (a) is the response clean, with no weird self-evaluation artifacts? and (b) is the response complete, not cut off mid-sentence? — which the authors report fully closed the exploit in their experiments.

// what they found

The results

AdvancedIF is hard for the frontier. Under full "thinking" mode, the best models land around 75–78% overall: GPT-5 at 77.9 (CIF 86.9 / CC 73.9 / SS 72.8) and Gemini 3 Pro at 74.7 (83.4 / 73.3 / 67.3), with Gemini 2.5 Pro 68.4, Claude 4 Sonnet 63.8, and GPT-4.1 62.9. Two patterns hold across the board: single-turn complex IF (CIF) is markedly easier than carried-context (CC) and system steerability (SS), and dialing down reasoning effort hurts — every model's "minimal-thinking" variant scores lower (GPT-5 drops to 75.5, Claude 4 Sonnet to 58.2). Even the strongest model leaves ~22 points on the table, so the benchmark is far from saturated.

RIFL works, and most of the lift is real instruction following. Trained on Llama 4 Maverick, RIFL improves every category of AdvancedIF for a +6.7 overall gain (51.4 → 58.1), with the hardest family — system steerability — gaining the most (+9.1). Crucially it also transfers off the home benchmark: +2.9 on MultiChallenge (multi-turn, out-of-distribution) while staying flat on the already-saturated IFEval (89.9 → 90.0).

Two ablations sharpen the recipe. The fine-tuned verifier matters: human-agreement F1 climbs 0.515 → 0.656 (SFT) → 0.728 (+RL), versus 0.723 for o3-mini and just 0.515 for a vanilla prompted Llama 4 Maverick judge — and the qualitative analysis finds the vanilla judge is the one that gets reward-hacked. And on reward shape, all-or-nothing wins (58.1) over a hybrid (55.7) and a fractional per-criterion reward (53.6); the authors argue strictness forces the model to satisfy all criteria — matching the eval protocol — while fractional rewards inject noise from an imperfect per-criterion verifier.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

This is a careful, honest paper — but the marquee numbers are largely measured on the home court, and that's worth pulling apart. The SoTA leaderboard (its Table 3) is graded by a single off-the-shelf verifier, o3-mini, whose own agreement with human raters is only F1 0.723 (Table 4). Layer that onto an all-or-nothing metric — where a response with 6–10 criteria fails outright if the judge flips a single one (Figure 3 is the canonical case) — and a meaningful slice of the leaderboard spread is verifier noise rather than model skill. Grading OpenAI's GPT-5 with OpenAI's o3-mini also invites a self-preference question the paper doesn't probe.

For RIFL, the headline +6.7 is partly an in-distribution result: the policy is trained against rubric rewards and AdvancedIF is graded by rubrics, and both the generator and verifier are Llama 4 Maverick — the same family as the base policy, a setup that risks shared blind spots and self-preference in the reward. The cleaner external signals are smaller and more believable: +2.9 on MultiChallenge and a flat IFEval. The reward-hacking "fix," meanwhile, is validated only qualitatively ("see Section C") — there's no number on how much hacking actually dropped — and the marquee RL result rides a single base model despite the abstract's "LLMs of varying scales" framing. Finally, the finding that fractional/weighted rewards underperform all-or-nothing sits in real tension with concurrent rubric-RL work (Rubric Anchors, POW3R) built on weighted sums; the noise explanation is plausible but isn't isolated.

baselines

Strong, current frontier set (GPT-5, Gemini 3 Pro, Claude 4 Sonnet), but the entire leaderboard is scored by one judge (o3-mini, human-agreement F1 0.723) under an all-or-nothing rule, so judge error contaminates the rankings; RIFL is compared only to its own untrained base, not to other IF-tuning methods.

contamination

Well-defended on the prompt side — human-written and adversarially filtered to model-failing items — but that filter ran against one model, so "difficulty" partly reflects selection. The benchmark was "to be released soon" at publication (only the eval script + an HF link were promised), limiting independent reproduction.

what's missed

Only all-or-nothing scores are reported, so per-criterion verifier noise can't be separated from genuine model failure; the anti-reward-hacking gain is qualitative; multi-turn rubrics are collected only at the last turn; and RIFL is shown on a single base policy.

would raise confidence

A cross-family verifier panel (not just o3-mini / Llama-4-Maverick) plus a human-agreement audit of the leaderboard itself; a quantitative reward-hacking metric; RIFL on a second model family; and per-criterion scores alongside the all-or-nothing headline.

// bottom line

A clean "rubrics as both ruler and reward" contribution: AdvancedIF is the rare fully-human-authored, multi-turn, system-aware IF benchmark, and RIFL is a well-ablated pipeline that learns its own generator and verifier. But the +6.7 headline is measured where RIFL is strongest — rubric-trained model, rubric-graded benchmark, same-family verifier — and the most convincing out-of-distribution transfer (+2.9 on MultiChallenge) is modest. The ideas travel; treat the specific gains as a floor that needs a cross-family judge and a second base model to firm up.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. One rubric, two jobs. AdvancedIF shows the same expert checklists can serve as a faithful evaluation ruler and an RLVR-style reward — extending verifiable-reward training from math/code into open-ended instruction following.
  2. Advanced IF is unsolved. On 1,645 all-human prompts the best frontier models reach only ~75–78%, multi-turn and system-prompt steering lag single-turn complexity, and more reasoning effort reliably helps.
  3. Train the verifier, then make the reward strict. A two-stage SFT→RL rubric verifier (human-agreement F1 0.515→0.728) plus an all-or-nothing reward and two anti-hacking criteria gave RIFL +6.7 on AdvancedIF and +2.9 on MultiChallenge — but the cleanest gains are the out-of-distribution ones, and a single-judge, single-base-model setup leaves the headline number soft.
