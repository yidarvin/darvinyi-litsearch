FinQA: A Dataset of Numerical Reasoning over Financial Data

The first benchmark to make a model read a real earnings filing — its table and its prose — and emit an explainable, executable arithmetic program. 8,281 questions written by finance professionals; the best 2021 system trails them by 30 points.

Zhiyu Chen, Wenhu Chen, Charese Smiley, Sameena Shah, Iana Borova, Dylan Langdon, Reema Moussa, Matt Beane, Ting-Hao Huang, Bryan Routledge, William Yang Wang

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

Reading a company's financials is a numerical-reasoning task disguised as a reading task. To answer "what was the change in shares vested?" or "what's the revolving-credit ratio?", an analyst has to locate the right figures — scattered across a structured table and the surrounding prose — and then chain several arithmetic steps: a ratio, a difference, a growth rate, sometimes a unit conversion. The numbers and the operations both matter, and a wrong answer in this domain can cost billions.

By 2021, numerical-reasoning QA existed, but it lived in the general domain. DROP asked for discrete reasoning over Wikipedia paragraphs; MathQA and MaWPS solved self-contained word problems; HybridQA and TabFact spanned tables and text but weren't about calculation. Crucially, almost all of these required one-step arithmetic, and none of them required domain expertise even to pose a meaningful question. Financial QA is harder on both axes: the reasoning is multi-step and the questions take a CPA to write. No dataset tested deep numerical reasoning over real financial documents — so it was an open question whether the large pre-trained models that aced general-domain QA could do an analyst's actual job.

// how it works

The dataset, and a baseline that reasons in programs

Building the data with finance professionals

FINQA is built on FinTabNet — the publicly filed earnings reports of S&P 500 companies from 1999–2019, with the tables already annotated. The authors first filtered to clean, reasoning-friendly pages: at most one table per page, no tables over 20 rows or with more than two description headers, and no nested or catalog-style layouts. That left 12,719 candidate pages.

The annotators are the differentiator. Rather than crowdworkers, the team hired 11 US-based finance experts (CPAs, MBAs) on UpWork, interviewed each on four sample pages, and trained them on the interface. For each page an expert writes up to two questions, and for each question supplies three things: (i) a question that is meaningful for financial analysis and requires calculation, (ii) a gold reasoning program of up to five steps, and (iii) the supporting facts — the exact sentences and table rows needed. Pay was ~$2/question (≈$35/hour); collection took eight weeks. The result: 8,281 examples over 2,789 report pages, split 6,251 / 883 / 1,147 (75% / 10% / 15%) with no report appearing in two splits.

A small domain-specific language

Programs are written in a 10-operation DSL: six math operations — add, subtract, multiply, divide, greater, exp — and four table aggregations — table-max, table-min, table-sum, table-average. Arguments are numbers from the report, table row names, or #n, a reference to the result of step n. The reasoning skews toward ratios: across the dataset, divide is 45.29% of operations, subtract 28.20%, add 14.98%, multiply 5.82%. Most questions are short chains — 59.10% are one step, 32.71% two steps, and only 8.19% need three or more.

Because the gold program is annotated, FINQA scores two things. Execution accuracy checks the final number. Program accuracy checks the reasoning: arguments are replaced with symbols and two programs count as correct only if they are mathematically equivalent — so a model can't get full credit for a lucky number.

add(a1, a2), add(a3, a4), subtract(#0, #1)   ≡   add(a4, a3), add(a1, a2), subtract(#1, #0) // program accuracy: two symbolic programs are equivalent when they reduce to the same computation

FinQANet: retrieve, then generate a program

A full report page can exceed 2,000 tokens — too long for BERT-era encoders — so FinQANet is a two-stage pipeline. The retriever linearizes each table row into a sentence template ("the risk-free interest rate of 2006 is 5%"), concatenates every candidate fact with the question, and trains a BERT classifier to score relevance; it keeps the top-3 facts, reordered by document position. This retriever reaches 89.66% recall at top-3 and 93.63% at top-5, versus 82.91% at top-5 for a TF-IDF baseline.

The program generator then decodes a program with an LSTM over the pre-trained-LM encodings. At each step it can emit a token from three sources: numbers and row names from the input, DSL special tokens (operators, constants), or step-memory tokens (#0, #1, …). The step-memory embedding for an operation is updated at its closing parenthesis, so the running reasoning path is carried forward; grammar masks at decode time keep every generated program structurally valid and executable.

// what they found

The results

The best system, FinQANet with a RoBERTa-large encoder, reaches 61.24% execution accuracy and 58.86% program accuracy. That beats the non-expert crowd (50.68%) but sits roughly 30 points below finance experts, who scored 91.16% execution / 87.49% program accuracy on a 200-example sample (with 92.65% inter-annotator agreement). The headline of the paper is this gap: large pre-trained models, strong on general QA, still fall well short of professionals on real financial reasoning.

The ablations are as informative as the headline. Naive approaches collapse: a TF-IDF + single-operation baseline gets 1.01%, and directly generating the answer instead of a program scores 0.30% — generating an explicit program is essential. An end-to-end Longformer over the whole page manages only 21.90%, confirming that the retrieve-then-generate split matters (long inputs flood the generator with distractor numbers). Encoder scale helps steadily — BERT-base 50.00 → BERT-large 53.52 → RoBERTa-base 56.10 → RoBERTa-large 61.24 — while domain-pretrained FinBERT (50.10) is no better than BERT-large, its ~30M-word corpus too small to matter. Swapping in gold retrieval lifts the model to 70.00%, so about 9 points are lost to retrieval errors alone.

Breaking the best model down (Table 3 of the paper) shows exactly where it cracks. Both modalities are needed: single-source inference scores 45.81% (table-only) and just 15.80% (text-only), both far below the 61.24% full result. By question type it is strongest on table-only questions (67.38%), weaker on text-only (54.86%), and weakest on questions that need both table and text (43.80%) — the most realistic and least common slice. And reasoning depth is decisive: 67.61% on one-step programs, 59.08% on two-step, and a collapse to 22.78% on programs with more than two steps. Programs that need a constant (e.g. a "1,000" to convert billions to millions, or an implicit "3" to average three years) drop to 43.88%. A manual look at 50 errors attributes 15% to the retriever; of the rest, roughly half are financial-knowledge gaps and half are numerical-reasoning mistakes — multi-step chains, unit conversions, and matching numbers to the right year.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

FINQA's design choices are mostly the right ones, and a few are genuinely ahead of their time. The standout is program accuracy: by grading the symbolic reasoning, not just the final number, it refuses to reward a model that stumbles onto the right answer — a discipline much of the LLM-eval field is only now rediscovering. The expert-annotation decision is validated in the paper itself: crowdworkers manage only 50.68%, so the task really does need a CPA to set the bar. And the ablations are honest about the pipeline's own weak points (retrieval costs ~9 points; multi-step programs collapse).

The caveats are mostly about when and how the benchmark was run. Every baseline is pre-LLM: BERT/RoBERTa/FinBERT encoders, NeRd, Seq2seq, Longformer. There is no GPT-3 few-shot, no chain-of-thought, no tool use — all of which existed in some form by 2021 and all of which the financial-reasoning line (FinanceBench and after) would soon show change the picture dramatically. So the dramatic "30-point gap to experts" is a snapshot of 2021 supervised pipelines, not a durable statement about model capability. The scope is also deliberately tidy: nested tables, tables over 20 rows, and catalog layouts are filtered out, programs are capped at five steps over a 10-op DSL, and the hardest, most realistic slice — questions needing both table and text — is only 14.15% of the set even though it's where models struggle most (43.80%). Two smaller things to flag: the expert ceiling rests on just two annotators over 200 questions, a thin and noisy upper bound; and the intro quotes a best execution accuracy of 65.05% while every results table and breakdown uses 61.24% — a stale headline number worth knowing if you cite the figure.

baselines

Thorough for 2021 (BERT/RoBERTa/FinBERT, NeRd, Seq2seq, Longformer), but entirely pre-LLM — no GPT-3 few-shot, no chain-of-thought, no tool use. Read the 30-point human gap as a 2021 supervised-pipeline result, not a capability ceiling.

contamination

Source filings are public S&P 500 reports (1999–2019) from FinTabNet — squarely inside modern web pretraining. The novel asset is the expert reasoning programs, not the underlying numbers, so memorized filings are a real leakage risk for today's LLMs.

what's missed

Filters out nested / >20-row tables and caps programs at 5 steps / 10 ops, so it measures clean, regular-layout reasoning. The hardest slice (table+text, 43.80%) is only 14% of the set, and the expert ceiling rests on 2 annotators / 200 questions. Intro says 65.05% exe acc; the tables say 61.24%.

would raise confidence

A few-shot / CoT LLM baseline; a larger multi-annotator human study; per-operation and per-company breakdowns; a held-out "hard" split of nested tables; and a time-split run to probe filing memorization.

// bottom line

FINQA was the first benchmark to force a model to read a real earnings filing's table and prose and emit an explainable, executable arithmetic program — and its program-accuracy metric set a higher bar than answer-matching. The 30-point human gap is a 2021 artifact that modern LLMs have largely closed, but the lasting contribution is the template it created: expert-written gold reasoning chains over public financial documents, which FinanceBench and the financial-reasoning line built directly on.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. The first financial numerical-reasoning benchmark. 8,281 QA pairs written by 11 finance experts over S&P 500 earnings reports, each with a gold executable reasoning program (≤5 steps, 10-op DSL) drawing on both tables and prose.
  2. Models trailed experts by ~30 points. FinQANet (RoBERTa-large) hit 61.24% execution accuracy — above the crowd's 50.68% but well below experts' 91.16%. It breaks on >2-step programs (22.78%), constant/unit-conversion programs (43.88%), and table+text questions (43.80%).
  3. Its legacy is methodological. Program accuracy via symbolic equivalence rewards correct reasoning over lucky answers, and the expert-annotated-gold-program recipe became the template FinanceBench and later financial-reasoning evals extended.
