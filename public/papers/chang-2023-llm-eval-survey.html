A Survey on Evaluation of Large Language Models

The paper that gave the sprawling, ad-hoc practice of testing LLMs a shared map: a three-axis taxonomy of what to evaluate, where, and how — assembled from 269 papers and 46 benchmarks, before agentic evals, LLM-as-judge, and RL-verified reasoning were part of the vocabulary.

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

By mid-2023 the practice of "evaluating LLMs" had exploded but never been organized. Every new model paper ran a different grab-bag of benchmarks; every capability claim (reasoning, factuality, safety, medical knowledge) was checked against a different, ad hoc slice of the literature; and there was no shared vocabulary for comparing one evaluation effort against another. The authors' own count captures the pace of the problem: a literature search for LLM evaluation papers returns roughly 0 in 2020, climbing to 33 in a single month by mid-2023 — an evaluation ecosystem growing faster than anyone was cataloguing it.

Chang et al. set out to be, in their own words, "the first survey to give a comprehensive overview of the evaluation on LLMs" organized around three questions that had never been asked together in one place: what to evaluate (which capabilities and tasks), where to evaluate (which datasets and benchmarks), and how to evaluate (which protocols and metrics actually measure it). The gap wasn't a missing benchmark — it was a missing map of the benchmarks that already existed, plus a framework for judging whether new ones were asking the right questions.

// how it works

The method

The survey is a systematic literature review, not an experiment — its "method" is the taxonomy itself. The authors read and organized 269 references into three orthogonal axes, each answered in its own section of the paper:

What to evaluate (Sec. 3). Seven top-level capability areas: general NLP tasks (natural language understanding, reasoning, natural language generation, multilingual ability, factuality), robustness / ethics / bias / trustworthiness, social science, natural science & engineering, medical applications, agent applications, and a catch-all "other applications" bucket (education, search & recommendation, personality testing). Each branch is populated with the specific papers that probed it — e.g. reasoning alone cites 16 separate studies.

Where to evaluate (Sec. 4). The authors compile 46 popular benchmarks into Table 7 and split them into three families: general-task benchmarks (MMLU, HELM, BIG-bench, C-Eval, Chatbot Arena, MT-Bench, AGIEval, and others), specific-downstream-task benchmarks (APPS, MATH, CUAD, ToolBench, MultiMedQA, and others), and multi-modal benchmarks (MME, MMBench, SEED-Bench, LAMM).

How to evaluate (Sec. 5). Two evaluation paradigms — automatic evaluation (Table 9 groups metrics into accuracy, calibration, fairness, and robustness families: exact match, F1, ROUGE, expected calibration error, demographic parity difference, attack success rate) and human evaluation — with a nod to emerging hybrids like PandaLM and LLM-Eval that use a model to score other models, the seed of what would later be formalized as "LLM-as-judge."

// what they found

The results

The output isn't a leaderboard, it's a synthesis. Section 6 distills the reviewed literature into concrete success/failure patterns for the LLMs of that era (GPT-3.5, GPT-4, ChatGPT, PaLM, LLaMA): where they succeed — fluent generation, sentiment analysis and text classification, arithmetic and logical reasoning, machine translation and question answering, and strong contextual comprehension; where they fail — natural language inference (poor at representing human disagreement), abstract reasoning, non-Latin/low-resource languages, visual-modal grounding, and a susceptibility to absorbing and amplifying the toxicity and social bias present in their training data.

The growth curve behind the survey is its own finding: the number of LLM-evaluation papers the authors could locate went from essentially zero before 2021 to 33 in the final month they counted (2023.06+) — a trajectory that, read from 2026, undersells just how much the field would keep accelerating.

Section 7 closes with seven "grand challenges": designing genuine AGI benchmarks, complete behavioral (not just static-task) evaluation, robustness evaluation, dynamic and evolving evaluation (to outrun contamination), principled and trustworthy evaluation systems, a unified evaluation framework spanning all LLM tasks, and — the most prescient — treating evaluation as a tool for model improvement rather than just a scorecard ("beyond evaluation: LLMs enhancement").

// does it hold up?

The eval read

Judge this one as a survey, not a benchmark paper: the question isn't "are the numbers real" (they're literature synthesis, and they check out against the cited sources) but "does the map still match the territory." The honest answer, from 2026, is: partially — and predictably so, given how the paper itself documents the field's growth curve.

The taxonomy's "what to evaluate" branch gives agent applications a single entry with seven citations (Huang et al., Karpas et al., Parisi et al., Qin et al. ×2, Schick et al., Shen et al.) — reasonable in July 2023, when ReAct and Toolformer were the state of the art. It arrived roughly two months before SWE-bench, AgentBench, and the wave of interactive-environment benchmarks (WebArena, GAIA, OSWorld, τ-bench, MLE-bench) that turned "agent evaluation" into its own subfield with dozens of dedicated benchmarks — none of which the taxonomy could have anticipated as a first-class branch rather than a footnote. Similarly, "how to evaluate" treats LLM-as-judge tools (PandaLM, LLM-Eval) as one bullet inside automatic evaluation, not the dominant evaluation paradigm it would become once AlpacaEval- and MT-Bench-style GPT-4-judge protocols standardized across the field — and it necessarily has nothing to say about RLVR (reinforcement learning with verifiable rewards), rubric-based grading, or the reward-hacking failure modes that later became central to post-training evaluation.

To the authors' credit, the paper isn't naive about its own shelf life: Section 7.4 explicitly names "dynamic and evolving evaluation" as a grand challenge precisely because "static and public benchmarks are likely to be memorized by LLMs, resulting in potential training data contamination" — a correct prediction of exactly the problem that motivated LiveCodeBench, LiveBench, and search-time-contamination studies a year or two later. The survey also went through nine arXiv revisions between July and December 2023, so it wasn't frozen at first release — but there has been no revision since, and the maintained GitHub tracker the authors point to is a list of papers, not a re-drawn taxonomy tree.

baselines

Coverage is broad for its moment (269 references, 46 benchmarks) but weighted toward the NLP/robustness/ethics literature that existed by mid-2023; agentic and RL-based evaluation get proportionally thin treatment because they were proportionally thin at the time.

contamination

The survey correctly flags benchmark memorization as a top-tier open problem (Sec. 7.4) but, being a synthesis rather than an experiment, never audits whether the 46 benchmarks it catalogues were themselves already contaminated in the models it discusses.

what's missed

Everything that postdates July–December 2023: SWE-bench-style agentic coding benchmarks, GAIA/OSWorld/WebArena-style interactive environments, GPQA/MMLU-Pro/HLE-style saturation-resistant reasoning benchmarks, RLVR and rubric-based reward evaluation, and meta-evaluation critiques of benchmark quality itself (leaderboard gaming, contamination audits, statistical error bars on evals).

would raise confidence

A periodically re-drawn version of Figure 1 — even an informal "taxonomy v2" blog post or repo update — showing which 2023 branches have since split into their own subfields, rather than leaving the tree as a single frozen snapshot.

// bottom line

This is the right survey to cite for "how do people organize LLM evaluation" — its what/where/how framing is still the one most later benchmark papers implicitly use to justify themselves. It is the wrong document to cite for "what should I evaluate today": a genuinely fast-moving field arguably outgrows any static taxonomy paper within 18-24 months, and this one is now three years past its last revision. Read it as a foundational map of the terrain circa 2023, not a current index.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. Chang et al. gave a scattered literature a shared vocabulary — what to evaluate, where, how — synthesizing 269 papers into 46 catalogued benchmarks across seven capability areas; most later Benchmarks & Evals surveys still borrow this three-axis frame.
  2. The survey's own growth chart (0 papers/month before 2021, 33 by mid-2023) predicted its own obsolescence: agentic benchmarks, LLM-as-judge as a dominant paradigm, and RLVR-style post-training evaluation all arrived within roughly a year of publication and sit outside the taxonomy as drawn.
  3. It correctly named its biggest blind spot in advance — Sec. 7.4's warning about static-benchmark contamination anticipated the dynamic-evaluation movement — but a taxonomy paper for a field moving this fast needs a living re-draw, not just a maintained reading list, to stay a current index rather than a historical one.
