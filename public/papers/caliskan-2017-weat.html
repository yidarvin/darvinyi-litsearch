Semantics derived automatically from language corpora contain human-like biases

The psychologist's Implicit Association Test, ported to word vectors: off-the-shelf GloVe embeddings trained on ordinary web text reproduce the exact spectrum of documented human biases — flowers-vs-insects, race, gender-career, gender-science — with effect sizes and significance levels comparable to the decades of IAT studies they replicate.

Aylin Caliskan, Joanna J. Bryson, Arvind Narayanan

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

By 2016, word embeddings — GloVe, word2vec — had quietly become the substrate underneath most of NLP: sentiment analysis, machine translation, search ranking, document classification. They are trained with no labels and no explicit notion of "bias," just co-occurrence statistics over hundreds of billions of words of ordinary web text, so the standard assumption was that they were neutral — a bare statistical summary of language, no more prejudiced than a word-frequency table. Complaints about "AI bias" up to that point were scattered and application-specific: Latanya Sweeney had shown Google ads for arrest records disproportionately appeared alongside searches for Black-sounding names; ProPublica had shown a recidivism-risk tool skewed against Black defendants. Each was a bias finding about one deployed classifier, on one task, discovered after the fact.

What was missing was a general instrument: a way to ask, of any word embedding, whether the representation itself — before it is ever wired into a downstream classifier — already encodes the same associations psychologists have spent decades documenting in human subjects. Psychology had exactly such an instrument for people: the Implicit Association Test (IAT), introduced by Greenwald, McGhee, and Schwartz in 1998, which infers implicit attitudes from how much faster subjects can pair two concepts they find similar (e.g., "flowers" and "pleasant") than two they find dissimilar. The IAT literature is large, its stimuli word lists are published, and its findings (racial and gender bias, but also innocuous ones like flowers-over-insects) are well replicated. The gap this paper fills is methodological transplantation: adapt the IAT's logic — swap reaction time for cosine similarity, and human subjects for a fixed vocabulary of word vectors — into a statistic with its own p-value and effect size, then run it, unmodified, on the actual GloVe embedding shipped and used in production NLP systems. If the same biases fall out with comparable strength, "AI bias" stops being anecdotal and becomes something you can quantify, replicate, and hold up against a specific, previously validated human baseline.

// how it works

The method

The authors deliberately use embeddings they did not train: pre-trained, publicly distributed 300-dimensional GloVe vectors (Pennington et al., 2014), fit on a "Common Crawl" web scrape of 840 billion tokens with a 2.2-million-word, case-sensitive vocabulary. Using the off-the-shelf artifact — not a custom-trained toy model — is the whole point: whatever bias the test finds is a property of the exact embedding NLP practitioners were already dropping into production pipelines, not an artifact of some unusual training recipe.

The core instrument, the Word Embedding Association Test (WEAT), mirrors the IAT's two-by-two structure directly. Take two sets of target words of equal size, X and Y (say, European-American names vs. African-American names), and two sets of attribute words, A and B (pleasant vs. unpleasant words) — always the exact stimuli lists published by the psychology study being replicated, never invented ad hoc, which forecloses cherry-picking word lists to inflate an effect. For any single word w, its association with the attribute is the gap between its mean cosine similarity to A and to B:

s(w, A, B) = meana∈A cos(w⃗, a⃗) − meanb∈B cos(w⃗, b⃗) // how much closer word w sits to attribute set A than to attribute set B, in cosine similarity

The test statistic sums that quantity over each target set and takes the difference — the differential association of the two target sets with the attribute:

s(X, Y, A, B) = Σx∈X s(x, A, B) − Σy∈Y s(y, A, B)

Significance comes from a permutation test: enumerate every way to re-partition X ∪ Y into two equal-size sets, recompute the statistic for each partition, and report the one-sided p-value as the fraction of random partitions whose statistic exceeds the observed one. The effect size is a Cohen's-d analogue — the same mean gap, normalized by the standard deviation of per-word associations across X ∪ Y — with a ceiling of 2.0. The authors are careful to flag a limit of the analogy: because the IAT's "subjects" are people and WEAT's are words, the two effect sizes and p-values are not numerically interchangeable, even when they replicate the same qualitative finding.

A companion instrument, the Word Embedding Factual Association Test (WEFAT), checks the flip side — whether the embedding also encodes true, non-normative regularities about the world. For a set of target words W each with a known real-valued property pw (e.g., the percentage of women in an occupation), WEFAT computes each word's normalized attribute association and fits a linear regression against pw; a strong correlation says the vector space has captured veridical structure, not just noise, which matters as a sanity check on the whole exercise — an embedding that returned high WEAT effect sizes but zero WEFAT correlation would be much less convincing evidence that anything meaningful was being measured.

// what they found

The results

Run against GloVe, WEAT reproduces every documented IAT bias the authors tested it against, at comparable or larger effect sizes, without any test needing tuned or re-selected stimuli. The résumé rows replicate Bertrand and Mullainathan's (2004) field experiment, in which nearly 5,000 otherwise-identical résumés were sent to 1,300 job ads with only the applicant's name varied — European-American names got 50% more callbacks. WEAT recovers the same directional prejudice purely from where those same names sit in GloVe's vector space relative to pleasant/unpleasant words, with no résumé, no employer, and no callback in the loop at all.

WEFAT's real-world correlations are the paper's strongest evidence that the embedding captures veridical signal, not just replicable noise. Projecting 50 occupation words onto the gender axis and regressing against each occupation's actual share of women workers (2015 U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics) gives a Pearson correlation of ρ = 0.90 (p < 10⁻¹⁸):

A second WEFAT test on androgynous first names — names given to both boys and girls in varying proportions — against 1990 U.S. Census name-gender statistics gives ρ = 0.84 (p < 10⁻¹³), weaker than the occupation result but, as the authors note, plausibly because the census data is older and less current than the 2015 labor statistics:

As a robustness check, the authors reran the WEAT and WEFAT battery on a second, independently trained embedding — word2vec on Google News — and report the same qualitative pattern (statistically significant effects, high effect sizes throughout), with the gender-association strength of occupation words correlating ρ = 0.88 (Pearson) / 0.86 (Spearman) between the two embeddings — though the paper does not publish a full parallel effect-size/p-value table for the word2vec run the way it does for GloVe.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

WEAT's central rhetorical move is also its central vulnerability: borrowing the IAT's validated stimuli lets the paper claim it isn't cherry-picking word baskets, but the paper is explicit, in its own methods section, that a WEAT effect size and p-value are not numerically comparable to an IAT effect size and p-value — "the meaning of these numbers is entirely different," because the IAT's subjects are people averaged over trials and WEAT's "subjects" are words averaged over a static corpus. That caveat is easy to lose once a table like the one above puts a GloVe d = 1.50 next to a human d = 1.35 — the visual and narrative pull is "this embedding is about as biased as a human," a claim the authors themselves decline to make but that the paper's framing invites. What WEAT actually establishes is a different, still valuable claim: that the same direction and rough magnitude of association shows up in both human reaction times and word-vector geometry, which is real evidence about where the associations come from (language itself), not a joint scale on which "amount of bias" can be read off and compared unit-for-unit.

The deeper construct-validity question is what a WEAT effect size is evidence of. An IAT effect size is downstream of a documented human cognitive process (differential reaction time under time pressure). A WEAT effect size is a fact about the geometry of a specific corpus's co-occurrence statistics — which is to say, it is largely a restatement of how often certain words appear near certain other words in Common Crawl. That the restatement lines up with independently documented human biases is the genuinely striking empirical result. But it leaves open — and the paper does not resolve — whether a high WEAT score on some new category translates into any specific downstream harm (a biased hiring classifier, a skewed translation, a search-ranking disparity) versus simply reflecting a cultural association that never gets acted on by any system built on top of the embedding. The paper's own sentiment-analysis and Google Translate examples in the discussion are illustrative, not measured: the Turkish-to-English gendered-pronoun anecdote (occupation words translate to "he" more often than "she") is a real, checkable downstream effect, but it is reported as a spot check on 50 words, not run as a systematic study with its own numbers.

Corpus-dependence is the other open question the paper only partially answers. Every headline number here is specific to one 2014, 840-billion-token Common Crawl GloVe model with static, whole-word (non-subword) vectors — one vector per word type, no context, no byte-pair encoding, and certainly no exposure to the RLHF-shaped, instruction-tuned, contextual sentence embeddings that dominate NLP a decade later. The word2vec/Google News robustness check (ρ = 0.88 gender-association correlation with GloVe) is reassuring but thin: it reports one correlation number across the two embeddings' occupation-gender associations, not a parallel replication of all eight WEAT tests with their own effect sizes and p-values on the second embedding. Whether the same effect sizes hold on today's much larger, differently curated, more heavily filtered and deduplicated training sets — or on contextual, subword-tokenized embeddings extracted from a modern LLM — is an empirical question this paper doesn't and couldn't answer, since none of that infrastructure existed yet in 2016.

baselines

The comparison points are the original IAT studies' own published effect sizes — a legitimate, independently validated baseline for "is this a real, previously-documented bias," but explicitly (per the authors) not on the same numerical scale as WEAT, so a side-by-side table invites a stronger reading ("equally biased") than the data supports.

contamination

Stimuli word lists are all lifted unmodified from the psychology papers being replicated (a genuine anti-cherry-picking discipline), but the paper reports only "a sample that we think are persuasive" of the tests actually run — e.g., a mental-illness-stigma IAT replication is mentioned in the discussion as successful but its numbers are never reported — so the denominator of attempted-but-unreported tests is unknown.

what's missed

An embedding-space association is not a measured downstream harm; the paper's translation/sentiment-analysis discussion of real consequences is anecdotal, not a systematic study, and static, single-vector-per-word GloVe/word2vec says nothing about contextual embeddings, which is what nearly all deployed NLP now uses.

would raise confidence

A full parallel effect-size/p-value table on the word2vec robustness run (not just one correlation number); a pre-registered/exhaustive list of every IAT test attempted, not just the persuasive subset; and a direct replication of the same battery on modern contextual, subword-tokenized embeddings to test whether 2016-era effect sizes still hold a decade of corpus and architecture changes later.

// bottom line

WEAT is a genuinely important methodological transplant — the first rigorous, borrowed-from-psychology instrument for asking whether a general-purpose language representation encodes documented human bias, and its central finding (yes, strongly and across many categories, with real-world veridical correlations to match) has replicated widely and seeded an entire measurement subfield. But treat a WEAT-style effect size as "worth investigating for downstream disparate impact," not as a certificate that a specific harm has occurred, or as a number on the same scale as a human IAT score — the paper itself says as much, even if the framing that follows it in the literature often doesn't.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. WEAT ports the Implicit Association Test into vector space — replacing human reaction time with word-vector cosine similarity and a permutation test — and, run unmodified on off-the-shelf 2014 GloVe embeddings, replicates a spectrum of documented IAT biases at comparable or larger effect sizes: flowers/insects (d = 1.50 vs. 1.35), race valence (d = 1.41 vs. 1.17), and gender-career (d = 1.81 vs. 0.72), gender-math (d = 1.06 vs. 0.82), and gender-science (d = 1.24 vs. 1.47) associations.
  2. WEFAT shows the same embedding also encodes true, veridical regularities, not just noise — occupation-word gender association predicts the actual 2015 BLS percentage of women in that occupation at ρ = 0.90 (p < 10⁻¹⁸), and androgynous-name gender association predicts 1990 census name-gender splits at ρ = 0.84 (p < 10⁻¹³) — evidence the signal WEAT is picking up is a real, general property of the representation rather than an artifact of the test.
  3. The paper itself flags that WEAT and IAT numbers aren't on the same scale — "the meaning of these numbers is entirely different" — a construct-validity caveat worth holding onto: a high WEAT effect size is evidence a corpus-derived representation encodes a documented cultural association, not a measurement of downstream discriminatory harm, and not (despite how often the two get equated in later "AI bias" discourse) a number directly comparable to a human implicit-bias score.
