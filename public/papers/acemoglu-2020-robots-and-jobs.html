Robots and Jobs: Evidence from US Labor Markets

Before this paper, "how many jobs will automation destroy" was answered by asking classifiers to rate occupations on technological feasibility. This paper answers it by watching an actual automation technology diffuse: using US commuting zones' exposure to industrial robots — instrumented by robot adoption in Europe — one more robot per thousand workers is found to cut local employment by 0.39 percentage points and wages by 0.77%, with a model-implied aggregate effect of roughly 400,000 US jobs lost between 1990 and 2007.

Daron Acemoglu (MIT) · Pascual Restrepo (Boston University)

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

By the mid-2010s the dominant way to quantify automation's threat to jobs was to hand-score occupations on how "feasible" it would be, in principle, to automate their component tasks — Frey and Osborne's Gaussian-process classifier over O*NET task descriptors being the canonical example, alongside similar exercises from the World Bank and McKinsey Global Institute. Acemoglu and Restrepo say plainly why that isn't enough: "such approaches are not informative about the equilibrium impact of automation since they do not take into account how other sectors and occupations will respond to these changes." A classifier can tell you a truck-driving job is technically automatable; it cannot tell you whether truck drivers actually lose their jobs once markets, wages, and downstream industries adjust — whether displaced workers migrate, whether cheaper transport expands trucking volume, whether local retail and construction absorb or shed labor as regional income falls. None of that shows up in a per-occupation feasibility score.

What was missing was a real automation technology, diffusing in the real economy, whose actual employment and wage consequences could be measured rather than projected. Industrial robots are a good candidate: the International Federation of Robotics defines one precisely — "an automatically controlled, reprogrammable, and multipurpose machine" — which excludes single-purpose equipment (looms, elevators, conveyor belts) and yields internationally comparable counts back to 1993. Robot stocks in the US and Western Europe roughly quadrupled between 1993 and 2007, concentrated overwhelmingly in automotive (38% of the installed base), electronics (15%), plastics and chemicals (10%), and metal products (7%). That gives the paper exactly what a feasibility score cannot: a technology with a measurable dose, a measurable date, and a set of US regions that received more or less of it for reasons plausibly unrelated to their own local economic trajectories.

// how it works

The method

The paper builds a task-based model (extending Zeira 1998, Acemoglu & Autor 2011, and the authors' own earlier framework) in which robots and workers compete to perform tasks within an industry. Robots create a displacement effect — directly replacing workers on tasks they used to do — offset by a productivity effect, as cheaper output raises labor demand elsewhere. The model shows both effects collapse into a single sufficient statistic at the local-labor-market level: a commuting zone's "exposure to robots," a Bartik-style measure built by weighting each industry's national robot-adoption intensity (the "adjusted penetration of robots," APR) by that zone's baseline employment share in the industry:

Exposurec = Σi ℓci · APRi(ℓci = industry i's 1990 employment share in zone c) // eq. (11)–(12): a zone is "exposed" if it started with lots of workers in industries that later adopted many robots

The threat to this design is obvious: if an industry adopts robots because of a local shock (a union wage push, a regional recession), US robot exposure is endogenous to the very labor-demand shocks the regression is trying to isolate. The fix is the paper's central empirical move: instrument US industry-level robot adoption with the adoption trend in five European countries — Denmark, Finland, France, Italy, and Sweden ("EURO5") — that are further along the robotics frontier, mostly for demographic reasons (aging societies with fewer middle-aged production workers adopt robots faster, a link the authors establish in a companion paper). US robot usage tracked the EURO5 average closely over 1993–2014, running about 20% lower throughout, which is what makes the European trend a plausible proxy for "how technologically ready an industry is to automate" that's purged of any US-specific local shock.

Data come from the IFR's yearly robot-supplier survey (50 countries, 1993–2014, ~90% of the world robot market, 19 industries) merged with EU KLEMS industry employment/output and US Census/ACS commuting-zone demographics across 722 commuting zones. The main specifications regress long-differenced (1990–2007) and stacked-differenced (1990–2000 and 2000–2007) changes in the employment-to-population ratio and log hourly wages on this exposure measure, with a battery of controls for demographics, baseline manufacturing composition, Chinese import exposure, and the declining share of routine jobs — precisely the confounders a good instrument needs to survive.

// what they found

The results

The headline: one more robot per thousand workers reduces a commuting zone's employment-to-population ratio by 0.39 percentage points and average wages by 0.77% (2SLS, preferred specification), relative to a zone with no robot exposure. That translates to roughly six fewer jobs per additional robot locally. Because commuting zones interact through trade and shared capital income, the local estimate overstates the national effect — cheaper goods and capital gains from robot-heavy zones partly benefit the rest of the country. Feeding the IV estimates through the model's general-equilibrium channel implies a smaller aggregate effect: −0.2 percentage points in the employment-to-population ratio and −0.42% in wages per additional robot per thousand workers, or about 400,000 US jobs over 1990–2007 given the observed ≈120,000-robot increase in that period — equivalently, 3.3 fewer jobs per additional robot once spillovers are netted out.

The effect is not evenly spread. It concentrates in manufacturing and specifically in routine-manual, blue-collar occupations — machinists, assemblers, material handlers, welders — the workers whose tasks robots are literally built to perform; the paper finds no offsetting positive employment effect in any other occupation group. Wages fall hardest for the bottom of the distribution: significant declines below the 35th percentile overall, extending up to the 85th percentile for workers without a college degree, but confined below the 15th percentile for college graduates, with no measurable effect at all for workers holding a master's or doctoral degree. Men are hit harder than women (−0.57 vs. −0.34 percentage points), and roughly two-thirds of a zone's total employment decline flows through nontradable spillovers — construction, retail, personal services — rather than direct manufacturing job loss, as reduced local income ripples through the rest of the local economy. For scale, the same commuting-zone design applied to Chinese import competition (Autor, Dorn & Hanson's "China shock") implies a 2.5× larger local employment decline than robots produced over the same window.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

As real-world causal evidence goes, this is about as careful as commuting-zone econometrics gets — and the authors are unusually candid about where it strains. Three places are worth sitting with.

First, the instrument leans hard on one industry. A footnote the paper doesn't bury: the automotive sector alone explains 67% of the cross-commuting-zone variation in robot exposure, and Rotemberg weights (the Goldsmith-Pinkham–Sorkin–Swift shift-share diagnostic) run 50–90% in the main specifications. In practice, a "19-industry, 722-commuting-zone" instrument is functionally much closer to a single natural experiment in auto-manufacturing regions — if a Detroit-era shock the controls don't fully absorb happens to correlate with automotive robot adoption, the whole estimate moves with it. The authors' defense — splitting exposure into automotive vs. non-automotive components and failing to reject that the two coefficients are equal — is reassuring but not dispositive; it shows the two pieces agree, not that either is clean.

Second, the headline aggregate number — the one everyone quotes, "0.2 percentage points, 400,000 jobs" — is not directly estimated. It's a calibration: the local 2SLS coefficients are fed through a general-equilibrium model that requires eight externally chosen structural parameters (elasticities of substitution across industries and traded varieties, the labor share in task production, a robot-to-worker productivity ratio of 3, a marginal propensity to consume leisure, and more), several sourced from unrelated studies (Oberfield & Raval, BCG survey data, Chetty et al.'s Frisch elasticities). The local effect is the paper's real causal contribution; the aggregate figure is a theory-driven extrapolation layered on top, and its precision is borrowed from the calibration, not the data.

Third — and the authors say this themselves — the estimate is a snapshot of one automation wave (industrial robots, 1990–2007/2014) that may not generalize to the next one. The conclusion explicitly warns that "the response of employment and wages may be different once robots become sufficiently widespread," and that reinstatement effects (new tasks created for labor, the subject of the authors' companion work) "might emerge only slowly." Despite that caveat, this coefficient has become the default real-world anchor cited against every subsequent classifier-based automation-risk estimate — including for large language models, a technology this design says nothing about.

baselines

OLS and 2SLS estimates are close and both negative, which argues against upward endogeneity bias — but the comparison that matters (robots vs. no robots) is never observed; the counterfactual comes from the calibrated model, not a held-out sample.

contamination

Not leakage in the ML sense, but a close analogue: the automotive industry supplies 50–90% of the instrument's identifying variation (Rotemberg weights), so this is much closer to a single-industry case study than a 19-industry panel.

what's missed

Task-reinstatement / new-job creation (the offsetting side of automation, measured only in companion papers), imprecisely estimated migration responses, and any evidence past the 2014 IFR data cutoff — this is a 1990s–2000s robot-diffusion estimate, not a general automation elasticity.

would raise confidence

An instrument that doesn't route so much of its variation through one industry; direct (not calibrated) estimates of the cross-zone spillover parameters; and out-of-sample replication in a different country/period — partially delivered since by studies like Dauth et al. on Germany.

// bottom line

This is the empirical number that classifier-based automation forecasts get checked against — and it deserves that role for the local effect, which is genuinely well-identified. But the famous aggregate headline is a calibrated extrapolation sitting on top of an instrument that leans on one industry more than the "19 industries, 722 commuting zones" framing suggests, describing one already-completed wave of robot diffusion rather than automation in general.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. One more robot per thousand US workers cuts local employment by 0.39 percentage points and wages by 0.77% (IV); after accounting for cross-region spillovers, the model-implied aggregate effect is 0.2 points / 0.42% — about 400,000 jobs over 1990–2007.
  2. Identification comes from instrumenting US robot adoption with adoption trends in five demographically-older European countries — but that instrument's cross-region variation is 50–90% attributable to the automotive industry alone, a concentration the paper documents but can't fully neutralize.
  3. Effects concentrate in manufacturing and routine-manual/blue-collar occupations, but roughly two-thirds of a zone's total job loss is an indirect spillover onto nontradables (construction, retail, personal services) — most of the damage isn't a robot literally taking someone's job.
