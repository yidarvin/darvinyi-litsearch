ResearchQA: Evaluating Scholarly Question Answering at Scale Across 75 Fields with Survey-Mined Questions and Rubrics

Turn 54K academic surveys into 21K research questions and 160K grading-rubric items across 75 fields — then show that even the best deep-research system covers only 75% of what experts say a good answer should contain.

Li S. Yifei*, Allen Chang*, Chaitanya Malaviya, Mark Yatskar · University of Pennsylvania

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

Deep-research tools and scientific assistants now promise to answer open-ended research questions — "what strategies reduce wall-clock training time for BERT models?" — with long, citation-backed prose. The trouble is grading them. A good answer to a research query has a huge space of acceptable forms, so token-overlap metrics like ROUGE are gameable and reference answers are awkward to compare against. The field's fallback is expert annotation: recruit researchers to read answers and judge quality.

That bottleneck quietly shapes what gets measured. Because the only experts most authors can conveniently enlist are their own colleagues, scholarly-QA benchmarks cluster in a handful of engineering and CS-adjacent fields. The largest hand-built efforts — QASPER, QASA, KIWI, ScholarQABench — top out at a few thousand queries and one or two domains, precisely because recruiting domain experts at scale is expensive. Whole swaths of science (medicine, ecology, materials, economics) go unevaluated, and a leaderboard built on NLP questions tells you little about how a system performs in oncology.

ResearchQA's insight is that research expertise is already written down. Survey articles exist precisely to consolidate a field's foundational questions and synthesize the evidence that answers them. If you can mine surveys at scale, you can manufacture both the questions and the criteria for grading answers — without recruiting a single new expert for the bulk of the work.

// how it works

The method

ResearchQA is a dataset of 21.4K queries paired with 160K rubric items across 75 research fields, all distilled automatically from academic survey articles. Every query ships with a short list of query-specific rubric items — concrete criteria a strong answer should satisfy, such as citing a particular paper, drawing a specific comparison, or naming a limitation. Each item is scored on a 0–4 coverage scale, turning an open-ended judgement into an absolute, checkable measure.

Mine surveys, not annotators

The pipeline (Figure 2) starts by ranking the top-20 publishing venues by h5-index for each field on Google Scholar. After manually consolidating Scholar's overly-specific or overly-general field taxonomy from 257 down to 94 fields, that yields 660 venues. Keyword search over Crossref, Semantic Scholar, and S2ORC for terms like "survey," "literature review," and "meta-analysis" returns 615K candidate articles, of which 134K are downloadable full text and 54K are classified as genuine surveys (a gpt-4.1-mini classifier, F1 = .80, precision = .87, prunes papers that merely mention "survey").

Every LLM call in the pipeline uses gpt-4.1-mini as the workhorse model M, chosen as a cost/quality trade-off. From the surveys, the pipeline keeps medium-length sections with at least 3 inline citations (dropping sections under 3 sentences / 800 characters, or over 300K characters), giving 319K quality sections out of 886K. It then extracts a hierarchical, tree-structured summary of each section and prompts M to write a query plus a reference answer grounded in the supporting sentences. Each query is tagged with a knowledge cut-off date equal to the source survey's publication year.

From query to a defensible rubric

Queries are filtered to be standalone (understandable without extra context) and low in answer variability (so experts would grade similarly): M scores each on a 1–10 scale and discards queries below 7 for standaloneness or above 4 for variability, plus anything with context-dependent phrasing ("the paper," "this study"). The result is 21K queries split into a 16.9K train set, a 703-query validation set, and a 3.7K test set that samples 50 queries from each of the 75 fields with enough data, so no single discipline dominates the evaluation.

Rubrics are generated in three flavors and then merged. Survey rubrics (RS) condition on both query and reference answer; parametric rubrics (RP) condition only on the query, catching criteria the survey omitted. The released hybrid rubric (RH) deduplicates and re-ranks the union of the two, drops items that cite papers not matchable on Google Scholar, and keeps the top 8 — about 7.5 items per query on average, roughly 61% of them drawn from the survey side. Items come in three deliberately diverse types: information-based (4 sampled), depth-based (2), and citation-based (2).

Scoring: coverage plus an ensemble judge

An LLM judge reads an answer A and its rubric R and rates each item's coverage on the 0–4 scale; averaging and dividing by 4 gives Coverage %, a recall-style measure of how much of the rubric the answer hit. For head-to-head ranking, the paper found that a direct "which answer is better?" judge alone is mediocre, so it introduces an ensemble judge that fuses the direct verdict with rubric coverage — each direct win is worth a flat 4 points per ordering, added to the summed coverage scores:

score(A) = 4 (IA + I′A) + Σ c ∈ Coverage(A,R) c // IA, I′A: did the direct judge prefer A in each of the two answer orderings · the higher-scoring answer wins, ties allowed

Both orderings are evaluated to blunt positional bias, and pairwise battles are aggregated with a Bradley–Terry (Elo-style) model, the same machinery as Chatbot Arena. To check this whole apparatus is trustworthy, 31 Ph.D.-level annotators from 8 fields validated the data: 90% of queries reflect genuine Ph.D. information needs and 87% of rubric items warrant at least a sentence of emphasis in a good answer.

// what they found

The results

Two questions: do the rubrics make an LLM judge trustworthy, and how do real systems score against them?

Rubrics rescue the judge

On their own, judges that directly predict the better answer agree with the human majority vote only 71% of the time (proprietary) and 64% (open-source) — well below the 84% human–human ceiling. Folding rubric coverage into the ensemble protocol lifts agreement to 74% and 69% respectively, shrinking the LLM-vs-human gap from 12.7% to 9.6%, a 24% relative reduction. Hybrid rubrics help in 7 of 8 validation fields and are the single best rubric type in 4 of them; tellingly, they let the open prometheus-2-8x7b judge match the direct-comparison accuracy of claude-4-sonnet.

No system covers the rubric

Across 7.6K head-to-head battles over 18 systems in four tiers of effort — parametric, naive retrieval, production retrieval, and deep-research APIs — the headline is a wall. No parametric or retrieval system exceeds 70% coverage. The single best system, Perplexity's sonar-deep-research, reaches only 75.29%, and it does so with an 82% win rate over the next-best system (gemini-2.5-pro) — a striking gap that says research-tuned tools are in a different league, but still far from saturating the benchmark.

Two mechanisms sit underneath the leaderboard. Retrieval has to be engineered: bolting naive Google-Scholar retrieval onto a model is often worse than its parametric self, and the same model swings by 7.6% coverage on average between naive and production retrieval. And models benefit from retrieval selectively — parametric Claude-Sonnet-4 trails Gemini-2.5-Pro, but once both retrieve, Claude pulls ahead. Raw model strength and retrieval plumbing are separate axes.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

ResearchQA's ambition — a 75-field scholarly-QA benchmark with grading rubrics, generated without per-query expert labor — is genuinely novel, and the authors do more self-auditing than most benchmark papers. But the design has load-bearing assumptions worth poking at, and the paper is admirably explicit about several of them.

The "70% wall" is partly a length artifact. Coverage is a recall measure, and recall rises with verbosity. Under the headline setup, answers are capped at 250 words; remove that cap and deep-research answers balloon — sonar-deep-research grows +435% in length (267 → 1431 words) and its coverage jumps +9.1 points to 85.3%, plateauing near 85% around 2K words (Figure 6). So "no system covers the rubric" is true under a specific length normalization; the more honest framing is that concise answers leave criteria on the table. The 250-word cap is a defensible choice (it controls a known length bias and matches the ~289-word average of human-written answers in ScholarQABench), but the leaderboard ordering and the headroom narrative both ride on it.

One model family builds and grades the test. Queries, rubrics, the hallucination filter, and the production judge are all gpt-4.1-mini. The paper cites the self-preference literature (Panickssery et al.) and the risk is real: a gpt-4.1-mini judge scoring gpt-4.1 / gpt-4o answers shares blind spots and vocabulary with them. Two things soften it — the top system (Perplexity's sonar) is not OpenAI, and the judge is validated against humans (Pearson .63 on coverage; 74% ensemble agreement). The judge tracks experts to within 0.11 on average coverage, but it over-counts the top bucket — rating items "completely covered" 26.0% of the time versus experts' 12.3% — so per-item coverage reads high at the extremes even where the rankings hold.

Rubric correctness is only lightly checked. "Hallucination removal" verifies that a cited paper is matchable on Google Scholar — not that the criterion is right or that the survey's claim is sound. Parametric rubrics carry a 15% vacuous/error rate (hybrid is cleaner, and 87% of items pass expert muster), but rubric validity ultimately inherits the subjectivity of the source surveys, which the authors acknowledge. And the 89.3% "failure" on citation items partly reflects how punishing a named-citation criterion is under a 250-word cap — it may over-penalize rather than purely measure a model deficiency, though such items are only 8.3% of the rubric.

baselines

Broad and current — 18 systems across parametric, naive/production retrieval, and two commercial deep-research APIs (o4-mini, sonar), with frontier models (gpt-4.1, gemini-2.5-pro, claude-4-sonnet). A fair spread of test-time effort tiers.

contamination

Directly studied. Source surveys are blocked from retrieval where controllable; post-hoc 6-gram title matching finds 20–30% leakage in production systems but only ~1.1% aggregate coverage gain (up in 8/13 systems, down in 5/13). Small, but unverifiable for closed retrieval stacks.

what's missed

Coverage is recall, so it rewards length and says nothing about precision, factuality, or hallucinated citations. The 250-word cap, not model ceilings, sets the "70% wall." Expert validation covers only 8 of 75 fields (4 biomed annotators stand in for 44% of the data).

would raise confidence

A non-OpenAI model regenerating rubrics and judging; a length-controlled leaderboard as the headline; a precision/factuality metric alongside coverage; expert validation spread beyond NLP/CV into the long tail of fields.

// bottom line

A clever, well-instrumented attempt to escape the expert bottleneck, and the most field-diverse scholarly-QA benchmark to date. Treat Coverage % as a length-sensitive recall score from a single-model judge, not an absolute quality grade — but the central findings (retrieval must be engineered, deep-research tools dominate, citations and comparisons are the hard part, and most science is under-evaluated) are robust to those caveats.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. Surveys are a free supply of expertise. Mining 54K survey articles yields 21K queries and 160K query-specific rubric items across 75 fields — escaping the per-query expert-annotation bottleneck that pins prior scholarly-QA benchmarks to a few CS-adjacent domains.
  2. Rubrics make LLM judges usable. Folding rubric coverage into an ensemble judge raises agreement with expert preferences from 71%/64% to 74%/69% and cuts the gap to the 84% human ceiling by 24% relative — even letting a small open judge match a frontier one.
  3. Everyone has headroom, and the metric has a thumb on the scale. No parametric or retrieval system clears 70% coverage (the best, a deep-research tool, reaches just 75%) and citations are the weakest criterion (89% not fully covered) — but coverage is recall, so the "70% wall" softens to ~85% once the 250-word cap is lifted. Read the leaderboard as length-normalized recall from a gpt-4.1-mini judge.
