A Baseline for Detecting Misclassified and Out-of-Distribution Examples in Neural Networks

Feed random Gaussian noise into an MNIST classifier and it answers with 91% confidence. This paper turns that embarrassing fact into a fix: take the network's own maximum softmax probability, flip it around, and you have a free, zero-retraining score for catching wrong predictions and out-of-distribution inputs — tested across vision, NLP, and speech, and still the reference floor every OOD detector published since has had to beat.

Dan Hendrycks, Kevin Gimpel

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

By 2016, deep classifiers were routinely deployed on inputs their training distribution never anticipated, and they had a specific, dangerous failure mode: they don't fail loudly. A network shown an image, sentence, or audio clip unlike anything it trained on tends to still emit a confident, peaked prediction rather than a shrug. The paper's own illustration of this is blunt: random Gaussian noise fed into a trained MNIST digit classifier gets assigned a maximum softmax ("predicted class") probability of 91% — an authoritative-looking answer to a question the network has no business answering at all. Amodei et al.'s "Concrete Problems in AI Safety" had just named this as a first-class AI safety issue; a medical model that confidently misdiagnoses instead of flagging uncertainty, or a self-driving perception stack that is sure about a scene it has never seen, is the practical version of the same problem.

Yet going into this paper, there was no established, easy-to-adopt baseline for either half of the problem: predicting whether a given prediction is wrong (error detection), or predicting whether an input comes from a different distribution than training (OOD detection). Researchers proposing new detection methods had nothing standard to compare against, no agreed evaluation protocol (accuracy is actively misleading here — a detector that always guesses "normal" can look great when abnormal examples are rare), and no common suite of tasks spanning more than one modality. The paper's contribution is explicitly to fill that gap: a simple, free method requiring no retraining and no extra data, paired with a rigorous evaluation protocol (AUROC/AUPR rather than raw accuracy), validated across computer vision, NLP, and speech recognition — so that anyone proposing something fancier has a floor to beat and a fair way to measure the beating.

// how it works

The method

The baseline itself needs no new training, no auxiliary data, and no architecture change: for any classifier that already ends in a softmax, take the maximum softmax probability (MSP) — the probability the network assigns to its own predicted class — and use it directly as a confidence proxy. Correctly classified and in-distribution examples tend to produce a high MSP; incorrect and out-of-distribution examples tend to produce a lower one. Flip the sign (or equivalently, threshold from the other side) and MSP becomes an anomaly score: the lower the max softmax probability, the more likely the example is misclassified or out-of-distribution. That's the entire mechanism — it reuses information the network was already computing for its forward pass.

The harder part is defining a fair way to score a detector. The paper rejects raw accuracy outright, since detection is a heavily imbalanced binary problem (abnormal examples are usually the minority class) where a lazy always-normal detector can look deceptively good. Instead it adopts two threshold-independent metrics: the Area Under the ROC curve (AUROC) — interpretable as the probability a random positive example scores higher than a random negative one, with 50% as chance and 100% as perfect — and the Area Under the Precision-Recall curve (AUPR), which is more sensitive to class imbalance since a random detector's AUPR tracks the base rate of the positive class rather than sitting fixed at 50%. The paper reports both directions of AUPR: treating the normal/correct class as positive ("Succ"/"In") and treating the abnormal/incorrect class as positive ("Err"/"Out"), since which one is "positive" changes the number substantially under class imbalance.

anomaly score = 1 − maxk p(y = k | x) // p(y=k|x): the network's softmax output; the max over classes k is the MSP; AUROC/AUPR are then computed by treating this score as the detector's output over held-out examples

To validate the baseline as broadly as possible, the paper defines standard tasks across three areas rather than sticking to vision alone. In computer vision: MNIST, CIFAR-10, and CIFAR-100 classifiers, with out-of-distribution examples drawn from SUN scenes, Omniglot handwritten characters, notMNIST typeface glyphs, grayscale CIFAR-10, and synthetic Gaussian/uniform noise. In NLP: IMDB binary sentiment classification (with Customer Review and Movie Review datasets as OOD), 20 Newsgroups / Reuters 8 / Reuters 52 text categorization (holding out unseen subject categories as OOD), and part-of-speech tagging on the Penn Treebank WSJ corpus and on tweets (with Twitter and English Web Treebank weblog data crossed as OOD for each other). In speech: a CTC-trained LSTM phone recognizer on TIMIT, with noise-corrupted TIMIT (eight Aurora-2 noise types) and Chinese speech (THCHS-30) as OOD.

Section 4 goes further and asks whether a network can be given something more powerful than its own softmax to score itself with. The abnormality module attaches an auxiliary decoder that reconstructs the input from the network's internal representations; once trained, its later layers are frozen and a small additional "scorer" network is trained on top — fed both the frozen classifier's features and the decoder's own confusion signal (how well it reconstructs a corrupted input) — to output a single normal/abnormal probability. Unlike the softmax baseline, this does require extra training and negative (corrupted) examples to learn from, so it trades the "free" property for a stronger signal.

// what they found

The results

Across every task in every modality, the maximum softmax probability reliably separates the two classes it's asked to separate — far above the random-guess "Base" rate reported alongside every number. For error detection (Table 1), AUROC/Base is 97/50 on MNIST, 93/50 on CIFAR-10, and 87/50 on CIFAR-100 — degrading as the underlying classifier's own test error rises (1.69% → 4.96% → 20.7%), exactly as expected since a harder classification problem gives the detector messier signal to work with. The same pattern holds in NLP (82/50 AUROC on IMDB sentiment, 89–91/50 across the three text-categorization datasets, 96/50 on WSJ POS tagging) — reliable error detection with nothing but the classifier's own confidence.

For OOD detection (Table 2), results are similarly strong on vision: CIFAR-10 vs. SUN images reaches 95/50 AUROC and 97/50 AUPR-Out; CIFAR-10 vs. all OOD sources combined reaches 96/50 AUROC; MNIST vs. Gaussian/Uniform noise reaches 90/50 and 99/50 respectively. The paper is explicit about a wrinkle here, though: the mean predicted probability on OOD examples is also often high in absolute terms (72–91% across the vision OOD rows) — softmax probability alone is a poor calibrated confidence estimate — yet OOD examples are still reliably lower than in-distribution examples in relative terms, which is all AUROC/AUPR require. NLP and speech follow the same shape: IMDB vs. Customer/Movie Reviews reaches 94–95/50 AUROC; TIMIT vs. eight distinct noise types clusters at 98–100/50 AUROC (average 97/50 across noise conditions), while the harder, more subtle WSJ-vs-Weblog OOD pair — described by the authors as "atypically close to the training distribution" — falls to just 61/50 AUROC, the weakest result in the paper and an early, explicit hint that MSP struggles precisely when the OOD shift is small.

Section 4's abnormality module is where the paper demonstrates its own baseline can be beaten. On TIMIT, training the module on clean vs. white/brown/pink-noised examples and testing on eight held-out Aurora-2 distortions the module never saw raises the average AUROC from 80% (plain softmax) to 97%, and average AUPR-Out from 80% to 98% (Table 10). On MNIST, averaged across five OOD sources (Omniglot, notMNIST, CIFAR-10bw, Gaussian, Uniform), the abnormality module pushes AUROC from 93% to a saturated 100%, and AUPR-In/AUPR-Out from 94%/94% to 100%/100% (Table 11). The one case where the module's edge doesn't hold: a fully-connected TIMIT network whose accuracy barely degrades under distortion (29.69% → 30.43% frame error) gives the plain softmax baseline almost nothing to work with, but the abnormality module still separates clean from distorted audio — the paper's evidence that richer internal signals can catch abnormality even when the classifier's own confidence can't.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

As a piece of evaluation methodology, this paper's core contribution has aged extremely well: AUROC/AUPR over Succ/Err/In/Out splits is still exactly how OOD detection papers report results a decade later, and "the MSP baseline" is still the row every new method's table has to include and beat. As a detector, though, MSP is now a famously weak one — and the paper's own scope reveals why, honestly, without the paper claiming otherwise (it is explicitly billed as a baseline, not a solution).

The OOD sets tested are mostly easy, "far" shifts. SUN scenes, Omniglot characters, notMNIST typefaces, and synthetic Gaussian/uniform noise are all dramatically different in low-level statistics from MNIST/CIFAR — exactly the regime where a crude confidence signal should work best. The one pairing that resembles a harder, "near" distribution shift — WSJ vs. Weblog text, which the authors themselves flag as "atypically close to the training distribution" — is also the paper's single weakest result (61/50 AUROC). That's a real tell: subsequent research (ODIN, Mahalanobis-distance detectors, energy-based scores) exists largely because MSP's headline numbers here don't transfer to the harder near-OOD regime the field cares about most.

The gap to what came next is large and now quantified by others. ODIN (Liang, Li & Srikant, 2017), which adds temperature scaling and input perturbation on top of the exact same softmax signal used here, reports reducing the false-positive rate at 95% true-positive rate from this paper's baseline 34.7% to 4.3% on a DenseNet/CIFAR-10 setup — an eightfold reduction in false alarms for the same detection budget, using only post-hoc tricks and no retraining. Mahalanobis-distance detectors, energy-based scores, and learned-confidence heads (DeVries & Taylor) all followed with further gains. MSP is not obsolete as a baseline — it's obsolete as a solution, and the paper's own framing ("we hope future research surpasses [this baseline]") anticipated exactly that trajectory.

Calibration and detection are shown to diverge, but the tension isn't resolved. The paper's own "Pred. Prob Wrong (mean)" numbers are a built-in admission that MSP is not a good calibrated confidence estimate in isolation — wrongly classified MNIST examples still average 86% predicted probability, CIFAR-10 examples 80%, IMDB 74% — even though the detector's relative ranking (AUROC) is strong. The paper correctly diagnoses this split between "useful as a ranking signal" and "useful as an absolute probability," but doesn't attempt to fix the latter; that had to wait for calibration-focused work like Guo et al.'s temperature scaling, published a few months later.

baselines

Fair for what the paper claims to be: a floor, not a competitor. It compares MSP only against the random-guess base rate, not against contemporaneous uncertainty methods like MC-dropout or the deep ensembles paper posted the same month (Lakshminarayanan et al., Dec 2016) — a reasonable scope for "here is a baseline," but it means the paper never shows whether MSP is competitive with the other simple options available at the time.

contamination

Not a modern leakage concern — every task reports disjoint train/test splits, and OOD sources are genuinely external datasets (SUN, Omniglot, notMNIST, THCHS-30) rather than held-out slices of the same corpus, so there's no risk of the "OOD" set secretly overlapping the training distribution.

what's missed

Near-OOD stress-testing (same-domain, different-class distribution shift) is essentially absent — the one near-OOD pair tested (WSJ/Weblog) is also the weakest result and isn't dwelled on; no variance/error bars across random seeds; no head-to-head against other 2016-era uncertainty baselines.

would raise confidence

A deliberate near-OOD suite (e.g. CIFAR-10 vs. CIFAR-100, which later papers made standard); repeated-seed AUROC/AUPR variance; and a direct comparison against MC-dropout or deep ensembles rather than only the random-guess base rate.

// bottom line

The evaluation protocol (AUROC/AUPR over Succ/Err/In/Out, tested across vision, NLP, and speech) is the durable contribution and still standard practice. The detector itself is a decade-old, thoroughly-beaten floor — ODIN alone cuts false positives at 95% recall from 34.7% to 4.3% on the same setup — but that's precisely why it's still cited: every OOD detection paper since has needed a free, zero-retraining reference point to report and beat, and this is that reference point.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. The maximum softmax probability (MSP) — one minus a classifier's own top predicted-class probability — is a free, zero-retraining anomaly score for both misclassification detection and out-of-distribution detection, usable on any already-trained softmax classifier.
  2. The paper's lasting contribution is the evaluation protocol as much as the detector: AUROC/AUPR (not accuracy) over Succ/Err/In/Out splits, validated across vision (MNIST/CIFAR), NLP (IMDB, 20 Newsgroups/Reuters, POS tagging), and speech (TIMIT) — still the standard way OOD detection papers report results.
  3. MSP works best on "far" distribution shifts (SUN scenes, synthetic noise, unrelated typefaces) and visibly weakens on "near" shifts (its own WSJ-vs-Weblog result, 61/50 AUROC) — the exact gap that ODIN, Mahalanobis-distance detectors, and energy-based scores were built to close, with ODIN alone cutting the false-positive rate at 95% recall from 34.7% to 4.3% on the same kind of setup.
