Beyond Goldfish Memory: Long-Term Open-Domain Conversation

State-of-the-art open-domain chatbots were trained and evaluated on single-session conversations of 2–15 turns, with encoders truncated to 128 tokens. MSC crowdsources human-human conversations spread across up to 5 sessions spaced hours-to-days apart, annotated with per-turn summaries that stand in as "extended personas," and shows retrieval- and summarization-augmented models beat plain encoder-decoder fine-tuning on both perplexity and human evaluation — the largest multi-session dialogue dataset before LoCoMo (2024) targeted the same gap at real multi-month scale.

Jing Xu, Arthur Szlam, Jason Weston · Facebook AI Research

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

Commonly used dialogue training and evaluation resources are large in example count but short in conversation length — typically 2–15 turns, confined to a single session. Partly for that reason, the leading open-domain systems of the time, Meena and BlenderBot, employ Transformers with encoder truncation lengths of only 128 tokens, which the authors note are "clearly incapable of incorporating long-term conversational context." A successfully deployed bot, by contrast, engages the same user across many conversations over time — capturing organic interest is exactly what drives continual reengagement — and the standard architectures were never built or tested for that setting. As the paper puts it: "it is unclear how well these models will perform on long or multi-session open-domain conversations," and, to the authors' knowledge, no public dataset existed to study the question at all.

Table 2 makes the gap concrete. Every prior open-domain dialogue dataset the paper compares against is confined to a single session: Pushshift Reddit (25.4 tokens/utterance, but only 3.2 utterances/episode), PersonaChat (11.9 tok/utt, 14.7 utts/episode), LIGHT (18.3, 12.9), Wizard of Wikipedia (19.7, 9.0), DailyDialog (14.5, 3.9), and Empathetic Dialogues (15.3, 2.6) — all "sessions per episode = 1." MSC's training data spans 3 sessions / 40.4 utterances per episode (4,000 episodes) or 4 sessions / 53.3 utterances per episode (1,001 episodes), and validation/test conversations extend to 5 sessions averaging ~66 utterances — pushing full-conversation context to an average of 1,614 tokens under the BlenderBot BPE tokenizer, more than 12× BlenderBot's own 128-token truncation window.

// how it works

The method

MSC is both a dataset-collection paper and a modeling paper: it introduces the Multi-Session Chat crowdsourcing protocol, then studies three long-context architectures fine-tuned on the resulting data.

Collecting Multi-Session Chat

Crowdworkers are assigned a persona to role-play rather than speaking about themselves — this mitigates privacy concerns and keeps conversations diverse even when the same worker appears in multiple chats. The personas are the 1,155 crowdsourced by Zhang et al. (2018) for PersonaChat, with validation and test drawing from a disjoint persona pool. Session 1 of every episode is literally an existing PersonaChat conversation — two strangers getting acquainted for the first time. For sessions 2 and onward, a random elapsed time is sampled (1–7 hours or 1–7 days) and workers are told to "chitchat with another worker for 6 turns, as if you were catching up since last time you two spoke," re-playing the same roles (though not necessarily the same individual workers) and expanding on prior shared details rather than starting fresh.

Two training splits were collected: 4,000 episodes running 3 sessions, and 1,001 episodes running 4 sessions; validation and test extend to 5 sessions, deliberately beyond the training distribution. After each session — including session 1 — a separate crowdworker task summarizes any new pertinent information into short sentences (or the label no_summary if a turn adds nothing new). These gold summaries, rather than the full session transcripts, are shown to workers in the next session as the primary reference — functioning as living, growing extensions of the original personas.

Three long-context architectures

All three approaches fine-tune BST 2.7B, the pre-trained model behind BlenderBot, on MSC:

1. Extended-truncation encoder-decoder. The simplest baseline just widens the input window: BST 2.7B's 128-token positional embeddings are extended to 256, 512, or 1024 tokens, with the new embedding parameters trained alongside the rest of the network during fine-tuning.

2. Retrieval augmentation. RAG (Lewis et al., 2020) scores memory items with a DPR bi-encoder and feeds the top matches to the decoder; FiD (Izacard & Grave, 2020) instead encodes each of the top-N retrieved items separately and lets the decoder attend across all of them, using either a plain pre-trained DPR retriever ("FiD") or the RAG-trained retriever ("FiD-RAG"). Memory items were tried as either individual dialogue utterances or whole sessions/session-summaries; whole sessions worked better and are used in the reported results, with N ∈ {3, 5, 6} tuned per method on validation.

3. Summarization memory augmentation (SumMem-MSC), the paper's proposed method. Storing raw dialogue history has two costs: there is a lot of it to retrieve from, and no processing has distilled what actually matters. SumMem-MSC instead trains a supervised encoder-decoder summarizer on MSC's own gold summaries — for each turn, deciding whether new pertinent information should be written to long-term memory, including the no_summary case. Because 42% of turns carry the no_summary label, training on the raw label distribution over-produces empty summaries at decoding time; the paper counters this by subsampling the no_summary class during training at rates K ∈ {5%, 25%, 50%, 100%}. A second component — reusing the same RAG/FiD/FiD-RAG retrieval machinery from step 2 — then retrieves from this compressed summary memory instead of raw utterances to generate the next response.

The results

Previous-session context helps, and the effect is far more pronounced on the turns that need it most. With no session history at all, a BST 2.7B model averages 9.08 perplexity across sessions 1–4 (at 1024-token truncation); adding raw dialogue history from earlier sessions drops that to 8.89, and gold session summaries to 8.80 — summaries edge out raw history at every context length tested. The gap widens sharply on session openings specifically — the re-engagement turn where a speaker reconnects using something learned earlier — where no-history context scores 9.96–10.99 across sessions 2–5 versus 6.98–7.96 for gold summaries, roughly a 2–3 perplexity-point swing. Ablating the summary model shows removing the time-elapsed feature barely matters (9.05 vs. 9.04), but dropping either the partner's or the speaker's own summary hurts more, with the self-summary mattering slightly more; a trained summarizer predicting its own summaries (rather than being fed gold ones) lands, as expected, between raw-history and gold-summary performance.

Because 42% of turns in the human data carry no summary at all, training the summarizer without correcting for this over-predicts empty summaries (a naive model reaches 61.8% no_summary sparsity at decoding, versus the human ground truth of 42%). Subsampling the no_summary class during training at K = 25% lands closest to the human rate (41.4% predicted sparsity) and — along with the lower K = 5% setting, which gives marginally better perplexity — meaningfully outperforms training on the raw, unsampled distribution. Separately, an ablation over how much MSC training data is used (Table 6) shows a large ~1.5-point perplexity gain from using more than one training session (10.5 → 8.94 average), but the gains taper fast: going from sessions 1–3 to the full 1–4 training set buys only a further 0.03 points on average.

The retrieval-augmented and summarization-memory models both clear the plain-truncation baseline, though by modest margins on raw perplexity (e.g., SumMem-MSC 2.7B FiD-RAG's 8.70 on session 2 vs. MSC 2.7B truncate-1024's 8.76). The paper is candid that these deltas are small; the architectural case for retrieval, rather than truncation, is that a retrieval-augmented memory "essentially never forgets the conversation, no matter how long it gets," where a fixed truncation window necessarily does. SumMem-MSC 2.7B (FiD-RAG) is the best configuration tested, and the gap is largest exactly where it matters — session openings — at 7.87 versus BlenderBot's 12.92.

Human evaluation (crowdworkers chatting live with each model for a simulated 5th session) tells a sharper story. All MSC-trained models beat BlenderBot on raw engaging-response rate and final rating, but by the paper's own significance test (t-test, p < 0.05), only the three SumMem-MSC variants clear significance against the BST 2.7B baseline — the plain-truncation MSC 2.7B models do not, despite scoring higher in the raw numbers.

The clearest signal is in the "referenced partner's topic" column: BlenderBot references something the partner said in an earlier session only 14.5% of the time; SumMem-MSC 2.7B (RAG) does so 33.8% of the time — more than double — while "referenced own topic" barely moves across models. That asymmetry is the paper's evidence that summarization memory specifically helps a model track and reuse what the other person said, not just its own prior statements.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

MSC identifies a genuine, previously untested gap and backs it with fresh crowdsourced human-human data rather than a repackaging of an existing corpus — a stronger provenance story than later LLM-generated long-dialogue benchmarks would have. It also does something many benchmark papers skip: it runs an actual significance test on its human-evaluation numbers, and reports the result honestly even where it complicates the story.

That significance test is worth reading carefully, because the paper's own abstract slightly outruns it. The abstract states plainly that "both techniques [retrieval-augmented and summarize-and-recall methods] outperform the standard encoder-decoder architectures" — true in raw numbers, but Table 8's bolding shows only the three SumMem-MSC variants are statistically significant against BlenderBot on engaging-response rate and final rating (p < 0.05); the plain-truncation MSC 2.7B models, despite also beating BlenderBot numerically (56.5%/3.29 and 54.2%/3.47 vs. 53.0%/3.14), do not clear that bar. A reader who takes the abstract at face value would credit truncation-only fine-tuning with more than the paper's own statistics support.

The baseline set is also narrow by construction. Every model compared is BST 2.7B fine-tuned some way — extended-truncation, RAG, FiD, FiD-RAG, or SumMem-MSC — against a single external reference point, vanilla BlenderBot. The Related Work section name-checks genuine long-context Transformer architectures (Longformer, Reformer, Sparse Transformers) as alternative solutions to the same truncation problem, but none of them is ever benchmarked; the paper never tests whether a model with real long-range attention, and no retrieval or summarization machinery at all, would close some of the gap on its own. And Session 1 of every single MSC episode is literally the pre-existing, widely-used PersonaChat dataset reused verbatim — "new data collection" starts at session 2, not session 1.

The dataset's core "long-term" claim is also narrower than the title suggests. Sessions are gapped by a simulated 1–7 hours or 1–7 days — the paper is explicit that "those lengths of time do not actually transpire in reality before the next chat session begins" — and validation/test conversations cap at 5 sessions and roughly 66 utterances total. There is no inter-annotator agreement reported for the engaging-response/topic-reference checkboxes crowdworkers filled in per turn, and no confidence intervals accompany any perplexity or human-eval number in the paper. This is precisely the ceiling LoCoMo (2024) — which uses MSC as its own direct Table 1 comparison point — was built to push past, scaling to 19.3 sessions and ~305 turns over genuinely simulated multi-month spans.

baselines

A single family: every result is BST 2.7B fine-tuned some way, compared to one external reference point (vanilla BlenderBot). Named long-context alternatives (Longformer, Reformer, Sparse Transformers) are cited but never benchmarked.

contamination

Not pretraining leakage, but every episode's Session 1 is the pre-existing public PersonaChat dataset reused as-is — "new" data collection begins at session 2.

what's missed

The abstract's "both techniques outperform" claim overstates what Table 8's own significance test supports — only the summarization-memory variants, not plain-truncation MSC 2.7B, are statistically significant vs. BlenderBot. No inter-annotator agreement or confidence intervals reported anywhere.

would raise confidence

A genuine long-context-architecture baseline with no retrieval/summarization; confidence intervals on the perplexity tables; inter-annotator agreement for the human-eval checkboxes; testing past 5 sessions to see if the retrieval/summarization advantage grows or shrinks at real long-term scale.

// bottom line

MSC correctly diagnosed a real hole in dialogue evaluation and shipped the first public dataset to test it, backed by an honest (if under-emphasized) significance test that its own headline framing doesn't fully carry through to the abstract. But "long-term" here means 5 simulated sessions of ~14 turns spaced hours-to-days apart, evaluated against a single fine-tuned model family — read it as the paper that made multi-session memory a testable question, not as a precisely calibrated measurement of how large the gap is, a job LoCoMo would take up two years later at a genuinely larger scale.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. Summarization memory wins, and it's the only variant that's statistically proven to. SumMem-MSC 2.7B (FiD-RAG) is the strongest configuration (7.87 session-opening perplexity vs. BlenderBot's 12.92), and on human evaluation only the three SumMem-MSC variants clear the paper's own p<0.05 significance test against BlenderBot — the plain-truncation MSC 2.7B models do not, despite the abstract's "both techniques outperform" framing.
  2. MSC is the first dataset built specifically for multi-session dialogue. Training episodes run 3–4 sessions (40.4–53.3 utterances/episode), validation/test run 5 sessions (~66 utterances), each new session annotated with crowdsourced summaries functioning as growing "extended personas" — versus 2.6–14.7 utterances and exactly 1 session for every prior open-domain dialogue dataset compared.
  3. Referencing the partner's history, not just your own, is the tell. BlenderBot references something the partner said in an earlier session only 14.5% of the time in human eval; SumMem-MSC 2.7B (RAG) does so 33.8% of the time — the clearest behavioral signature that summarization memory changes what the model actually uses, not just how fluent it sounds.
  4. "Long-term" here means 5 simulated sessions, not months. Sessions are gapped by a simulated 1–7 hours/days (the paper is explicit these gaps don't actually elapse in real time), and every episode's session 1 is the pre-existing PersonaChat dataset reused verbatim. This is exactly the ceiling LoCoMo (2024) — which lists MSC as its own Table 1 comparison point — was built to push past, scaling to 19.3 sessions and ~305 turns over genuinely simulated multi-month spans.
