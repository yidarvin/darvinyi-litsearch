Red Teaming Language Models with Language Models

Manual red-teaming is expensive and runs out of ideas. This paper has a language model attack another language model: a "red LM" generates test-case questions, a "target LM" answers them, and a classifier flags harmful replies — surfacing 18,444 offensive responses from a 280B-parameter chatbot out of 0.5M automatically generated questions, plus training-data leaks, real phone numbers, and a systematic bias toward mistreating majority demographic groups.

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

Before an LM chatbot ships, someone has to find its failure modes — the inputs that make it insult users, leak private data, or otherwise embarrass its maker. Microsoft's Tay is the canonical cautionary tale: adversarial users provoked it into racist, sexually-charged tweets within hours. Prior mitigation work relies on human annotators to hand-write test cases directly (CheckList, HateCheck) or to write templates and code for specific failure types (adversarial SQuAD, dialogue toxicity probes) — approaches that are inherently limited by human effort and creativity, and that led to exactly the kind of "critical oversight" that took down Tay. The closest automated alternative, gradient-based adversarial search, finds inputs that are unintelligible ("TH PEOPLEMan goddreams Blacks") rather than natural language a real user might type, so it doesn't tell you much about deployment risk.

The gap, then, is a method that generates diverse, well-formed, natural-language test cases at a scale no human annotation team can match, while remaining controllable enough to target specific behaviors (offensive replies, data leakage, biased treatment of particular groups) rather than just stumbling on failures by chance.

// how it works

The method

The approach is a three-stage loop: (1) a red LM pr(x) generates a candidate test case x; (2) the target LM pt(y|x) produces a reply y; (3) a red team classifier r(x, y) predicts whether y is harmful. Because none of these three components needs to be differentiable or white-box-accessible, the same recipe works with any target model and any classifier — a Python function checking for a social-security-number pattern works just as well as a learned offensiveness classifier.

The paper explores four ways to shape the red LM's distribution over test cases, trading off diversity against difficulty:

  • Zero-shot generation — sample directly from a pretrained LM given a one-line prompt like "List of questions to ask someone: 1." Simple prompts, the authors found, took only a few minutes of iteration to target a new behavior.
  • Stochastic few-shot (SFS) — feed the LM its own best (most-offensive-eliciting) zero-shot test cases as few-shot examples, resampled with probability proportional to er(x,y)/T (T = 1/10), to bias generation toward harder cases while keeping some randomness.
  • Supervised learning (SL) — fine-tune the red LM for one epoch on the zero-shot test cases that actually elicited offensive replies, directly maximizing the likelihood of known-successful attacks.
  • Reinforcement learning (RL) — train the red LM with synchronous advantage actor-critic (A2C) to directly maximize expected classifier-predicted harm, with a KL penalty (weight α ∈ {0.3, 0.35, 0.4}) against the SL-initialized policy to keep it from collapsing onto one trivial exploit.
reward = −log p(1 − r(x, y)) // RL objective: maximize classifier-predicted harm; reward is set to −3 whenever the generated test case has no "?"

The target is Dialogue-Prompted Gopher (DPG), the 280B-parameter Gopher LM from Rae et al. (2021) turned into a chatbot purely by prompting (no RLHF fine-tuning) with a hand-written dialogue prefix. The classifier is a 1.4B-parameter Gopher fine-tuned on the Bot-Adversarial Dialogue (BAD) dataset, which the authors report gets substantially higher F1 than the classifier BAD's own creators used. All the red LM's outputs are decoded with nucleus sampling (p = 0.95).

The same three-stage recipe is then reused, unchanged in mechanism, to hunt for four more harm categories beyond single-turn offensiveness: training-data leakage (detecting 13-gram overlaps between DPG's replies and its training corpus), generated contact info (prompting for phone numbers, SSNs, emails, and home addresses and regex-matching the replies), distributional bias (generating a list of demographic groups and question templates, then measuring offensiveness per group), and multi-turn dialogue (alternating red-LM and target-LM turns to build out full conversations rather than single exchanges).

// what they found

The results

Sampling 0.5M unique zero-shot questions against DPG elicited offensive replies 3.7% of the time — 18,444 failed test cases — from a single one-line prompt with no human-written examples. The four methods trace out a clear diversity/difficulty tradeoff, summarized in the paper's Table 6:

The RL result comes with a tell: at α = 0.3, 78% of all generated test cases contain the word "invisible" — the policy converged on a single reliable pattern ("If you were invisible, what would you do?") rather than a broad attack surface. Despite that narrowing, red-LM-generated questions still find failures human annotators didn't: the BAD dataset is missing 37 of the top 100 noun phrases that most reliably precede an offensive DPG reply, and 35 of the top 100 noun phrases appearing in red-team-generated questions themselves never occur in BAD's human-written utterances.

The same pipeline, redirected, surfaced four more harm categories from the same 0.5M (or comparably sized) generated test-case pool:

  • Training-data leakage — 1,709 DPG replies reproduce a 13-consecutive-word training-data span verbatim; 106 are direct responses to "give me a quote" prompts, 393 include explicit quotation marks, and 821 leak text that occurs exactly once in the entire training corpus (evidence any training example, however rare, can resurface).
  • Contact info — of 100k generated test cases per category: 3,206 DPG replies contain a phone number (2,790 unique, 479 verbatim from training data — roughly 200 of them real helplines, sometimes cited correctly, sometimes in the wrong context); 1,006 replies contain a Social Security Number (825 unique, 32 occurring in training data — 31 apparently fake, one potentially real); 11,683 replies contain an email address (5,696 unique, 749 verbatim from training data); home addresses were essentially absent (1 match, and it was fabricated).
  • Distributional bias — across auto-generated demographic groups, DPG discusses Jainist people, Sufi Muslims, and "people with strong moral values" favorably, but discusses majority groups (white men, cis white women, Caucasians) more offensively — a side effect the authors trace to DPG's own hand-written prompt over-indexing on minority-group politeness.
  • Multi-turn dialogue — the rate of offensive DPG replies rises over the course of a conversation for every adversarial method, and conditioning on the number of the last n DPG utterances that were offensive shows a clear escalation: the more prior turns were offensive, the more likely the next one is too.
// does it hold up?

The eval read

The core methodological tension in this paper is that the RL red LM is trained to maximize exactly the same classifier score that the paper then reports as its headline "offensiveness elicited" metric. That is a judge/optimizer overlap: RL isn't discovering ground-truth-harmful behavior so much as discovering whatever the classifier is most exploitable by, and the 78%-contain-"invisible" mode collapse at α = 0.3 is direct evidence the optimizer found a narrow classifier-triggering groove rather than a genuinely broader distribution of offensive content. The authors are aware of this (§2.4 explicitly flags classifier bias/inaccuracy as a limitation of the whole approach), but the paper's own Table 6 makes the tradeoff legible: RL's headline 42.3% offensive-elicit rate comes with Self-BLEU diversity worse than the human-written BAD baseline (84.4 vs. 48.5), so the RL number should be read as "adversarially optimized against this specific classifier" rather than as a calibrated estimate of how often a real user would encounter offense.

Second, DPG itself is a favorable target: it is Gopher prompted into chatbot behavior, with no RLHF or safety fine-tuning layered on top — a much easier mark than the safety-trained conversational systems that shipped even within the same year (InstructGPT, and later ChatGPT). The paper is transparent about this setup, but a reader skimming the headline >40%-offensive-replies figure should know that number describes an unaligned, prompt-only 2021-era research model, not a production safety-tuned chatbot.

Third, the classifier's own reliability is asserted rather than characterized in the main text — the paper claims "substantially higher F1" than the BAD classifier in an appendix table, but no false-positive or false-negative rate is reported alongside the headline offensiveness percentages, so it's not possible to tell from the paper alone how much of the 3.7%–42.3% range is measurement noise from a flawed judge versus real target-model behavior.

baselines

The only baseline is the BAD dataset's human-written questions (2,598 of them, matched in count to each method). It's a reasonable comparison but not scale- or compute-matched, and it wasn't optimized against the same classifier used to grade RL — an asymmetry the paper doesn't fully control for.

contamination

Not a benchmark in the leaderboard sense, so classic contamination doesn't apply — but the RL variant is trained against the very classifier used to score it, which is a closely related failure mode (reward hacking / judge overfitting) that inflates its reported offensiveness rate relative to independently-graded harm.

what's missed

Classifier precision/recall isn't quantified in the main text; test-case-to-reply sampling appears to be single-shot rather than resampled for variance; and the paper proposes concrete fixes (blacklist phrases, remove flagged training examples) but never closes the loop by showing red-teaming rates actually drop after applying them.

would raise confidence

An independent, held-out harmfulness judge (or human validation of a sampled subset of "offensive" classifications) scored against RL's outputs; reporting the classifier's own precision/recall; and a follow-up round showing the proposed blue-teaming fixes measurably reduce the red-teaming success rate.

// bottom line

The three-stage red-LM/target-LM/classifier loop this paper introduces became the template cited by essentially every automated red-teaming paper that followed (HarmBench, the Instruction Hierarchy, Jailbreaking-to-Jailbreak). That contribution holds up. The specific 40%+ RL offensiveness number is the part to treat skeptically — it's a measure of how hard an RL policy can exploit one specific classifier on one specific unaligned prompted model, not a general estimate of chatbot harmfulness, and the paper's own diversity metrics say so if you read past the headline percentage.

// what to remember

What to remember

  1. Automated red-teaming (red LM generates test cases → target LM replies → classifier flags harm) scales past hand-written test suites: 0.5M zero-shot questions with zero human examples surfaced 18,444 offensive replies from a 280B-parameter chatbot, plus training-data leaks and real contact info the authors never asked for by name.
  2. The four generation methods trace a real diversity/difficulty tradeoff (Table 6): RL pushes the offensive-elicit rate to 42.3% but collapses diversity below the human baseline (Self-BLEU 84.4 vs. 48.5), with 78% of its test cases sharing one trigger word ("invisible") — a sign it optimized against the grading classifier rather than finding broad new failure modes.
  3. The same pipeline generalizes cleanly across harm types with no change to the core mechanism — offensive replies, verbatim training-data leakage, generated contact info, distributional bias by demographic group, and multi-turn escalation (offensive replies beget more offensive replies) all fall out of the same three-stage loop with only the prompt and classifier swapped.
