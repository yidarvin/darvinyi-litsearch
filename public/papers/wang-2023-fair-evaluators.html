Large Language Models are not Fair Evaluators

The LLM-as-a-judge paradigm has a load-bearing flaw: GPT-4 quietly prefers whichever response it reads first, and ChatGPT prefers the second — so swapping the order of two answers can reverse the verdict. Vicuna-13B "beats" ChatGPT on 66 of 80 questions when a ChatGPT judge reads it second. Three cheap calibration tricks — reason-before-you-score, average-both-orders, and entropy-gated human review — pull both judges back to human-level agreement.

Peiyi Wang, Lei Li, Liang Chen, Zefan Cai, Dawei Zhu, Binghuai Lin, Yunbo Cao, Qi Liu, Tianyu Liu, Zhifang Sui · Peking University / HKU / Tencent Cloud AI

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

By mid-2023 the field had a measurement problem. N-gram metrics (BLEU, ROUGE) and even model-based ones (BERTScore, BARTScore) don't capture whether an open-ended answer is actually helpful; human evaluation does, but it's slow and expensive. The pragmatic compromise that swept through the literature was LLM-as-a-judge: hand a strong model — GPT-4 — a question and two candidate answers, and ask it to score and compare them. The Vicuna / MT-Bench pipeline made this the default, and it was already being used to rank models, build leaderboards, and synthesize preference data for training.

The catch is that LLMs are famously sensitive to how a prompt is phrased. So the authors ask a deceptively simple question: is the judge robust to a perturbation that should be completely irrelevant — the order in which the two candidates appear? The answer is no. As shown below, presenting the same two responses in the opposite order flips GPT-4's scores from 9–7 to 7–9, even when the prompt explicitly orders the model to ignore presentation order. A judge that can be hacked by a swap isn't just noisy — it silently contaminates every win rate, leaderboard, and preference label built on top of it.

// how it works

Measuring the bias, then calibrating it away

The diagnosis comes first. Using the standard three-slot template T(Q, R1, R2) from the Vicuna paradigm, the authors query the judge twice per example — once as T(q, r1, r2) and once with the responses swapped, T(q, r2, r1) — and define a Conflict Rate: the fraction of examples on which the two runs disagree. It is a pure self-consistency check; a perfectly fair judge would score 0%.

Conflict Rate = Σi 𝟙( ERr12i ≠ ERr21i )N // disagreement between the two orderings of the same pair, over N examples

The fix is a three-part calibration framework that keeps the same judge but changes how it is queried and aggregated:

1 · Multiple Evidence Calibration (MEC)

The vanilla template asks for the conclusion (the scores) first and an explanation afterward. In an autoregressive model that ordering is backwards: the score is committed before any reasoning that might justify it, so the explanation can't actually support it. MEC flips the template to generate the evaluation evidence first, then the scores, so the verdict is conditioned on the reasoning. To damp sampling noise it draws k = 3 such evaluations (at temperature 1) and ensembles them.

2 · Balanced Position Calibration (BPC)

MEC reasons better but doesn't remove the positional preference itself. BPC does the obvious robust thing: evaluate each candidate in both positions and average. Combined with MEC's k samples, each response gets 2k scores (k as Assistant 1, k as Assistant 2); the calibrated score is their mean, and the higher mean wins. Averaging the two orders cancels a position-1 (or position-2) bias by construction.

CSR = Σi=1k ( SiR + S′iR )2k // S and S′ = the response's scores in the first and second positions; average over both orders × k samples

3 · Human-in-the-Loop Calibration (HITLC)

Some pairs are genuinely too close for the model to call. HITLC decides when to ask a human by computing a Balanced Position Diversity Entropy (BPDE) over the 2k win/tie/lose verdicts: high entropy means the model keeps changing its mind, so the example is flagged for human review. Routing only the top-β% highest-entropy examples to annotators buys most of the accuracy of full human labeling at a fraction of the cost.

// what they found

The bias is real, asymmetric, and worst on close calls

The two judges are biased in opposite directions: GPT-4 favors the response in the first position, ChatGPT the response in the second. The effect is large enough to manufacture a winner. With ChatGPT as the judge, Vicuna-13B's win rate against ChatGPT's own answers swings from 2.5% when placed first to 82.5% when placed second — a 66-of-80 "victory" conjured purely by seating order. GPT-4 is steadier but not immune (51.3% → 23.8%), and its conflict rate of 46.3% on the same comparison means it contradicts itself on nearly half the pairs.

Crucially, the bias is quality-dependent. Vicuna vs Alpaca — a lopsided matchup — has a conflict rate of only 5.0% for GPT-4, because the gap is too wide for position to flip it. The damage concentrates where the two answers are genuinely close: binning GPT-4's pairs by score gap shows conflict lives almost entirely in the smallest gap (≤1) and vanishes by gap ≥3. Position bias is, in effect, a coin-flip the judge reaches for whenever it can't actually separate two answers.

Calibration recovers human-level agreement

The authors hand-annotate win/tie/lose for ChatGPT vs Vicuna-13B on all 80 Vicuna-benchmark questions (three annotators, majority vote) and score each method's accuracy and Cohen's κ against it. Human annotators themselves average 71.7% / 0.54. Stacking MEC and BPC lifts GPT-4 from a vanilla 52.7% to 62.5% (+9.8 points) and ChatGPT from 44.4% to 58.7% (+14.3) — and adding 20% human review via HITLC pushes GPT-4 to 73.8% (above the human average) and ChatGPT to 71.3%, while cutting annotation cost from $30 to $18.3, a 39% saving.

Two details argue the gain is really debiasing, not just variance reduction. First, MEC(k=3)+BPC(k=3) beats MEC(k=6) at equal sample budget — averaging across the two orders helps more than drawing six samples in one order. Second, ablations land on k=3 and temperature 0.6–1.0 as the sweet spot (both 0.2 and 1.4 are worse), and the framework transfers to a direct "comparing" template as well as the scoring one. A per-category breakdown shows MEC+BPC improves nearly every one of the nine question types, with the biggest lifts for ChatGPT on common-sense, coding, and math.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

The core finding — that LLM judges carry a real, exploitable positional bias, opposite in direction across models — is robust and has aged extremely well; "swap-and-average" is now table stakes for any serious LLM-as-judge setup. But the quantitative claims rest on a thin foundation worth naming.

The ground truth is 80 questions from a single benchmark, annotated by three of the co-authors. That ceiling is itself noisy and non-independent: the human annotators disagree enough that their average κ is only 0.54 (individual accuracies span 68.8%–76.3%), and they know the paper's hypothesis. So "ChatGPT reaches the human average" is a claim against a soft, in-house reference. The dramatic 66/80 flip is also partly a tie-breaking artifact: Figure 3 shows conflict is almost entirely confined to the score-gap-≤1 band, i.e. pairs the judge genuinely can't separate. Position bias is the real mechanism, but the headline magnitude is inflated by how many Vicuna-vs-ChatGPT answers are near-ties — and BPC's "elimination" of conflict is partly definitional (the paper marks conflict rate N/A under BPC, because averaging both orders removes it by construction).

Finally, the evidence is narrow in scope. Only two judges are tested (gpt-3.5-turbo-0301 and gpt-4, March 2023), the bias direction is idiosyncratic to each, and the candidate models (Vicuna-13B, Alpaca-13B) are 2023-era. The title says judges aren't "fair," but the demonstrated unfairness is specifically positional, on two-candidate scoring — verbosity bias, self-enhancement bias, and many-candidate ranking are out of frame (its concurrent sibling, MT-Bench, covers the former two).

baselines

Vanilla (conclusion-first) scoring from the Vicuna/MT-Bench paradigm is the right baseline, and the MEC(k=3)+BPC vs MEC(k=6) equal-budget comparison cleanly isolates debiasing from variance reduction. But only two judge models, both OpenAI, both early-2023.

contamination

Not the focus, but the human reference is self-annotated by the authors (knew the hypothesis, not blind to it beyond response order) — a confirmation-bias surface the paper doesn't fully neutralize.

what's missed

n=80, one benchmark; only positional bias of pairwise scoring; no current judges; the 66/80 figure conflates "position bias" with "forced choice on near-ties." BPC also doubles inference (two orders × k samples = 6 GPT-4 calls/pair).

would raise confidence

Independent, blinded annotators on a larger, more diverse set; more judge families (Claude, Gemini, open models); disentangling tie-breaking from position preference; and reporting how much downstream leaderboard rank actually moves after calibration.

// bottom line

A small, sharp paper whose mechanism outlived its numbers. The exact win-rate swings are tied to two 2023 OpenAI models and an 80-example self-annotated set, but the lesson is permanent: never trust a single-order LLM verdict on a close call — run both orders and average, and make the judge reason before it scores. Those two habits are now standard practice precisely because of results like this.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. LLM judges have an exploitable positional bias. GPT-4 prefers the first response, ChatGPT the second; swap the order and the verdict can flip. With ChatGPT judging, Vicuna-13B "beat" ChatGPT on 66 of 80 questions just by being placed second.
  2. Swap-and-average is the cheap, robust fix. Evaluating both orderings (BPC) and forcing the judge to reason before scoring (MEC) recovered +9.8 (GPT-4) and +14.3 (ChatGPT) points of human agreement at near-zero extra cost — and beat simply sampling more in one order.
  3. Bias bites hardest on close calls. Conflict concentrates where the two answers are near-equal (score gap ≤1) and vanishes when one is clearly better. A single-order verdict on a near-tie is barely better than a coin flip; entropy-gated human review (HITLC) catches exactly those cases.
