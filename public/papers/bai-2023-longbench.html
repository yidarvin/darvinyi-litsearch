LongBench: A Bilingual, Multitask Benchmark for Long Context Understanding

The first benchmark built to test whether LLMs actually use a long context rather than merely accept one as input — 21 datasets across six task categories, in English and Chinese, with a truncation ablation and a memorization-vs-context control baked into the evaluation itself.

Yushi Bai, Xin Lv, Jiajie Zhang, Hongchang Lyu, Jiankai Tang, Zhidian Huang, Zhengxiao Du, Xiao Liu, Aohan Zeng, Lei Hou, Yuxiao Dong, Jie Tang, Juanzi Li — Tsinghua University / Zhipu AI

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

By mid-2023, context windows were the hottest number on a model card. LongNet promised billion-token attention, RMT and Compressive Transformers offered recurrent memory, positional interpolation let Llama-family models stretch past their training length, and ChatGLM2-32k and LongChat-32k had already shipped 32k-token variants. What none of these efforts had was a benchmark that tested whether any of it actually worked — whether a model handed 20,000 words of context could use that context, as opposed to simply not crashing when given it.

The benchmarks that did exist for long text were narrow in ways that mattered. Long Range Arena (Tay et al., 2020) evaluated efficient Transformer architectures on synthetic, encoder-only tasks trained from scratch — useful for comparing attention mechanisms, useless for evaluating an instruction-tuned LLM's reading comprehension. SCROLLS and its successor ZeroSCROLLS covered real long-document tasks but stayed English-only and comparatively narrow in task variety. None of them addressed a second, quieter gap: the leading long-context model families of the moment — ChatGLM2, GLM-130B — were bilingual, and there was no benchmark that could evaluate long-context ability in Chinese at all, let alone hold English and Chinese versions of the same task to the same yardstick.

LongBench's answer is to build one benchmark broad enough to catch multiple failure modes at once: single- and multi-document reading comprehension, summarization, few-shot in-context learning, purpose-built synthetic stress tests, and repository-level code completion — all bilingual, all standardized into a single input/output format so evaluation stays fully automatic even at these lengths.

// how it works

The method

LongBench comprises 21 datasets across 6 task categories, in both English and Chinese: single-document QA (750 examples), multi-document QA (800), summarization (600), few-shot learning (800), synthetic tasks (600), and code completion (1,000) — 4,550 test instances in total. Average context length is 6,711 words for English data and 13,386 characters for Chinese data, with individual documents running to tens of thousands of tokens.

Every task is cast into a common triple: an input I (typically short — a question or instruction), a context C (the long sequence — a book, a set of Wikipedia passages, a meeting transcript, a code repository), and an answer A. Formalizing everything this way is what lets a single harness evaluate NarrativeQA book comprehension, GovReport summarization, and RepoBench-P code completion with the same pipeline.

The 21 datasets come from three sources: 6 are lifted directly from existing datasets (NarrativeQA, Qasper, TriviaQA, and others), 10 are adapted from existing datasets but restructured for the long-context setting (few-shot tasks like TREC and SAMSum are built by sampling and concatenating a variable number of training examples — 100 to 600 for TREC — into the context), and 5 are built and annotated from scratch by the authors. That last group includes MultiFieldQA-en/zh (documents from legal filings, government reports, and academic papers, annotated by PhD students with evidence placed at random positions to avoid a lost-in-the-middle bias) and three synthetic tasks — PassageRetrieval-en/zh and PassageCount — designed to isolate specific long-range dependency patterns rather than reuse whatever pattern an existing dataset happened to have.

PassageCount is the most demanding of these by design: the model is given a shuffled, repeated set of 17–50 Wikipedia passages and must report how many unique passages are present. Unlike a QA task, which can often be solved by finding one relevant chunk, PassageCount cannot be solved without aggregating the entire context.

Scoring is fully automatic — ROUGE-L for summarization, F1 for extractive QA, classification accuracy or exact-match for few-shot/synthetic tasks, and edit similarity for code — a deliberate choice to keep evaluation cheap at these lengths, at the cost of the metric-fidelity issues discussed below. A held-out 13-dataset subset, LongBench-E, is separately resampled so each dataset has a roughly even split of examples in the 0–4k, 4k–8k, and 8k+ word ranges, letting the authors read a model's degradation curve as length increases independent of which task it's doing.

The results

The authors benchmark 8 models current to mid-2023: the commercial GPT-3.5-Turbo-16k, and seven open 7B-scale models spanning different context-extension strategies — Llama2-7B-chat-4k, LongChat-v1.5-7B-32k, XGen-7B-8k, InternLM-7B-8k, ChatGLM2-6B, its scaled-position-embedding variant ChatGLM2-6B-32k, and Vicuna-v1.5-7B-16k.

The commercial model leads, but the more informative result is what happens when the same base architecture is given more context and continued training: ChatGLM2-6B-32k improves 62% relative over plain ChatGLM2-6B (25.7 → 41.4 overall), and LongChat-v1.5-7B-32k improves 19% relative over its Vicuna base — the paper's headline evidence that scaled positional embeddings plus continued long-sequence fine-tuning, not just a bigger stated context limit, is what moves the needle.

Two diagnostics do more work than the leaderboard itself. First, a truncation ablation: feeding GPT-3.5-Turbo-16k, ChatGLM2-6B-32k, and Vicuna-v1.5-7B-16k the same inputs truncated to 4k, 8k, or their full length shows GPT-3.5-Turbo-16k dropping from 44.7 → 44.2 → 39.1 and ChatGLM2-6B-32k dropping from 41.5 → 39.3 → 35.4 as context shrinks — proof both models are genuinely drawing on the extra tokens. Vicuna-v1.5-7B-16k, by contrast, barely moves (30.5 → 30.5 → 30.2): its score isn't context-limited at all, meaning its 16k context claim is doing essentially nothing for it on this benchmark.

Second, a memorization-vs-context control: the authors also query GPT-3.5-Turbo-16k, Llama2-7B-chat-4k, and ChatGLM2-6B-32k with the question alone, no context at all. On NarrativeQA and Qasper this "w/o context" score is near-zero (4.7 and 12.4 F1 for GPT-3.5-Turbo-16k), as expected. But on the Wikipedia-derived multi-hop datasets — HotpotQA, 2WikiMultihopQA, MuSiQue — GPT-3.5-Turbo-16k scores 31.7, 28.9, and 15.0 F1 with zero context supplied, evidence that a meaningful share of the "long-context comprehension" score on those three datasets is really pretraining recall of Wikipedia.

Context-compression experiments round out the picture: retrieval-based compression (chunking the context and keeping only the top-scoring passages by embedding similarity) helps the weakest model, Llama2-7B-chat-4k (19.9 → 24.0 average with the best retriever), but actively hurts the strongest one, ChatGLM2-6B-32k (36.1 → 34.4 at best) — retrieval is a crutch for models that can't otherwise handle the full context, not a free upgrade. Summarization-based compression is worse across the board: GPT-3.5-Turbo-16k's summarization-task average falls from 23.9 to 18.0 once the context itself is pre-summarized before answering.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

LongBench is unusually candid about its own limitations — the paper's own Limitations section flags both the automatic-metric fragility and the coupling with instruction-following, and Table 6's memorization control is a genuine, self-administered contamination check rather than something a critic has to dig for. That transparency is the paper's strongest asset, but it doesn't erase the underlying issues.

baselines

All 8 models are mid-2023 vintage — GPT-3.5-Turbo-16k is the only commercial model, and there's no GPT-4, no Claude, no Gemini. Fair for its moment, but it means the leaderboard froze fast: within a year, 100k–1M-token context windows made the paper's 4k–32k regime look like the easy end of the problem, which is exactly why the same team shipped LongBench v2 the following year.

contamination

The authors' own w/o-context ablation shows GPT-3.5-Turbo-16k scoring 31.7 F1 on HotpotQA and 28.9 on 2WikiMultihopQA with no context given at all — three of the eight multi-doc/single-doc QA datasets are Wikipedia-derived and partially answerable from parametric memory, so a chunk of "long-context understanding" credit on those specific tasks is really recall, not context use.

what's missed

Macro-averaging F1/ROUGE-L/accuracy/edit-similarity across 21 heterogeneous datasets into one "Overall" number flattens real differences in kind. PassageCount — the one task explicitly designed to require using the whole context rather than a localized chunk — sits near the score floor for nearly every model (single digits to low tens), a genuine long-range aggregation failure the averaged headline score quietly buries.

would raise confidence

An LLM-as-judge or human layer on the generation-heavy tasks (summarization, few-shot) to catch what n-gram metrics miss — a gap the authors themselves flag but don't close here; and a rerun of the same 21 datasets against today's frontier long-context models, to see how much of the field's progress since 2023 is genuine context understanding versus context-window inflation.

// bottom line

LongBench's real contribution isn't the leaderboard number, it's the pair of diagnostics wrapped around it — the truncation ablation and the memorization control — that let you tell a model genuinely using its context window from one that just accepts a longer prompt without benefiting from it. That distinction mattered in 2023 and still matters, but the benchmark's own 4k–32k length regime and 2023-vintage model roster have been superseded by exactly the kind of harder, longer successor (LongBench v2, RULER, and others) that its own findings predicted was needed.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. LongBench was the first benchmark to test long-context understanding rather than long-context acceptance — 21 datasets, 6 task categories, bilingual English/Chinese, standardized for fully automatic evaluation.
  2. Its most durable contribution is methodological: a truncation ablation that separates models genuinely using extra context (GPT-3.5-Turbo-16k, ChatGLM2-6B-32k) from ones that aren't (Vicuna-v1.5-7B-16k, flat at ~30.5 regardless of truncation), plus a w/o-context control that catches Wikipedia-memorization contamination on 3 of its 21 datasets.
  3. Continued training with scaled positional embeddings delivered the single largest jump in the paper — ChatGLM2-6B → ChatGLM2-6B-32k improved 62% relative — while retrieval-based context compression only ever helped the models that were weakest at long context to begin with, never the strongest.
