LongBench v2: Towards Deeper Understanding and Reasoning on Realistic Long-context Multitasks

503 human-annotated, human-reviewed multiple-choice questions spanning 8k to 2M words across six realistic long-context task families — hard enough that human experts, searching the document, average only 53.7% accuracy in 15 minutes. The best model answering directly manages 50.1%; only o1-preview's extended reasoning clears the human bar, at 57.7%.

Yushi Bai, Shangqing Tu, Jiajie Zhang, Hao Peng, Xiaozhi Wang, Xin Lv, Shulin Cao, Jiazheng Xu, Lei Hou, Yuxiao Dong, Jie Tang, Juanzi Li

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

The same Tsinghua/Zhipu team that built the original LongBench came to distrust their own benchmark's headline numbers. By late 2024, context windows had raced from 8k to 128k and beyond, and long-context leaderboards looked increasingly saturated — but the authors argue existing benchmarks were measuring the wrong thing. They "often focus on extractive questions, where answers are directly found in the material, a challenge easily handled by modern long-context models and RAG systems, as evidenced by their perfect recall in the Needle-in-a-Haystack test." A model that can locate a sentence is not the same as a model that can reason about a hundred-page document.

Two more issues compound the retrieval problem. Many benchmarks lean on synthetic tasks that don't resemble how people actually use long context, and their metrics — F1, ROUGE, or costly and bias-prone LLM-as-a-judge scoring — are unreliable enough to make head-to-head model comparisons noisy.

// the bet

Build a benchmark around four constraints simultaneously: length (8k to 2M words, weighted toward the sub-128k range models actually claim to support), difficulty (hard enough that human experts using search tools can't answer quickly), coverage (real-world scenarios, not synthetic needle tasks), and reliability (a uniform multiple-choice format with distractors, so scoring never depends on a fuzzy string metric or a judge model).

// how it works

The method

LongBench v2 contains 503 multiple-choice questions spread across six major task categories and 20 subtasks: single-document QA, multi-document QA, long in-context learning, long-dialogue history understanding, code repository understanding, and long structured data understanding. Context length ranges from 8k to 2M words (median 54k, average 104k), with the majority of documents under 128k. Every item ships as a long text plus a question, four choices, a groundtruth answer, and supporting evidence.

Sourcing real documents, not synthetic ones

Unlike prior benchmarks where "long documents are pre-defined or synthesized by the benchmark designers," LongBench v2 asks 97 annotators — holding or pursuing a bachelor's degree from top universities — to upload documents they personally read or used (research papers, novels, legal filings, financial reports, code repositories, and more) and write a multiple-choice question against that material. Uploads under 8,192 words are auto-rejected as too short, as are documents with high overlap with existing submissions.

Difficulty is enforced twice. First, an automated review runs three fast 128k-context LLMs — GPT-4o-mini, GLM-4-Air, GLM-4-Flash — over the question; if all three answer correctly, it's rejected as too easy. Second, a manual review by one of 24 trained human experts applies a checklist (in English, not counting-based, not solvable via search engine, evidence-necessary, objective) and times how long they take to answer with the source files in hand; a correct answer inside 3 minutes sends the item back for revision. A "hard" label is reserved for items where "no more than 1/3 LLMs get correct answer" and "human reviewer spend more than 10 minutes."

hard ⇔ (≤1/3 automated LLMs correct) ∧ (human review time > 10 min) // the difficulty gate that decides which items enter the "hard" split (311 of 503)

The incentive structure is transparent about what it's paying for: a base reward of 100 CNY per accepted item, length bonuses of 20/40/50 CNY for data over 32k/64k/128k words, and a 50 CNY bonus for hard-set data, on top of 25 CNY per human review. Collecting the final 503 items cost 100,000 CNY and over two months. A final audit sampled 70 accepted items and found 68/70 fully correct and 67/70 "Google-proof" (unanswerable via a 15-minute web search) — an estimated 3% error rate.

The result: 192 items classified "Easy," 311 "Hard"; 180 "Short" (<32k), 215 "Medium" (32k–128k), 108 "Long" (>128k); and rejection reasons split roughly 4% illegal question, 7% insufficient difficulty, 4% wrong answer.

// what they found

The results

The authors evaluate 10 open-source models (all with 128k context windows) and 7 proprietary models, under both zero-shot and zero-shot-plus-chain-of-thought prompting. Human experts score 53.7% within the 15-minute limit — well above the 25% random-guessing floor, but far from a clean sweep. Directly answering, the best model is GPT-4o at 50.1%, just short of the human line. Only o1-preview, which reasons at length before answering, clears it: 57.7%, "surpassing the human baseline by 4%."

Scale still matters — small models (GLM-4-9B-Chat, Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct, GPT-4o-mini) cluster "around 30%," while their larger counterparts (GLM-4-Plus, Qwen2.5-72B-Instruct, GPT-4o) reach "around or above 40%." But reasoning matters more: chain-of-thought prompting lifts open-source models by an average of 3.4%, and inference-time reasoning widens the gap further — o1-preview beats GPT-4o by +7.6%, and o1-mini beats GPT-4o-mini by +8.5%. Length interacts with this too: on the Short (<32k) split, "the best-performing model surpass[es] human performance by 15.4%," but on Medium (32k–128k) items "the top model shows a 5.6% performance gap compared to human accuracy."

Retrieval doesn't solve it

A RAG ablation splits context into 512-token chunks and retrieves the top-N (4 through 256) by embedding similarity. Qwen2.5-72B and GLM-4-Plus plateau past a 32k retrieval budget — Qwen2.5 actually does better with 32k retrieved than with the full 128k context (+4.1%) — while only GPT-4o keeps improving out to 128k, and even then lands 0.6% below its own no-RAG score. The authors read this as confirmation that LongBench v2's questions "cannot be solved solely through retrieval."

A memorization probe with a caveat

Stripping the context entirely and asking models to answer from the question alone — following the same protocol as the original LongBench — drops most models to "an overall accuracy ranging from 25% to 30%," near random. But two task categories don't fully collapse: single-document QA and code-repository understanding stay elevated without context, which the authors attribute to models having "seen some of the documents, novels, or code repositories during training."

// does it hold up?

The eval read

LongBench v2's multiple-choice format is a real fix for the F1/ROUGE unreliability problem that plagued its predecessor, and sourcing documents from annotators' own reading (rather than synthesizing or scraping public corpora wholesale) is a sensible attempt to blunt contamination. But three of the paper's own numbers are worth reading skeptically rather than taking as a settled verdict.

First, the headline "reasoning beats humans" claim rests on a single model. Only o1-preview and o1-mini represent inference-time reasoning in this evaluation — there's no o3-class, Gemini-2.5-class, Claude-4-class, or DeepSeek-R1-class model, because none existed at the December 2024 submission date. A 4-point margin (57.7% vs. 53.7%) from one reasoning model, on a 503-item benchmark, is a thin foundation for the paper's central claim about "scaling inference-time compute" — and by the time anyone reads this in 2026, the frontier reasoning field has moved through several more generations untested here.

Second, the paper's own memorization probe undercuts its contamination story rather than settling it. Section 4.3 shows single-document QA and code-repository understanding scores stay well above the 25% random floor even with zero context — a signal the authors correctly identify as likely memorization, but then don't quantify further (no n-gram overlap check against pretraining-adjacent corpora, no per-item flagging of which single-doc-QA items are driving the elevated w/o-context score). Annotators submitting personally-read documents reduces the risk that a specific question leaked, but it does nothing to stop a well-known novel or popular code repository itself from being in a model's training data.

Third, the multiple-choice format trades one gameable surface for another. F1/ROUGE unreliability is gone, but nothing in the paper reports an option-order permutation test — shuffling which choice holds the correct answer — to rule out positional bias, a well-documented failure mode in LLM multiple-choice evaluation. With only 503 items and subtask cells as small as 15 (Knowledge graph reasoning) or 18 (Table QA), a few points of movement from either positional bias or plain sampling noise could shift a subtask ranking without reflecting a real capability difference — a risk the authors themselves flag in their Limitations section ("less stable results that are more vulnerable to randomness").

baselines

Reasonably broad for December 2024 (10 open-source + 7 proprietary, including o1-preview/o1-mini), but already dated — no o3/Gemini-2.5/Claude-4/R1-class reasoning models, so "reasoning beats humans" rests on one model family.

contamination

Mitigated at the question level via annotator-sourced personal documents, but the paper's own w/o-context ablation shows single-doc-QA and code-repo scores well above random — likely source-document memorization the authors flag but don't further quantify.

what's missed

No option-order permutation / positional-bias check on the MC format; no cost-normalized comparison of o1-preview's extra inference compute against its 7.6-point gain over GPT-4o; several subtask cells (n=15–20) are too small for stable rankings.

would raise confidence

Per-subtask confidence intervals given small n; an MC-shuffle robustness check; a refreshed leaderboard with 2025–26 reasoning models; explicit contamination screening on the single-doc-QA / code-repo items the memorization ablation flagged.

// bottom line

LongBench v2's real contribution is methodological: a human-reviewed, multiple-choice, deliberately-hard-to-retrieve format that finally separates "deep understanding" from "needle-in-a-haystack recall." That's a genuine step up from its own predecessor. But its most-quoted number — o1-preview edging past human experts — is one reasoning model's 4-point margin on a 503-item test, evaluated without a positional-bias check or cost normalization, and its own contamination ablation quietly points at the exact two task categories (single-doc QA, code repo) where the paper's defenses are weakest.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. 503 multiple-choice questions, 8k–2M words, six task families — single/multi-doc QA, long in-context learning, long-dialogue history, code-repo understanding, structured data — built via a 97-annotator + 24-reviewer pipeline with automated (three-LLM) and manual (human-timed) review gates, at a cost of 100,000 CNY over two months.
  2. Human experts hit only 53.7% within 15 minutes (vs. 25% random); GPT-4o directly answering manages 50.1%, but o1-preview's extended reasoning reaches 57.7% — a 4-point margin over humans, and the paper's central evidence that inference-time reasoning, not raw scale, is what long-context "deep understanding" now needs.
  3. Retrieval alone doesn't crack it: RAG plateaus past 32k for most models, and even without any context at all, single-doc-QA and code-repo scores stay above the random floor — a memorization signal the paper surfaces but leaves largely unresolved.
