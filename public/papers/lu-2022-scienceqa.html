Learn to Explain: Multimodal Reasoning via Thought Chains for Science Question Answering

A 21,208-question benchmark of grade-school science exams — the first at scale to annotate every answer with a lecture and an explanation. That single design choice turned a multiple-choice dataset into a testbed for whether a model can produce a reasonable chain of thought, not just pick the right letter.

Pan Lu, Swaroop Mishra, Tony Xia, Liang Qiu, Kai-Wei Chang, Song-Chun Zhu, Oyvind Tafjord, Peter Clark, Ashwin Kalyan

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

By 2022, science question answering had become a favourite probe for an AI system's multi-hop reasoning and interpretability — but every available dataset gave up something essential. The multimodal science sets (AI2D, TQA, DVQA, VLQA, FOODWEBS, Geometry3K, IconQA) pair questions with diagrams, yet they ship answers with no annotated rationale: you can see whether a model is right, never why. The datasets that do annotate reasoning — WorldTree's explanation graphs, OpenBookQA and QASC supporting facts, entailment trees — are text-only, small, and narrow in topic.

That left a hole exactly where it mattered. To study whether a model's chain of thought is reasonable (and not just whether its final guess is lucky), you need a benchmark that is simultaneously large, multimodal, domain-diverse, and annotated with the reasoning behind each answer. None existed. ScienceQA was built to be that benchmark — and, with it, the authors ask a sharper question than "can a model answer?": can a model learn to explain, and does generating the explanation make it reason better?

// how it works

The benchmark, and the chain-of-thought recipe

The data. ScienceQA is 21,208 multiple-choice questions collected from elementary- and high-school science curricula, spanning grades 1–12. They cover three subjects — natural, social, and language science — organised into 26 topics, 127 categories, and 379 skills. Each item carries a question, two to five options, an optional multimodal context (an image, a text passage, or both), the gold answer, and — the signature contribution — a lecture (general background knowledge for the question's class, ~125 words on average) and an explanation (the specific reason this answer is correct, ~48 words). Of the 21,208 questions, 48.7% have an image context, 48.2% a text context, 30.8% both, and 33.9% none; 83.9% carry a lecture and 90.5% an explanation. The split is 60/20/20 (12,726 train / 4,241 val / 4,241 test).

The idea. Treat the annotated lecture+explanation as a chain of thought (CoT) the model should emit alongside its answer, and ask whether producing it improves reasoning. Two settings are studied:

  • Fine-tuned UnifiedQA — a T5-based text QA model fine-tuned to output the answer followed by the lecture and explanation.
  • Few-shot GPT-3 (text-davinci-002) via CoT prompting — in-context examples demonstrate the "The answer is X. BECAUSE: <lecture> <explanation>" format, with no parameter updates.

A crucial caveat baked into the setup: neither language model ever sees pixels. Images are first turned into a caption by a separate ViT + GPT-2 captioner, and that caption is fed in as text. The native VQA baselines (Top-Down, BAN, DFAF, ViLT, Patch-TRM, VisualBERT) do consume the image directly.

// what they found

The results

On answer accuracy, the story is layered. Native multimodal VQA models top out at 61.87% (VisualBERT) — well above the 39.83% random-chance floor, but far from solving the task. A fine-tuned UnifiedQABASE reaches 70.12%, and adding the chain of thought lifts it +3.99 to 74.11%. The headline number is few-shot GPT-3 with CoT at 75.17%. Humans score 88.40%, leaving a ~13-point gap overall — and a much larger 20.07-point gap on image-context questions, where the best model manages only 67.43%.

The explanations themselves. Judged by human annotators, GPT-3 (CoT)'s generated rationales are 88.5% relevant, 78.8% correct, and 84.5% complete, with 65.2% meeting the "gold" bar (relevant and correct and complete). Interestingly, fine-tuned UnifiedQA (CoT) scores higher on automatic similarity to the reference (0.811 Sentence-BERT cosine vs. GPT-3's 0.595) but lower on human gold (56.9%) — a clean reminder that n-gram/embedding overlap is not the same as a human-judged good explanation.

The most striking number is an upper bound. If you feed the gold explanation into the prompt rather than make the model generate it, GPT-3's accuracy jumps to 94.13% (+18.96). Correct explanations are enormously informative — the open problem is producing them, not using them. Two further findings sharpen the picture:

  • CoT buys data efficiency. UnifiedQA (CoT) matches plain UnifiedQA's accuracy using only ~40% of the training data (Figure 4) — like humans, the model learns faster when taught the reasoning, not just the label.
  • Order matters, for a mundane reason. Generating the answer first (QCM→ALE, 73.6%) beats every rationale-first format by up to ~22 points (QCM→ELA, 51.5%) — because when a long lecture+explanation is generated first, GPT-3 frequently hits the token limit or stops early before ever emitting the answer.

Finally, blind studies confirm the benchmark isn't a shortcut: question-only and image-only inputs both score near random chance, so no single modality leaks the answer, and removing any input component hurts — every part of the question carries signal.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

ScienceQA's lasting value is the data — and that part holds up beautifully. As an argument that "CoT improves reasoning," though, the evidence is more fragile than the framing suggests, and the "multimodal" label deserves an asterisk.

Start with the headline. Few-shot GPT-3's CoT gain is only +1.20% (75.17 vs. 73.97), and zero-shot GPT-3 already hits 74.04% with no chain of thought at all. Table 3 reports these averages with no confidence intervals, and although the paper does plot variance elsewhere — Figure 7(a), from four repeats per setting — that prompt-type spread is wide (the QCM→AE box reads roughly 67–78% off the plot). A ~1-point delta with no interval on the headline comparison is not strong evidence that CoT helps few-shot accuracy here — the convincing CoT effects are elsewhere: the fine-tuned UnifiedQA gain (+3.99), the data-efficiency curve, and the interpretability of the generated rationales.

Then the modality. For the strongest models, ScienceQA is effectively a text benchmark in multimodal clothing: GPT-3 and UnifiedQA never see an image — they read a generated caption. The models that actually consume pixels (the VQA baselines) cap out at 61.87%, the 20-point human gap is concentrated in the image subset, and a third of the benchmark (33.9%) has no context at all, where a text LM answers at ~78–80%. So the celebrated 75.17% is mostly grade-school text QA; the genuinely hard, image-grounded core is where everything still struggles.

baselines

Broad and era-appropriate (VQA models, UnifiedQA, GPT-3 text-davinci-002), plus random and human references. But the two strongest "models" route around the images via captions, so the headline isn't a fair test of multimodal reasoning — and the native VQA baselines that do see images aren't given the CoT treatment.

contamination

Built from open grade-school curricula (IXL Learning resources, per the paper's appendix) that plausibly overlap GPT-3's pretraining corpus; no decontamination or leakage check is reported. The strong zero-shot GPT-3 number (74.04%) would be consistent with prior exposure.

what's missed

No error bars on the main table; explanation "correctness" is annotator judgment, not verified against ground-truth science; and CoT faithfulness is untested — a right answer can ride a wrong explanation (and vice versa), so generating a plausible rationale ≠ reasoning through it. The 94% upper bound leaks the answer by feeding gold explanations in.

would raise confidence

Report variance/CIs on Table 3; evaluate models that natively ingest the image rather than a caption; run a contamination analysis; add a faithfulness probe (does perturbing the explanation change the answer?); and isolate an image-required subset from the text-answerable items.

// bottom line

The dataset is the contribution and it earned its place: 21k multimodal science questions with grounded lectures and explanations, the first at this scale, which made ScienceQA a standard rationale-supervised testbed and a direct ancestor of MathVista, MMMU, and OlympiadBench. The "chain of thought helps" claim is real but narrow — strong on interpretability and data efficiency, marginal and arguably noisy on few-shot accuracy — and the multimodality is partly nominal, because the best results are produced by a text model reading a caption rather than seeing the picture.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. The data is the legacy. 21,208 multimodal grade-school science questions (3 subjects, 26 topics, grades 1–12), the first at scale to annotate every answer with a lecture and an explanation — which is why it became the go-to CoT-with-rationales testbed and seeded the multimodal-reasoning lineage that escalates it (MathVista, MMMU, OlympiadBench all cite it).
  2. CoT's payoff here is interpretability and efficiency, not raw accuracy. The few-shot accuracy gain is ~1 point (with no confidence interval on the headline comparison), while teaching the rationale lets a fine-tuned model match its accuracy with 40% of the data — and the 94% gold-explanation upper bound shows correct reasoning would help enormously if models could reliably produce it.
  3. "Multimodal" comes with an asterisk. The strongest results come from a text LM reading a generated caption; native VQA models lag at 61.9%, and the 20-point human gap lives in the image subset. Treat the headline 75% as mostly text QA, not vision-grounded reasoning.
