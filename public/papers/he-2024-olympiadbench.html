OlympiadBench: A Challenging Benchmark for Promoting AGI with Olympiad-Level Bilingual Multimodal Scientific Problems

8,476 olympiad-grade math and physics problems — bilingual, multimodal, and each carrying an expert step-by-step solution — assembled to outrun saturating benchmarks. The best model of its day, GPT-4V, cleared just 17.97% overall and a mere 10.74% in physics.

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

By early 2024 the standard math-reasoning benchmarks were running out of headroom. GSM8K is grade-school arithmetic — a few computation steps, text-only — and MATH, while competition-level, was "increasingly being surmounted." Physics fared worse: SciQ, ScienceQA, MMLU-STEM, and C-Eval-STEM all pose physics as elementary-to-high-school multiple-choice, where a model can guess or pattern-match rather than reason. A benchmark that saturates tells you nothing about the frontier.

The multimodal benchmarks of the day didn't fill the gap either. MathVista and MMMU are broad multi-discipline visual suites that, by the authors' read, "do not concentrate on delving into the complexity of mathematics problems." So there was no single resource that was simultaneously (1) genuinely olympiad-hard, (2) free-response rather than multiple-choice, (3) multimodal — problems with the diagrams real physics and geometry require, (4) bilingual (English and Chinese), and (5) shipped with expert worked solutions for every item.

OlympiadBench is the attempt to build exactly that: a difficulty ceiling drawn from the hardest human competitions — the International Mathematical and Physics Olympiads, national and regional contests, and the Chinese College Entrance Exam (Gaokao) — so that "the pinnacle of human intellectual achievement" becomes the measuring stick, and the score gap to humans is wide enough to track progress for years.

// how it works

The method

OlympiadBench is 8,476 problems, all free-response. The split is 6,142 math : 2,334 physics, 2,125 English : 6,351 Chinese, and 4,869 (57%) carry at least one image — diagrams that must be read to solve the problem, not decoration. Every problem ships with an expert step-by-step solution (100% coverage), which both eases annotation and makes the dataset useful for studying reasoning, not just scoring it. Questions average 253 tokens (max 3,745) and solutions 347 tokens (max 4,223), images excluded.

Problems come in two flavours. Open-ended problems with a definitive answer (6,728, 79%) are the auto-gradable core; theorem-proving problems (1,748, 21%) ask for a full proof and have no reliable automatic grader. To make answers machine-checkable, every open-ended answer is typed as one of five categories — numeric, expression, equation, interval, tuple — and the prompt template (below) tells the model exactly how to format and to end with a \boxed{…} answer, in English or Chinese to match the problem.

Scoring is an automatic equivalence pipeline, not string matching. Numeric answers are compared to a tolerance (default 1e-8, relaxed per-problem for physics), equations are normalized to one side and divided to test mathematical equivalence, intervals and tuples are compared element-wise, and expressions are checked with SymPy: the answer is correct iff the symbolic difference reduces to zero.

correct ⇔ SymPy.simplify( amodel − agold ) = 0   |  |amodel − agold| < ε  (ε=1e-8) // symbolic equivalence for expressions; tolerance for numerics — the score is micro-average accuracy over open-ended problems

Models are evaluated zero-shot with this single template; the dataset's knowledge-point labels are deliberately withheld from the prompt to test native ability. Theorem proving has no auto-scorer, so the authors fall back to manual sampling of GPT-4V's proofs. To curb leakage, problems are pulled from official competition PDFs and converted to markdown — sources less likely to already sit verbatim in a pretraining corpus.

// what they found

The results

The benchmark did its job: it was brutally hard for 2024 models. In the standard multimodal setting, the best model — GPT-4V — managed an average accuracy of just 17.97%, with 21.70% on math but only 10.74% on physics. Every other model was near the floor: Qwen-VL-Max 10.09%, Gemini-Pro-Vision 4.22%, and the open-source LLaVA-NeXT-34B and Yi-VL-34B at 3.65% and 3.42%.

Three asymmetries stand out. Physics is far harder than math for every model — it demands physical laws and world knowledge on top of calculation. Images hurt: on the text-only slice, GPT-4 reaches 29.93% (math 32.00%, physics 16.24%) and GPT-4V's text-only version jumps to 29.07% — so multimodal questions, not the math itself, are a large part of the wall. And language matters: bilingual-trained models like Qwen-VL-Max and Yi-VL-34B do better on Chinese than English problems.

A standout efficiency result: on the text-only slice, the 7B DeepSeekMath-7B-RL scores 17.02% overall (math 18.09%) — outperforming or matching the far larger Gemini-Pro-Vision (7.34%) and rivalling Qwen-VL-Max on math — evidence that targeted math pretraining and RL beats raw scale on this kind of reasoning.

Where the headline accuracy can't see — the proofs and the failure modes — the picture is starker. On theorem proving (manual check), GPT-4V solved only 6 of 81 sampled Math-Zh_COMP problems, all "relatively simple and classic conclusions," and was essentially unable to finish a proof within the token limit on the English competition set. A hand-coded error analysis of 164 sampled failed open-ended problems found that mistakes are dominated by reasoning, not arithmetic: in math, logical-reasoning / conceptual-confusion errors account for 42% and expression-calculation errors 27% (value-calculation errors 0%); in physics, logical/induction errors are 45% and insufficient classification discussion 25%. The model breaks down on how to reason, not on multiplying numbers.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

OlympiadBench is a genuinely well-built dataset, but read it as a 2024 snapshot, not a live leaderboard. Its entire model panel — GPT-4V, Gemini-Pro-Vision, Qwen-VL-Max, LLaVA/Yi-34B — predates the reasoning-model era; the headline "17.97%" says nothing about how an o-series or Gemini-2.5-class model would do, and later work treats OlympiadBench as a saturating sub-benchmark. The value here is the corpus, not the numbers.

The grader is the second caveat. The auto-scorer is final-answer-only: it checks whether the boxed answer is symbolically equivalent to the gold answer. That makes it blind to the very thing the title cares about — reasoning. The paper's own error taxonomy flags "correct answers with a false process," and SymPy/extraction failures produce "incorrect judging" cases on the other side. So the metric rewards getting the number right by luck and penalizes correct reasoning that formats badly — on a benchmark whose whole premise is process quality. And the 21% of the set that is theorem proving has no automatic grader at all; it's evaluated only by manual sampling of one model, so a fifth of the benchmark is effectively un-runnable at scale and absent from the headline.

Leakage deserves scrutiny too. Olympiad problems (IMO, IPhO) and Gaokao questions are among the most heavily reproduced, solved, and discussed content on the web; converting from official PDFs reduces verbatim-string leakage but does nothing about the solutions saturating the internet. There is no time-gated or held-out split and no contamination probe (contrast LiveBench or contamination-controlled code benchmarks) — leakage is "minimized," never measured. Finally, the "Olympiad-level" framing is uneven: a large share of the set is the Chinese College Entrance Exam, which is hard but not olympiad-hard, and 75% of items are Chinese, so cross-model comparisons partly track multilingual training mix rather than reasoning alone.

baselines

Fair for its moment but now obsolete — GPT-4V/Gemini-Pro-Vision/Qwen-VL-Max are pre-reasoning-model; the 17.97% ceiling is a historical artifact, not current SOTA.

contamination

PDF→markdown sourcing lowers verbatim leakage, but olympiad and Gaokao solutions are ubiquitous online. No held-out/time-gated split and no contamination measurement — best-effort, not verified.

what's missed

Final-answer-only grading can't see reasoning (rewards right answers from wrong work; penalizes good work that mis-formats); the 21% theorem-proving slice has no auto-grader and is excluded from the headline.

would raise confidence

A time-gated/held-out split with a contamination probe; an automatic or rubric-based proof grader; reporting process correctness, not just final answers; and a re-run on current frontier reasoning models.

// bottom line

A hard, careful, bilingual-multimodal scientific-reasoning corpus whose lasting worth is as a stress test and a hub linking the math-reasoning line (MATH, GSM8K, DeepSeekMath) to later expert-rubric evals — but its leaderboard is dated, a fifth of it (proofs) can't be auto-graded, and a final-answer scorer can't actually watch the reasoning the title is about.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. 8,476 olympiad-grade math + physics problems, free-response, bilingual (2,125 EN / 6,351 ZH), 57% with required diagrams, every problem with an expert step-by-step solution — built to outlast saturating benchmarks.
  2. It was brutally hard in 2024: best model GPT-4V scored 17.97% overall and just 10.74% in physics; failures were dominated by reasoning errors (42–45%), not arithmetic, and proofs were almost entirely unsolved (GPT-4V got 6/81 on a sampled proof set).
  3. The grader is the soft spot: automatic scoring is final-answer-only (SymPy equivalence + numeric tolerance) so it can't see the reasoning, and the 21% theorem-proving slice has no automatic grader at all — making OlympiadBench a great corpus whose headline metric undersells what it's measuring.
