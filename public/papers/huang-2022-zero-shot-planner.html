Language Models as Zero-Shot Planners: Extracting Actionable Knowledge for Embodied Agents

Large pre-trained LLMs can decompose a high-level task like "make breakfast" into a plausible mid-level plan with no training at all — the surprise is how good the plans read. The catch is that they rarely map onto the specific admissible actions an embodied agent can execute, so this paper adds three inference-time fixes — semantic action translation, autoregressive trajectory correction, and similarity-based example selection — to close most of that gap.

Wenlong Huang, Pieter Abbeel, Deepak Pathak, Igor Mordatch · UC Berkeley / CMU / Google

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

By early 2022, LLMs pre-trained on huge text corpora were known to encode a lot of "world knowledge" — but almost entirely in linguistic form, probed via question answering or fact retrieval. This paper asks a more actionable version of that question: "Can world knowledge learned by large language models (LLMs) be used to act in interactive environments?" Concretely, can a frozen LLM ground a high-level task expressed in natural language ("make breakfast") into a sequence of steps an embodied agent can actually execute ("open fridge", "grab milk", "close fridge")?

Prior approaches to this problem trained on explicit step-by-step demonstrations collected for one specific environment — supervised sequence models that learn what a correct plan looks like from labeled data, and that scale only as well as that labeled data does. The authors instead test whether a sufficiently large, purely pre-trained LLM already contains this planning knowledge, extractable with no gradient updates and no environment-specific training at all — only in-context prompting. The twist that motivates the rest of the paper: they find this mostly works for producing plausible-sounding plans, but "the plans produced naively by LLMs often cannot map precisely to admissible actions" — a large model will confidently generate a step like "microwave the chocolate milk" when the environment has no "microwave" verb or "chocolate milk" object, or phrase a valid action ambiguously ("open TV" when the environment expects "switch on TV").

// how it works

The method

The testbed is VirtualHome, a simulated household environment where activities are expressed as programs: sequences of steps in the form [action] ⟨arg⟩(idx) drawn from 42 atomic actions (e.g. "walk", "open"). Using the ActivityPrograms knowledge base (2,821 human-annotated entries, 292 distinct high-level tasks), the authors hold out 88 tasks for evaluation and keep 204 as a pool for prompting/demonstration selection. Two metrics do the work throughout: executability — does the plan parse and satisfy the environment's pre-/post-condition constraints across all 7 household scenes — and correctness, judged by 10 Mechanical Turk annotators per plan (with a longest-common-subsequence match against human-written programs used as a cheaper proxy elsewhere).

The baseline ("Vanilla ⟨LM⟩") simply prompts a causal LLM (GPT-2, GPT-3, Codex, GPT-Neo) with one example task+plan pair and samples a completion, picking the sample with highest mean token log-probability. The paper's contribution is three inference-time techniques layered on top, requiring no modification to model weights:

  • Admissible action parsing by semantic translation. Enumerate every admissible environment action, embed it and the LLM's free-form output phrase with a separate frozen "Translation LM" (Sentence-BERT/RoBERTa), and pick the admissible action with highest cosine similarity to the generated phrase.
  • Autoregressive trajectory correction. Rather than translate an entire plan after the fact (which compounds errors), interleave generation and translation one step at a time: sample k candidate next-step phrases, translate immediately, and append the translated, admissible action back into the prompt before generating the next step — so later steps are always conditioned on grounded actions, not free-form ones. This also lets the model terminate early if scores fall below a threshold.
  • Dynamic example selection. Instead of a single fixed demonstration, pick whichever demonstration task's name is most semantically similar (again via the Translation LM) to the query task — e.g. using "Shave" as the in-context example for "Apply lotion".

Combining all three yields "Translated ⟨LM⟩", the paper's final method. A bank of all 47,522 valid action-argument combinations is embedded once and cached, so the extra inference cost over the vanilla baseline is small.

// what they found

The results

First, the surprising positive: prompted with just one example, GPT-3 175B and Codex 12B generate plans that human annotators judge as correct or more correct than human-written plans themselves (human-written plans are constrained by VirtualHome's syntax and vocabulary in ways free-form LLM output isn't). But raw executability tells the other half of the story:

The headline number: for Codex 12B, the proposed pipeline "improve[s] executability of action plans from 18% to 79%" — 18.07% → 78.57% — with no change to model weights. GPT-3 175B moves 7.79% → 73.05%. But correctness drops alongside it (GPT-3 175B: 77.86% → 66.13%; Codex 12B: 64.87% → 54.88%), which the paper frames directly as "a trade-off between executability and correctness." A fine-tuned GPT-3 13B baseline — trained on VirtualHome demonstrations the zero-shot methods never see — lands at 66.07% executability and the highest LCS of any method (34.08%) at comparable correctness (64.92%), so supervision still wins on the metric that best tracks a literal match to human plans, even though the zero-shot translated models reach higher raw executability.

Two further checks matter for reading the headline number correctly. First, an ablation isolating each of the three techniques (Table 2, not reproduced in full here) shows action translation is by far the most load-bearing: removing it alone drops Translated Codex 12B from 78.57% to 31.49% executability. Second, when researchers instead give the model detailed how-to instructions (not just a task name), Translated Codex 12B reaches 32.87% LCS — within 1.1 points of the supervised LSTM baseline's 34.00%, without any fine-tuning.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

The paper is unusually careful about the trade-off it reports — it names the correctness drop in the abstract itself rather than burying it, and it runs a second check (Section 5.2) that keeps the headline "18% to 79%" number honest: the percentage of plans that are both executable and correct. That combined metric is the one that actually matters for a usable embodied agent, and it tells a more sobering story than the topline executability jump.

Human-written plans are 100% executable and 65.91% of them are also judged correct. The best zero-shot method here — Translated GPT-3 175B — reaches only 35.23% executable-and-correct, and Translated Codex 12B trails at 27.27%. So while the paper's own "18% to 79%" framing reads as a large win, the metric that would actually predict whether a deployed agent completes the task successfully shows the gap to human-level performance is still roughly 2×, not closed. Reading only the executability number risks over-crediting the method.

baselines

Reasonably fair and, in one place, self-skeptical: the fine-tuned GPT-3 13B baseline is a real supervised comparison, not a strawman, and it wins on LCS. The paper also catches its own confound — smaller models like GPT-2 post surprisingly high executability, but the authors show via LCS that this is because they repeat the demonstration example or emit too-short plans rather than genuinely solving the task, and flag it explicitly rather than reporting the raw number uncritically.

contamination

Not discussed (era-typical for Jan 2022). GPT-3/Codex pretraining-data overlap with VirtualHome's task/program templates is not audited.

what's missed

The headline executability gain is inseparable from a correctness cost, and the combined executable-and-correct rate tops out at 35.23% vs. 65.91% for humans — a gap the abstract's framing doesn't foreground. Correctness comes from only 10 MTurk annotators per plan with no inter-annotator agreement statistic reported (only standard error of the mean). Everything is evaluated inside one simulated household domain (VirtualHome) at the level of a symbolic environment graph — the paper is explicit that it addresses only "mid-level" grounding, not real embodiment, low-level sensorimotor control, or perception, and does not incorporate any observation/feedback loop from the environment during planning.

would raise confidence

Report inter-annotator agreement (e.g. Cohen's κ), not just SEM; evaluate the executable-and-correct rate as the primary headline metric rather than a section-5 secondary check; test beyond one simulated domain, ideally with real robot or multi-environment execution; and study whether adding environment observations/feedback (which the paper explicitly excludes) closes more of the correctness gap than translation alone.

// bottom line

The core finding — that a frozen, purely pre-trained LLM already contains extractable, actionable planning knowledge, and that three cheap inference-time techniques (semantic translation, trajectory correction, dynamic example selection) can make its output far more executable with no training — is real and was foundational to the LLM-agent wave that followed. But the paper's own combined executable-and-correct metric shows the harder problem is still mostly open: even the best zero-shot method reaches barely over half of human-level task success, and the correctness cost of chasing executability is explicit, not hidden — a rare instance of a paper's own honesty being the sharpest critique of its headline number.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. Zero-shot planning knowledge is real. Large enough pre-trained LLMs (GPT-3 175B, Codex 12B), prompted with a single example, decompose high-level household tasks into plans human annotators judge as correct or more correct than human-written ones — with no fine-tuning at all.
  2. Three inference-time fixes close most of the executability gap. Cosine-similarity translation to admissible actions, one-step-at-a-time trajectory correction, and similarity-based dynamic example selection push Codex 12B's executability from 18.07% to 78.57% and GPT-3 175B's from 7.79% to 73.05%, with no weight changes.
  3. The trade-off is real and the paper says so. Correctness drops as executability rises, and the combined executable-and-correct rate — the metric that matters most — reaches only 35.23% for the best method vs. 65.91% for humans; the harder mid-level-grounding problem remains open.
