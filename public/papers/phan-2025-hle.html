Humanity's Last Exam

After frontier models cleared 90% on MMLU, the field lost its yardstick. Humanity's Last Exam is 2,500 closed-ended academic questions, written by ~1,000 experts and kept only if frontier models fail them — a benchmark rebuilt to have headroom again. At release every model scored under 14%, and was confidently wrong.

Long Phan, Alice Gatti, Ziwen Han, Nathaniel Li, … (Center for AI Safety & Scale AI; ~1,000 contributors)

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

Benchmarks are supposed to track progress, but the field's standard yardsticks have been overrun. Frontier LLMs now score above 90% on MMLU and up to ~96% on MATH, and clear ~75% even on graduate-level GPQA. Once a benchmark pins against its ceiling it stops discriminating: you can no longer tell a state-of-the-art model from a merely strong one, and a two-point gain is indistinguishable from noise, prompt luck, or contamination. The instrument that was supposed to measure the frontier no longer resolves it.

Figure 1 makes the problem concrete: the same models that clear 50–96% on GPQA, MATH, and MMLU collapse into the low single digits on a test pitched at the frontier of human knowledge. HLE's premise is to rebuild that headroom — assemble closed-ended, automatically-gradeable academic questions that (a) have a single unambiguous, verifiable answer, (b) cannot be shortcut by a quick web search, and (c) current frontier models get wrong. The point is to make the headline number small again, because a small number at the frontier is the only kind that carries information.

// how it works

The method

HLE is a global, incentivized crowd-sourcing effort. Nearly 1,000 subject-matter experts across more than 500 institutions in 50 countries — mostly professors, researchers, and graduate-degree holders — contributed questions, drawn in by a $500,000 prize pool ($5,000 for each of the top 50 questions, $500 for each of the next 500) plus paper co-authorship. Every question is closed-ended and machine-gradable: 24% are multiple-choice (five or more options) and the remaining 76% are exact-match (the model must emit a specific string); about 14% are multimodal, requiring a model to read both text and an image. Each submission had to come with a known, unambiguous solution that a non-expert could not recover by quick internet retrieval.

The filtering pipeline (Figure 2) is what makes the set hard. Each submitted question first runs through an LLM difficulty check: it advances only if frontier models already fail it. Across the drive the organizers logged over 70,000 model attempts, surfacing roughly 13,000 questions that stumped the models; those went to two rounds of expert peer review (reviewers hold graduate degrees in the relevant field), narrowing to ~6,000 candidates and finally to organizer/expert approval. The released benchmark is 2,500 public questions, with an additional private held-out set kept back specifically to detect overfitting and gaming of the public split. Subjects skew quantitative (Figure 3): mathematics alone is 41% of the set.

Grading is automated. Because answers are precise and close-ended, the authors use o3-mini as an LLM judge to check a model's answer against the reference while allowing equivalent formats (a fraction vs. a decimal, an estimate within tolerance). Each model is also asked to report a confidence, which feeds a calibration metric — the root-mean-square gap between stated confidence and actual correctness:

RMSCE = √ Σb (nb/N)·(accb − confb)2  // confidences are binned; each bin b compares the model's stated confidence (confb) to how often it was actually right (accb), weighted by the bin's share of questions (nb/N). A perfectly calibrated model scores 0; HLE reports RMSCE > 70% for every model tested.
// what they found

The results

No model tested came close to passing. On the full benchmark, GPT-4o scored 2.7%, Grok 2 3.0%, Claude 3.5 Sonnet 4.1%, and Gemini 1.5 Pro 4.6%. Reasoning models did better but not by much: Gemini 2.0 Flash Thinking 6.6%, o1 8.0%, and — on the text-only subset — DeepSeek-R1 8.5% and o3-mini (high) 13.4%, the top score in the paper. DeepSeek-R1 and o3-mini are not multimodal, so they were run only on the text-only questions, a strictly easier slice than the full set the other models faced.

The more damning result is calibration. Every model posted an RMS calibration error above 70% — GPT-4o 89%, Gemini 1.5 Pro 88%, Grok 2 87%, Claude 3.5 Sonnet 84%, o1 83%, Gemini 2.0 Flash Thinking 82%, o3-mini 80%, DeepSeek-R1 73%. On questions they almost always get wrong, models still answer with high confidence instead of signaling uncertainty; the paper reads this as direct evidence of hallucination. Reasoning models also spend heavily for their few points — average completion tokens run into the thousands per question, highest on mathematics.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

HLE does the one thing a saturated field most needs — it restores measurement headroom — and the calibration finding is its most durable contribution. But several things complicate the headline. The top-line 13.4% is not apples-to-apples: o3-mini and DeepSeek-R1 were evaluated only on the text-only subset, so the "best model" number sits on a different, easier test than the rest of the table. The name oversells, too: "Humanity's Last Exam" is a closed-ended short-answer quiz — it rewards crisp, verifiable facts and derivations, not the open-ended judgment, synthesis, or long-horizon work that defines most expert labor. And with mathematics at 41% of questions, a high HLE score is as much a quantitative-reasoning signal as a measure of broad "human knowledge."

Two structural points matter most. First, the grader is itself a model — o3-mini, which also appears as an evaluated system. Exact-match grading is fairly constrained, but the judge's equivalence calls (fractions vs. decimals, estimates within tolerance) inject noise that the reported accuracies silently inherit, and the paper does not headline a judge-vs-human grading-agreement rate. Second, and most predictably, a static, public, gradeable benchmark depreciates: the 2,500 public questions are exactly the kind of artifact that flows into training data and tool pipelines, and within months of release frontier reasoning-plus-tool systems were posting far higher than the single digits here. The private held-out set is the right hedge — but it is the public number everyone quotes, and the paper does not track the public-vs-private gap over time.

baselines

Current for early 2025 (GPT-4o, o1, Gemini 2.0 Flash Thinking, DeepSeek-R1, o3-mini), but R1 and o3-mini are scored only on the text-only subset — so the 13.4% "best" isn't on the same test as the rest, and no human-expert accuracy baseline is reported.

contamination

The public 2,500 are released and gradeable, so they leak into training and tooling; the headline "<14%" aged within months as reasoning + tool-use systems climbed. A private held-out set guards overfitting, but its gap to the public set isn't tracked over time.

what's missed

Closed-ended, verifiable answers only — no open-ended reasoning, research, or long-horizon tasks. With 41% mathematics the score is heavily quantitative, not uniform "human knowledge." Grading rides on an LLM judge (o3-mini) without a reported human-agreement rate.

would raise confidence

A human-expert accuracy baseline; judge-vs-human grading agreement; the public-vs-private gap reported over time; a multimodal-matched comparison so the top number isn't a text-only subset; per-subject difficulty normalization given the math skew.

// bottom line

HLE succeeded at its actual goal: after MMLU and its peers hit the ceiling, it handed the field a frontier yardstick with real headroom, and surfaced the sharper finding that frontier models are confidently wrong rather than uncertain. Just don't over-read the name — it's a hard, math-heavy, closed-ended academic exam, and its public score is a depreciating asset: a useful year-or-two progress gauge, not a finish line for "human knowledge."

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. Saturation kills measurement. Once models clear ~90% on MMLU and ~96% on MATH, the metric stops discriminating. HLE rebuilds headroom with 2,500 expert-written questions chosen precisely because frontier models fail them — 2.7% to 13.4% at release.
  2. The signature result is calibration, not capability. Every model posted an RMS calibration error above 70%: asked questions they almost always miss, models answer confidently instead of abstaining. The gap HLE exposes is honesty about uncertainty.
  3. A public, static, closed-ended benchmark depreciates. The private held-out set — not the widely-quoted public number — is what actually guards against overfitting, and the "<14%" headline is a snapshot of early 2025, not a stable ceiling.
