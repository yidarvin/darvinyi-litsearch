Avoiding Reasoning Shortcuts: Adversarial Evaluation, Training, and Model Development for Multi-Hop QA

Twenty-six of fifty sampled HotpotQA bridge questions turn out to be answerable by keyword-matching alone. ADDDOC, a model-independent procedure that swaps in a fake-answer adversarial document, exploits exactly that: it drops a strong bi-attention baseline's exact-match score from 42.32 to 26.67 — then the paper shows what closes the gap back up.

Yichen Jiang, Mohit Bansal

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

HotpotQA's distractor setting pairs each question's two gold paragraphs with eight TF-IDF-selected distractors — but the crowd workers who write the questions never see those distractors; they only see the two gold paragraphs when composing the question. That means there's no guarantee the assembled ten-paragraph context actually forces a model through both reasoning hops. Jiang & Bansal call the failure mode a reasoning shortcut: a model can locate the answer by matching a few question keywords against a sentence elsewhere in the context, skipping the intended first hop entirely.

Figure 1 shows the canonical case. The question asks what Kasper Schmeichel's father was voted by the IFFHS in 1992; the intended reasoning chain runs from Kasper Schmeichel to his father Peter Schmeichel (hop one), then from Peter Schmeichel to what he was voted by the IFFHS in 1992 (hop two). But a model can also just search the context for a sentence matching "voted, IFFHS, in 1992" — and because none of the distractor documents happen to contain another such sentence, that shortcut lands on the correct answer without ever identifying "father of Kasper Schmeichel." The authors quantify how common this is: sampling 50 bridge-type dev questions, 26 of them (52%) contain this kind of shortcut.

// how it works

The method

The core contribution is ADDDOC, a three-step, model-independent procedure for generating an adversarial document that eliminates a question's reasoning shortcut without invalidating its true answer. Given the two gold paragraphs p₁ and p₂ (p₂ contains the literal answer span), ADDDOC: (1) generates a fake answer by finding, for each non-stopword in the true answer, a substitute among the top-10 closest words in 100-d GloVe space that shares no substring longer than 3 characters with the original (e.g., "Mumbai" → "Delhi", "Goalkeeper" → "Defender"; falling back to a randomly sampled answer from the dev set if this search fails); (2) samples a new document title from the entire HotpotQA dev-set title pool to replace p₂'s title, severing the true bridge-entity link; (3) substitutes every occurrence of the old title and answer tokens with the new ones throughout p₂ to produce p′₂, which now supports a shortcut-consistent but false answer while leaving the real answer chain untouched. A final "title-balancing" step adds one more document elsewhere in the context containing the new fake title, so the model can't learn a new shortcut ("an unbalanced novel title means fake").

Four adversarial dev-set variants are built by varying the number of adversarial documents per gold document (4 or 8) and how they're mixed into the context (randomly inserted or prepended): Add4Docs-Rand, Add4Docs-Prep, Add8Docs-Rand, Add8Docs-Prep. The paper evaluates a strong 1-hop "bi-attention + self-attention" baseline (the architecture from Yang et al.'s original HotpotQA paper) against a proposed 2-hop model: it borrows a recurrent control unit from Hudson & Manning's MAC network, which computes a fresh attention distribution over question words at each of two reasoning hops and uses it to bias the query-to-context attention — intuitively, first attending to "father of Kasper Schmeichel," then to "voted by IFFHS in 1992." An optional bridge-entity supervision signal additionally supervises the model to predict the bridge-entity span after the first bi-attention layer.

// what they found

The results

Trained on regular data and evaluated with exact match (EM), the 1-hop baseline scores 42.32 on the regular dev set but only 26.67 on the Add4Docs-Rand adversarial set — a 15.65-point drop that the paper attributes directly to shortcut exploitation. Adding sentence-level supporting-fact supervision to the same 1-hop model recovers much of it (34.00 adversarial EM), and the proposed 2-hop model does further: 34.71 without supervision, and its adversarially-trained variant with supporting-fact supervision reaches 46.87 adversarial EM — almost matching its own 47.08 regular-dev EM after adversarial training.

Robustness generalizes across adversary variants: on the four held-out settings (Add4/8Docs × Rand/Prepend) after Add4Docs-Rand adversarial training, 2-hop+sp scores 46.87 / 47.14 / 44.28 / 44.44 EM, consistently ahead of the 1-hop+sp baseline's 44.65 / 44.51 / 43.42 / 43.59. An ablation confirms both architectural pieces matter: removing the control unit (2-hop − Ctrl) or the bridge-entity supervision (2-hop − Bridge) both hurt performance relative to the full 2-hop model, and the paper notes precisely that "1-hop Base is same as 2-hop without both control unit and bridge-entity supervision."

Two sanity checks support the causal story. First, manually verifying 50 sampled Add4Docs-Rand examples, the authors find 0 cases where the fake answer contradicts the true answer — the adversary construction doesn't quietly break ground truth. Second, error analysis on the 1-hop baseline's adversarial failures shows the model's predicted span falls inside an injected adversarial document in 96.3% of cases (95.4% for the adversarially-trained baseline) — near-total evidence that the shortcut, not the intended reasoning chain, drove the model's original correct answers.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

The 1-hop baseline is Yang et al.'s own bi-attention + self-attention model — the paper describes it as "a strong near-state-of-the-art model on HotpotQA," with a footnote dating that claim to "the time of submission: March 3rd, 2019." That's a fair, contemporary comparison for its era, though it's a pre-BERT, GloVe-embedding architecture; the qualitative shortcut-exploitation finding plausibly generalizes to modern retrieval-augmented and LLM-based multi-hop systems, but the specific 42.32→26.67 EM drop doesn't directly transfer as evidence about them.

Worth flagging on methodology: by the paper's own description, replacement titles are "randomly sampled from all document titles in the HotpotQA dev set" and fallback fake answers are sampled "from the entire pool of answers in the HotpotQA dev set" — so ADDDOC draws its substitutions from the same closed dev-set vocabulary the model is evaluated on, rather than genuinely novel entities. That's a narrower stress test than an adversary built from an open-world title/answer space would be, though the title-balancing step (adding a second document with the same new title) is a real, thoughtful attempt to prevent a "new shortcut" workaround.

The paper is candid that some questions can't be fixed by ADDDOC at all — Figure 4 (appendix) shows an example where the true answer is derivable from a single document regardless of any adversary, because the question's second gold document alone contains everything needed. But the paper never quantifies what fraction of the dev set falls into this "structurally unfixable" bucket, so it's unclear how much of the residual gap between regular and adversarial EM (e.g., 47.08 vs. 46.87 for the best model) reflects genuine robustness versus an adversary that simply couldn't touch those examples.

The paper reports EM throughout rather than F1, justified in one sentence ("the overall result trends and take-away's are the same even for F1 scores") but the F1 numbers themselves are never shown — a reader has no way to independently check that claim from the paper alone.

baselines

Fair for 2019 — the 1-hop model is HotpotQA's own official near-SOTA baseline as of the leaderboard snapshot the paper cites, not a weakened strawman.

contamination

Not classic data contamination, but the adversarial titles/answers are sampled from the same train/dev pool the model sees elsewhere, so the adversary is in-distribution rather than testing generalization to unseen entities.

what's missed

No quantified estimate of how many dev questions are structurally unfixable by ADDDOC (single-document-answerable regardless of adversary), which bounds how tight the regular-vs-adversarial EM gap could ever get.

would raise confidence

Reported F1 alongside EM, an open-vocabulary (out-of-dev-pool) adversary variant, and a count of the "unfixable" example subset.

// bottom line

ADDDOC is a careful, model-independent adversarial-evaluation methodology — directly in the lineage of Jia & Liang's AddSent for SQuAD — that converts a qualitative worry ("models might be shortcutting") into a quantified, targeted stress test with a manual no-contradiction check and a spans-in-adversarial-document error analysis to back up its causal claim. The 2-hop control-unit model is explicitly framed as an "initial architecture" to motivate future work rather than a competitive SOTA proposal, and the results support that framing: it helps, but doesn't fully close the regular/adversarial gap on its own.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. 26 of 50 sampled HotpotQA bridge-type dev questions (52%) contain a reasoning shortcut — a keyword match that bypasses the intended first hop.
  2. ADDDOC's fake-answer adversarial documents drop a strong 1-hop baseline's EM from 42.32 to 26.67, with 0 of 50 manually checked cases contradicting the true answer.
  3. In 96.3% of the 1-hop baseline's adversarial failures, its predicted span falls inside the injected adversarial document — direct evidence of shortcut exploitation, not reasoning failure.
  4. A 2-hop control-unit model with supporting-fact supervision and adversarial training reaches 46.87 adversarial EM, nearly matching its own 47.08 regular-dev EM — but the gap isn't fully closed, and the paper doesn't quantify how many examples are structurally unfixable by its adversary.
