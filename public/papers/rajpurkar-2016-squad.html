SQuAD: 100,000+ Questions for Machine Comprehension of Text

The benchmark that turned reading comprehension into a leaderboard sport: 100,000+ crowdsourced questions whose answer is always a span you can lift straight out of the passage — large, human-written, and exact-match gradable all at once.

Pranav Rajpurkar, Jian Zhang, Konstantin Lopyrev, Percy Liang · Stanford

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

By 2016, reading comprehension had a quality–scale dilemma. The datasets that were genuinely high quality — human-written questions over real passages, like MCTest (Richardson et al., 2013) with its 2,640 questions, or Berant et al. (2014) — were far too small to train the data-hungry neural models that were starting to win everywhere else. The datasets that were genuinely large — CNN/Daily Mail (Hermann et al., 2015) and the Children’s Book Test (Hill et al., 2015), with hundreds of thousands to over a million examples — were semi-synthetic cloze tasks: predict a blanked-out word or entity, automatically generated from naturally occurring text. They scaled because no human wrote the questions, but Chen et al. (2016) had just shown that CNN/Daily Mail “requires less reasoning than previously thought” and was “almost saturated.”

Everything in between forced a different compromise. Multiple-choice formats (MCTest, science exams) hand the model a tiny answer set; IR-style benchmarks like WikiQA reduce the task to sentence selection. None of them deliver all three things at once: large enough to train modern models, written by real people, and open-ended yet still cheap to grade automatically. SQuAD’s bet was that one design choice — require every answer to be a literal span of the passage — could thread that needle.

// how it works

The method

The whole benchmark turns on a single constraint: the answer to every question is a contiguous span of text in the passage. There are no answer choices. A system has to select the correct span out of all O(L²) possible spans in the passage — far more open-ended than multiple choice, yet, because the answer is always a substring, still trivially checkable against a reference. That span trick is what lets SQuAD be simultaneously human-authored, large, and auto-gradable.

The dataset was built in three stages. Passage curation: using Project Nayuki’s internal Wikipedia PageRanks, the authors took the top 10,000 English articles, sampled 536 uniformly at random, split out individual paragraphs, stripped images/figures/tables, and discarded paragraphs under 500 characters — leaving 23,215 paragraphs. Articles were split 80/10/10 into train/dev/test. Question–answer collection: crowdworkers on the Daemo platform (Amazon Mechanical Turk backend; ≥97% HIT acceptance, ≥1,000 HITs, US/Canada) wrote up to 5 questions per paragraph and highlighted each answer, paid \$9/hr. To stop them parroting the text, copy–paste was disabled and a prompt nudged them to use their own words. Additional answers: every dev and test question got at least 2 more answers (so ≥3 total) for robust scoring — and 2.6% of them were flagged unanswerable-by-span by at least one extra worker, yet kept in. The result is 107,785 question–answer pairs on 536 articles, almost two orders of magnitude larger than MCTest.

To show the questions actually demand reasoning, the authors characterized the dev set three ways. Answer types are diverse: dates and other numbers make up 19.8%, proper nouns (person/location/other entity) 32.6%, common noun phrases 31.8%, and the remaining 15.8% are adjective phrases, verb phrases, clauses, and other. Hand-labeling 192 examples for reasoning type found syntactic variation in 64.1%, lexical synonymy in 33.3%, multiple-sentence reasoning in 13.6%, and world-knowledge lexical variation in 9.1% (categories overlap). And they built an automatic syntactic-divergence measure — the edit distance between the unlexicalized dependency paths from a shared anchor word to the question’s wh-word and to the answer span — to stratify questions by how far the question’s structure drifts from the answer sentence (Figure 2).

Models are scored with two metrics, both ignoring punctuation and the articles a/an/the: exact match (did the prediction equal some reference exactly?) and macro-averaged token F1 (treat prediction and reference as bags of tokens, take the best F1 over the ≥3 references, average over questions). Against this the paper pits three baselines and a strong feature-based model: a sliding-window word/bigram-overlap baseline (with a distance extension), and a logistic regression over candidate spans — restricted to constituents from a Stanford CoreNLP parse — with roughly 180 million mostly-lexicalized and dependency-tree-path features.

// what they found

The results

The headline gap is the whole point of the paper. The feature-rich logistic regression reaches 51.0% F1 / 40.4% EM on the test set — a large jump over the sliding-window baseline (20.0% F1) and random guessing (4.3% F1), but far below human performance of 86.8% F1 / 77.0% EM. Human scores are estimated by treating a second annotator’s answer as the “prediction” and scoring it against the others; the mismatch is mostly inclusion/exclusion of non-essential phrases (e.g. monsoon trough vs. movement of the monsoon trough), not real disagreement.

Where does the model lose? Not at finding the right sentence — it picks the answer-bearing sentence with 79.3% accuracy — but at pinning down the exact span inside it. Feature ablations show lexicalized and dependency-tree-path features carry the model: dropping both collapses dev F1 from 51.0 to 35.8. And performance degrades exactly where the questions get harder. By answer type, the model scores 72.1% F1 on dates but only 31.2% on verb phrases (humans stay near 90% throughout). By syntactic divergence, the model falls off a cliff while humans don’t (Figure 3). Crucially, the authors note this gap was already closing fast: within four months of release, neural models (Wang & Jiang, 2016) hit 70.3% F1, more than halving the human gap.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

Read as a 2016 dataset paper, SQuAD is exemplary — but the very design choices that made it tractable are also its ceiling, and a decade of follow-up benchmarks reads like a list of patches to its weaknesses.

The span/extractive constraint is the load-bearing simplification — and its own ceiling. Requiring every answer to be a passage span makes scoring trivial, but it bounds what the benchmark can test: nothing requires abstraction, counting, synthesis across the document, or saying “the passage doesn’t answer this.” The baselines literally can’t exceed 77.3%, because only that fraction of dev answers are parse constituents and the candidate generator only proposes constituents. And exact match is brittle: it dings a correct answer for a stray article or an extra modifier, which is why the authors lean on token F1 and ≥3 references to absorb the noise. When the metric needs three answers and a softer scorer to be fair, the metric is doing some of the model’s work.

No unanswerable questions, and surface cues that a weak model exploits. In v1.0 every question is guaranteed answerable by a span, so a system never has to abstain — it can always confidently point somewhere. Figure 3 is the tell: a humble logistic regression holds ~75% F1 at zero syntactic divergence and only falls as the question’s structure drifts from the answer sentence, while humans stay flat. That a feature model does this well on low-divergence questions means a lot of SQuAD is solvable by lexical matching — precisely what Jia & Liang (2017) later weaponized with adversarial distractor sentences, and what SQuAD 2.0 (Rajpurkar et al., 2018) addressed by adding 50,000+ unanswerable questions. The 2.6% of questions one extra annotator already marked unanswerable was an early signal.

The “human” number is an agreement proxy, not a true ceiling. 86.8% F1 is one annotator scored against the others under EM/F1’s pedantry — it understates what a careful human with unlimited time could do, and it is capped by the same brittle metric. So when models passed 86.8% by 2018, that didn’t mean machines “read better than people”; it meant they reached the annotation noise floor of an extractive task.

baselines

Intentionally weak (random, sliding-window, one logistic regression) — correct for a dataset paper meant to show headroom, but the 51.0→70.3 F1 jump within four months shows the gap was closing before the ink dried.

contamination

Now severe. The passages, questions, and answers are all over the public web (stanford-qa.com, countless repos) and baked into every modern pretraining corpus; v1.1 and v2.0 are saturated. SQuAD today reads as a leakage probe and historical baseline, not a frontier test.

what’s missed

Extractive spans mean no abstraction, counting, synthesis, or abstention; EM is token-pedantic; constituent-only candidates cap baselines at 77.3%; and questions overlap the answer sentence lexically enough that a feature model rides surface cues (Figure 3).

would raise confidence

Unanswerable and adversarial questions (delivered by SQuAD 2.0), divergence-stratified reporting as standard, free-form answers with a generative metric, and a contamination-controlled held-out set.

// bottom line

SQuAD’s lasting contribution isn’t the 51.0-vs-86.8 gap — it’s the design pattern. A span-extraction format that is at once large, human-written, and exact-match gradable turned reading comprehension into a leaderboard sport, drove the 2016–2019 neural-reader boom (BiDAF through BERT), and seeded SQuAD 2.0, Natural Questions, and the whole extractive-QA lineage. Its weaknesses — surface-cue solvability, no abstention, brittle EM, and now total contamination — are exactly the problems the next decade of benchmarks were built to fix. That’s why it’s still worth reading: it’s the template, and the cautionary tale, in one paper.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. The span trick is the whole idea. Forcing every answer to be a contiguous passage span made SQuAD large, human-authored, and auto-gradable (EM/token-F1) at the same time — the design move that made reading comprehension a leaderboard task and set the template for extractive QA.
  2. The numbers that mattered: 107,785 questions on 536 Wikipedia articles; a 180M-feature logistic regression hit 51.0% F1 against 86.8% human F1 — a gap neural models more than halved within four months (70.3% F1) and erased entirely by 2018.
  3. Its limits seeded the field. No unanswerable questions (→ SQuAD 2.0), surface-cue solvability that a weak model exploits (Figure 3; → adversarial SQuAD), brittle exact match, and — today — pervasive pretraining contamination. SQuAD is now a saturated historical baseline and a leakage probe, not a frontier eval.
