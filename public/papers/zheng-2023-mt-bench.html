Judging LLM-as-a-Judge with MT-Bench and Chatbot Arena

The paper that turned "ask a strong model to grade" into a method. On open-ended chat, a GPT-4 judge agrees with expert humans 85% of the time — above the 81% humans agree with each other — and the two benchmarks it shipped, MT-bench and Chatbot Arena, became the default way the field ranks chat models.

Zheng, Chiang, Sheng, Zhuang, Wu, Y. Zhuang, Lin, Li, Li, Xing, Zhang, Gonzalez, Stoica

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

By mid-2023, instruction-tuned, RLHF-aligned chatbots were everywhere — and the benchmarks meant to measure them had quietly stopped working. Aligned models are strongly preferred by human users over the base models they were built on, yet that preference does not show up on traditional benchmarks: MMLU and HELM can barely tell an aligned chat model apart from its unaligned base. The thing users actually care about — does the assistant follow my instructions over a multi-turn conversation and answer open-ended questions well — was exactly the thing nothing measured.

The authors sort existing evaluations into three buckets that all miss this. Core-knowledge benchmarks (MMLU, HellaSwag, ARC, GSM-8K, HumanEval, AGIEval) grade short, automatically-checkable answers to closed questions. Instruction-following suites (Flan, Self-Instruct, Super-NaturalInstructions) open up slightly but stay task-bounded. Conversational sets (CoQA, MMDialog, OpenAssistant) are the closest in spirit but too easy to challenge a modern chatbot. None of them score human preference in open-ended, multi-turn dialogue — the direct measure of a chatbot's usefulness.

Human evaluation is the gold standard for that, but it is "exceptionally slow and costly." So the real gap is methodological: is there a scalable, automated stand-in for human preference judgments? The paper's wager is that a strong LLM, already RLHF-aligned to human taste, can grade other models' answers — "LLM-as-a-judge" — and that this can be validated against, rather than assumed to match, real human votes.

// how it works

Two benchmarks and a judge

The paper ships three things that have to be read together: two new human-preference benchmarks, and a systematic study of using an LLM to approximate the humans.

MT-bench is a set of 80 hand-written multi-turn questions, 10 in each of 8 categories — writing, roleplay, extraction, reasoning, math, coding, knowledge-I (STEM) and knowledge-II (humanities/social science). Each is two turns (an opener plus a follow-up like "now explain it like I'm five"), deliberately chosen to be hard enough to separate strong chatbots and to probe core skills like reasoning and math. Chatbot Arena is the complement: a public platform where a user asks the same question to two anonymous models side-by-side and votes for the better answer, identities revealed only after voting. One month of operation collected ~30K votes from 2,114 unique IPs — preference data "in the wild," with no fixed question set.

LLM-as-a-judge is the scalable surrogate for the humans those benchmarks rely on. A strong model (GPT-4 in the lead role) is given the question and the candidate answers and asked to judge, in one of three modes:

  • Pairwise comparison — shown two answers, pick the better one or call a tie.
  • Single-answer grading — assign one answer a score from 1–10. Cheaper and scales linearly, but absolute scores drift more than relative ones.
  • Reference-guided grading — first make the judge solve the problem itself, then show that solution as a reference while grading (used for math).

An MT-bench score is just single-answer grading averaged over every turn — each turn scored out of 10, averaged across all 160 = 80 questions × 2 turns:

MT-bench score = 1160  Σ 160 turns  judge_score(turn)  ∈ [1,10] // single-answer grading, 80 questions × 2 turns, GPT-4 as judge

Crucially, the paper does not just propose the judge — it stress-tests it and documents where it breaks:

  • Position bias — judges favor whichever answer comes first. To measure it they feed two near-identical GPT-3.5 answers and swap order; a judge is "consistent" only if its verdict survives the swap.
  • Verbosity bias — a "repetitive list" attack pads an answer with restated points (no new information) to see if the judge prefers length.
  • Self-enhancement bias — whether a judge over-rewards answers in its own style/from its own family.
  • Limited math/reasoning grading — judges get misled by a confidently-wrong answer even on problems they can solve when asked directly.

And it pairs each with a mitigation: swap positions and only count a win that holds in both orders (else tie); few-shot exemplars to steady consistency; chain-of-thought + reference-guided grading for math; and a multi-turn prompt that shows the judge the full conversation at once so it stops losing track of which response is whose.

// what they found

GPT-4 grades like a human — mostly

The headline is an agreement number. On MT-bench, counting only non-tie votes, GPT-4's pairwise judgments agree with expert humans 85% of the time — higher than the 81% two humans agree with each other (first turn; 85% vs 82% on the second). GPT-4 single-answer grading lands almost identically (85% / 84% vs humans), and the two GPT-4 modes agree with each other 97%. The same pattern holds on the larger, in-the-wild Chatbot Arena data: GPT-4 vs human agreement reaches 87%.

Two qualifiers the paper supplies itself matter a lot. First, agreement is not flat: it climbs from about 70% when two models are close in quality to nearly 100% when they're far apart — GPT-4 looks most human-like exactly on the easy, lopsided comparisons. Second, the judge can improve human judgments: when a labeler disagreed with GPT-4 and was shown its reasoning, they deemed it reasonable in 75% of cases and changed their own vote 34% of the time.

The win-rate curves are the visual proof: rank nine Arena models by win rate and the GPT-4, GPT-3.5, GPT-4-single and human curves sit almost on top of each other.

The biases are real but unequal — and almost everything rides on the judge being GPT-4. On the position-bias test only GPT-4 stays consistent more than 60% of the time; weaker judges flip constantly. Under the verbosity ("repetitive list") attack, GPT-3.5 and Claude-v1 are fooled over 90% of the time while GPT-4 resists. Few-shot exemplars lift GPT-4's consistency from 65.0% to 77.5% (at 4× the prompt cost), and the chain-of-thought + reference-guided trick cuts the math-grading failure rate from roughly 70% to 15% (14/20 → 3/20 judgments).

Finally, MT-bench scores separate the field cleanly where MMLU can't: GPT-4 8.99, GPT-3.5 7.94, Vicuna-13B 6.39, Alpaca-13B 4.53, LLaMA-13B 2.61. And the two benchmark families measure different things — fine-tuning Vicuna-7B on just 4.8M tokens of high-quality dialogue jumps its MT-bench style sharply while barely moving MMLU — so the paper argues for a hybrid regime: capability benchmarks and preference benchmarks, because no single number determines model quality.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

This is a genuinely field-defining paper — it legitimized model-graded evaluation and Elo-style preference ranking, and the LMSYS leaderboard it seeded ran the conversation about model quality for two years. But the validation rests on narrower ground than the 85% number suggests, and the paper is admirably honest about most of it.

The judge and the champion are the same model. Of the three judges tested, only GPT-4 actually works: it's the lone judge above 60% position-consistency and the only one that resists the verbosity attack. GPT-4 is also the top-ranked model on the very MT-bench leaderboard it grades (8.99, well clear of the field). The paper measures the conflict — a GPT-4 judge boosts its own answers' win rate by ~10% (Claude-v1 by 25%) — and then concedes it "cannot determine" whether this is real self-enhancement bias "due to limited data," because you can't restyle a response into another model's voice without changing its quality. So the most consequential bias for a GPT-4-judges-GPT-4 benchmark is left explicitly unresolved.

The 85% is pooled over a wide quality gap. The model set spans GPT-4 down to LLaMA-13B, and by the paper's own Figure 2, agreement is ~70% for near-peer models and ~100% for mismatched ones. The interesting, modern case — ranking two strong, close models — is where the judge agrees with humans least, so the impressive headline partly reflects how easy the comparisons were. The human side is thin too: 58 labelers (mostly grad students, not domain experts) producing ~3K votes, and "helpfulness" is collapsed into a single scalar that the authors note conflates accuracy, relevance and creativity — with safety and honesty excluded outright.

Contamination is the unusual kind. The 80 MT-bench questions are hand-written, so there's no train/test leak at release — but they're tiny and have been public since 2023, and verbosity/position bias mean a model can climb by being longer or better-placed rather than better. Three years on, MT-bench scores cluster near the ceiling and the community has largely moved to harder, refreshable successors (Arena-Hard and the live Arena Elo). The method outlived the benchmark.

baselines

Only three 2023 judges (GPT-4, GPT-3.5, Claude-v1); GPT-4 is the sole one that works and the top-ranked model graded — judge and champion share a family.

contamination

Questions are hand-written (no train leak), but the 80-item set is small, fully public since 2023, and now near-saturated; the live "leak" is judge bias, not data.

what's missed

85% is pooled across a huge quality range — for near-peer models agreement falls toward 70%. Helpfulness is one scalar; safety and honesty are out of scope.

would raise confidence

Agreement reported per difficulty band, a cross-family (non-GPT) judge, a controlled self-enhancement test, expert labelers, and larger refreshable question sets.

// bottom line

Read "GPT-4 agrees with humans 85%" as "GPT-4 ≈ human on easy, lopsided pairs, using a judge from the same family as the winner." That's still a landmark result — it made LLM-as-a-judge respectable — but the self-enhancement and verbosity biases the paper documents are precisely the failure modes the later rubric-RL work in this atlas (Online Rubrics, Rubric Reward Hacking, POW3R) spends its time fighting.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. LLM-as-a-judge became a method here. A GPT-4 judge matches expert human preference 85% of the time on non-tie votes — above the 81% humans agree with each other — making model-graded evaluation a scalable, explainable surrogate for expensive human ratings.
  2. The biases are quantified, not hand-waved. Position bias (GPT-4 consistent only 65% of the time), verbosity bias (weak judges fooled >90% by a padding attack; GPT-4 8.7%), and self-enhancement (GPT-4 +10% / Claude-v1 +25% on their own answers, left unresolved) — and the mitigations (swap-and-tie, reference-guided grading, full-conversation prompts) are still the standard playbook.
  3. Preference and capability benchmarks complement, not replace. A 4.8M-token fine-tune lifts MT-bench style without moving MMLU; no single benchmark fixes model quality. MT-bench (80 questions) plus Chatbot Arena (crowdsourced Elo) became the default chat evals and the seed of the LMSYS leaderboard.
