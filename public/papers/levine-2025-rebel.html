Relevance Isn't All You Need: Scaling RAG Systems With Inference-Time Compute Via Multi-Criteria Reranking

Chasing context relevance alone hits an information bottleneck — higher retrieval precision, worse answers. REBEL reranks retrieved documents on a handful of secondary criteria (depth, diversity, authoritativeness, recency…) via chain-of-thought, optionally inferring the criteria per query, and in doing so turns extra inference-time compute into both better retrieval and better answers — no fine-tuning required.

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

Retrieval-augmented generation has one near-universal objective for its retrieval stage: find the context most relevant to the query. Dense retrievers, cross-encoder rerankers like Cohere Rerank, and zero-shot LLM rerankers are all tuned, explicitly or implicitly, to push topical relevance as high as possible before the generator ever sees a document.

The trouble is that relevance is not the thing you actually care about. You care about the final answer. And a long line of theory — the information bottleneck (Tishby, Pereira & Bialek, 1999), multi-criteria decision theory (Figueira et al., 2005), the probability ranking principle (Robertson, 1977), and classic diversity-aware reranking like MMR (Carbonell & Goldstein, 1998) — warns that optimizing a single criterion systematically throws away other properties of a good solution. Squeeze only for relevance and you can starve the generator of depth, multiple viewpoints, authoritative sourcing, or recency.

This is not just a theoretical worry. The paper builds directly on the ARAGOG study (Eibich, Nagpal & Fred-Ojala, 2024), where the RAG pipelines with the highest retrieval relevance frequently produced the lowest answer quality. The authors reaffirm this in the modern LLM setting and crystallize the gap into two problems: (1) there is a real relevance-vs-answer-quality tradeoff that conventional rerankers fall into, and (2) existing RAG systems have no knob that converts more inference-time compute into better end-to-end quality — spending more on a relevance-only reranker just buys more relevance, not better answers.

// how it works

The method

REBEL — "RErank BEyond reLevance" — is a reranking recipe that lives entirely in the prompt: no fine-tuning, no extra training data, no new model. It comes in two flavors, both built on chain-of-thought reasoning.

One-turn: a fixed multi-criteria rubric

The simplest version is a single reranking prompt that asks the LLM to grade each retrieved document not only on relevance (0–10) but on five fixed secondary criteria, each 0–5: depth of content, diversity of perspectives, clarity and specificity, authoritativeness, and recency. The model reasons step-by-step — analyze the document, score each property independently, compute a composite, then sort and filter — and produces a final ordering using a weighted sum:

Final Score  =  Relevance  +  Σi  wi · Propertyi // one-turn: 5 fixed criteria; wi = 0.5 for every criterion in all experiments (left untuned); documents with relevance < 3 are discarded, the rest sorted by Final Score

Because the secondary scores are weighted at 0.5 each, a document that is slightly less on-topic but markedly deeper, more authoritative, or more recent can legitimately outrank a hyper-relevant but thin one — exactly the move a relevance-only reranker cannot make.

Two-turn: infer the criteria per query

The fixed five criteria are a blunt instrument — a medical question wants recency and authoritative sourcing; a history question wants perspective range and historical depth. The two-turn variant adds a first "meta" turn. A Reranking Prompt Generator reads only the user's query, infers which secondary properties matter for that query, specifies their weights, and emits a bespoke reranking prompt; a second turn then runs that generated prompt over the retrieved documents. The meta-prompt itself carries five k-shot examples (tax policy, lower-back-pain treatments, ancient-China history, …) demonstrating how to invent criteria, write a rubric, and define the composite score. This is where "inference-time compute" enters: the system spends an extra LLM call to tailor its own ranking criteria.

How it's measured

The end-to-end target metric is answer similarity (Tonic Validate): a rubric-based LLM judge rates, on a 0–5 scale, how well the generated answer matches a reference answer. As a secondary metric the authors track retrieval precision — the fraction of retrieved chunks an LLM judge marks topically relevant (binary per chunk, then averaged). They combine the two into a single system quality = answer similarity × retrieval precision, and measure speed as generated output characters per second.

// experimental setup

The setup is inherited from ARAGOG: an AI-arXiv corpus of 423 papers (13 used to seed questions, the other 410 as distractor noise), 107 question–answer pairs generated with GPT-4 and human-reviewed, chunked at 2000 tokens / 200 overlap, embedded with OpenAI text-embedding-3-large. GPT-4o does all generation and reranking (including the meta-prompt); GPT-4 is the evaluator — deliberately a different model so it never grades its own outputs. Every technique is run 10 times with 95% confidence intervals.

// what they found

The results

The headline is a single, dense figure (the paper reports results only as a plot — no numeric table — so the values below are read off it). The left panel plots answer similarity against retrieval precision; the right panel plots system quality against speed.

The bottleneck is real. Among relevance-only rerankers, pushing precision down-weights answer quality: Cohere Rerank reaches ≈0.885 precision but only ≈4.09 answer similarity, and LLM Rerank climbs to ≈0.93 precision while collapsing to ≈3.89 — both below the ≈4.23 you get from doing no reranking at all (≈0.86 precision). The best-fit line through the relevance-only methods slopes downward — the modern restatement of the information bottleneck.

REBEL breaks the tradeoff. The one-turn rubric lands at ≈0.885 precision and ≈4.30 answer similarity — above no-reranking on both axes — at a speed (~55 char/s) comparable to a normal LLM reranker. The two-turn variant reaches the top-right corner of the plot: ≈0.945 retrieval precision and ≈4.34 answer similarity, the best of any method on both axes — but at ~16 char/s, roughly 5× slower than no reranking.

A new compute-for-quality curve. The right panel is the payoff: relevance-only methods are stuck near ≈3.6 system quality no matter how fast or slow they run, whereas REBEL traces a genuine frontier — no rerank (≈3.63 @ ~87 char/s) → one-turn (≈3.81 @ ~55 char/s) → two-turn (≈4.10 @ ~16 char/s). For the first time spending more inference compute on reranking buys measurably better end-to-end quality.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

Start with what's genuinely well done. The evaluation is unusually careful about confounds: the same dataset and the same LLM judge grade both answer similarity and retrieval precision, so you can't dismiss REBEL's win as "it just happens to do well on a different relevance dataset." The judge (GPT-4) is deliberately a different model from the generator (GPT-4o) to avoid literal self-grading; everything is run 10× with confidence intervals; and the prompts are fully disclosed. The "two-turn relevance-only" ablation is the right control and lands exactly where it should — with the relevance-only methods — which is real evidence that multi-criteria scoring, not just the extra turn or the chain-of-thought, is the active ingredient.

Now the load-bearing weaknesses. The benchmark is tiny and singular. 107 questions over a corpus of AI/ML arXiv papers — a domain where "depth, authoritativeness, recency" are obviously valuable and where GPT-4o has deep priors. The setup is inherited wholesale from one prior paper (ARAGOG). There is no second dataset, no other domain, no adversarial or out-of-domain split, so the central claim — that relevance-only reranking generically hurts answers — rests on a single, in-distribution slice. The authors concede the generalizability limit.

Every number is LLM-judged, and the judge's tastes line up with what REBEL optimizes. REBEL explicitly steers GPT-4o to favor context that is deep, diverse, authoritative, and well-written; the reference answers were written by GPT-4 and are deliberately long (median ~30 tokens, chosen precisely so the metric rewards thoroughness); and the grader is a GPT-4 rubric judge. Splitting GPT-4 (judge) from GPT-4o (generator) blocks the crudest self-preference, but the two share training lineage and stylistic priors — so "context selected for depth/authority → more comprehensive answer → higher rubric score" could partly be the same aesthetic rewarding itself. There is no human evaluation of the final answers to anchor the LLM judgments.

The effects are small relative to the noise. On a 0–5 scale, one-turn REBEL (≈4.30), no-rerank (≈4.23), and HyDE (≈4.25) sit within visibly overlapping 95% intervals, and no significance tests are reported. The "information bottleneck" is a best-fit line through a handful of points, and the celebrated "new tradeoff curve" is essentially three points. The two-turn margin (≈4.34) is the most convincing, but it is the slowest method — the lowest characters-per-second of any variant (the paper notes only "additional inference time," not a precise multiplier). Finally, the headline system-quality metric is a defined product (answer similarity × retrieval precision) with no theoretical grounding, "inference compute" is proxied by characters-per-second, and the rubric weights are pinned at 0.5 with no sensitivity analysis — so several of the most quotable claims depend on construction choices that were never stress-tested.

baselines

Reasonable and current for 2025 (No Rerank, HyDE, Cohere cross-encoder, zero-shot LLM Rerank) plus a clean relevance-only ablation — but all driven by a single model family (GPT-4o), with Cohere the lone non-GPT reranker. No fine-tuned reranker baseline.

contamination

Not directly tested. The corpus is public AI/ML arXiv papers that GPT-4o/GPT-4 have almost certainly seen, and questions are GPT-4-generated — so the generator may "know" answers independent of retrieval, which can flatter every method but especially muddies what retrieval quality is contributing.

what's missed

Whether the result holds outside AI-paper Q&A; whether a human (not GPT-4) agrees the REBEL answers are better; whether the gains survive significance testing given overlapping CIs; how sensitive everything is to the untuned 0.5 weights and to the choice of judge model.

would raise confidence

Multiple datasets and domains; human ratings of final answers; an independent, non-GPT judge; per-criterion and per-weight ablations; and reporting answer similarity on its own (not just the answer-sim × precision product) with significance tests.

// bottom line

A crisp, well-motivated demonstration that relevance-maximizing reranking can actively hurt answers, plus a prompt-only fix that converts extra inference compute into both better retrieval and better answers. The mechanism is sound and the controls are thoughtful — but the evidence is one small, in-domain benchmark graded entirely by GPT-family judges, with overlapping error bars and an author-defined headline metric. Treat REBEL as a strong recipe to A/B in your own RAG stack, not a settled empirical law.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. Relevance ≠ answer quality. The relevance-only rerankers (Cohere, zero-shot LLM Rerank) raised retrieval precision yet pushed answer similarity below doing no reranking at all — an information bottleneck restated for the LLM era.
  2. The fix is entirely in the prompt. Score documents on secondary criteria — depth, diversity, clarity, authoritativeness, recency — alongside relevance, combine with a weighted sum, and (optionally) infer the criteria per query in a first "meta" turn. No fine-tuning, no new training data.
  3. It makes reranking an inference-time-compute lever. One-turn REBEL ≈ the cost of a normal LLM reranker for a quality bump; two-turn is the slowest variant (~5× lower throughput than no reranking by characters/sec) but reaches the top score (≈0.945 precision, ≈4.34/5 answer similarity) — a compute-for-quality knob relevance-only methods don't have.
  4. Compelling hypothesis, thin proof. The whole result rests on one 107-question in-domain benchmark graded by GPT-4, with overlapping confidence intervals and an author-defined "system quality" metric — convincing as a recipe and a direction, not yet as a law.
