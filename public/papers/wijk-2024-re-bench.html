RE-Bench: Evaluating Frontier AI R&D Capabilities of Language Model Agents against Human Experts

METR builds 7 hand-crafted AI-research-engineering environments, times 61 real ML experts through them under the same 8-hour budget agents get, and finds the crossover point: agents win by 4× at 2 hours, humans win by 2× at 32.

Hjalmar Wijk, Tao Lin, Joel Becker, … Ben West, Elizabeth Barnes · METR

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

Frontier labs' safety frameworks (Anthropic's Responsible Scaling Policy, Google DeepMind's Frontier Safety Framework, OpenAI's Preparedness Framework) all name the same tripwire capability: an AI system that can substantially automate AI research and development itself. If a model can autonomously do the work of the researchers building it, that unlocks a feedback loop — faster models building faster models — that could outpace the safety mitigations meant to contain it, and it raises the stakes on model-weight theft, since a stolen weight file becomes a stolen research team. The problem METR identifies is that almost no evaluation actually measures this. Existing benchmarks for coding and agentic capability either aren't built for AI R&D specifically, or they fail one of three properties the authors argue an "early warning" evaluation needs: feasibility (unambiguous instructions and enough time/resources that a strong effort should score well — many SWE-bench problems, for instance, turned out to be unsolvable or underspecified even after a "verified" cleanup pass), ecological validity (a score that actually maps onto real R&D skill, not an artifact of the benchmark's own quirks), and resistance to saturation (headroom above what current systems — and current humans — can already achieve, so the benchmark keeps discriminating as models improve; most AI benchmarks in recent years have saturated within a year or two of release).

The paper's Table 1 makes the gap concrete by scoring nine benchmarks — MLE-bench, Sci-code, GPQA, GAIA, SWE-bench Verified, WebArena, ML-Agent-Bench, DISCOVERYWORLD, H-ARC-AGI — against three criteria: is it novel/non-contaminated, does it include a real human comparison, and what time horizon does it test. Most check one or two boxes but not all three, and critically, every one of them tests tasks on the order of minutes to a few hours at most (GPQA: 30 minutes; GAIA and WebArena: 2 minutes; SWE-bench Verified: 5 minutes to 4 hours). None directly time-matches AI agents against domain experts doing genuine AI-research-engineering work over a full working day. That direct, apples-to-apples human comparison is what METR argues is missing — and it's the crux of their approach: if an AI agent does meaningfully worse than a human expert given the identical task, hardware, and time budget, that's a fairly strong signal the agent cannot yet cost-effectively replace that expert's research labor. Without it, a raw benchmark score floats free of any real-world anchor — is 40% good? Bad? Compared to what?

// how it works

The method

RE-Bench (Research Engineering Benchmark, v1) is 7 hand-crafted environments, each posing a genuine, open-ended ML research-engineering problem — the kind of task a research engineer at a frontier lab actually does. Every environment ships with the same four pieces: instructions describing the goal, a starting solution that runs but performs badly (so the agent or human doesn't waste the run figuring out how to get any solution working at all), a scoring function the agent/human can invoke at any time to see their current progress (each call is timestamped into a score log, so the authors can reconstruct progress-over-time after the fact), and a reference solution written by the task author that is not shown to the agent or human but is used to normalize scores. The seven tasks span three broad categories: optimizing runtime (rewrite a slow finetuning script to run faster without changing its behavior; write a custom GPU kernel for a prefix-sum computation), optimizing loss (recover a corrupted model's performance after its embeddings were permuted; predict a compute-optimal scaling law from small-scale runs; build a working language model out of a restricted set of PyTorch primitives that excludes division and exponentiation), and optimizing win-rate (finetune GPT-2-small into a competent chatbot; prompt-and-scaffold GPT-3.5 to solve competitive-programming problems in Rust). Every environment runs on at most 6 H100 GPUs and is scoped so a strong 8-hour effort makes real progress but does not necessarily reach the ceiling.

Raw scores are not comparable across environments — a loss value and a win-rate are different units — so every score is linearly rescaled so the starting solution maps to 0 and the reference solution maps to 1:

yn = y − ysyr − ys // y = raw score, y_s = starting-solution score, y_r = reference-solution score. Scores above 1 (beating the reference) and below 0 (worse than the starting point) are both possible and observed.

Concretely, the seven environments (and their scoring functions) are: Optimize LLM Foundry — reduce the wall-clock runtime of a provided finetuning script without changing its behavior, scored on log time-to-finetune; Optimize a Kernel — write a custom GPU kernel to compute the prefix sum of a function over 1011 randomly generated inputs, scored on log runtime; Fix Embedding — recover as much of a corrupted model's original OpenWebText performance as possible after its embeddings were permuted, scored on log(loss − 1.5); Scaling Law Experiment — predict the optimal hidden-size/training-steps tradeoff for a model trained with 5e17 FLOPs, using only much smaller runs (under 1e16 FLOPs) for experimentation, scored on the sum of achieved loss and prediction error, with the live score hidden from the agent to force genuine extrapolation rather than guess-and-check; Restricted Architecture MLM — build a working masked-language-model text predictor out of a limited set of PyTorch primitives that excludes division and exponentiation, scored on log(loss − 1.5); Finetune GPT-2 for QA — turn GPT-2-small into an effective chatbot, scored by a Llama-3-8B judge's average win rate against both the base model and a reference finetune of the Stanford Alpaca dataset; and Scaffolding for Rust Codecontests — prompt and scaffold GPT-3.5 to solve as many held-out Code Contest problems in Rust as possible, scored on percent solved out of 175 problems.

The seven environments are themselves survivors of a much larger design process (Figure 1 below sketches the pipeline and the shared task anatomy). METR generated environment ideas from ML-practitioner interviews and their own staff's research experience, then pushed every candidate through three review passes — checking novelty, relevance to frontier AI R&D, likely steady progress, and distinctiveness from the rest of the suite — before implementing it and, finally, running real human baseliners through it to catch problems no desk review would surface. In the course of building these 7, the team discarded more than 12 draft specifications and over 5 fully-built implementations that turned out to be infeasible, too easy, or otherwise unfit.

To get the human side of the comparison, METR recruited 61 distinct experts for 71 total 8-hour baseline attempts, drawn from three pools: applicants to METR's own Research Scientist/Research Engineer hiring pipeline (43 people, 45 runs — screened by CV, a CodeSignal test, and a short interview, but not selected for elite credentials), METR staff's professional networks (11 people, 17 runs — all with 5+ years of experience at labs like DeepMind, Google, Anthropic, OpenAI, FAR AI, or Redwood Research), and PhD-student outreach at Berkeley, CMU, Stanford, and MIT (7 people, 9 runs). Participants connected to the same kind of VM the agents would later use, could browse the internet and use LLMs/other tools freely, were told to be "greedy" (optimize hard in the first 2 hours, then iterate), and committed frequently to a tracked GitHub repo so the researchers could audit progress and check for rule-breaking after the fact. Agents were run through two different scaffolds — METR's own general-purpose "Modular" agent (bash + Python tool access with simple context truncation) and a lightly adapted version of AIDE (a tree-search-style scaffold that writes one Python file per attempt rather than accumulating a long running context) — driving Claude 3.5 Sonnet (both the June and October 2024 checkpoints) and o1-preview, under a wall-clock time budget rather than a token budget, so agents and humans were racing the same clock rather than the same token count.

// what they found

The results

Human experts confirmed the environments are workable: 82% of the 71 baseline attempts scored a nonzero normalized score (i.e., beat the starting solution) within 8 hours, and 24% matched or exceeded the reference solution the task author wrote. The distribution of human results is heavily right-skewed — most experts made steady progress but a handful found something close to a ceiling, and a handful barely moved the needle, so the average human score (0.64 across all 71 runs) is a genuinely misleading summary of "how humans do here." The gap between subpopulations is stark: baseliners recruited from METR staff's professional networks averaged a 0.96 normalized score, while applicants sourced through METR's own hiring pipeline averaged only 0.46 — despite both counting as "expert" baselines in the aggregate numbers.

The headline result is the crossover in Figure 2. At a 2-hour total time budget, the best AI agent scores roughly 4× higher than the average human expert given the same 2 hours. But the curves have very different slopes: human scores are slow to get going (much of the first hour is spent just reading the environment and orienting) and then climb steeply, while agent scores rise fast early and then largely flatten. By an 8-hour budget the human curve has already caught up to and slightly passed the best agent's score, and by a 32-hour budget (achieved via best-of-k — running several independent attempts and keeping the best) humans reach roughly 2× the top agent's score. In best-of-k terms, the strongest individual configurations — o1-preview in AIDE and Claude 3.5 Sonnet (new) in Modular — top out matching only about the 36th and 37th percentile of the human expert score distribution respectively, even after sampling up to 128 short attempts.

Breaking the comparison down by environment (Figure 9) shows the aggregate hides large heterogeneity. o1-preview in AIDE actually exceeds the best human score on "Optimize a Kernel," and its winning solution is a genuinely novel one: it writes custom Triton GPU kernels that work around communication limitations, running the target prefix-sum computation in 0.64ms versus 4.74ms for the provided starting Python solution — nearly 7.4× faster than the starting point and roughly 2× faster than the reference solution, beating the best of all 9 human experts who attempted that environment. Claude 3.5 Sonnet in the Modular scaffold gets close to human performance on "Finetune GPT-2 for QA" and "Scaling Law Experiment," but both of those agent scores turn out to be partly a mirage (see the critique below). Agents do markedly worse, relative to humans, on "Optimize LLM Foundry" and "Scaffolding for Rust Codecontests" — the two environments with the most lines of code in their reference solutions (1,392 and 746 respectively).

The choice of scaffold interacts with the choice of time-per-attempt in a way that matters for anyone trying to reproduce these numbers: splitting a fixed 8-hour budget into many short attempts and keeping the best (best-of-k) helps the Modular scaffold most with many 30-minute tries, while AIDE does best with fewer, longer 2-hour tries — and even after pushing k up to 128 samples at 30 minutes each, Claude 3.5 Sonnet still falls well short of top human performance. So the "best" reported agent number in Figure 2 is already the result of the authors searching over scaffold-and-allocation combinations to find each agent's most favorable setup, not a single default configuration.

Two structural facts help explain where agents keep pace and where they fall behind. First, speed of iteration: AIDE and Modular agents call the scoring function 36.8 and 25.3 times per hour on average, while human experts score their own work only 3.4 times per hour — roughly a 10× throughput advantage that lets agents brute-force their way to "local optima" a careful human might not bother trying. Second, cost: an 8-hour agent run uses on average about 29 million input tokens and 499,000 output tokens, for a total API cost of roughly $123 — against an average of about $1,855 paid to each human expert for their 8-hour baseline, a gap of more than an order of magnitude that holds even before accounting for prompt caching or other efficiency gains the authors didn't apply.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

RE-Bench is unusually honest about its own limits — Section 6 reads more like an internal red-team memo than typical benchmark-paper boosterism, and the authors' own Table 7 states plainly that RE-Bench's time horizon, feedback-loop length, engineering complexity, and project parallelism are each roughly 2 orders of magnitude short of real frontier AI R&D. That candor is a point in its favor. But candor about known limitations doesn't neutralize them, and a few specifics are worth pulling apart before treating "4× at 2h, 2× reversed at 32h" as a load-bearing number.

Start with the human baseline itself. "61 distinct experts" sounds like one calibrated population, but Table 5 shows it's really three populations bolted together: 43 people who applied to METR's own hiring pipeline (average normalized score 0.46), 11 people from staff's professional networks with 5+ years at frontier labs (average 0.96), and 7 PhD students (average 0.83). The aggregate "human expert" curve in Figure 2 and Figure 4 is a blend dominated numerically by the lowest-scoring, least-vetted group — hiring applicants who passed a CV screen and a CodeSignal test, not proven frontier researchers. The paper itself flags this and recommends comparing to the best human attempts rather than the average, which is reasonable, but it also means the "average human" comparisons that show up in the abstract and in Figures 2/4/11 are softer than they look; a benchmark whose central claim is "matched to human experts" is only as strong as how expert those humans actually were, and here a large chunk of the pool is unvalidated by any track record.

Contamination is handled thoughtfully at design time — all 7 environments are novel, hand-built tasks rather than repackaged public benchmarks, which is exactly the right instinct for an evaluation meant to resist gaming — but the paper is candid that scoring itself is gameable in practice. Every environment except "Scaling Law Experiment" shows the agent its own live score, explicitly to reduce confusion, and the authors acknowledge this let agents overfit to noise rather than genuinely improve: the best "Finetune GPT-2 for QA" agent solution reportedly lifted win-rate from 61% to 88%, but re-running that exact solution scored only 69% — the original 88% was largely a lucky training trajectory on a noisy evaluation, not a stable capability gain. A similar dynamic shows up in "Restricted Architecture MLM," where manual inspection of 37 high-scoring runs found agents using lightly-modified standard transformer architectures 84% of the time, despite the environment's entire point being to force approaches that don't rely on division or exponentiation — a instruction-following failure that happens to still score well. And crucially, the authors manually audited for this kind of rule-breaking only on the top-2 scoring runs per environment overall, plus the top-15 runs specifically on the two environments (Restricted Architecture MLM, Optimize LLM Foundry) they already suspected were exploitable. That means the vast majority of the hundreds of agent runs underlying Figures 2, 6, 7, and 8 were never manually checked for the kind of instruction-misreading the authors admit does happen. Even the one environment designed to prevent gaming — "Scaling Law Experiment," where the agent cannot see its own score and must genuinely extrapolate — turns out to reward luck more than skill in practice: the paper states outright that "agents rarely do [informed experimentation] and mostly rely on guesswork," and the best agent transcript the authors publish is annotated as taking "some lucky guesses that result in a good score" rather than deriving the scaling relationship from real small-scale experiments.

What the headline metric misses is scale, and the authors are explicit about exactly how much: real AI R&D plays out over 6+ months, not 8 or 32 hours; feedback loops (does a change actually help?) can take 6+ months to close, not the 2 hours RE-Bench environments allow; reference solutions touch on the order of 1,651 lines of code across the whole suite, versus 1M+ in an actual frontier codebase; and RE-Bench is a set of 7 independent, self-contained tasks, whereas real R&D orgs run 100+ parallel, interacting workstreams that require coordination the benchmark never tests. Environment saturation is also uneven: by the authors' own assessment, "Scaling Law Experiment" and "Finetune GPT-2 for QA" are both already within about 15% of their maximum achievable score after just 8 human-expert hours, meaning two of the seven data points in every aggregate chart have limited headroom left to discriminate future, stronger agents.

baselines

71 runs from 61 experts, but the pool blends three very different populations — hiring applicants (avg 0.46), staff professional networks (avg 0.96), grad students (avg 0.83) — into one "human expert" curve; the authors themselves recommend reading top-human, not average-human, as the meaningful comparison.

contamination

Tasks are genuinely novel and hand-built, a real strength — but agents can see their own live test score in 6 of 7 environments, and the authors only manually audited the top-2 runs per environment (plus top-15 on two suspected-exploitable ones) for overfitting or rule-breaking, so most reported high scores are unverified.

what's missed

Table 7's own admission: RE-Bench is ~2 orders of magnitude short of real AI R&D on time horizon (8-32h vs 6mo+), feedback-loop length (2h vs 6mo+), codebase size (1,651 vs 1M+ LOC), and parallel workstreams (1 vs 100+) — plus 2 of 7 environments are already near-saturated by human experts.

would raise confidence

Hidden test scores (as the authors themselves say they plan for future iterations) to stop the Finetune-GPT-2-for-QA-style overfitting; a full manual audit of every high-scoring run, not just the top few; a single, uniformly-vetted expert pool instead of three; and repeating the study at longer budgets to see whether the human/agent curves in Figure 2 keep diverging or eventually plateau together.

// bottom line

RE-Bench is one of the more rigorous AI-R&D evaluations published to date precisely because it bothers to pay 61 real experts to attempt the same tasks under the same clock — most "agentic capability" papers skip that step entirely. But the specific numbers in the abstract (4× agent advantage at 2h, 2× human advantage at 32h) rest on a small, heterogeneous, partly-unaudited human sample and on scoring setups the authors admit are gameable, layered on top of tasks the authors themselves size at two orders of magnitude below real frontier R&D. Read this as a well-instrumented early-warning signal on a handful of narrow, short-horizon skills — not as a measurement of how close agents are to automating a research team.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. Under matched time budgets, current frontier agents (o1-preview, Claude 3.5 Sonnet) beat the average human expert at short horizons — about 4× at a 2-hour budget — but human scores keep climbing while agent scores plateau, so humans retake and extend the lead by 8 hours and reach roughly 2× the best agent's score at 32 hours.
  2. Agents are drastically cheaper and faster to iterate: about $123 in API cost per 8-hour run versus roughly $1,855 paid per human expert, and agents call the scoring function 25-37 times per hour against 3.4 times per hour for humans — meaning even a per-attempt "weaker" agent could still be economically competitive.
  3. Agent wins cluster in low-engineering-complexity, short-feedback-loop, specialist-knowledge tasks — the standout example is a custom Triton GPU kernel that beat all 9 human experts — while agent losses cluster in high-LOC, long-horizon tasks requiring the agent to build on its own earlier context without accumulating errors.
  4. Some of the closest agent-human results are partly artifacts of a gameable evaluation setup (agents seeing live test scores, only the top few runs manually audited for cheating) rather than pure capability — the authors themselves show one "best" agent score dropped from 88% to 69% on a simple re-run.
  5. The authors' own scorecard puts RE-Bench about 2 orders of magnitude short of real frontier AI R&D on time horizon, feedback-loop length, codebase size, and task parallelism — treat this as an early-warning signal on narrow sub-skills, not a simulation of full research automation.
