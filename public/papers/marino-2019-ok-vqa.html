OK-VQA: A Visual Question Answering Benchmark Requiring External Knowledge

Standard VQA benchmarks turn out to be answerable almost entirely from pixels — the paper's own audit of VQA's “age annotations” found more than 78% of questions answerable by a 10-year-old. OK-VQA instead hand-filters 14,055 questions over 14,031 COCO images down to ones the image genuinely cannot answer (a photo of stuffed teddy bears asks which U.S. president they're named for), and shows that 2019 state-of-the-art VQA models collapse to 26–28% accuracy on it, with a naive Wikipedia-retrieval baseline barely closing the gap.

Kenneth Marino, Mohammad Rastegari, Ali Farhadi, Roozbeh Mottaghi

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

Most VQA benchmarks up to this point — VQA, VQA v2, COCO-QA, Visual Madlibs, Visual 7W, CLEVR — turn out to test recognition more than reasoning: counting, colors, simple attributes, "what is this," questions whose answers sit entirely inside the pixels. The authors back this with a striking existing statistic rather than a new claim: VQA v1 collected "age annotations" for 10,000 of its questions, where an MTurk worker guessed how old someone would need to be to answer them, and more than 78% of those questions can be answered by someone 10 years old or younger — a rough but telling signal that very little background knowledge is actually required to do well on the leaderboard.

Prior work had tried to add a knowledge requirement — KB-VQA and FVQA — but both tie every question to a single fact pulled from a small, fixed, structured knowledge base of subject-relation-object triplets (e.g. "dog is mammal"), and questions are authored by selecting one of those triplets, so answering them tests whether a model can consume a pre-identified fact, not whether it can figure out what to look up or where. Triplets are also a narrow representation: a lot of real-world knowledge (a company's founding year, why teddy bears are named after a president) doesn't reduce cleanly to one relation. Both datasets are also small — FVQA has 5,826 questions over 2,190 images, KB-VQA just 2,402 over 700.

OK-VQA's gap, then, is a dataset that is simultaneously (1) large enough to train and benchmark modern models, (2) genuinely unanswerable from image + question alone — enforced by manual human filtering rather than an automatic template — and (3) tied to open, unstructured knowledge (real Wikipedia text, or whatever a system chooses to retrieve) instead of a fixed triplet store, so a model has to decide what to query and how to fuse retrieved text with the image rather than consume a fact handed to it.

// how it works

The method

Collection starts from random COCO images, reusing COCO's own 80k/40k train/val split as OK-VQA's train/test split. In round one, MTurk workers write a question given an image — the same "try to fool a smart robot" prompt as the original VQA dataset — but with extra instructions: don't ask what's in the image or how many of something there is, and make sure the question needs some outside knowledge. That free-form prompt produces a lot of noise (basic counting questions, questions answerable without the image, nonsensical ones), so the authors manually filtered the raw pool: from 86,700 collected questions down to 34,921 that plausibly required real external knowledge. In round two, 5 independent MTurk workers each answer every surviving question-image pair, giving five answer annotations per question (VQA v1 used ten).

A final de-biasing pass removes the same kind of language-prior shortcut VQA v2 later targeted at scale: any question is dropped if one answer occurs as the single most common answer for more than 5 instances of that question (their example: "what season is it?" defaulting to "winter" whenever there's snow, ignoring the images that show other seasons), and any question with zero inter-annotator agreement is dropped too. That filtering is what brings the set down to its final size: 9,009 training questions + 5,046 test questions = 14,055 total, over 14,031 unique COCO images.

A separate labeling pass then assigns each surviving question a knowledge category: 5 MTurk workers each pick one of 10 fixed categories — Vehicles & Transportation; Brands, Companies & Products; Objects, Material & Clothing; Sports & Recreation; Cooking & Food; Geography, History, Language & Culture; People & Everyday Life; Plants & Animals; Science & Technology; Weather & Climate — with no-majority questions bucketed as "Other."

To give the field an actual knowledge-retrieval baseline rather than just a benchmark, the authors also built ArticleNet. For a question-image pair, it forms Wikipedia search queries by combining question words with the labels a pretrained ImageNet classifier, a Places2 scene classifier, and a COCO object detector produce for the image; hits the Wikipedia search API for the top article per query; and keeps the sentences most relevant to the query by word overlap. A GRU-based network then reads the retrieved title and top sentences and is trained to predict whether/where the ground-truth answer appears in that text (architecture in Figure 5 of the paper — parallel GRUs over the article title and each candidate sentence, feeding word-level and sentence-level classifiers). At inference, ArticleNet answers with the word maximizing the product below — the highest-scoring word in the highest-scoring sentence:

answer = argmaxi awi · asent(word score × sentence score) // ArticleNet's answer-selection rule (§3, "Benchmarking")

To fuse this retrieved knowledge with a standard VQA model, MUTAN+AN and BAN+AN feed ArticleNet's top retrieved-sentence hidden states into an end-to-end memory network, then concatenate the memory network's output with the VQA model's own fusion-layer hidden state before the final classifier. An oracle variant (MUTAN/AN oracle, BAN/AN oracle) simply takes whichever of the two systems' raw predictions matches the ground truth, giving an optimistic upper bound on how much complementary signal the retrieved knowledge carries.

// what they found

The results

Scale and difficulty first, against prior knowledge-VQA datasets (Table 1 in the paper): OK-VQA's 14,055 questions over 14,031 images dwarfs FVQA's 5,826/2,190 and KB-VQA's 2,402/700 while staying genuinely knowledge-based, unlike the 1.1M-question VQA v2, which the authors argue mostly doesn't need outside knowledge at all. Within the dataset, 12,591 of the 14,055 questions are unique phrasings drawn from 7,178 unique question words, and there are 14,454 unique answers — with the top 2,000 most frequent answers covering only 88.5057% of the test set, a much longer answer tail than closed-vocabulary VQA benchmarks typically have.

The category breakdown (Figure 3) is uneven: Plants & Animals (17%) and Vehicles & Transportation (16%) are the largest named categories, followed by Cooking & Food (15%) and Sports & Recreation (12%), while Science & Technology (2%) and Weather & Climate, Brands/Companies/Products, and Geography/History/Language/Culture (3% each) are comparatively rare. The "Other" bucket absorbs 12% of all questions — larger than six of the ten named categories, and tied with Sports & Recreation.

The headline benchmarking result (Table 2): every current state-of-the-art VQA model degrades sharply. MUTAN, the strongest pure vision-language baseline, scores only 26.41% overall on OK-VQA and BAN scores 25.17% — far below the 72.41% open-ended accuracy the best model achieved in the 2018 VQA Challenge on the standard, non-knowledge VQA task. A question-only baseline with no image at all (Q-Only) still reaches 14.93%, meaning the margin a strong image-aware model buys over ignoring the picture entirely is only about 11–12 points — much narrower than the corresponding gap on ordinary VQA.

ArticleNet alone is weak (5.28% — it can only answer with words that appear in its own imperfectly retrieved Wikipedia sentences), but fusing its retrieved-sentence representations into the VQA models gives a small, real lift: MUTAN 26.41% → 27.84% with AN, BAN 25.17% → 25.61% with AN. The oracle upper bounds — take whichever of the two systems' answers is correct — reach 28.47% (MUTAN/AN oracle) and 27.59% (BAN/AN oracle), suggesting the retrieved knowledge carries complementary signal current fusion isn't fully extracting. Per-category, the authors note ArticleNet's contribution is largest for Brands/Companies (18.95%→23.95%), People & Everyday Life (20.09%→25.05%), and Science & Technology (20.48%→24.76%) — categories they suggest are better covered by Wikipedia than, say, Plants & Animals, where MUTAN+AN actually scores slightly lower than MUTAN alone (30.44%→29.70%).

Two ablations round out the story. Swapping MUTAN's visual backbone from ResNet152 (26.41%) to ResNet50 (24.74%) to ResNet18 (23.64%) costs relatively little (Table 3), while dropping the image entirely (Q-Only, 14.93%) costs roughly 9–11 points — visual grounding clearly matters, but better generic image features aren't the bottleneck; retrieval and reasoning are. And a scale ablation (Figure 7) trains MUTAN on progressively larger random subsets of the training data (5%→100%) and finds the OK-VQA score climbing steadily from roughly 2 up to the full 26.4%, with no sign of saturating — more knowledge-VQA training data would likely keep helping.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

The entire premise of OK-VQA rests on one design decision: a human decides whether a crowd-written question "requires external knowledge." That filtering removed the large majority of raw submissions — 86,700 down to 34,921, then further down to 14,055 after de-biasing — but the paper describes no adjudication protocol for the judgment call itself: no second annotator layer re-confirming "this specific question truly cannot be answered from the image plus common sense," no reported inter-rater agreement for the knowledge-requirement filter (only for the separate answer-agreement filter used to remove biased questions), and no audit of borderline cases. "Requires knowledge no pixels supply" and "requires common sense an individual annotator simply didn't consciously register as inferable from the scene" (recognizing a building style as a church, a plant as a citrus tree) sit on a spectrum, and a single filterer's intuition is doing a great deal of unaudited work deciding which side of that line each of tens of thousands of questions falls on.

The 10-category knowledge taxonomy inherits the same problem one level up. Five MTurk workers each assign a category per question, and anything without a plurality winner defaults to "Other" — which absorbs 12% of the dataset, more than six of the ten named categories (Brands & Companies 3%, Geography/History/Culture 3%, Weather & Climate 3%, Science & Technology 2%, Objects/Material/Clothing 8%, People & Everyday Life 9%). That's a visible signal of category-boundary disagreement the paper never quantifies with an agreement score, yet all of the per-category analysis in the paper — including the claim that ArticleNet "is particularly helpful for brands, science, and cooking categories" — is built on labels whose consistency was never measured.

The 2019 benchmark numbers (MUTAN 26.41%, best oracle 28.47%) were measured against vision-only fusion models with no access to external text beyond the toy ArticleNet pipeline. But the facts these questions require — teddy bears and Theodore Roosevelt, cows producing milk, humans having 23 chromosome pairs — are exactly the kind of widely documented, Wikipedia-grade trivia a modern LLM has memorized verbatim, and the images are drawn from COCO, one of the most heavily reused image sets in vision-language pretraining. A modern multimodal model answering OK-VQA today may not be demonstrating "retrieve-then-reason over knowledge it doesn't have memorized" at all — it may simply be pattern-matching a familiar-looking stock photo to parametric trivia, a fundamentally easier capability than the retrieval-and-fusion problem the 2019 baselines struggled with. The paper predates this concern entirely and offers nothing — no held-out re-annotation on fresh images, no text-only-vs-image ablation designed to isolate memorization — that would let a reader separate genuine visual-knowledge reasoning from memorized trivia keyed to a recognizable photo.

baselines

MUTAN and BAN were genuine 2019 state-of-the-art VQA models reused faithfully, and the ResNet-depth ablation (Table 3) is a fair sanity check that the drop isn't just weak image features. But the knowledge baseline, ArticleNet, is a shallow keyword-search-plus-GRU pipeline that scores only 5.28% on its own — likely understating how much a stronger dense-retrieval system could close the gap, which makes "even retrieval barely helps" partly an artifact of a weak retriever rather than an inherent property of the task.

contamination

No contamination story exists for 2019-era baselines with zero external text access at inference — but the questions are built from widely documented Wikipedia-grade facts over COCO images, both maximally likely to already be memorized by any modern LLM or VLM. Today's high scores on OK-VQA may conflate "correctly retrieves the fact" with "already knows the fact from pretraining and recognizes a familiar stock photo," which the 2019 baselines could not do.

what's missed

No inter-annotator agreement is reported for either the core "does this need outside knowledge" filter or the 10-category knowledge-type labels, which dump 12% of questions into an unquantified "Other" bucket — the taxonomy underlying all of the paper's per-category analysis is itself unaudited for consistency.

would raise confidence

A published inter-rater agreement score (or an adjudicated second pass) on both the knowledge-requirement filter and the category labels; a stronger dense-retrieval baseline to separate "retrieval is genuinely hard" from "this particular retriever is weak"; and a periodic contamination check — e.g. comparing modern parametric recall against retrieval-augmented answers — now that models increasingly may already know the underlying trivia outright.

// bottom line

OK-VQA is a real, useful push past pixel-only VQA — the scale (14,055 questions across 14,031 images), the explicit anti-bias filtering, and the honest degradation of 2019 state-of-the-art models (72.41%→26.41%) make a credible case that outside knowledge, not cleverer fusion, was the bottleneck for those systems. But its central label — "this specific question requires external knowledge" — was set by unaudited human judgment with no reported agreement statistic, its knowledge taxonomy leaves 12% of questions in an unexplained "Other" bucket, and its underlying facts are exactly the kind of well-documented trivia a modern pretrained model has already memorized. A high score on OK-VQA today says less about retrieval-and-reasoning ability than the paper's framing implies.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. OK-VQA is 14,055 questions over 14,031 COCO images, hand-filtered from an initial pool of 86,700 MTurk submissions down to 34,921 knowledge-requiring questions and then de-biased to the final set — deliberately smaller and slower to collect than VQA v2's 1.1M questions, trading scale for questions that plumb one of 10 knowledge categories (Plants & Animals 17%, Vehicles & Transportation 16%, and Cooking & Food 15% are the largest) instead of image content.
  2. State-of-the-art 2019 VQA models collapse on it: MUTAN, the best pure vision-language baseline, manages only 26.41% (BAN: 25.17%) versus the ~72.41% open-ended accuracy the best model reached on standard VQA in the 2018 Challenge — and a naive Wikipedia-retrieval baseline (ArticleNet) only lifts MUTAN to 27.84%, with an oracle combining both capping out at 28.47%, showing that 2019-era knowledge retrieval barely closes the gap.
  3. The benchmark's core judgment calls — "this question needs external knowledge" and "this question belongs to category X" — are unaudited human filtering decisions with no reported inter-annotator agreement, and the underlying facts (presidential trivia, animal biology, chromosome counts) are exactly the kind of thing a modern pretrained LLM has memorized, making a high score on OK-VQA today a different, and likely easier, achievement than the one the 2019 authors set out to measure.
