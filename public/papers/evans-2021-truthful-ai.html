Truthful AI: Developing and governing AI that does not lie

A framework paper arguing AI systems should be held to truthfulness — not honesty — and proposing a practical "negligent falsehoods" standard, certifier/adjudicator institutions, and a technical research agenda, while naming its own biggest risk up front: a truthfulness-evaluation mechanism captured for censorship or propaganda.

Owain Evans, Owen Cotton-Barratt, Lukas Finnveden, Adam Bales, Avital Balwit, Peter Wills, Luca Righetti, William Saunders

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

In late 2021, language models were just becoming fluent enough to be mistaken for a person's writing — GPT-3, T5, and consumer assistants like Siri and Alexa were the state of the art the authors had in mind — and this paper anticipated a coming shift: AI systems that choose, from among many coherent statements they could make, whichever ones best fit some selection criterion (an AI selling a product picks statements likely to lead to a sale; an AI optimized for engagement picks statements likely to be clicked). If truth isn't one of the criteria being optimized for, the paper argues a system can produce statements that are damaging to others without ever forming an "intention to deceive" in any human sense — and because AI-produced statements can be personalized and delivered to millions of people at once, the potential for harm scales in a way that a single lying human never could.

Human truthfulness is governed by a patchwork of social norms and narrow laws — perjury, fraud, defamation — none of which map cleanly onto software. In 2021 there was no equivalent standard, law, or technical convention for what an AI system that produces false statements should be held to. The paper's central bet is that this gap is worth closing deliberately and early, before an ad hoc reaction to some future high-profile AI falsehood ends up setting whatever norm sticks: the authors argue explicitly that "there is a real possibility that the core of the initial standards persists – constitution-like – over decades" as AI-produced speech comes to represent a large share of all communication.

// how it works

The framework

This isn't an empirical paper — there's no dataset, model, or experiment. Its contribution is a normative framework, built in three deliberately separated moves that match the paper's own three-part structure: identify a truthfulness standard, sketch institutions that could enforce it, and outline the technical work needed to build systems that meet it.

The standard. The paper draws a sharp line between honesty (a statement matches what the system believes) and truthfulness (a statement matches the world, regardless of belief), and argues for holding AI systems to truthfulness rather than honesty. Truthfulness sidesteps the philosophically fraught question of what it means for an AI system to "believe" something, and it closes a specific failure mode the authors call out directly: a system trained under strong incentive to produce false statements could become "strategically-deluded" — it could come to genuinely believe the falsehoods it's rewarded for stating, which would let it pass an honesty check while remaining harmful. Since perfect truthfulness is unachievable, the paper's actual proposed bright line is narrower: a standard against negligent falsehoods — statements a system "should have been able to recognise as unacceptably likely to be false." This is deliberately modeled on human negligence law rather than a standard requiring proof of intent or of downstream harm, which the paper argues is "difficult and expensive to judge, as it requires tracing causality of events well beyond the statement made." One proposed operationalization: judge a statement as negligent by comparing it against what other AI systems, in a maximally similar situation, would have said.

The institutions. The paper sketches the roles needed to make a truthfulness standard enforceable: certification bodies that evaluate whether a deployed AI system is robustly truthful, and adjudication bodies that rule on whether a specific contested statement violated the standard, sitting between the AI developer, the AI system, whoever is directing it (the "principal"), and the end user.

The paper deliberately leaves the governance model open — industry-led self-regulation via private certifying bodies, top-down legal mandates, or some mix — while arguing the bottleneck is the same either way: enforcement requires someone who can actually detect a violation, or a way for compliant systems to credibly signal adherence.

The technical agenda. Finally, the paper sketches research directions for building systems that meet the standard: filtering training corpora for truthfulness, retrieval from trusted sources, and RLHF-style feedback in the near term; and, since the authors argue that as AI systems exceed human-level competence it becomes as hard for a person to fact-check them as "a hunter-gatherer evaluating a scientific claim like 'birds evolved from dinosaurs,'" moving past "training black-box AI with human examples as the gold standard" toward scalable-oversight ideas like AI-assisted human supervision (bootstrapping) or transparency tools that function as an analogue of a lie detector.

// what they found

The proposals and predictions

Because this is a position paper, its "results" are the specific proposals and claims it commits to — several concrete enough to check against what's actually happened in AI safety and evaluation research in the years since.

On benefits, the paper distinguishes two timelines: some benefits (fraud detection, more reliable AI-brokered deals between humans, reduced principal-agent problems) only need today's systems to be more truthful; others compound only once AI plays a much larger economic role, and some — like meaningfully improving democratic epistemics on controversial topics — require systems to engage with contested questions at all, which the paper flags as the point where power struggles over "what counts as truthful" become most likely.

On costs, the paper identifies three that persist even under a well-implemented standard. First, some falsehoods are genuinely beneficial: fiction, simplified explanations for children, protecting a whistleblower's identity or commercially sensitive information, or a white lie about a haircut — and a truthfulness mandate would suppress the ones that only work if the audience doesn't realize they're false (caveats can rescue the fiction case, but not the white-lie case). Second, the very trust that a strong truthfulness standard builds could make the occasional remaining falsehood more damaging, not less, precisely because people would be primed to believe it. Third, compliance and enforcement carry a real financial cost that raises the price of deploying AI.

The paper's sharpest and most self-critical prediction is about capture: it names, in its own abstract, the risk that "evaluation mechanisms for eventual truthfulness standards could be captured by political interests, leading to harmful censorship and propaganda" — the exact failure mode where a truth-arbitration mechanism gets treated as settled and then used to suppress dissenting-but-arguably-true claims. Its proposed mitigations are qualitative design principles rather than a mechanism: target only "egregious untruths" instead of enforcing conformity, ensure truth-adjudication methods "should not be strongly anchored on precedent," and explicitly let AI systems propose alternative or unconventional views so long as they're truthful about those views being contested.

One connection worth naming: this paper's lead author, Owain Evans, is also a co-author of TruthfulQA (Lin, Hilton & Evans), released within a month of this paper in 2021 — the closest thing to an empirical instrument for the standard argued for here, scoring models against human-judged truthfulness rather than defining it in the abstract. And the specific honesty-vs-truthfulness split this paper draws is cited directly by later work: the MASK benchmark (2025) is built explicitly around "disentangling honesty from accuracy," the same distinction this paper introduces.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

Read as a piece of concept engineering, this paper holds up well: the honesty/truthfulness distinction is precise, motivated by a real and specific failure mode (strategic delusion under an honesty-only check), and it has visibly shaped later empirical work — MASK's honesty-vs-accuracy split is this paper's framing, operationalized five years later. But read as an "eval-rigor" contribution — which is the lens this page applies to every paper — its central weakness is that it never builds the instrument its own argument calls for.

The "negligent falsehoods" standard is proposed as the paper's practical bright line, but it is never operationalized here: no dataset, no worked example scoring a real 2021 system (GPT-3 is discussed only anecdotally, via the "breaking a mirror brings seven years of bad luck" example), no scorable protocol. Contrast this with TruthfulQA, published by an overlapping author within weeks, which took a version of the same idea and shipped an actual benchmark. This paper stays entirely at the level of definitions and diagrams — a deliberate scope choice the authors are explicit about, but one that means none of its central claims are falsifiable in the way a benchmark paper's claims are.

The paper's most checkable prediction is institutional: that truthfulness norms would be established "deliberately or organically" over the following years, potentially through the certifier/adjudicator apparatus sketched in Diagram 3. With hindsight to 2026, that isn't what happened — no binding cross-industry truthfulness certification or adjudication body has emerged. What happened instead was empirical and benchmark-driven: TruthfulQA, MASK, hallucination leaderboards, and voluntary model/system cards, none of which resemble the formal certifier-and-adjudicator institutions this paper describes. The paper under-weighted how much faster benchmark-driven, non-institutional pressure would move relative to the kind of governance apparatus it sketches.

There's also a structural gap in the negligent-falsehoods standard itself, which the paper only partially addresses. Its own proposed way of judging negligence is to compare a statement "with statements made by other AI systems (designed for similar purposes) when placed in a maximally similar situation" — which has an obvious failure mode if false statements are correlated across models: shared pretraining data, shared RLHF raters, or shared benchmark contamination could make an entire population of systems negligent in the same way, which would validate a bar that's actually too low. The paper explicitly notices this — "another problem is if all AI systems in a domain have similar incentives, and thus make similar (false) statements" — and its own suggested fix (evaluators building a dedicated comparison AI) doesn't fully resolve it: a purpose-built comparator trained the same way as the systems it's judging could inherit the same correlated blind spot, a possibility the paper doesn't address.

And on the paper's own headline worry — capture of the evaluation mechanism for censorship or propaganda — the proposed defenses (aim to "avoid egregious untruths" rather than force conformity; ensure adjudication methods "should not be strongly anchored on precedent") are design principles, not measurable safeguards. There's no proposed audit or metric for detecting when an adjudication mechanism has, in fact, been captured — a conspicuous gap in a paper whose central concern is exactly that. To the authors' credit, they are unusually candid about the limits of their own proposal: "nobody has full knowledge of what's true; every mechanism we can specify would make errors" is a rare admission for a paper arguing for a new institutional standard.

baselines

None — this is a conceptual/governance paper with no experiments, models, or evaluated systems; there is nothing to benchmark.

contamination

Not applicable in the usual sense. The closer analogue is definitional precision: the paper is careful to hold "truthfulness" and "honesty" apart from their everyday overlapping meanings, and explicit that its practical bright line (negligent falsehoods) is a compromise short of "perfect truthfulness," which it admits is unachievable in practice.

what's missed

No worked evaluation of any real system against the negligent-falsehoods standard; no concrete audit mechanism for detecting adjudication-body capture despite naming capture as the central risk; the population-comparison basis for "negligence" is vulnerable to correlated errors across models sharing training data or RLHF raters — a risk the paper names but doesn't resolve.

would raise confidence

A worked case study scoring a real system's outputs against the negligent-falsehoods standard (which TruthfulQA effectively supplied, independently, within weeks); a concrete proposal for auditing adjudication-body capture rather than just naming the risk; a discussion of how correlated failure across models undermines a population-relative negligence bar.

// bottom line

A careful and self-aware framework paper whose central distinction — truthfulness over honesty, operationalized as avoiding negligent falsehoods — has aged well and visibly shaped later work. But it is a governance essay, not an evaluation method: it names the right problem and the right risks, including its own biggest one (capture of the evaluation mechanism), without shipping the dataset, metric, or audit tool that would let anyone actually check whether a given AI system meets the bar it proposes. That gap was filled by other papers, not this one.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. Truthful AI argues for holding AI systems to truthfulness (statements match the world) rather than honesty (statements match belief), because truthfulness closes a specific loophole: a system trained under strong incentive to state falsehoods could become "strategically-deluded" and pass an honesty check while still causing harm.
  2. Its practical bright line is avoiding "negligent falsehoods" — statements a system should have recognized as unacceptably likely to be false, judged relative to what comparable AI systems would say in the same situation — rather than requiring proof of intent or of downstream damage, which the paper argues is too costly to trace.
  3. The paper's own headline worry — that a truthfulness-evaluation mechanism could be captured by political interests for censorship or propaganda — is still unresolved five years later, and the field's actual response has looked more like empirical benchmarks (TruthfulQA, MASK) than the certifier/adjudicator institutions this paper sketches.
