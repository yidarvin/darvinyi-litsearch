Understanding R1-Zero-Like Training: A Critical Perspective

A scalpel taken to the R1-Zero story. Qwen2.5 base models already behave like fine-tuned math solvers before any RL; the famous "response length keeps growing" curve is partly a bug in GRPO that mechanically inflates wrong answers; and a one-line fix — Dr. GRPO — restores an unbiased gradient. The minimalist recipe still reaches 43.3% on AIME 2024 with a 7B model in 27 hours on 8× A100s.

Zichen Liu, Changyu Chen, Wenjun Li, Penghui Qi, Tianyu Pang, Chao Du, Wee Sun Lee, Min Lin · Sea AI Lab · NUS · SMU

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

DeepSeek-R1-Zero made a startling claim: apply reinforcement learning directly to a base LLM — no supervised fine-tuning first — and reasoning emerges on its own. Two phenomena became the iconic evidence. First, the model's average response length climbs steadily through training. Second, an "Aha moment" appears: the model spontaneously starts saying "wait," "let me reconsider," and other self-reflection patterns. The accompanying narrative was seductive — pure RL, from a blank base, discovers long chain-of-thought reasoning from scratch.

A wave of open replications followed, and almost all of them built on the Qwen2.5 model family. That choice is exactly where this paper drives its wedge. Nobody had cleanly separated two contributions that are tangled together in every R1-Zero result: how much comes from the base model (which may already be a strong math solver), and how much from the RL itself. Worse, the headline signals had not been audited. Is rising response length a reasoning signal, or an optimization artifact? Is the "Aha moment" really induced by RL, or was it already sitting in the base model? And tellingly, the prior studies arguing "there's no real Aha emergence" had never actually tested DeepSeek-V3-Base — the 685B base model the real R1-Zero was trained from.

So the authors probe a wide range of base models (Qwen2.5-Math-1.5B/7B, Qwen2.5-7B, Llama-3.1-8B, DeepSeek-Math-7B, and DeepSeek-V3-Base-685B) along three axes that matter for R1-Zero training, before any RL touches them.

// how it works

The method

The paper has two analytical halves and one fix.

Part 1 — Templates turn base models into policies (and Qwen is special)

A base model trained for sentence completion models p(x); R1-Zero training needs a question-answering policy π(·|q). A prompt template is what bridges the two. The authors compare the R1 template (the <think>…</think><answer>…</answer> scaffold), the Qwen-Math template, and no template. They measure (a) answering rate — does the model answer rather than continue the text, judged by GPT-4o-mini; (b) exploration — pass@8, because if a base policy can never sample a correct trajectory there is no reward signal and RL is dead on arrival; and (c) self-reflection counts via cross-validated keyword + LLM detection.

The surprise: Qwen2.5 base models answer best with no template at all (100% answering rate), and dropping the template lifts Qwen2.5-Math accuracy by roughly 60% over standard 4-shot prompting. The authors hypothesize Qwen2.5-Math was pretrained on concatenated question–answer text — i.e. the "base" model is effectively already SFT-like. DeepSeek-V3-Base, by contrast, has the lowest no-template answering rate: a nearly pure base model — and it still shows the Aha moment.

Part 2 — GRPO secretly biases the optimization

With a rule-based verifier as the reward, the usual concern that drives the KL penalty — keeping the policy near where a learned reward model is accurate — disappears, so the authors set β = 0 (no KL) throughout. That leaves the policy-gradient objective. GRPO (Shao et al., 2024) samples a group of G responses per question, scores them, and sets each token's advantage to the group-normalized reward, while normalizing each response's loss by its length 1/|oᵢ|. Both of those normalizers, the paper argues, are biases:

  • Response-level length bias (from dividing by |oᵢ|): for a correct response, shorter answers get larger per-token updates → the policy is pushed toward brevity. For an incorrect response, a longer answer is penalized less (its negative advantage is spread over more tokens) → the policy drifts toward ever-longer wrong answers. This is the real engine behind "length keeps growing."
  • Question-level difficulty bias (from dividing by std(R)): questions that are nearly all-right or all-wrong have std≈0 and get up-weighted, distorting the objective by difficulty.

The fix is almost embarrassingly simple: delete both normalizers. Drop 1/|oᵢ| and drop std(R). The authors call the result Dr. GRPO ("GRPO Done Right"). Their derivation shows this recovers the plain Monte-Carlo policy gradient with a mean baseline — i.e. the unbiased PPO objective — and that the resulting advantage equals REINFORCE-Leave-One-Out (RLOO) up to a constant scale that folds into the learning rate.

Ãi,t  =  R(q, oi) − mean({R(q,o1),…,R(q,oG)}) // Dr. GRPO advantage — the unbiased baseline, with ÷ std(R) removed and the loss ÷ |oi| removed (replaced by a constant token budget)

The authors also point out that this is not unique to GRPO: popular open-source PPO implementations — trl, OpenRLHF, verl, and the R1-Zero replication codebases SimpleRL-Zero and Open-Reasoner-Zero — all normalize the loss by response length too, a misalignment with the PPO objective that predates GRPO and probably leaked in from pretraining's loss.mean() convention.

Putting the insights together gives the minimalist recipe: Qwen2.5-Math-7B + Dr. GRPO + MATH (level 3–5) questions + the Qwen-Math template → Oat-Zero-7B, trained in 27 hours on 8× A100 GPUs.

// what they found

The results

On five math benchmarks at a matched 3k-token generation budget, Oat-Zero-7B averages 51.4% and posts 43.3% on AIME 2024 — the strongest AIME number among comparable 7B "zero-RL" models, beating SimpleRL-Zero-7B (46.6 avg), PRIME-Zero-7B (48.0), and OpenReasoner-Zero-7B (43.0 @ 3k). Note where it wins and loses: Oat-Zero dominates AIME (43.3 vs the next-best 26.7) while sitting mid-pack on MATH500 (80.0) and Minerva (30.1).

Dr. GRPO's real win is efficiency, not accuracy

In the main run, Dr. GRPO and vanilla GRPO reach similar training reward and benchmark accuracy (across multiple seeds, Appendix C reports Dr. GRPO's accuracy edge as small but statistically significant). The headline difference is what happens to length. GRPO keeps inflating the length of incorrect responses — climbing from roughly 1.0k to 1.8k tokens long after reward has plateaued — while Dr. GRPO holds it flat. The lesson: the "length keeps growing → reasoning is emerging" story is confounded by the optimization bias; remove the bias and the spurious growth stops, at the same or slightly better score. Dr. GRPO's clear win is token efficiency and mitigating "overthinking."

Templates, question coverage, and "less is more"

RL lifts every template to a comparable ~40% plateau given a suitable question set — but the path differs. With the R1 template, narrow question coverage caps the plateau; with the Qwen-Math template, training on the simpler and out-of-distribution GSM-8K set nearly doubles hard-test accuracy. The authors' reading: because the Qwen2.5-Math base already has the math ability, RL is mostly reinforcing existing reasoning behaviors, not infusing new knowledge — "applying templates in fact destroys the capability before RL reconstructs it," so one "should be more conservative in claiming the huge gains brought about by pure RL."

Domain pretraining raises the RL ceiling

Can R1-Zero training work on a model that is weak at math to begin with? On vanilla Llama-3.2-3B, RL barely moves the needle (3.3 → 6.8 avg). Continual-pretraining it on math first changes everything: Llama-3.2-3B-FineMath rises to 14.8, and a concatenated-QA continual-pretrain (Oat-Zero-3B) reaches 20.7 — and the same GRPO length-blowup reappears on Llama, again fixed by Dr. GRPO. Domain knowledge in pretraining, not the RL algorithm, is what lifts the ceiling. (For reference, Oat-Zero-1.5B averages 42.1, edging out R1-Distill-Qwen-1.5B at an 8k budget, 41.5.)

// does it hold up?

The eval read

This is two papers wearing one title, and they hold up very differently. The diagnosis-and-fix half — GRPO's length bias, the matching bias in trl/verl/OpenRLHF, and the clean Dr. GRPO derivation — is rigorous, falsifiable, and has been widely adopted. The "new SOTA" half deserves more skepticism, and the authors, to their credit, hand you most of the ammunition.

Start with the comparison budget. Every baseline is evaluated at a 3k-token generation cap, chosen because Qwen2.5-Math's base context is 4k. But models trained for longer outputs are hobbled at 3k: R1-Distill-Qwen-7B scores 28.5 avg @ 3k and 54.7 @ 8k. At its native budget that distilled model beats Oat-Zero-7B's 51.4. The paper reports both numbers — so it is transparent — but it means "state-of-the-art" is scoped to comparable zero-RL methods at a short budget that structurally flatters a method whose whole selling point is shorter responses, not the absolute frontier. The headline AIME 43.3 also rests on a 30-problem benchmark where each question is worth 3.3 points, and the main bar chart shows point estimates without error bars (a significance/ablation study lives in Appendix C, not in the headline figure).

The deeper caveat is the one the paper itself raises: its central claim — that Qwen2.5-Math base models are "already SFT-like," pretrained on concatenated Q–A text — is an unverified hypothesis. They have no access to Qwen's pretraining corpus; the ~60% no-template jump is strong circumstantial evidence, not proof of mechanism. And Oat-Zero-7B is built on exactly that base, so a large part of its apparent "RL gain" is plausibly elicitation of latent ability rather than new capability — which is precisely the deflationary point of the rest of the paper. Finally, every result here is math; whether the base-model bias and the length bias generalize to code or other domains is untested.

baselines

Fair set of 7B zero-RL peers (SimpleRL-Zero, PRIME-Zero, OpenReasoner-Zero), but all judged at a 3k-token cap that disadvantages long-output models. R1-Distill-Qwen-7B overtakes Oat-Zero at its native 8k budget (54.7 vs 51.4) — reported, but easy to miss under "SOTA."

contamination

No leakage audit of the eval sets, and the core "Qwen base is pre-SFT'd on Q–A text" claim is a hypothesis without access to Qwen's pretraining data. If true, the math benchmarks partly measure pretraining exposure, not RL.

what's missed

AIME 2024 is 30 problems (43.3% ≈ 13/30) → high variance; the headline figure has no error bars. Dr. GRPO's clear win is token efficiency; its accuracy edge over GRPO is small in the main figure (reported as statistically significant across seeds in App. C). Self-reflection "Aha" is decoupled from accuracy, but rests on keyword+LLM detection heuristics.

would raise confidence

Multi-seed error bars on AIME; a base model deliberately trained without Q–A concatenation to test the elicitation hypothesis causally; all baselines reported at multiple budgets; results beyond 7B and beyond math.

// bottom line

Read this as a deflation of the "pure RL discovers reasoning from scratch" narrative, not as a new capability ceiling. The robust, reusable contributions are the length-bias diagnosis and the unbiased Dr. GRPO fix — they travel far beyond this paper. The 43.3% AIME headline is the fragile part: it rides a base model the authors themselves argue is quasi-SFT'd, under a generation budget that favors short answers.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. The iconic "response length keeps growing" curve in R1-Zero training is partly an optimization artifact: GRPO's 1/|oᵢ| normalizer penalizes long wrong answers too little. Dr. GRPO — delete the 1/|oᵢ| and std(R) terms — recovers an unbiased PPO/RLOO gradient and keeps length flat at the same accuracy. The same length bias hides in trl, verl, and OpenRLHF.
  2. Qwen2.5(-Math) base models already behave like fine-tuned math solvers — best with no template, a ~60% jump — so much of the "RL gain" reported on them is elicitation, not emergence (the paper's "already SFT-like" reading is its own explicit, unverified hypothesis). The "Aha moment" appears in base models too (including the pure DeepSeek-V3-Base) and its frequency does not correlate with accuracy.
  3. What actually lifts the ceiling is domain pretraining, not the RL algorithm: math continual-pretraining takes Llama-3.2-3B's RL result from 6.8 → 20.7. Less can be more — Oat-Zero-7B reaches 43.3% AIME 2024 in just 27 hours on 8× A100s — but evaluate "pure RL" claims against a genuine base model and a fair generation budget.
