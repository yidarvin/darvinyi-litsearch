Energy-based Out-of-distribution Detection

A classifier's softmax confidence is not the same number as its logits' free energy — it's a shifted, biased version of it, and the shift happens to erase exactly the magnitude information that separates in-distribution inputs from out-of-distribution ones. This paper names that quantity, shows it can be read straight off any pre-trained classifier's logits for free, and demonstrates that fine-tuning to widen its gap works better than fine-tuning the softmax directly.

Weitang Liu, Xiaoyun Wang, John D. Owens, Yixuan Li

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

By 2020 the standard way to flag an out-of-distribution (OOD) input was still the one Hendrycks & Gimpel proposed in 2016: take a classifier's softmax output and use the maximum softmax probability (MSP) as a confidence score — low confidence means OOD. The trouble is that neural networks can produce arbitrarily high softmax confidence for inputs that look nothing like their training data. Because softmax is a normalized distribution over classes, a network can be "sure" an unfamiliar image belongs to some class even when its raw evidence for every class is weak — the normalization discards exactly the information (the overall magnitude of the logits) that would say so. ODIN (Liang, Li & Srikant, 2018) patched this with temperature scaling plus input perturbation, and the Mahalanobis-distance detector (Lee et al., 2018) sidestepped softmax entirely by fitting class-conditional Gaussians in feature space — but both bolt extra machinery, and often extra hyperparameters, onto a training signal (cross-entropy loss) that was never designed with OOD detection in mind.

This paper's starting observation is that the fix doesn't require new machinery at all — it requires looking at a quantity classifiers already compute. Section 3.2 works out the exact algebraic relationship between the softmax confidence score and the network's energy (defined below): the log of the softmax confidence equals the energy plus the maximum logit, log maxy p(y|x) = E(x;f) + fmax(x). Since fmax(x) tends to be large and E(x;f) tends to be low specifically for in-distribution inputs, this shift systematically inflates the confidence score and is a source of the overconfidence problem — the score a practitioner actually reads off is a biased view of the more informative underlying quantity. Figure 2 makes this concrete with one real pair of examples from a CIFAR-10-trained WideResNet: an in-distribution CIFAR-10 image and an out-of-distribution SVHN image whose softmax confidences are 1.0 and 0.99 respectively — visually indistinguishable — while their energy scores are −11.19 and −7.11, a large and usable gap.

// how it works

The method

The paper's move is to reinterpret an ordinary discriminative classifier through the lens of an energy-based model (EBM, LeCun et al. 2006), where any real-valued function of an input can define an energy, and lower energy is associated with higher probability density. For a classifier f(x): ℝᴰ → ℝᴷ producing logits, the free energy over inputs is defined directly from those logits via the log-sum-exp of the softmax denominator:

E(x; f) = −T · log Σi efi(x)/T // f_i(x): the i-th logit; T: temperature (the paper uses T = 1, making the score parameter-free); no re-training or extra data required

Why should this quantity track OOD-ness? Rewriting the standard negative-log-likelihood loss in terms of energy shows that ordinary cross-entropy training already pushes down E(x,y) = −f_y(x) for the ground-truth label on in-distribution training data, while pulling up the energy of every other label — the free energy E(x;f) is a smooth approximation of that same push-down quantity. So without adding any new loss term, a classifier trained the normal way is already implicitly shaping its energy surface to be low for in-distribution data; the paper's contribution is to notice this and read the resulting energy off directly, rather than only reading off the post-softmax confidence that discards it. Figure 1 sketches the resulting detector: an input's energy is computed from the pre-trained network, and it's flagged OOD if its negative energy falls below a threshold τ chosen on in-distribution validation data.

Because the energy score is parameter-free at T = 1, it can replace MSP as a pure post-hoc score on any already-trained classifier — no retraining, no extra data, no threshold search beyond the one every detector needs. But the paper goes further: since a plain cross-entropy-trained model's implicit energy gap is a side effect rather than an optimization target, it proposes explicitly widening that gap with an energy-bounded fine-tuning objective. The classifier is fine-tuned with the usual cross-entropy loss on in-distribution data plus a regularizer built from two squared hinge terms — one that penalizes in-distribution energy above a margin min, and one that penalizes auxiliary out-of-distribution energy below a margin mout:

Lenergy = 𝔼(xin,y) (max(0, E(xin) − min))² + 𝔼xout (max(0, mout − E(xout)))² // x_out is drawn from an auxiliary outlier set (80 Million Tiny Images, with CIFAR-10/100 overlap removed); optimal margins found via validation search: m_in = −23, m_out = −5 for CIFAR-10, m_in = −27, m_out = −5 for CIFAR-100

This is the same auxiliary-data fine-tuning recipe as Outlier Exposure (OE, Hendrycks et al. 2019), which instead regularizes the softmax output toward a uniform distribution on outlier samples — the energy paper's claim is that shaping the energy gap directly is a more effective training signal than shaping the softmax distribution indirectly, using the identical auxiliary data and training budget for a fair comparison.

// what they found

The results

On a CIFAR-10-trained WideResNet, averaged across six OOD test sets (Textures, SVHN, Places365, LSUN-Crop, LSUN-Resize, iSUN), swapping the post-hoc score from softmax confidence to energy — with the same pre-trained network, no retraining — takes average FPR95 from 51.04% to 33.01%, an 18.03-point drop, while AUROC ticks up from 90.90% to 91.88%. Figure 3 shows why: the energy-score distributions for in-distribution CIFAR-10 and out-of-distribution SVHN samples separate more cleanly than the corresponding softmax-score distributions, which pile up in spiky peaks near 1.0 for both populations.

Fine-tuning with the energy-bounded objective, using 80 Million Tiny Images as the auxiliary outlier set, pushes further: average FPR95 on CIFAR-10 drops to 3.32% (vs. OE's 8.53% under the identical training recipe) with AUROC at 98.92% and AUPR at 99.75%. On the harder, 100-class CIFAR-100 in-distribution setting, energy fine-tuning reaches 47.55% average FPR95 versus OE's 58.10% — a 10.55-point improvement — though the absolute numbers stay much higher than on CIFAR-10 across every method, reflecting how much harder OOD detection gets as the in-distribution task itself grows more fine-grained.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

The headline framing — "energy score is a parameter-free, drop-in improvement over softmax" — is true and useful, but it undersells how much of the paper's strongest result depends on a resource plain post-hoc scoring doesn't have: auxiliary outlier data at training time. It's worth separating the two regimes the paper reports, because they aren't equally free.

The fine-tuned numbers need what the post-hoc numbers don't. Energy fine-tuning's best results (3.32% FPR95 on CIFAR-10, 47.55% on CIFAR-100) require access to 80 Million Tiny Images as an auxiliary, unlabeled OOD-like training set, plus a validation sweep over margin hyperparameters (min ∈ {−3,−5,−7}, mout ∈ {−15,−19,−23,−27}). That's a legitimate apples-to-apples comparison against Outlier Exposure, which uses identical data and budget — but it's a materially different claim from the "just read the logits" pitch used to sell the post-hoc score, and a practitioner without access to a suitable large, diverse auxiliary set can't reach these numbers at all.

Pure post-hoc energy isn't unconditionally state-of-the-art. Table 2's own numbers show the post-hoc energy score losing to Mahalanobis by a wide margin on CIFAR-100 without any fine-tuning: 73.60% vs. 54.04% average FPR95, and 79.56% vs. 84.12% AUROC. Mahalanobis needs its own machinery — fitting class-conditional Gaussians over intermediate-layer features, an input-perturbation magnitude, and (in the original formulation) a logistic regression over layers calibrated with a handful of validation OOD samples — so it isn't "free" either, but the paper's framing (energy score as parameter-free and broadly comparable-or-better than ODIN) doesn't foreground that Mahalanobis, its most feature-space-heavy competitor, still wins outright on the harder in-distribution dataset. Per-dataset detail (Table 4) shows the same pattern isn't uniform even within CIFAR-10: on Texture as the OOD set, plain energy score's AUROC (85.22) is actually worse than plain softmax's (88.50), even though its FPR95 is better (52.79 vs. 59.28) — a reminder that FPR95 and AUROC don't always rank methods the same way, and the paper leads with FPR95 throughout.

The temperature ablation quietly complicates ODIN's core claim. ODIN's central mechanism is that temperature scaling improves OOD separability for the softmax score. This paper's own Figure 4(a) shows the opposite for the energy score: as T increases from 1 to 1000, FPR95 gets worse, monotonically, across every OOD dataset tested — the paper's conclusion is that T = 1 (i.e., no temperature tuning at all) is already optimal for energy scoring. The two results aren't strictly contradictory (they're operating on different score constructions), but the paper doesn't reconcile why the same knob helps one scoring rule and hurts the other, leaving a genuine open question about what temperature is actually doing in each case.

baselines

Reasonably strong and current for late 2020: MSP, ODIN, and Mahalanobis cover the major pre-trained-model detectors, and Outlier Exposure is the right like-for-like fine-tuning comparison since both methods share the same auxiliary data and training budget. JEM and generative hybrids (Glow, IGEBM) round out the generative-modeling comparison.

contamination

Handled explicitly: the auxiliary 80 Million Tiny Images set has CIFAR-10/CIFAR-100 overlap removed before use, which is the standard precaution for this training regime. No broader train/test leakage concern for a paper with six disjoint, well-established OOD test sets.

what's missed

No stress test on why Mahalanobis wins on CIFAR-100 pre-trained (is it feature richness at deeper layers, or something else?); no ablation isolating how much of the fine-tuned gain comes from the auxiliary data's diversity/scale versus the energy-shaped loss itself; no discussion reconciling the temperature-scaling reversal against ODIN's finding.

would raise confidence

A controlled comparison holding the auxiliary outlier set fixed while varying only the fine-tuning objective (energy-bounded vs. OE) at multiple auxiliary-set sizes, to isolate how much of the gain is the loss shape versus the data; and a direct discussion of the temperature-scaling reversal versus ODIN.

// bottom line

The core contribution — that the free energy is the theoretically grounded, unshifted quantity softmax confidence approximates badly — is a real and durable insight, and the parameter-free post-hoc swap is genuinely useful with zero cost. But "energy beats everything" is really two separate, unevenly-resourced claims: a modest, free win over softmax at inference time, and a much larger win at fine-tuning time that leans on auxiliary OOD data the way Outlier Exposure already did — and even then, Mahalanobis's heavier feature-space machinery still wins outright on CIFAR-100 without any fine-tuning at all.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. Softmax confidence is a biased, shifted view of a classifier's logits: log max_y p(y|x) = E(x;f) + f_max(x), where the free energy E(x;f) = −T·log Σᵢ exp(fᵢ(x)/T) is the unshifted quantity that's theoretically aligned with the input's likelihood. Reading energy instead of softmax confidence off an already-trained network needs no retraining and no extra data.
  2. That swap alone cuts average FPR95 on CIFAR-10 from 51.04% (softmax) to 33.01% (energy) — an 18.03-point improvement, parameter-free at T = 1. Fine-tuning with a two-sided energy-margin hinge loss on auxiliary outlier data (80 Million Tiny Images) pushes it to 3.32%, beating Outlier Exposure's 8.53% under an identical training budget.
  3. The story isn't unconditionally in energy's favor: without fine-tuning, Mahalanobis still beats post-hoc energy score on CIFAR-100 (54.04% vs. 73.60% FPR95), and the paper's own temperature ablation shows scaling T hurts the energy score — the opposite of what ODIN found for the softmax score, a tension the paper doesn't resolve.
