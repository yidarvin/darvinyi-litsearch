Remote Labor Index: Measuring AI Automation of Remote Work

CAIS and Scale AI sourced 240 real freelance projects — 6,000+ hours of professional work valued at over $140,000 — then had humans judge whether frontier agents could pass as the hired worker. The best agent, Manus, completed just 2.5% to a standard a client would accept.

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

AI capability is tracked almost entirely through research benchmarks of knowledge and reasoning — MMLU, GPQA, FrontierMath, Humanity's Last Exam. They are clean to grade and they are saturating fast. But near-perfect scores on graduate-level quizzes say almost nothing about the question policymakers and workers actually care about: how much economically valuable work can today's AI actually do, end to end?

The benchmarks that do reach for "real work" each capture only a sliver. SWE-bench and SWE-Lancer measure software engineering; OSWorld, WebArena, and GAIA measure basic computer and web use; GDPval measures a set of writing, search, and administrative subtasks shared across professions. Each is a useful signal in isolation, yet none captures the diversity and complexity of the broader remote-labor economy — the design, audio-video, CAD, architecture, and operations work that people are actually paid to deliver. Performance on a coding benchmark tells you little about whether an agent can ship a 3D product render or a brewing-themed merge game.

So there has been no standardized, empirical measure of the trajectory of AI automation — nothing that grounds the automation debate in actual economic transactions rather than projections. The Remote Labor Index (RLI) is built to be exactly that: a benchmark of whole freelance projects, each anchored to a real price and a real gold-standard deliverable produced by the human who was hired to do it.

// how it works

The method

RLI is 240 end-to-end freelance projects. Each project has three parts: a brief (the text describing the work), input files (everything needed to do it), and a human deliverable — the gold-standard artifact a paid professional actually produced. Because the brief and inputs come from the person who completed the work, they are guaranteed sufficient to finish the job, and the recorded time and price make every project an anchored economic transaction rather than a synthetic task.

Sourcing real work, bottom-up

The team scoped collection with the Upwork taxonomy: of its 64 categories, they filtered out work that can't be cleanly benchmarked — physical labor, jobs requiring client interaction, back-end development, or anything (like SEO) that needs waiting to evaluate — leaving 43 eligible categories. They then went bottom-up, recruiting 358 verified Upwork freelancers (averaging 2,341 hours worked, 89 prior jobs, and $23,364 in earnings) to sell samples of their genuine past work, paid $15–$200 per project (average $41). That yielded 550 raw projects; freelance-platform sourcing produced 207, and a long-tail pass (custom commissions plus high-quality digital work found online, used only with permission and pricing) added another 40. After multiple rounds of review, cleaning, and standardization to a common schema, the final benchmark settled at 240 projects spanning 23 of Upwork's 64 categories — led by video (13%), CAD (12%), graphic design (11%), game development (10%), and audio (10%).

Difficulty and economic value

These are not toy tasks. RLI projects took humans a mean of 28.9 hours (median 11.5; max 450) and cost a mean of $632.6 (median $200; max $22,500). In aggregate the benchmark represents over 6,000 hours of real work valued at $143,991 — the dollar prize an agent that completed every project would earn. To guard against contamination, only 10 projects are public (released with the open-sourced evaluation platform); the other 230 are a private test set, none of the briefs are searchable, and a domain blocklist covers the long-tail items whose deliverables exist somewhere online.

Four metrics, one human anchor

Performance is read through four lenses. Automation rate is the fraction of projects where the AI deliverable is judged to complete the job at least as well as the human gold standard — i.e. would be accepted by a reasonable client. Elo captures relative quality from pairwise comparisons, with the human baseline canonicalized to 1,000 and a 400-point gap meaning 10:1 odds. Dollars earned sums the prices of completed projects (out of $143,991). And autoflation — the signature metric — treats RLI as a fixed bundle of goods and tracks how much cheaper that bundle becomes when each project is done by whoever is cheapest, human or AI:

autoflation = 1 − Σp min( cost(Hp), minj cost(AIj,p) )Σp cost(Hp) // cost(H) = price of the human deliverable; cost(AI) = ∞ when the agent fails the project — so a failed agent never lowers the bundle's price

Evaluation is fully human — and holistic

Judging these deliverables is itself a demanding, agentic task — rendering dozens of file formats, doing multimodal analysis — so the authors argue LLM-as-judge is not yet feasible and grade everything manually with trained workers and subject experts. They tried granular per-project rubrics first and found them inadequate: a deliverable could tick rubric boxes yet plainly fail professional standards (especially in design). Instead, evaluators take a holistic stance — "would a reasonable client accept this as the commissioned work?" — on a 3-point scale (worse than human / as good and acceptable / exceeds the human), with a written justification. Each judgment is a majority vote of three independent evaluations, and inter-annotator agreement on the automation rate reaches 94.4%.

// what they found

The results

The headline is blunt: current AI agents perform near the floor. Across six frontier agents — ChatGPT agent, GPT-5, Claude Sonnet 4.5, Grok 4, Gemini 2.5 Pro, and Manus — the highest automation rate is 2.5% (Manus), with the rest clustered between 0.8% and 2.1%. Every agent fails the vast majority of projects at a level that would be accepted as commissioned work. The economic metrics track this floor: Manus earns just $1,720 of the $143,991 on offer, and Gemini 2.5 Pro only $210.

Absolute scores being floored doesn't mean nothing is moving. The Elo metric — relative quality across all projects, including unsolved ones — shows steady, measurable progress, with rankings that roughly track model recency. But the gap to humans is vast: the best agent's Elo of 509.9 sits nearly 500 points below the human baseline of 1,000, far past the 400-point, 10:1-odds threshold.

Why agents fail — and where they win

Clustering the evaluators' written justifications across roughly 400 evaluations, the failures are mostly mundane execution, not deep reasoning gaps. By category (not mutually exclusive): poor quality 45.6%, incomplete or malformed deliverables 35.7%, corrupted or unusable files 17.6%, internal inconsistencies 14.8%. Representative misses: videos 8 seconds long instead of 8 minutes, child-like drawings of basic geometric shapes, a house whose appearance changes across 3D views, robotic voice-overs, and floor plans that don't match the supplied sketches. A recurring theme is that agents can't verify and fix their own work when it requires interactive audiovisual judgment.

The wins concentrate where today's models are already strong: audio editing/mixing/production, image generation (ad and logo creation), report writing, and code for interactive data visualization — for example, Sonnet 4.5 successfully built a self-hosted World Happiness Report dashboard. Tellingly, for GPT-5 the command-line (CLI) scaffold beat the computer-use (CUA) scaffold on both automation rate (1.7% vs 0.8%) and Elo (436.7 vs 431.6), suggesting current models can't yet fully exploit computer-use environments.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

RLI's central claim — that frontier agents automate essentially none of real remote work — is well-supported and important, and the contamination hygiene is unusually serious for an economics-flavored benchmark. But three design choices shape how far the "2.5%" number should travel.

First, this is as much a scaffold-maturity result as a model-capability one. The GPT-5 CLI-vs-CUA gap (1.7% vs 0.8% on the same model) shows that how an agent is wired into the environment swings the score by as much as the differences between models. The headline therefore measures "current agent products," not "current models" — a distinction that matters when one of the six entrants (Manus) is a closed commercial agent whose deliverables were largely generated back in June 2025, while others were run months later. Comparing a June agent to October models on a single leaderboard quietly mixes time periods.

Second, the automation rate is a harsh binary: a deliverable counts only if it's at least as good as the human's, so a project that's 90% there scores identically to one that's empty. That's a defensible bar for "could this replace the freelancer," but it collapses a lot of partial economic value, and it's why the floored 2.5% coexists with visibly rising Elo. Elo is the partial-credit fix, yet it's purely relative among AIs anchored to a fixed human=1,000, and its ternary inter-annotator agreement is only 56.9% (versus 94.4% for the binary judgment) — so the "steady progress" signal is noisier than the floor it sits beneath.

Third, scope. By construction RLI excludes client-interaction work, teamwork, back-end development, and anything that needs waiting — the authors are explicit that a 100% RLI score would not mean full automation of remote labor. And because project prices are recorded at completion time (some up to five years old) and not inflation-adjusted, the $143,991 figure understates current value. Finally, the evaluation is entirely human and holistic: faithful to how real clients judge work, but expensive, hard to reproduce from 10 public projects, and — by the authors' own prediction — likely to see agreement fall as AI deliverables get good enough that "acceptable or not" becomes a genuinely close call.

baselines

Strong and current — GPT-5, Sonnet 4.5, Grok 4, Gemini 2.5 Pro, ChatGPT agent, Manus — but results conflate model and scaffold, and Manus's deliverables predate the others by months.

contamination

Handled well: 230 private projects, only 10 public, no searchable briefs, a domain blocklist for long-tail items. The trade-off is thin external reproducibility.

what's missed

A binary "≥ human" bar hides partial value; RLI excludes client-facing, team, back-end, and waiting work; prices aren't inflation-adjusted; holistic human grading won't scale and may get noisier as models improve.

would raise confidence

Re-run all agents in one time window; separate model vs scaffold contributions; a larger public set; a validated automated grader checked against the human holistic calls; per-category automation tracked over time.

// bottom line

RLI is the most economically grounded automation benchmark yet, and its verdict is hard to argue with: end-to-end automation of real remote work is near zero today, even as knowledge benchmarks saturate. Read the 2.5% as a floor on a moving target — the autoflation and Elo lenses exist precisely because the headline number is designed to start near zero and be watched over time, not to be the whole story.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. The number that matters: 2.5%. The best of six frontier agents (Manus) completes just 2.5% of real freelance projects to a client-acceptable standard, earning $1,720 of a $143,991 bundle. Economic automation of end-to-end remote work is essentially near zero today.
  2. Grounding in real transactions makes RLI hard and diverse. Briefs, gold-standard human deliverables, and recorded prices and hours make its projects over 2× longer than HCAST or GDPval, and ~80% is non-software, non-writing work the field rarely measures.
  3. It's a tracking instrument, not a pass/fail snapshot. Elo (human = 1,000; best AI ≈ 510) registers steady relative progress while absolute automation stays floored, and "autoflation" reframes the bundle as a price index that can deflate as agents improve.
  4. Today's failures are execution, not genius. Rejections cluster on poor quality (45.6%), incomplete work (35.7%), and corrupted files (17.6%) — agents struggle to verify and finish, and can't yet exploit computer-use scaffolds. The wins are audio, image generation, writing, and data-viz code.
