The Leaderboard Illusion

An audit of 2 million Chatbot Arena battles across 243 models and 42 providers finds an undisclosed policy of private testing and selective disclosure, wildly unequal data access, and silent model deprecation — all of which systematically favor a handful of proprietary providers over open-weight and open-source ones.

Shivalika Singh, Yiyang Nan, Alex Wang, Daniel D'souza, Sayash Kapoor, Ahmet Üstün, Sanmi Koyejo, Yuntian Deng, Shayne Longpre, Noah A. Smith, Beyza Ermis, Marzieh Fadaee, Sara Hooker

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

Chatbot Arena, now run under the LMArena banner, became the de facto proving ground for ranking large language models by letting anyone submit a prompt, watch two anonymized models answer it, and vote for the better response. Unlike a fixed multiple-choice benchmark that a well-resourced lab can memorize, contaminate, or quietly overfit to, Arena's test set is supposed to renew itself continuously through crowdsourced traffic — a genuinely dynamic evaluation that should be much harder to game than a static leaderboard. That property is a large part of why Arena earned outsized influence over model releases, media coverage of "who's winning" in AI, and even academic citation practice.

This paper doesn't ask whether Arena's questions are contaminated. It asks something the field had mostly taken on faith: is the machinery that turns millions of anonymous votes into a single ranked list actually fair? The authors — several from Cohere Labs, who had themselves submitted the Command and Aya model families to Arena and noticed their own models appeared to be shown to users far less often than comparably-ranked proprietary competitors — set out to audit Arena's operational policies directly, not just its prompt distribution. Over roughly sixteen months (January 2024–April 2025) they assembled five overlapping datasets spanning 2 million battles, 243 models, and 42 providers, combining Arena's own public releases with a proprietary battle feed Arena shares with select providers, a purpose-built crawl designed to catch behavior Arena doesn't publish, and reconstructed leaderboard history pulled from Arena's Hugging Face commit log.

What they find is a set of policies — most of them undisclosed — that structurally favor whichever handful of providers have the resources and access to exploit them: the ability to privately test many model variants before choosing which one to make public, disproportionate access to the free, community-generated prompt-and-preference data that Arena exists to collect, and a deprecation process that quietly retires far more models than its own public documentation admits to. Any one of these would be a footnote. Together, the paper argues, they mean Chatbot Arena's rankings increasingly measure a provider's access to and engagement with the Arena mechanism itself, rather than pure model capability — a distortion that's easy to miss precisely because Arena's dynamism made everyone assume the opposite was true.

// how it works

The method

Chatbot Arena ranks models with the Bradley-Terry (BT) model, the same paired-comparison framework long used to rate chess players and sports teams. Each model i gets a latent strength parameter βi, and the probability that model i is judged better than model j in any given battle is 1/(1 + e(βj−βi)). BT is a principled way to turn incomplete, noisy pairwise votes into a global ranking, but the guarantee only holds under three assumptions: unbiased sampling (the battles a model plays are representative, not cherry-picked after the fact), transitivity (rankings compose correctly across models that never played each other, provided the task distribution stays consistent), and a fully connected comparison graph (every model links, directly or through a chain of shared opponents, to every other model). The paper's four result sections each interrogate one of these levers from a different angle.

To interrogate that question at scale, the authors built five datasets (their Table 1): a 1.8-million-battle historical-battles set spanning April 2023–January 2025 that merges Arena's public releases with the proprietary battle feed Arena shares with providers like Cohere; 197,217 single-turn prompts captured from Cohere's own production API traffic between November 2024 and April 2025 (62% routed to Aya models, 38% to Command models); a crawled random-sample-battles set of 5,864 battles from January–March 2025 built specifically to surface private testing, since Arena strips private battles before releasing any other dataset; and 14,300 leaderboard snapshots reconstructed from Arena's public Hugging Face commit history, covering 243 uniquely identified models across 42 providers.

The central mechanical claim — the one the rest of the paper's argument depends on — is that "private testing plus the option to retract" constitutes a best-of-N selection procedure, and best-of-N selection violates BT's unbiased-sampling assumption by construction. If a provider privately tests N variants whose true strengths are noisy draws around some base skill level and then publishes only the best-scoring one, the reported score is a maximum order statistic, not a single unbiased sample — and the expected value of a maximum is provably larger than the expected value of any individual draw whenever there is any variance in the underlying estimates:

E[β̂Best] > E[β̂k]  for every k ∈ {1,…,N} // whenever N ≥ 2 and Var(β̂k) > 0 — proved via order statistics in Appendix C, and simulated in Figure 7: starting from a true latent score of 1200, the expected maximum discovered score climbs well above 1200 as more private variants are tested.

Crucially, the authors don't stop at simulation. Several of the paper's own Cohere-affiliated authors ran the experiment for real on the live leaderboard: they submitted two byte-identical checkpoints of Aya-Vision-8B to Arena without disclosing the duplication, and separately submitted two genuinely different Aya-Vision-32B checkpoints that had each looked like the strongest "final candidate" model on different internal evaluations. That turns an abstract concern about selection bias into a concrete field experiment against the actual production ranking system — a level of rigor most benchmark critiques don't attempt.

The paper's other two mechanisms are tested the same way — simulate first, then check the real leaderboard for the signature. For model deprecation, the authors build a synthetic four-model world (A, B, C, D) with distinct, task-specific win probabilities across two evolving prompt categories, run 2,000 simulated battles split across a "Task-1-heavy" phase and a "Task-2-heavy" phase, and compare BT rankings when all four models stay active throughout versus when Model D is deprecated after phase one — a manipulation that, they show, flips the relative order of the models that remain. For comparison-graph connectivity, they invoke a classical result (Ford Jr., 1957): the maximum-likelihood BT estimate doesn't exist at all if the model pool can be split into two groups with no comparisons crossing the split, or where all cross-group comparisons go one way — and they simulate a sparse, near-disconnected comparison graph (built by restricting which model pairs are allowed to battle) alongside a dense one on the same seven synthetic models, showing that four of the seven models' rankings diverge from their true skill order once the graph gets sparse enough.

// what they found

The results

Private testing is real, concentrated, and it works. Auditing the random-sample-battles crawl, the authors identify 27 distinct private Meta variants tested in the run-up to the Llama 4 release in a single month, plus 10 for Google, 7 for Amazon, and single-digit counts for Nvidia, Alibaba, Reka, StepFun, and xAI (Figure 6) — Cohere's 4 private variants in that window belong to the authors' own validation experiment described below, not organic private testing (before this, Cohere had never tested privately on Arena). Counting Meta's parallel testing on Arena's vision leaderboard brings its total to 43 private variants. No academic lab shows up in this data at all — the authors read that absence as evidence that only a subset of providers were even aware private testing was an option. The pattern also lines up with anecdotally "suspiciously fast" leaderboard turnover: OpenAI's GPT-4.5 and xAI's Grok-3 both reached the top of the leaderboard within the same day (March 4, 2025), and Google's Gemini (Exp 1114) was overtaken by OpenAI's ChatGPT-4o (20241120) and then reclaimed the top spot with Gemini (Exp 1121) one day apart, on November 20–21, 2024 — a cadence far more consistent with parallel private variants being staged for release than with a single provider training, evaluating, and shipping a new frontier model twice in a week.

The paper backs the mechanism with a live field experiment, not just a simulation. Submitting two byte-identical checkpoints of Aya-Vision-8B — the most conservative possible test, since any score gap can only be attributed to selection noise rather than a real capability difference — produced Arena scores of 1069 and 1052: a gap large enough that 4 other models on the leaderboard sit between the two "variants" of the exact same model (Figure 9, left). Submitting two genuinely different Aya-Vision-32B checkpoints that had each looked like the strongest final candidate on different internal evals widened the effect to 1097 vs. 1059, with 9 distinct models landing between them (Figure 9, right). In a separate check, simply increasing the number of variants tested from 1 to 3 raised the share of Arena's daily prompt volume the authors' own models captured from 5.9% to 19.4% — private testing isn't only a score-selection trick, it's also a data-acquisition tactic.

Data access is not close to equal. Google and OpenAI alone are estimated to have received 19.2% and 20.4% of all prompts and battle outcomes ever recorded on the Arena, respectively; OpenAI, Google, Meta, and Anthropic together account for 62.8% of all Arena data — 68 times the combined share received by Allen AI, Stanford, Princeton, and UC Berkeley put together. Viewed by license class rather than by company (Figure 4), a combined 83 open-weight models are estimated to have received only 29.7% of all Arena data, and 41 fully open-source models a mere 8.9%, while proprietary models alone account for 61.4%. Beyond private-variant counts, two further policies drive this: sampling rate (how often a provider's models are shown to Arena users at all — Google and OpenAI peak at a 34% daily rate versus 3.3% for Reka, roughly a 10x spread) and deprecation (below) — both of which the paper finds are applied far more inconsistently than Arena's own stated policy suggests.

That data access converts into measurable performance gains — on Arena's own terms. To quantify the payoff, the authors fine-tuned a 7B Cohere Command-family base model on three otherwise-identical training mixtures that varied only in what share came from real Arena battles: 0%, 30%, and 70%. Evaluated on ArenaHard (a 500-prompt benchmark the field treats as a 98.6%-correlated proxy for the live Arena distribution), win rate against Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct climbed from 23.5% (0% arena-mix) to 42.7% (30%) to 49.9% (70%) — relative gains of 81.7% and 112.3% over the no-arena-data baseline — even though this training run used only a modest, publicly-derivable slice of Arena data, nowhere near what a provider with full API access could assemble. Tellingly, the same models' performance on the out-of-distribution MMLU benchmark did not improve in step: it slipped from 66.5% to 64.4% at 30% arena-mix before partially recovering to 65.9% at 70% — evidence pointing toward fit to Arena's specific distribution rather than a general capability gain.

Arena's own distribution keeps moving — and repeating itself. A further complication: "the Arena" the field treats as a single fixed target is neither fixed nor fully novel. Tracking prompt language share from April 2023 to January 2025, the authors find English's share dropped from over 80% to nearly 50%, while Russian prompts grew from 1% (April 2023) to 15.7% (December 2024) and Chinese prompts more than doubled to 16.4% by March 2024 before falling back to 6.2% by January 2025 — multilingual prompts overall grew from 23.9% to 43.5% of all traffic over the period. At the same time, a meaningful share of what looks like fresh, crowdsourced traffic isn't: analyzing Cohere's own API-routed prompts, the authors measure that de-duplication removes an average of 20.14% of prompts month-to-month (peaking at 26.5% in March 2025), and even a strict exact-match criterion finds 7.3% of December 2024's prompts reappearing verbatim in January 2025 — a rate that rises to 9% under a relaxed semantic-similarity threshold. A 12,000-character prompt limit and a developer-heavy user base further skew what gets asked at all (a released 33K-sample Arena dataset, the authors note, contains dozens of questions about Star Trek and zero about Chaucer). A provider with continuous, high-volume access to last month's traffic has, in effect, already previewed a meaningful slice of next month's test distribution.

Deprecation quietly erases open models from the comparison graph. Arena's own FastChat backend lists 47 models as officially deprecated, but the authors' audit of leaderboard-stats finds 205 of the 243 tracked models have been silently deprecated — sampling reduced to near-zero with no public notice — and 64% of that silently-dropped group is open-weight or fully open-source. By license class, 87.8% of open-weight models and 89.0% of open-source models are eventually deprecated or made inactive, versus 80.0% of proprietary models.

The paper's simulations show why this matters beyond bookkeeping: BT rankings assume the comparison network stays connected and that the task distribution a model was evaluated under stays comparable across models. When a model is deprecated partway through a shift in what kinds of prompts Arena users are asking, its historical battles no longer represent current conditions — and in the paper's four-model toy simulation, deprecating just one model mid-stream is enough to flip the relative rank order of the models that remain active.

The paper closes its results with five reforms it says it shared directly with Chatbot Arena before publication: prohibit retracting any tested score (including private variants) after submission; cap the number of concurrently-tested private variants per provider (they suggest 3) and disclose the cap publicly; retire models by a transparent, license-blind rule — e.g., the bottom 30th percentile within each of the proprietary/open-weight/open-source groups — rather than the current ambiguous "may be retired after 3000 votes" policy; actually adopt the variance-reducing active-sampling rule Chatbot Arena's own creators proposed in 2024 but, per this audit, never deployed in practice; and publish a running, comprehensive list of deprecated and inactive models rather than only the 47 in FastChat's public list.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

This is a paper that spends thirty pages auditing someone else's measurement instrument, which means its own methodology deserves exactly the scrutiny it applies to Chatbot Arena. Two threads are worth pulling on.

The first is how much of the paper's causal language is actually earned. The abstract and conclusion use words like "gamification," "willful engagement," and "a new low for the AI research field" — strong claims about intent. The evidence that directly supports a causal mechanism is narrower and, to the authors' credit, genuinely well designed: the controlled fine-tuning experiment (0%/30%/70% arena-mix) is a real intervention with a held-everything-else-fixed comparison, and the Aya-Vision duplicate-checkpoint submission is a live field experiment against the actual production leaderboard rather than a synthetic simulation — both are unusually rigorous for a benchmark critique. But the leap from "Meta tested 27 private variants before Llama 4" to "a handful of top-tier industry labs willfully gamed the leaderboard" is an inference from opportunity, incentive, and a suspicious pattern (same-week leaderboard swaps), not a demonstration that any specific submission was selected to deceive. The paper itself acknowledges, in its own recommendations, that "there are valid reasons to offer private testing" — internal regression checks, safety review, staged rollouts — which sits uneasily next to the framing elsewhere. The rhetoric outruns what the evidence, on its own, proves about intent versus mere opportunity.

The second is a subtler tension inside the overfitting argument. The paper spends several pages establishing that Arena's own prompt distribution is non-stationary — language mix shifts substantially quarter to quarter, and 20–33% of monthly prompts are exact or near-duplicates of prior months. That's the paper's own evidence that "the Arena" is a moving, partially-repeating target. But the overfitting experiment that quantifies the payoff from arena-data access measures generalization against ArenaHard — a fixed, 500-prompt benchmark snapshot whose 98.6% correlation with Arena was itself measured at a single point in time. If the live distribution drifts the way the paper's own duplication and language-shift analysis shows, a static proxy benchmark from one snapshot is exactly the kind of target a model can overfit to without that overfitting reflecting durable advantage on the actual, moving Arena. The paper doesn't reconcile this: its two headline findings — the Arena is a moving target, and access to Arena data yields large, durable-looking gains — are measured against two different notions of what "the Arena" even is.

There's also a small but real internal inconsistency worth flagging for anyone tempted to cite the more dramatic number: the results-summary box for Section 3 states that "testing just 10 variants yields a notable increase of approximately 100 points in the maximum score identified," while the body text describing that same simulation (Figure 7) says "testing just 20 variants yields a notable increase of approximately 50 points" — and the chart itself shows roughly a 30-point gain at N=10 and a 50-point gain at N=20, matching the body text, not the summary box. It's a minor slip in an otherwise carefully quantified paper, but worth knowing before repeating the "100 points" figure.

On method transparency: attributing private models to a specific provider relies on asking each anonymous model to self-identify, which the authors flag as approximate; and several of the underlying datasets (the Cohere API prompt sample, the scraped random-sample) are proprietary and can't be released for independent replication — a mild irony given the paper's own central demand is more transparency from Arena. Both limitations are disclosed rather than hidden, which is to the paper's credit, but they mean the striking headline percentages (19.2%, 20.4%, 62.8%) rest on a data pipeline that readers can't independently check.

baselines

The controlled fine-tuning experiment (fixed budget, only the arena-mix ratio varied) is a fair, deliberately conservative causal test; the live duplicate-checkpoint submission is rarer still — a field experiment against the real production leaderboard, not a synthetic simulation.

contamination

Not classic train/test leakage, but a structural analogue: 20–33% of monthly Arena prompts are near-duplicates of the prior month's, so recent high-volume API access functions like a contamination channel — seeing last month's traffic previews a chunk of next month's test set.

what's missed

No estimate of how much the current, real leaderboard order would actually change under the paper's five proposed reforms — every distortion is quantified via simulation or a narrow controlled experiment, never as a counterfactual re-ranking of today's top models.

would raise confidence

Independent audit of Arena's raw, pre-deduplication logs; a public before/after re-ranking once Arena adopts any of the five reforms; and replication of the arena-mix overfitting result at a data scale closer to what a proprietary lab with full API access could actually assemble.

// bottom line

The mechanism the paper documents — best-of-N private testing breaks an explicit statistical assumption of the ranking model Arena uses, and the authors show it both in theory and on the live leaderboard — is convincing on its own terms. The stronger claim, that this amounts to willful, coordinated gaming by a handful of labs, is argued more by pattern and incentive than demonstrated directly, and a couple of the analyses (the ArenaHard-vs-non-stationary-Arena tension, the "10 vs. 20 variants" inconsistency) reward a second read before you cite the headline numbers.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. Chatbot Arena's rankings depend on an undisclosed policy: providers can privately test many model variants and publish only the best-scoring one. Meta tested at least 27 private variants (43 counting its vision leaderboard) before Llama 4.
  2. Even zero-skill-difference gaming works: submitting two identical Aya-Vision-8B checkpoints produced Arena scores of 1069 vs. 1052 — a gap large enough to place 4 other models between them.
  3. Data access is starkly unequal: Google and OpenAI alone received an estimated 19.2% and 20.4% of all Arena data; a combined 83 open-weight models got 29.7%, and 41 fully open-source models got just 8.9%.
  4. That access converts into real, if narrow, performance gains — ArenaHard win rate rose from 23.5% to 49.9% as arena-mix training data went from 0% to 70% — but out-of-distribution MMLU performance didn't move the same way, hinting at overfitting rather than general improvement.
  5. 205 of 243 tracked models have been silently deprecated (versus 47 officially), disproportionately open-weight and open-source (64% of the silently-dropped group) — and the paper's simulations show this alone can flip relative rankings once the underlying task distribution shifts.
