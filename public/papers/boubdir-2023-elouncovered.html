Elo Uncovered: Robustness and Best Practices in Language Model Evaluation

Chess ratings were built for players whose skill drifts over a season and who play a bounded schedule. LLMs are static and get compared in whatever order a leaderboard happens to run its votes. This paper asks whether the Elo system survives that mismatch — and finds that a ranking can flip from nothing but match order and hyperparameter choice.

Meriem Boubdir, Edward Kim, Beyza Ermis, Sara Hooker, Marzieh Fadaee · Cohere for AI / Cohere

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

By late 2023, the Elo rating system — designed by Arpad Elo in 1978 to rank chess players from win/loss outcomes — had quietly become the default scoring layer for human-preference LLM evaluation: "A vs. B" paired comparisons feed straight into an Elo update, the same mechanism Chatbot Arena itself was running on at the time (it would later switch to Bradley-Terry coefficients). The paper's core observation is that this borrowed machinery carries assumptions from its original domain that don't obviously transfer.

Chess Elo assumes competitors with evolving skill inside a roughly sequential, bounded tournament schedule. LLMs are the opposite: a model's weights are frozen at evaluation time (no skill drift to track), yet comparisons between models can happen in any order and in parallel — nothing about the true, static "which model is better" fact should depend on which pairwise vote a system happened to log first. If the Elo update is sensitive to that arbitrary ordering, the resulting ranking is measuring something other than the models' actual relative quality.

The authors formalize this as a test of two axioms any sound comparative-evaluation method should satisfy: reliability (a ranking shouldn't depend on the order matches were logged, or swing wildly with hyperparameter choices) and transitivity (if A beats B and B beats C, A should rank above C). Neither axiom had been rigorously checked for Elo applied to LLMs before this paper — despite the system already underpinning real leaderboards.

// how it works

The method

The Elo mechanics themselves are simple: given ratings RA, RB for two models, the expected score is a logistic function of the rating gap, and after each match the rating updates by a K-factor-scaled surprise term.

EA = 11 + 10(RB−RA)/400      R′A = RA + K(SA − EA) // S_A ∈ {0,1} is the observed match outcome; K controls how fast ratings move per match — chess uses K=16–32, but there's no established value for LLM comparisons

To stress-test this update rule without the expense of collecting fresh human feedback for every scenario, the authors first build a synthetic testbed: model A and B are given a fixed win probability P(A beats B) ∈ {0.51, 0.55, 0.6, 0.65, 0.7, 0.8, 0.9}, and Ngames = 1000 match outcomes are drawn as i.i.d. Bernoulli trials. Both ratings start at 1400. Critically, they don't just run one sequence — they take the same 1000 outcomes and reshuffle them into Nperms independent permutations (from 1 up to 10,000), replaying the identical set of match results in a different order each time. If Elo were truly order-invariant, the final ratings shouldn't depend on which permutation was played.

They then vary the K-factor (1, 8, 16, 32, 64) across the same win-probability grid, and separately test transitivity with four constructed scenarios spanning "A dominates B, B dominates C" down to "A, B, and C are all near-tied" — checking whether the Elo-implied hierarchy A > B > C actually falls out of the ratings. Finally, to validate that the synthetic findings aren't an artifact of simulation, they repeat the same order/hyperparameter sweeps on real human preference data: 400 prompts (100 each from SODA, CommonsenseQA, CommonGen, and AdversarialQA, all part of the P3 corpus) comparing Flan-T5 and Dolly model pairs, reused from the authors' prior data-prioritization work.

// what they found

The results

Ordering matters most exactly when it matters most — near-tied models. At P(A beats B) ≥ 0.6, a single game sequence (Nperms=1) already gives fairly stable, correctly-ordered ratings. But at P(A beats B) ≈ 0.51 — models of genuinely similar quality, the common real-world case — a single sequence leaves the two models' Elo traces "frequently intertwined," with no reliable separation. Averaging over Nperms ≥ 100 replays of the identical outcome set is needed before the ranking stabilizes at all, and the paper shows this stabilization plateaus once Nperms exceeds ~100.

The K-factor amplifies or erases that instability. For a single sequence at P(A beats B)=0.51, the Elo-score gap SA−SB swings from −3 at K=1 to −112 at K=64 — i.e. at a high K-factor, the model constructed to be worse (B) ends up rated 112 points above the model constructed to be better (A), from hyperparameter choice alone, holding the actual outcomes fixed. Averaging over 100 permutations shrinks but does not eliminate the swing: at P=0.51 the gap still ranges from +14 (K=1) to +24 (K=64), a 71% relative change from the K-factor alone.

Transitivity is not guaranteed, especially between similarly-matched models. Table 1 tests four scenarios; the R scenario (A≫B, B≈C) and B scenario (A≈B, B≫C) both produce hierarchy violations at Nperms=100, K=1 — flagged with asterisks in the paper's own table. In scenario R, model C ends up with a higher averaged Elo (1361.38 ± 0.38) than model B (1342.70 ± 0.53), even though the construction intends B and C to be roughly tied and both below A. In scenario B, model B (1453.84 ± 0.61) outranks model A (1433.84 ± 0.41), inverting the intended near-tie. Raising K to 16 restores the expected ordering in both cases — showing the violation is a K-factor artifact, not just noise (the gaps are several standard errors wide).

The same instability shows up on real human-annotated LLM comparisons, not just simulation. Flan-t5-xxl vs. Flan-t5-xl — a decisive matchup (recorded win rate 0.64 vs. 0.36) — ranks Flan-t5-xxl above Flan-t5-xl consistently across every K-factor and Nperms combination tested. But Dolly-v2-7b vs. Dolly-v2-12b — a near-coin-flip matchup (0.51 vs. 0.49) — actually flips which model wins depending on the hyperparameter cell: Dolly-v2-12b wins at K=32 (both Nperms settings) and also at K=8 with Nperms=1; Dolly-v2-7b wins at K=1 and K=16 regardless of Nperms, but also takes over at K=8 once Nperms rises to 100 — the same K-factor cell changing winner purely from replaying more permutations of the identical annotations.

From these results the authors distill concrete guidelines: run Nperms ≥ 100 wherever possible; prefer a smaller K-factor when win rates are close to 50/50 to damp spurious swings; use a larger K-factor only when one model clearly dominates, to speed convergence; and never assume transitivity holds automatically, particularly among closely-matched models.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

This is a genuinely early, correctly-aimed warning shot — it flags exactly the fragility that Chatbot Arena's own follow-up work later addressed by switching from raw Elo to Bradley-Terry coefficients with confidence intervals. But the paper's own evidentiary base is narrower than its abstract's framing ("current comparative evaluations of LLMs") suggests, in a few specific ways worth naming.

The real-world validation is two model pairs, both already dated by the time of publication. Every non-synthetic result rests on Flan-T5 (2022) and Dolly-v2 (2023) — instruction-tuned models several capability generations behind what leaderboards like Chatbot Arena were already ranking by late 2023 (GPT-4, Claude 2, and dozens of open chat models). The paper doesn't test whether the same ordering/K-factor sensitivity reproduces on a genuinely close, frontier-model pairing under real crowd voting — only on two Flan/Dolly comparisons plus one cross-family pair in the appendix, extrapolated to a much broader claim about "current" LLM leaderboards.

Ties are excluded from every experiment, real and synthetic alike. The paper states plainly that "tie outcomes have been excluded from this analysis," reducing every comparison to a clean binary. Real pairwise LLM evaluation platforms (Chatbot Arena included) route a substantial share of votes to "tie" or "both bad" — outcomes that change the effective sample size and the variance of the update. Whether the reliability and transitivity failures shown here persist, worsen, or shrink once realistic tie rates are folded back in is left as acknowledged future work, but the abstract's framing doesn't carry that caveat forward.

Elo is stress-tested in isolation, not against the alternatives the related-work section names. Section 8 cites Glicko and TrueSkill as existing statistical upgrades to Elo, but the paper never runs its own ordering/K-factor/transitivity battery against either — so a reader can't tell from this paper alone whether those alternatives are actually more robust under the exact same stress tests, or just differently fragile. The paper's own prescription is "tune Elo's hyperparameters and average over permutations," not "here is quantitative evidence you should switch systems."

Every simulation is a cold start from a shared, arbitrary initial rating. All synthetic and real experiments reset both models to 1400 before replaying permutations. Production leaderboards never do this — a new model enters an already-populated, spread-out rating pool. It's untested whether the ordering-sensitivity shown here is partly a transient effect of the shared 1400 starting point rather than a property of Elo updates in a mature, already-differentiated rating pool.

baselines

Real-world validation covers exactly two model pairs (Flan-T5, Dolly-v2), both already dated relative to the frontier LLMs that Elo-based leaderboards were ranking by the paper's own publication date.

contamination

Not applicable in the usual train/test sense — but tie outcomes are excluded throughout, and every simulation restarts from a shared, arbitrary 1400 rating rather than a populated leaderboard's actual starting conditions.

what's missed

No head-to-head test of the alternative rating systems (Glicko, TrueSkill, Bradley-Terry) the related-work section itself names as more sophisticated; no test of whether findings hold once ties are reintroduced or the model pool is large and continuously rotating, as on a live arena.

would raise confidence

Repeat the same ordering/K-factor/transitivity battery on a close, frontier-model pair from a live arena; include ties; and run the identical stress tests against Bradley-Terry and TrueSkill to show whether the fragility is Elo-specific or shared.

// bottom line

The diagnosis — Elo's order- and hyperparameter-sensitivity is worst precisely where LLM comparisons live, among closely-matched models — is real and later vindicated by the field's own move away from raw Elo. But the evidence for it is thin outside the synthetic setting: two dated model pairs, no ties, no cold-start-vs-populated-pool comparison, and no direct test of whether the cited alternatives actually do better. Read this as a well-aimed early warning that correctly anticipated a real problem, not as a comprehensive audit of Elo on the leaderboards it was already powering.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. Elo ratings for LLMs can be unreliable by construction: at a near-50/50 win probability, a single match sequence leaves two models' ratings "frequently intertwined," and the K-factor alone can flip the ranking's sign (a −3 to −112 swing in the score gap across K=1→64 at P=0.51).
  2. Transitivity (A≫B, B≫C ⟹ A≫C) is not automatic — Table 1 shows constructed near-ties inverting at K=1, Nperms=100, confirmed with real Flan/Dolly human-annotation data where the winning model literally changes depending on K-factor.
  3. The fix the authors offer is procedural, not architectural: Nperms ≥ 100 and a smaller K-factor for close matchups — guidance that anticipated, but didn't yet test against, the field's later move to Bradley-Terry-based leaderboards like Chatbot Arena.
