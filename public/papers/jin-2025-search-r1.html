Search-R1: Training LLMs to Reason and Leverage Search Engines with Reinforcement Learning

Extends DeepSeek-R1-style outcome-only RL to search: the LLM's rollout interleaves <search> calls with its own reasoning, retrieved passages are masked out of the policy-gradient loss for stability, and a single exact-match reward is enough to teach the model when and how to search.

Bowen Jin, Hansi Zeng, Zhenrui Yue, Jinsung Yoon, Sercan Ö. Arık, Dong Wang, Hamed Zamani, Jiawei Han · UIUC, UMass Amherst, Google Cloud AI Research

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

By March 2025, two ways to hook an LLM up to a search engine dominated: retrieval-augmented generation (RAG), which retrieves once based on the input query and stuffs the results into context, and search-as-a-tool prompting, where a model like ReAct or IRCoT is prompted to interleave reasoning and search calls, or Toolformer is fine-tuned to do so on labeled trajectories. Neither is optimized end-to-end for the interaction itself: prompting-based multi-turn retrieval (IRCoT) is not trained to search well, and tool-use fine-tuning needs large-scale, high-quality annotated trajectories that are expensive to collect and hard to scale.

Meanwhile, reinforcement learning with pure outcome rewards — no supervision on the reasoning trace itself — had just been shown to elicit strong reasoning behavior in DeepSeek-R1 and OpenAI-o1. But nobody had shown that the same recipe works when the "environment" includes a non-differentiable external tool. The paper frames three open questions: can RL training stay stable once retrieved context (tokens the model didn't generate) enters the rollout; can a model learn multi-turn, interleaved reasoning-and-search rather than a single retrieve-then-answer step; and is a simple outcome-based reward — with no process supervision, no reward model — enough to teach useful search behavior at all.

// how it works

The method

SEARCH-R1 treats the search engine R as part of the RL environment rather than a pre- or post-processing step. The policy is optimized to maximize E[r(x,y)] − β·D_KL[π_θ(y|x;R) || π_ref(y|x;R)], where a sampled output y is generated by the model interleaved with live retrieval calls — formally π_θ(·|x) ⋈ R, "generation interleaved with retrieval," rather than a single forward pass conditioned on pre-fetched context.

Mechanically, the rollout is a loop over four special tokens: the model reasons inside <think></think>, and whenever it needs external information it emits a query inside <search></search>. The system pauses generation, executes the query against the retriever, and splices the results back in as <information></information> — after which the model keeps reasoning, can search again, and eventually commits inside <answer></answer>. A rollout terminates on a final answer or after a fixed action budget B. No content-specific bias is imposed beyond this structural format — the model isn't told when to search or how many times.

Retrieved-token loss masking

A rollout mixes model-generated tokens with retrieved tokens the model never produced. Optimizing both with the same policy-gradient objective distorts training, so SEARCH-R1 defines an indicator I(y_t) that is 1 for LLM-generated tokens and 0 for retrieved tokens, and restricts both the PPO/GRPO objective and the KL term to I(y_t)=1 positions. Retrieved text still conditions generation — it's just excluded from the gradient and from the loss's normalization.

SEARCH-R1 is compatible with both PPO (which needs a learned value function via GAE) and GRPO (which instead samples a group of G rollouts per question and uses their mean reward as the baseline, avoiding a critic entirely). The reward itself is deliberately minimal: outcome-only exact match (EM) between the predicted and gold answer, with no format reward — the authors report the model already develops strong structural adherence without one — and no learned reward model at all.

// what they found

The results

Trained on a merged NQ + HotpotQA set and evaluated across seven QA benchmarks (general: NQ, TriviaQA, PopQA; multi-hop: HotpotQA, 2WikiMultiHopQA, Musique, Bamboogle) with Qwen2.5-3B/7B Base and Instruct, SEARCH-R1 delivers 24% average relative improvement on the 7B model and 20% on the 3B model over RAG baselines, under a deliberately matched comparison — same retriever (E5 over a 2018 Wikipedia dump), same number of retrieved passages, same training data, and the same pretrained backbone across every baseline.

SEARCH-R1 beats R1 — the same RL recipe trained without search access — on every dataset, confirming that the gains come from learning to search, not just from RL-style outcome training in general. Gains hold on both in-domain sets (NQ, HotpotQA) and out-of-domain sets (TriviaQA, PopQA, 2Wiki, Musique, Bamboogle) it was never trained on, and the 7B model shows a noticeably larger gap over its best non-RL competitor than the 3B model does — the paper's read is that larger models are better at learning how to search, not just at answering.

Two ablations stand out. Retrieved-token masking is not a nicety: on Qwen2.5-7B-base with PPO, removing it drops the seven-dataset average from 0.431 to 0.343 — a bigger swing than several of the architectural choices the paper studies. And more retrieved passages isn't strictly better: sweeping top-k ∈ {1, 3, 5} peaks at k=3 (avg 0.431), with k=1 worse (0.375) and k=5 slightly worse again (0.400) than k=3 — evidence that irrelevant passages can dilute the reasoning context rather than help it.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

The controlled comparison is a genuine strength here — most search-RL papers don't hold the retriever, corpus, and training data fixed across every baseline the way this one does. But the choices in reward design and evaluation setup narrow what the headline numbers actually establish.

An unusually fair baseline comparison, with one narrow corpus. RAG, IRCoT, SFT, Search-o1, R1 (no search), and rejection sampling are all run with the identical retriever (E5), the identical 2018 Wikipedia dump, and the identical Qwen2.5 checkpoints — a level of control that isolates SEARCH-R1's own contribution rather than confounding it with a better retriever or bigger corpus. The flip side is that everything happens inside one static, closed Wikipedia snapshot; there is no test against live or online search, the exact generalization check that R1-Searcher — posted five days earlier on arXiv, working an adjacent multi-hop QA RL-search setup — runs explicitly (Google Search API on Bamboogle) to show its policy transfers beyond the training corpus.

Exact-match is a strict, and arguably mismatched, reward for open-ended QA. EM gives zero reward to a correct answer phrased differently from the gold string — a known harshness for multi-hop questions with several valid surface forms. R1-Searcher's own reward-design ablation (on an adjacent multi-hop QA setup) finds that F1-based rewards outperform EM/CEM-based rewards by up to 52.6%, and that EM specifically produces the shortest, weakest-performing responses of the rewards it tests — a direct, cross-paper case that this paper's binary EM choice likely leaves some real performance on the table, not just precision on the metric.

No contamination check on well-worn benchmarks. NQ, TriviaQA, HotpotQA, 2WikiMultiHopQA, and PopQA are all standard, extensively-republished QA sets that plausibly overlap with Qwen2.5's pretraining corpus. Nothing here screens whether the model can already answer a training example from parametric memory alone before crediting the gain to search — the kind of pass@k contamination filter that DeepResearcher (also GRPO-on-search-agents, trained a few weeks later) explicitly adds as a fix to this exact gap in the line of work.

baselines

Unusually well-matched (same retriever, corpus, and training data across every method) — a real methodological strength. But every comparison happens inside one closed Wikipedia snapshot; no online-search generalization test.

contamination

Not addressed. NQ/TriviaQA/HotpotQA/2Wiki/PopQA are heavily-seen benchmarks; no pass@k or memorization screen distinguishes "learned to search" from "already knew the answer."

what's missed

Binary EM reward likely undercounts correct-but-reworded answers (see R1-Searcher's own F1-vs-EM ablation); GRPO's late-training reward collapse (Fig. 5/Table 6, appendix) is reported but not root-caused; only Qwen2.5 backbones are tested, no cross-family check.

would raise confidence

An online-search or held-out-corpus generalization test; an F1 or LLM-judge reward variant alongside EM; an explicit contamination/pass@k filter on the training and eval sets; a second model family to confirm the recipe isn't Qwen-specific.

// bottom line

A carefully controlled ablation of what interleaved search-and-reason RL actually needs (retrieved-token masking, PPO vs. GRPO, top-k, base vs. instruct) — the baseline discipline is genuinely better than most papers in this cluster. Read the 24%/20% headline as gains within a fixed Wikipedia sandbox under a strict EM reward, not yet as evidence the recipe generalizes to open-web search or survives a friendlier reward.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. Search becomes part of the RL rollout, not a pre-processing step. Four special tokens — <think>, <search>, <information>, <answer> — let the model interleave reasoning with live, multi-turn retrieval calls under a single RL objective.
  2. Retrieved-token loss masking is the load-bearing trick. Excluding retrieved tokens from the policy-gradient and KL terms takes the 7B average from 0.343 to 0.431 — a bigger effect than most of the paper's other ablations combined.
  3. Works with either PPO or GRPO, with a real trade-off. GRPO converges faster but can collapse late in training; PPO is slower to start but stable; final performance is comparable either way.
  4. The comparison is unusually fair, but narrowly scoped. Every baseline shares the same retriever, corpus, and training data — a real strength — but everything runs inside a static 2018 Wikipedia snapshot under a strict exact-match reward, with no contamination screen and no online-search test.
