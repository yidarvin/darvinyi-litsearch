Why Are There Still So Many Jobs? The History and Future of Workplace Automation

Two centuries of automation panic have shared one flawed premise — that the amount of work is fixed, so any task a machine takes over is a job destroyed. Autor's task-based framework and Polanyi's paradox explain why automation instead raises the value of the tasks people alone can still do.

David H. Autor

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

Automation anxiety is not new — Autor opens by tracing it back two centuries, from Luddite textile workers smashing machines in the 1810s, through TIME's 1961 cover story "The Automation Jobless" warning that "automation may prevent the economy from creating enough new jobs," to Lyndon Johnson's 1964 National Commission on Technology, Automation, and Economic Progress, empaneled specifically because productivity was rising so fast it might outstrip the economy's demand for labor. Every one of these panics predicted mass technological unemployment; none of them arrived. The US employment-to-population ratio rose across the 20th century even as automation eliminated entire categories of work outright — agricultural employment alone fell from 41% of the US workforce in 1900 to just 2% by 2000. Yet by 2015 the anxiety was back with serious intellectual weight behind it: Erik Brynjolfsson and Andrew McAfee's The Second Machine Age argued that "computers, robots, and other digital technologies are acquiring [ordinary workers'] skills and abilities at an extraordinary rate."

The gap this essay targets is conceptual, not just empirical: the debate had been conducted almost entirely in terms of substitution — machines directly displacing workers from tasks — reasoned about as if the total quantity of work available were fixed, so that every task a machine claims is a job permanently subtracted from human employment (the "lump of labor" intuition). That framing has no room for the pattern automation has actually produced across two centuries: it eliminates large numbers of jobs task-by-task while repeatedly failing to shrink the total number of jobs in the economy. Autor sets out to supply the missing mechanism — not "automation is weaker than it looks," but a specific account of why substitution and net job growth have coexisted, and how far that coexistence can be expected to continue.

// how it works

The task-based framework

The mechanism Autor leans on hardest is complementarity: automation doesn't just substitute for labor, it also raises the value of the tasks that remain for people to do. His preferred illustration is Kremer's (1993) O-ring production function — named for the Challenger disaster's failed rubber seal — in which a work process is a chain of tasks where any single failure sinks the whole output. In that structure, making one link in the chain more reliable (say, by automating it) mechanically raises the economic value of every other link that still depends on human judgment, because that remaining link is now closer to being the binding constraint on total output.

output ∝ q1 × q2 × … × qn // Kremer's (1993) O-ring logic: task reliabilities qᵢ multiply, so automating (raising) any one qᵢ raises the marginal value of improving every other qᵢ still performed by a human

He grounds this in James Bessen's (2015) account of ATMs and bank tellers: US ATM counts roughly quadrupled from 100,000 to 400,000 between 1995 and 2010, and the number of tellers per branch fell by more than a third between 1988 and 2004 — real, large substitution. Yet total US bank-teller employment nonetheless rose modestly, from about 500,000 to roughly 550,000 over 1980–2010, because cheaper branch operations let banks open more than 40% more urban branches, and the surviving teller role shifted from cash-handling into "relationship banking" — a bundle of interpersonal tasks ATMs cannot perform.

Whether that complementarity actually shows up as more jobs and higher wages in a given occupation depends on three forces Autor is explicit are not automatic. First, workers benefit only if they supply tasks that are complemented rather than purely substituted — a construction worker who cannot drive an excavator loses out even as construction overall gets more productive. Second, the elasticity of labor supply into the complemented tasks can dissipate the wage gains entirely: Hsieh and Moretti (2003) show free entry into real-estate brokerage fully offset the wage gains from rising house prices. Third, the demand elasticity for a sector's output — combined with its income elasticity — decides whether productivity growth raises spending on that output or shrinks it; agriculture (spectacular productivity gains, income-inelastic demand) sheds employment share for the mirror-image reason health care (also productivity-improving, but income-elastic) has not, an instance of Baumol's (1967) "cost disease" running in both directions.

The taxonomy underneath all of this is the task framework Autor built with Frank Levy and Richard Murnane in 2003: computers substitute for routine tasks — codifiable, rule-following work, whether cognitive (bookkeeping, filing) or manual (repetitive assembly) — because a programmer can fully specify the procedure in advance. What they cannot substitute for is anything that resists that specification, a constraint Autor names Polanyi's paradox, after the philosopher-chemist Michael Polanyi's 1966 observation that "we know more than we can tell": a person who writes a persuasive paragraph, identifies a bird species from a fleeting glimpse, or cracks an egg cleanly over a bowl cannot fully articulate the rule they're following, so no programmer can transcribe that rule into code either. That residual sorts into two families sitting at opposite ends of the wage distribution — "abstract" tasks (problem-solving, persuasion, creativity: professional, managerial, technical work) and "manual" tasks (situational adaptability, in-person interaction: food service, cleaning, home health) — which is exactly the shape that predicts occupational polarization: growth pulling apart at the top and bottom of the wage scale as the codifiable middle is automated away.

// what they found

Two automation eras, and a warning sign in the second one

The historical record backs the mechanism up — with an increasingly important asterisk. Built from Katz and Margo's (2014) Census/IPUMS/ACS series, the paper's Figure 1 shows the pre-1980 automation wave was almost uniformly favorable to workers: from 1940–1980, agricultural employment fell nearly 4 percentage points per decade while professional, technical, and managerial employment grew about 3 points per decade (2.5 points for professionals/technicians, 0.5 for managers), and the broad middle — service, skilled blue-collar, clerical/sales — held stable or grew. After 1980, the shape changes. Figure 2 plots percentage-point employment changes by decade, 1979–2012, across ten occupational categories: the four middle-skill groups (sales; office/administrative support; production, craft and repair; operators/laborers) held 60% of US employment in 1979, fell to 49% by 2007, and to 46% by 2012 — a genuine hollowing of the middle, with growth concentrating instead in high-wage professional/managerial/technical work and in low-wage personal-care and food/cleaning-service work. A companion figure in the essay shows the same barbell pattern in 16 European economies over 1993–2010: middle-wage occupations lost employment share in every single country, despite enormous differences in labor-market institutions across the sample — evidence, Autor argues, of a common technological force rather than any one country's policy regime.

Employment polarization does not translate cleanly into wage polarization, though. Autor's wage-by-skill-percentile figure shows real wages rising fastest and most consistently at the top of the distribution throughout 1979–2007, while wage growth at the bottom was actually negative below roughly the 15th percentile — and below 5 percentage points up to the 70th percentile — during 1999–2007 specifically, even though the low-wage occupations were simultaneously gaining employment share fastest in that period. He reads the divergence through the same three mitigating forces: manual-task labor supply is elastic (low training requirements), diluting the wage gains from complementarity across a large pool of entrants, while abstract-task labor supply is comparatively inelastic over the short-to-medium run (a college-to-graduate pipeline runs five to ten years), so the same demand-side gains show up more fully as wages rather than being competed away.

The essay's own crack in the good-news story is worth dwelling on, because it complicates the argument rather than confirming it. Plotting smoothed employment-share changes by 1979 skill percentile, the shape is a clean "U" in 1979–1989 and 1989–1999 — losses concentrated in the middle, gains at both tails, exactly what the task model predicts. By 1999–2007 the U has become a one-sided ramp: the top two deciles of the skill distribution show no relative employment growth at all, with only a modest recovery in 2007–2012.

Autor's own read is that this is demand-side, not a sign that automation is finally eating abstract work: corporate information-processing equipment and software investment stood at just 3.5% of GDP in early 2014 — a level last seen in 1995 — which he takes as evidence of a lingering dot-com-bust hangover rather than a surge of high-skill-displacing IT investment. He offers this as his best interpretation, not as something the essay tests against the obvious rival explanation (that skill-biased technical change was simply reaching a new part of the task ladder, which would show the same investment dip).

// does it hold up?

The eval read

This is a synthesis essay, not an empirical paper with an identification strategy to interrogate — so the sharper question is what kind of evidence the argument actually rests on, and whether its historical read survives the technology that arrived after 2015. On the first count, the essay is candid about running more on illustration than on estimation: the ATM/teller story is one case study from Bessen (2015), not a systematic accounting of how many displaced middle-skill tasks were complemented versus how many simply vanished with no offsetting task created. The "three mitigating forces" — task substitutability, labor-supply elasticity, demand elasticity — are a taxonomy for explaining outcomes after the fact, not parameters anyone measures ex ante to predict which occupations will net out as winners. And the essay's own U-shape-to-ramp finding is its most useful self-check, but Autor's explanation for it (a dot-com IT-investment hangover, not technology reaching abstract tasks) is an interpretation of one aggregate investment series, not something tested against the rival account.

On the second, larger count — does the argument survive contact with what came after — the honest answer is: partially, and the essay itself hands you the reason to doubt it. Polanyi's paradox is explicitly a claim about tasks nobody can codify because the practitioner cannot articulate the rule ("we know more than we can tell"). But Autor's own machine-learning section already previews the loophole: rather than teach a machine explicit rules, he writes, "engineers develop machines that attempt to infer tacit rules from context, abundant data, and applied statistics" — a description of the exact paradigm (large-scale statistical pattern learning from data rather than hand-coded rules) that would go on to produce the modern large language model within a decade of this essay's publication. LLMs are not narrow automation confined to one well-defined task the way an ATM or a Kiva warehouse robot is; they are general-purpose systems trained on precisely the kind of tacit, hard-to-codify language and reasoning work — drafting a persuasive paragraph, summarizing a document, writing code, holding a conversation — that this essay places in the safely human "abstract" category specifically because it resists codification. If one technology can absorb a wide swath of abstract-task work simultaneously, the essay's central mechanism (automate the codifiable middle, raise the value of the abstract and manual tasks left at the edges) may not degrade as gracefully as it did for ATMs and industrial robots, because there may be no equally large class of newly valuable human tasks for the complementarity to flow into if the same general-purpose model can plausibly absorb those too. Autor is not reasoning about anything resembling an LLM in 2015 — GPT-2 is still three years away — and the essay's own frontier examples of automation's limits (the Google car needing a human hand-off outside its pre-mapped environment, the Kiva robot that still cannot pick items off a shelf) are all instances of environmental control, the strategy the essay explicitly distinguishes from machine learning as the less generalizable of the two paths around Polanyi's paradox.

baselines

None to speak of, by design — a synthesis essay reasoning from historical employment/wage series (Katz & Margo 2014; Census/IPUMS/ACS) and a small set of illustrative cases (ATMs, Kiva warehouses, the Google car), not a paper estimating a treatment effect against a stated counterfactual.

contamination

Not applicable in the ML sense; the analogous concern is selection in the illustrations — the ATM/teller and Kiva examples are chosen because they cleanly demonstrate complementarity, and the essay doesn't quantify how many contemporaneous automation episodes went the other way (pure task loss, no offsetting task created).

what's missed

Whether the post-2000 flattening from a "U" to a "ramp" (its own Figure 5) reflects complementarity running out versus a temporary IT-investment hangover is asserted, not adjudicated — and the entire argument is built and evidenced against pre-2015 automation (ATMs, industrial robots, GPS/Kiva-style environmental control), before general-purpose machine learning existed at LLM scale.

would raise confidence

A version of the skill-percentile employment-share breakdown extended through the LLM era, or any demonstration of a comparable task-reinstatement mechanism operating for a genuinely general-purpose (rather than narrow) automation technology, would show whether Polanyi's-paradox-as-safety-net survives contact with machines built specifically to infer tacit rules from data.

// bottom line

The best empirically grounded rebuttal to naive automation panic through 2015 — and the essay the field's more pessimistic later entries (Frey & Osborne's occupation-level risk score, Acemoglu & Restrepo's and Graetz & Michaels's causal robot-exposure estimates) either react against or build directly on. Its complementarity mechanism is real and historically well documented. Whether it survives a genuinely general-purpose technology that can absorb many abstract tasks at once, rather than one narrow task at a time, is the open question this essay hands to everyone who has cited it since 2022.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. Two centuries of automation panics have shared the same flawed premise — that the amount of work in an economy is fixed, so any task machines take over is a job permanently lost. The historical record instead shows automation raising the value of the tasks that remain, not just destroying the ones it replaces.
  2. Polanyi's paradox — "we know more than we can tell" — is the load-bearing constraint: tasks people can't consciously articulate (judgment, flexibility, common sense) can't be explicitly programmed, which is why routine bookkeeping fell to computers decades before persuasive writing or object recognition did.
  3. US occupational polarization (1979–2012: middle-skill employment share 60% → 49% → 46%) is exactly what the task model predicts, but the paper's own Figure 5 shows even this optimistic reading was already softening into a one-sided ramp before 2015 — right as the essay's account of machine learning "inferring tacit rules from data" describes the mechanism that would go on to power the large language models this framework has never been tested against.
