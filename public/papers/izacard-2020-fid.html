Leveraging Passage Retrieval with Generative Models for Open Domain Question Answering

Retrieve up to 100 passages, encode each one independently, then let a single decoder fuse all of them via cross-attention — a generative reader whose accuracy keeps rising the more (possibly redundant) evidence you throw at it, unlike extractive readers, which plateau around 10–20 passages.

Gautier Izacard, Edouard Grave — Facebook AI Research / ENS, PSL University / Inria, Paris

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

By mid-2020, open-domain QA had split into two lineages that each had a ceiling. The first is closed-book generative: Roberts et al. (2020) showed that a pretrained T5 can answer factoid questions with no external documents at all, purely by recalling facts baked into its weights during pretraining — but doing that well requires models with up to 11 billion parameters, since every fact the model might need has to be memorized. That makes the approach expensive to train and, worse, expensive to query at inference time. The second lineage is retrieve-then-extract: systems like DPR (Karpukhin et al., 2020), REALM (Guu et al., 2020), and ORQA (Lee et al., 2019) retrieve a handful of passages from Wikipedia and then have a BERT-style reader pull out an answer span. This is far cheaper per query, but extractive readers have a structural weakness — a span classifier scores one passage (or a short list) at a time and struggles to aggregate evidence spread across several passages, and empirically their accuracy tends to plateau once you retrieve much past 10–20 passages, because the model has no clean mechanism to synthesize across a longer, noisier evidence set.

A few concurrent efforts — RAG (Lewis et al., 2020) and SpanSeqGen (Min et al., 2020) — had started fusing retrieval with generation rather than extraction, but both fuse a comparatively small number of documents (RAG marginalizes a Dirichlet-style mixture over its top 5–10). The open question this paper asks is blunt: if you swap the extractive reader for a sequence-to-sequence one, how many retrieved passages can you actually feed it before it stops helping — and does the answer look anything like the 10–20-passage ceiling that plagues extractive readers?

// how it works

The method

Fusion-in-Decoder (FiD) is deliberately a two-stage, off-the-shelf pipeline rather than a jointly trained system. Stage one retrieves the top-N passages for the question using either BM25 or a frozen DPR encoder — trained by someone else, not fine-tuned here. Stage two is the actual contribution: a sequence-to-sequence reader, initialized from pretrained T5 (or BART), that turns those N passages plus the question into an answer string.

The mechanism is the whole idea, and it is almost aggressively simple. Each retrieved passage is prefixed with special tokens — question:, title:, context: — concatenated with the question, and encoded by the T5/BART encoder independently of every other passage: passage 3's encoding never attends to a single token of passage 7. Only after all N passages have been encoded are their token representations concatenated into one long sequence, and it is the decoder — generating the answer autoregressively — that cross-attends over this full concatenation and is the only place in the model where evidence from different passages ever meets. Hence "fusion in decoder": fusion happens exactly once, late, and only in the decoder's cross-attention.

cost(encoder) = N × O(L2)    vs.    cost(a jointly-encoded reader) = O((N·L)2) // N = number of retrieved passages, L = tokens per passage. Because each passage only self-attends within itself, encoder cost grows linearly in N — a jointly-encoded reader that concatenated passages before the encoder would pay quadratically in N·L instead.

That asymmetry is the paper's design bet: processing passages independently in the encoder trades away any chance for the encoder itself to reason across documents, in exchange for cost that scales linearly rather than quadratically with the number of retrieved passages — which is precisely what lets the experiments push retrieval all the way to 100 passages, an order of magnitude beyond what RAG or extractive readers typically use.

Training details: models are initialized from pretrained T5-base (220M parameters) or T5-large (770M parameters), fine-tuned independently per dataset with Adam at a constant learning rate of 10⁻⁴ and 10% dropout, for 10k gradient steps at batch size 64 on 64 Tesla V100 32GB GPUs, evaluating every 500 steps and keeping the best checkpoint by Exact Match on the validation set. Both training and testing retrieve 100 passages by default, truncated to 250 word pieces each; NQ and TriviaQA use DPR retrieval, SQuAD uses BM25 (following the finding in Karpukhin et al. (2020) that DPR underperforms BM25 on SQuAD specifically). Answers are produced with plain greedy decoding — no beam search, no constrained decoding to force the output to be a span of the input.

// what they found

The results

FiD sets new state-of-the-art numbers on the two datasets that matter most for this lineage. On NaturalQuestions, FiD-large reaches 51.4 EM and even the 220M-parameter FiD-base reaches 48.2 EM — both above every prior system in the paper's comparison table, including RAG (44.5), DPR (41.5), REALM (40.4), SpanSeqGen (42.5), and closed-book T5-11B (36.6). On TriviaQA, FiD-large reaches 67.6 EM on the open test set and 80.1 EM on the hidden test set (vs. RAG's 56.1 / 68.0 and closed-book T5's 60.5 on the hidden set). On SQuAD open, FiD-large's 56.7 EM edges out the prior best, Path Retriever's 56.5 — but Path Retriever's F1 of 63.8 is actually slightly higher than FiD-large's 63.2, so the SQuAD win is a wash rather than a clean improvement.

The paper's headline finding, though, is about parameter efficiency and evidence aggregation together: "On NaturalQuestions, the closed book T5 model obtains 36.6% accuracy with 11B parameters, while our approach obtains 44.1% with 770M parameters plus Wikipedia with BM25 retrieval." A 770M-parameter reader paired with the cheaper of the two retrievers (BM25, not even DPR) beats an 11B-parameter closed-book model by 7.5 points — using, in the paper's words, "roughly the same amount of memory to store information," which is offered as evidence that explicit, retrievable text is a competitive substitute for parametric memorization.

Quoted directly: "increasing the number of passages from 10 to 100 leads to 6% improvement on TriviaQA and 3.5% improvement on NaturalQuestions." The paper contrasts this with extractive systems, whose performance "seems to peak around 10 to 20 passages" — the central empirical claim of the paper is this divergence in scaling behavior, not just the raw SOTA numbers.

The paper also studies a compute trade-off: training with fewer passages and only briefly fine-tuning at 100 passages afterward recovers most of the accuracy of training on 100 passages from scratch. Concretely, fine-tuning for 1,000 extra steps at 100 passages reaches 46.0 EM on NaturalQuestions using 147 GPU-hours, versus 425 GPU-hours to train on 100 passages throughout — a ~2.9× reduction in training compute for a 0.5-point accuracy gap (46.0 vs. 46.5 EM).

// does it hold up?

The eval read

The comparison set (DPR, REALM, RAG, ORQA, closed-book T5, GPT-3 few-shot) is the right, current 2020 field, and Table 1 is honest about where FiD does not clearly win — the SQuAD F1 loss to Path Retriever is reported rather than hidden. But two structural aspects of the evaluation deserve scrutiny. First, the retriever is entirely frozen and reused from prior work (DPR's own checkpoint, or off-the-shelf BM25) — none of the reported gain is attributable to better retrieval, all of it to the reader. That's a fair framing of the contribution, but the paper never runs the natural controlled ablation of "same DPR-retrieved passages, extractive reader vs. FiD reader," which would isolate architecture from retrieval quality more cleanly than juxtaposing rows from different papers with possibly different exact retrieval settings.

Second — and this cuts against the paper's own framing — the encoder never sees more than one passage at a time. All of the "fusion" the title promises happens in the decoder's cross-attention over already-independently-computed representations; there is no mechanism for the encoder to notice that passage 12 and passage 47 are jointly necessary to answer a question, since it never looks at both at once. NaturalQuestions, TriviaQA, and SQuAD are overwhelmingly single-hop factoid datasets, so this limitation is invisible in these results — but nothing here demonstrates genuine multi-hop or compositional reasoning across passages. The claim that "sequence-to-sequence models offer a flexible framework to efficiently aggregate and combine evidence from multiple passages" is really a claim about the decoder learning to find and copy the one relevant fact out of up to 100 mostly-redundant or irrelevant retrieved passages — which is a real and useful skill, but a narrower one than "combining evidence" implies.

baselines

Fair and current for 2020 — DPR, REALM, ORQA, RAG, closed-book T5, GPT-3 few-shot are all the right comparison points, and the one loss (SQuAD F1 vs. Path Retriever) is reported rather than omitted.

contamination

Not discussed. NQ (2019) and TriviaQA (2017) are both Wikipedia-derived, and T5's C4 pretraining corpus is a large Common Crawl scrape that plausibly already contains passages overlapping the retrieval corpus and even gold answer strings — the paper never checks train/pretrain overlap, so how much of the reader's "generation" is really retrieval-grounded copying vs. parametric recall from pretraining is unmeasured.

what's missed

No inference-time cost is reported — only training GPU-hours. Encoding up to 100 passages per query at inference is exactly the expense the closed-book lineage was trying to avoid, and the paper never quantifies query-time latency or FLOPs to let a reader weigh "770M params + retrieval" against "11B params, no retrieval" on a like-for-like serving-cost basis. There is also no groundedness check: unlike an extractive span, a generative answer can be produced without ever appearing in any retrieved passage, and the paper never measures how often that happens.

would raise confidence

A controlled retrieval-fixed ablation (identical DPR passages, extractive vs. FiD reader); a multi-hop benchmark such as HotpotQA to test whether the "aggregation" claim survives when passages must be jointly reasoned over rather than merely searched; reported inference latency/FLOPs at N=100; and a pretrain/eval overlap audit for NQ and TriviaQA against T5's and BART's pretraining corpora.

// bottom line

A simple, almost minimal idea — encode retrieved passages independently so the cost stays linear, and push all cross-passage fusion into the decoder — lets a 220M–770M parameter reader beat an 11B-parameter closed-book model and every contemporary retrieve-then-extract system on NQ and TriviaQA, and unlike extractive readers, it keeps improving all the way out to 100 retrieved passages. But that scaling result is demonstrated only on single-hop factoid QA with a frozen, borrowed retriever; the paper is silent on inference cost, contamination, and whether generated answers are actually grounded in retrieved text, so "generative models are good at combining evidence from multiple passages" should be read as "good at finding the one useful passage among a hundred," not as evidence of genuine cross-document reasoning.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. Fusion-in-Decoder: encode each retrieved (question, passage) pair independently in the encoder — no cross-passage attention — then concatenate and let only the decoder cross-attend jointly. Cost scales linearly in the number of passages, not quadratically, which is what makes retrieving 100 passages practical at all.
  2. More passages just keeps helping: Exact Match rises monotonically from 5 to 100 retrieved passages (+3.5 points on NQ, +6 on TriviaQA going from 10→100), the opposite of extractive readers, which the paper reports peak around 10–20 passages.
  3. Parameter-efficient vs. closed-book: a 770M-parameter FiD reader with BM25 retrieval (44.1% NQ) beats an 11B-parameter closed-book T5 (36.6% NQ) — explicit, retrievable text substitutes for a large fraction of parametric memorization.
  4. Training compute is negotiable, accuracy mostly isn't: training on fewer passages and briefly fine-tuning at 100 recovers nearly all the accuracy of training on 100 passages throughout (46.0 vs. 46.5 EM) for roughly a third of the GPU-hours (147 vs. 425).
