Interleaving Retrieval with Chain-of-Thought Reasoning for Knowledge-Intensive Multi-Step Questions

One-shot retrieve-and-read can't know what to retrieve for the second hop of a question until the first hop has already been reasoned through. IRCoT (Interleaved Retrieval guided by Chain-of-Thought) alternates a chain-of-thought sentence with a fresh BM25 retrieval call, each guiding the other, with no training required — improving GPT-3 retrieval recall by up to 21 points and QA F1 by up to 15 points across four multi-step QA benchmarks, and cutting factual errors in the generated reasoning itself by up to 50%.

Harsh Trivedi, Niranjan Balasubramanian, Tushar Khot, Ashish Sabharwal

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

Chain-of-thought prompting (Wei et al., 2022) had already shown LLMs could generate step-by-step natural-language reasoning for multi-step questions — as long as everything the model needed was already inside its parameters or its prompt. For open-domain, knowledge-intensive questions, the standard fix is retrieve-and-read: use the question to pull relevant text from an external corpus (RAG, REALM, and related one-step retrievers), then read it. The paper's claim is that this one-step approach is insufficient once a question requires more than one hop, because what to retrieve depends on what has already been derived, which in turn depends on what was previously retrieved.

Their running example: "In what country was Lost Gravity manufactured?" A one-step retriever, querying on the question alone, pulls up the Wikipedia page for the roller coaster Lost Gravity — which never mentions a country. Only after inferring that Lost Gravity was manufactured by Mack Rides can a system form the query that actually surfaces "Mack Rides is a company from Germany." Without retrieval, a model reasoning purely from parametric memory is liable to hallucinate that first step; without having generated that first step, there's no lexical or semantic handle to retrieve the second piece of evidence at all. Retrieval and reasoning need to inform each other, step by step — which one-step retrieve-and-read structurally cannot do.

Prior multi-step approaches each fall short in a different way: SelfAsk and DecomP decompose the question but hand retrieval off to a separate single-hop QA model or a plain BM25 call, without CoT integration; ReAct frames the problem as reasoning-plus-action steps but needs a much larger fine-tuned model (PaLM-540B) to outperform plain CoT. None had been shown to work with smaller models and no additional training.

// how it works

The method

IRCoT needs three ingredients: a base retriever that returns paragraphs for a text query (the paper uses BM25 via Elasticsearch), a language model with zero/few-shot CoT generation ability, and a handful of annotated demonstrations pairing a question with a hand-written reasoning chain and its supporting paragraphs. No training happens — everything is delivered through in-context demonstrations (15 questions per dataset, drawn from a pool of 20 hand-written CoTs).

The retriever first collects a base set of K paragraphs using the question Q as the query. It then alternates two steps until termination:

  • Reason: using the question, all paragraphs collected so far, and the CoT sentences generated so far, generate the next CoT sentence. Only the first generated sentence is kept per step, even if the model produces more.
  • Retrieve: use that latest CoT sentence as a new query to pull K more paragraphs, adding them to the growing collected set.

The loop terminates when a CoT sentence contains "answer is:" or a maximum step count (8 in their experiments) is reached; the total collected-paragraph budget is capped at 15 so demonstrations still fit in context. On termination, every collected paragraph is handed to a separate QA reader — using either Direct or CoT prompting, whichever the base LM performs better with (Flan-T5-* prefers Direct; GPT-3 prefers CoT) — to produce the final answer. IRCoT was tested on open-domain versions of HotpotQA, 2WikiMultihopQA, MuSiQue, and IIRC, with both OpenAI's GPT-3 (code-davinci-002) and Flan-T5 at four sizes (base/large/XL/XXL, 0.2B–11B).

// what they found

The results

Against a one-step retriever (OneR) using the same underlying LM, IRCoT's retrieval recall improves substantially on every dataset. With Flan-T5-XXL: +7.9 points on HotpotQA, +14.3 on 2WikiMultihopQA, +3.5 on MuSiQue, +10.2 on IIRC. With GPT-3, the gains are larger still: +11.3, +22.6, +12.5, and +21.2 points respectively. Downstream QA F1 follows the same pattern — for Flan-T5-XXL, IRCoT QA beats OneR QA by 9.4 (HotpotQA), 15.3 (2WikiMultihopQA), 5.0 (MuSiQue), and 2.5 (IIRC) points; for GPT-3, by 7.1, 13.2, and 7.1 points (IIRC does not improve for GPT-3, which the authors attribute to IIRC's answers already being largely present in GPT-3's parametric knowledge, evidenced by a comparably strong no-retrieval baseline there).

The gains hold up out-of-distribution — using CoT demonstrations written for one dataset while evaluating on another — and scale down: IRCoT improves recall over OneR "for all model sizes, including the 0.3B model," and "IRCoT with a 3B model even outperforms OneR and NoR with a 58X larger 175B GPT3 model" on every dataset tested. The paper also manually audited 40 GPT-3-generated CoTs per dataset for factual errors: IRCoT reduces factual errors over one-step retrieval "by 50% on HotpotQA and 40% on 2WikiMultihopQA" — a claim about the quality of the reasoning trace itself, not just the final answer.

Against contemporaneous few-shot ODQA systems — Internet-Augmented QA, RECITE, ReAct, SelfAsk, and DecomP — IRCoT QA with GPT-3 posts the highest reported numbers where comparisons are available (e.g., 49.3 EM / 60.7 F1 on HotpotQA vs. RECITE's 37.1 / 48.4 and ReAct's 35.1 EM). The paper is explicit, however, that this is "not head-to-head" since the systems differ in knowledge corpus, base LLM, and API access.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

IRCoT's core claim rests on a genuinely controlled comparison — NoR, OneR, and IRCoT QA are the same reader LM with only the retrieval strategy varied, run across four datasets, two model families, and both IID and OOD splits, with results reported as mean ± std over three demonstration sets. That's a well-built ablation. Two other parts of the paper are weaker: the leaderboard-style SOTA table, and the paper's dependence on a since-deprecated commercial model it never contamination-checked.

baselines

The internal NoR/OneR/IRCoT ablation is fair — same reader, same demonstrations, only retrieval varies. Table 1's comparison against SelfAsk/DecomP/ReAct/RECITE/Internet-Augmented QA is explicitly flagged by the authors themselves as not apples-to-apples (different knowledge corpora, LLMs, and APIs), yet it's still presented in a leaderboard format that invites reading the raw numbers as a ranking.

contamination

The GPT-3 experiments run entirely on code-davinci-002, which OpenAI deprecated after submission — the paper's own Limitations section admits the deprecation is "making the reproduction of these experiments challenging despite our best efforts." No contamination analysis is offered for whether that model (or Flan-T5) had memorized the HotpotQA/2WikiMultihopQA/MuSiQue/IIRC dev-set subsets used for the 500-question test samples; the NoR (no-retrieval) baseline already scores 47.5–52.1 F1 on some datasets purely from parametric knowledge, which is consistent with either genuine world knowledge or partial memorization and the paper doesn't try to distinguish the two.

what's missed

The retrieval metric is a nonstandard "fixed-budget optimal recall," which the authors justify (footnote 7) by noting the retrieved set is unranked and its size varies with the number of CoT steps — but this also means the headline recall numbers aren't directly comparable to Recall@k/MAP figures reported elsewhere in the retrieval literature. The paper is upfront that IRCoT costs an extra LLM call per CoT sentence, but never reports the resulting latency or dollar cost against a paid API.

would raise confidence

A standard Recall@k alongside the fixed-budget metric so results compare directly to prior retrieval work; a contamination check on the deprecated code-davinci-002's exposure to the specific dev-set questions used; and reported latency/cost overhead versus OneR, since the per-sentence LLM calls are the method's explicit trade-off.

// bottom line

The controlled internal comparison — same LM, same demonstrations, only the retrieval strategy changed — is real and consistent: 21-point recall gains, 15-point F1 gains, and a 40–50% cut in factual errors, replicated across four datasets, model scales from 0.2B to 175B, and OOD splits. Where a careful reader should discount the paper's own framing is the SOTA leaderboard table (which the authors themselves caveat as not head-to-head) and the total absence of any contamination check on a commercial, now-deprecated API.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. Multi-step QA breaks one-shot retrieve-and-read because what to retrieve is a function of intermediate reasoning, not just the original question — IRCoT fixes this with no training, by using the latest CoT sentence itself as the next retrieval query.
  2. The effect holds from 0.2B to 175B parameters, and a 3B Flan-T5-XL running IRCoT beats a 175B GPT-3 running one-step retrieval on every dataset tested — the gain comes from the retrieval strategy, not from model scale.
  3. IRCoT also measurably reduces hallucination in the generated reasoning trace itself (up to 50% fewer factual errors on HotpotQA), not just in the final answer — evidence the retrieved evidence is actually grounding intermediate steps, not just the last one.
