Measurement and Fairness

A framework borrowed wholesale from the quantitative social sciences: most of the quantities ML fairness argues about — recidivism risk, teacher effectiveness, socioeconomic status — are unobservable theoretical constructs, not directly-measured facts, and many "fairness" harms are really mismatches between a construct and the proxy standing in for it.

Abigail Z. Jacobs (University of Michigan), Hanna Wallach (Microsoft Research)

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

The fairness-in-ML literature by 2019 had accumulated a long list of documented harms — COMPAS's racially skewed error rates, value-added teacher scores that fluctuated wildly year to year, healthcare risk models that under-referred Black patients — and a parallel cottage industry of mathematical fairness definitions (demographic parity, equalized odds, predictive parity, individual fairness) meant to prevent them. Jacobs and Wallach argue the field is missing something underneath all of this: many of the quantities these systems claim to measure — recidivism risk, teacher effectiveness, socioeconomic status, patient benefit, and ultimately fairness itself — are unobservable theoretical constructs. They cannot be measured directly. They can only be inferred from measurable proxies, via a measurement model that encodes assumptions about how the proxy relates to the construct.

The quantitative social sciences (psychometrics, political science, education) have a century of machinery — measurement modeling — for making those assumptions explicit and testing them. Computer science, the authors argue, has none of this: practitioners routinely treat a proxy (an arrest record, a standardized test score, last year's medical spending) as if it were the construct, rather than one fallible operationalization of it. Collapsing that distinction is exactly where fairness-related harms sneak in, because nobody is checking whether the operationalization actually captures what it claims to.

Concretely: COMPAS operationalizes "risk of recidivism" as "a new misdemeanor or felony arrest within two years." That is a specific, assumption-laden choice — arrests are not crimes, and policing intensity varies by neighborhood and race — but the debate that followed Angwin et al.'s 2016 ProPublica investigation was conducted entirely in the language of which parity metric COMPAS satisfied, never in the language of whether "arrest-within-two-years" is a valid stand-in for "recidivism" in the first place. That is the gap the paper targets: a vocabulary and a set of tests for the construct-to-operationalization step that quantitative fairness debates skip past.

// how it works

The method

The paper's engine is the distinction between a construct (an abstraction — e.g. a person's height, or their socioeconomic status) and its operationalization as a latent variable inside a measurement model that links it to observable properties. Even the simplest case, measuring height with a ruler, involves assumptions: does hair count? shoes? standing or sitting height? The paper formalizes a single measurement as ĥₙ = h + εₙ — a "true" latent value plus measurement error — and shows that even this toy setup breaks under a real assumption failure: on dating-site self-reports, men over-report height more often and by larger margins than women, so the error term is not the well-behaved, unbiased noise the model assumes. Get the error model wrong and every downstream number is systematically off in a way correlated with a demographic group.

ĥn = h + εn,  εn ~ N(0, σ²) // the measurement error model for a single observation — "well-behaved" errors are an assumption, not a given

From there the paper walks through progressively harder constructs — socioeconomic status (proxied by income), teacher effectiveness (proxied by the EVAAS value-added model's regression on students' test-score deltas), COMPAS's recidivism-risk score, and a healthcare enrollment model that proxies "patient benefit" with predicted future care costs — to show that every one of these operationalizations is a specific, contestable set of assumptions, not a neutral measurement.

The paper's actual contribution is a toolkit for testing those assumptions, unifying vocabulary from Messick, Loevinger, Jackman, and Quinn et al. into two buckets:

Construct reliability

Roughly "precision": do repeated measurements of the same, unchanged thing agree? The paper singles out test–retest reliability as the aspect most relevant to fairness — e.g. a teacher scoring 6/100 one year and 96/100 the next on the same value-added model is a reliability failure, not evidence that the teacher's effectiveness actually swung that wildly.

Construct validity — seven aspects

Roughly "unbiasedness," and explicitly a matter of degree, not a yes/no checkbox. The paper's fairness-oriented conceptualization unites seven sub-aspects: face validity (does it pass a plausibility "sniff test"?), content validity — itself split into contestedness (is there one agreed theoretical understanding, or several conflicting ones?), substantive validity (does the operationalization use the right properties, and only those?), and structural validity (does it capture the right relationship between them?) — plus convergent validity (does it correlate with other validated measures of the same thing?), discriminant validity (does it correlate with other constructs only to the degree they're actually related — and not, say, near-perfectly with race when it shouldn't?), predictive validity and hypothesis validity (do the measurements support independent, substantively interesting downstream facts?), and finally consequential validity (what are the real-world consequences, including who the measurement disadvantages, of using it?).

// what they found

The results

Because this is a conceptual framework paper rather than an empirical one, its "results" are diagnostic re-readings of well-known fairness scandals through the seven-aspect lens — and the framework does surface something each scandal's original parity-metric framing missed:

  • Value-added teaching models fail test–retest reliability. The book Weapons of Math Destruction documents a New York City teacher, Tim Clifford, scoring 6 out of 100 in one year and 96 the next on the same EVAAS model — a swing the authors argue is far more consistent with an unreliable instrument than with an actual, near-total reversal in teaching quality. A second teacher, Sarah Wysocki, was flagged by the same model despite strong reviews from her principal, colleagues, and parents — a convergent-validity failure (the score didn't agree with other, independently-validated signals of the same construct).
  • COMPAS fails content (substantive) validity. By defining recidivism as an arrest within two years, COMPAS's risk score is a measurement of policing patterns as much as of underlying reoffending — the paper argues this substitution is exactly what let Angwin et al.'s 2016 finding (Black defendants disproportionately mis-scored as high-risk, white defendants disproportionately mis-scored as low-risk) become a racial-disparity story about score calibration rather than about the construct definition that produced it.
  • The Obermeyer et al. healthcare model fails discriminant validity. A high-risk care-management model that operationalized "needs the most care" as "will cost the most" enrolled a patient population that was only 18% Black — a correlation with race the authors argue should have been a red flag long before the underlying mechanism (Black and white patients with comparable health needs historically accrue very different costs, because of unequal access) was diagnosed.
  • The individual-vs-group fairness debate is reframed as a values debate, not a math debate. The paper argues these are two different theoretical understandings of fairness — not two operationalizations of the same one — so no amount of additional mathematics resolves which to use; that choice is a value judgment the framework says should be made explicitly rather than "couched in mathematics" where it's inaccessible to the people the system affects.
// does it hold up?

The eval read

As a piece of conceptual scholarship, "Measurement and Fairness" is careful and genuinely synthesizes disparate traditions (Messick's consequential validity from education, Loevinger's construct-validity taxonomy from psychology, Jackman's reliability/validity split from political science) into a single vocabulary that fairness researchers didn't previously have. But it is worth being precise about what kind of contribution this is, and what it does not do.

The paper's own five worked examples are height, socioeconomic status, teacher effectiveness (EVAAS), COMPAS's recidivism score, and the Obermeyer et al. healthcare risk model — and every fairness-relevant harm illustrated through them traces back to a case already publicly documented and diagnosed by other researchers before this paper applied its validity vocabulary to it: Angwin et al.'s COMPAS investigation, O'Neil's reporting on EVAAS, Obermeyer et al.'s own audit of the healthcare model, even the dating-site height-inflation finding (Toma et al.) and the Universal Credit failure (Human Rights Watch) embedded in the two toy examples. That is a legitimate way to demonstrate a framework's explanatory power, but it also means the paper offers no test of whether the seven-aspect checklist can prospectively catch a harm that hasn't already been found by other means — the demonstrated skill is re-narration in a sharper vocabulary, not prediction.

The framework is also explicitly qualitative where a practitioner might want it to be actionable: face validity is called a "sniff test," and construct validity as a whole is described as "always a matter of degree, to be supported by critical reasoning" rather than a pass/fail gate. That is philosophically honest (validity genuinely isn't binary), but it also means the paper gives a team building a deployment-bound risk score no decision rule — how many of the seven aspects need to look shaky, and how shaky, before the measurement shouldn't be used? The paper's own examples (COMPAS, EVAAS) all failed multiple aspects badly and obviously in hindsight; it's untested whether the framework discriminates well on the harder, more marginal cases practitioners actually face.

baselines

Not applicable in the usual sense — there is no competing framework being benchmarked against. The nearest comparison is treating fairness definitions (demographic parity, equalized odds, etc.) purely as competing operationalizations with no measurement-theory backstop; the paper's implicit baseline is "the status quo of fairness research," and it argues, rather than measures, that its lens does better.

contamination

Not a data-driven paper, so no leakage risk in the usual sense — but there is a selection-effect risk: every illustrative case is a scandal already well-documented by others, which is closer to "fitting a narrative to known outcomes" than to a blind test.

what's missed

Any worked example of applying the seven-aspect checklist to a system before deployment or before a harm was independently discovered; any attempt to operationalize the checklist itself (e.g. quantitative thresholds, an audit template, a scoring rubric) beyond "assess and reason critically."

would raise confidence

A prospective case study — a team applying construct reliability/validity checks to a system pre-launch and catching something novel — plus a more concrete decision procedure (even a qualitative rubric) for when validity concerns are severe enough to block deployment rather than merely to note.

// bottom line

A genuinely useful import: the construct/operationalization distinction and its seven-aspect validity vocabulary give fairness researchers language for a failure mode — mistaking a proxy for the thing it proxies — that mathematical parity definitions have no way to even express. But the paper's evidence is entirely retrospective re-diagnosis of already-known scandals, and the framework offers no threshold or decision procedure, so its practical power as a pre-deployment audit tool (rather than a post-hoc explanatory lens) remains unverified.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. Quantities like "recidivism risk," "teacher effectiveness," and "socioeconomic status" are unobservable theoretical constructs — treating their measurable proxies (arrest records, test-score deltas, income) as the construct itself is where the paper locates many fairness-related harms.
  2. The paper's fairness-oriented construct validity has seven aspects — face, content (contestedness / substantive / structural), convergent, discriminant, predictive, hypothesis, consequential — plus test–retest reliability, unifying traditions from psychometrics, political science, and education into one vocabulary for surfacing construct/operationalization mismatches.
  3. The individual-vs-group fairness debate, and fairness-definition debates more broadly, are reframed as debates about different values (which theoretical understanding of fairness to hold), not debates that more mathematics can resolve — a claim the paper argues should be made explicit rather than hidden inside equations.
