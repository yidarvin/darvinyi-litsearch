Training Compute-Optimal Large Language Models

Given a fixed compute budget, how big should a language model be — and how much data should it see? DeepMind trains over 400 models to re-derive the answer, finds that the era's flagship models are badly undertrained, and proves it by training Chinchilla (70B) to beat Gopher (280B) at the same compute.

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

By early 2022 the recipe for a frontier language model was simple: make it bigger. The largest dense transformer, Megatron-Turing NLG, had reached 530B parameters — over 3× GPT-3's 175B from two years prior. Yet almost every one of these giants, GPT-3, Jurassic-1, Gopher, MT-NLG, had been trained on roughly the same ~300 billion tokens. The field was pouring its growing compute budgets almost entirely into parameters and leaving the data fixed.

That habit traced directly back to Kaplan et al. (2020), whose scaling laws were read to say that, given a 10× larger compute budget, you should make the model 5.5× bigger but train on only 1.8× more data — exponents of roughly a = 0.73 on parameters and b = 0.27 on tokens. Because a frontier model is typically trained once, getting this allocation wrong is enormously expensive: you spend an eight-figure compute bill on a model that is the wrong shape. The gap this paper attacks is whether that 0.73 / 0.27 split — and therefore the entire "just scale parameters" strategy — was actually compute-optimal. It was not.

// how it works

The method

The question is a constrained optimisation: for a fixed compute budget C (with FLOPs ≈ 6ND for N parameters and D training tokens), choose N and D to minimise the final training loss L. The authors attack it empirically, training over 400 models from 70M to 16B parameters on 5B to 500B tokens, then estimating the optimal frontier three independent ways — a deliberate hedge against any single fitting methodology being wrong.

Approach 1 — minimum over training curves

Fix a family of model sizes, train each for several different durations, and read off the lower envelope of loss versus FLOPs. Fitting power laws Nopt ∝ Ca and Dopt ∝ Cb to that envelope gives a = 0.50, b = 0.50.

Approach 2 — IsoFLOP profiles

For each of 9 fixed FLOP budgets (6×1018 to 3×1021), sweep model size and plot final loss. Each curve has a clear valley — a single best model size for that budget (Figure 1). Projecting the valleys outward gives a = 0.49, b = 0.51.

Approach 3 — a parametric loss surface

Fit a closed-form loss to every run, decomposing it into an irreducible entropy term plus finite-model and finite-data penalties, then minimise it under the FLOP constraint:

L̂(N, D) = E + ANα + BDβ // fitted by Huber loss + L-BFGS: E = 1.69, A = 406.4, B = 410.7, α = 0.34, β = 0.28 → a = 0.46, b = 0.54

The three methods use different data and different fits, yet they agree: the exponents on parameters and tokens are both ≈ 0.5. In plain terms — for every doubling of model size, you should also double the training data. That is a sharp break from Kaplan's 0.73 / 0.27.

The test: Chinchilla

Predictions are cheap; the paper spends real compute to falsify them. They take Gopher's exact training budget (5.76×1023 FLOPs) and, instead of a 280B model on 300B tokens, train the compute-optimal shape the analysis predicts — the optimum at Gopher's budget lands between 40 and 70B parameters, and they take the upper end: a 70B-parameter model, "Chinchilla", on 1.4T tokens (4× the data, one-quarter the parameters). If the scaling law is right, this smaller model should win.

// what they found

The results

Chinchilla uniformly and significantly outperforms Gopher — and the larger GPT-3 (175B), Jurassic-1 (178B) and MT-NLG (530B) — across a broad evaluation suite, while being 4× smaller and therefore much cheaper to fine-tune and serve.

  • MMLU: a state-of-the-art 67.6% average 5-shot accuracy across the 57 subjects (the abstract rounds it to 67.5%) vs Gopher's 60.0% — clearing the June 2023 expert forecast of 63.4% a full year early, against an average human-expert ceiling of 89.8%.
  • BIG-bench: 65.1% vs Gopher's 54.4%, with Chinchilla worse than Gopher on only 4 of 62 tasks.
  • The Pile: lower bits-per-byte than Gopher on every subset.
  • Reading comprehension & QA: beats both Gopher and GPT-3 on all considered tasks, and MT-NLG 530B on all but one.

The deeper payoff is the table the paper builds for the whole field (Table 3): at Gopher's compute, the optimal model is ~67B params on 1.5T tokens; a 175B model (GPT-3's size) should see 3.7T tokens; a 280B model (Gopher), 5.9T; and a 520B model (Table 3's round figure for MT-NLG's 530B), 11T. Measured against that, GPT-3 and Gopher were trained on an order of magnitude too little data.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

This is one of the most consequential empirical claims in modern ML, and the headline is well-supported: three independent fits converge on equal scaling, and the Chinchilla-vs-Gopher head-to-head is a clean, predict-then-train falsification test that the smaller model passes decisively. The fragility is not in the direction of the result — it is statistical and in the fine print of how far the law can be pushed.

baselines

Fair and current for 2022: Gopher, GPT-3, Jurassic-1 and MT-NLG are all re-evaluated under one shared protocol (from Rae et al. 2021), so Chinchilla's wins are not protocol artifacts. The catch is sample size — the entire large-scale validation rests on one confirming pair (Chinchilla vs Gopher) at the budget that matters; there are no intermediate-scale confirmations between the small sweep and the 70B model.

contamination

Acknowledged but never measured. The paper flags train–test leakage specifically for the language-modelling comparisons (WikiText-103, The Pile), where Chinchilla's 4× larger corpus "may artificially enhance the results," and deliberately down-weights those benchmarks in favour of MMLU and BIG-bench as "tasks for which leakage is less of a concern." That is the right instinct, but the corpus is never audited against the eval sets, and the Discussion concedes only that larger datasets "will require extra care to ensure train-test set overlap is properly accounted for." The risk is side-stepped, not quantified.

what's missed

Three assumptions bound the claim. (1) All fits use less than one epoch of data — silent on the multi-epoch / data-constrained regime. (2) The frontier is assumed to be a pure power law, yet the authors observe concavity at high compute (Appendix E), so the true optimum may be even smaller. (3) The objective is upstream LM loss and the cost model is FLOPs ≈ 6ND — neither prices in inference, which is exactly where a smaller model pays off most.

would raise confidence

More large-scale confirming runs at other budgets; explicit train–test contamination audits of the corpus against the eval suites; and an inference-aware cost model. That last point proved load-bearing — once you charge for serving, the optimum shifts toward even smaller, even more over-trained models, which is what later practice (and Llama-style training) actually did.

// bottom line

A rare paper that changed an industry's default. The equal-scaling result and the "~20 tokens per parameter" rule of thumb reset how labs allocate compute, and Chinchilla's win is a genuine, decisive demonstration. Read it for the conclusion, not the constant: the exact optimum is fragile (one confirming run, single-epoch, pure power-law, no inference cost), and subsequent work has rightly pushed the token count well past "Chinchilla-optimal."

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. Scale parameters and data equally. Three independent estimates put the optimal exponents at a ≈ b ≈ 0.5 — double the model, double the tokens. Kaplan's 0.73 / 0.27 had badly over-weighted parameters, and the whole "just make it bigger" era was a misallocation of compute.
  2. Chinchilla is the proof, not just the model. A 70B model on 1.4T tokens beats Gopher (280B / 300B tokens), GPT-3, Jurassic-1 and MT-NLG (530B) at equal compute — 67.6% on MMLU, 65.1% on BIG-bench — while being 4× cheaper to fine-tune and serve.
  3. "Chinchilla-optimal" became the reference point. The ~20-tokens-per-parameter rule reset training norms across the field; later work (inference-aware over-training, multi-epoch / data-constrained scaling) departs from the exact optimum, but it departs from this paper.
