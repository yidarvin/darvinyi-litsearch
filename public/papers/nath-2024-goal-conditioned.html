Learning Goal-Conditioned Representations for Language Reward Models

Reward models score a whole answer with one number and say nothing about where it went wrong. Scale AI adds a contrastive, goal-conditioned auxiliary loss that reshapes the reward model's hidden states into an approximate Q-function — lifting reward AUROC by up to 0.09 on math, localizing the exact reasoning step that breaks, and steering Llama-3 toward more helpful generations — all with no extra annotation.

Vaskar Nath, Dylan Slack, Jeff Da, Yuntao Ma, Hugh Zhang, Spencer Whitehead, Sean Hendryx · Scale AI

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

The reward model (RM) is the quiet bottleneck of RLHF. The standard recipe trains it with the Bradley-Terry paired loss on human preference data: given a preferred and a dispreferred completion for the same prompt, push the scalar reward of the winner above the loser. The RM then emits one number for the entire sequence. That scalar is enough to rank two answers, but it carries no information about which step in a long chain of reasoning is the mistake, or whether a half-finished trajectory is even heading somewhere good.

People have fixed this before with process or fine-grained reward models that score individual steps or spans — but those typically need expensive per-step or per-token human annotations, which is exactly the cost RLHF was trying to contain.

Meanwhile, classic reinforcement learning has known for years that representation quality drives sample efficiency and generalization, and that you can manufacture cheap supervision for it — data augmentation, auxiliary losses, and especially goal-conditioned contrastive objectives that pull together state representations along the same successful trajectory. One line of work even shows that the similarity between learned state representations is a value function up to a constant. Almost none of that machinery had been carried over to language reward models.

The question this paper asks: can a reward model get the credit-assignment benefits of a Q-function — a dense, intermediate sense of "is this trajectory on track?" — without any new labels, simply by reshaping its own hidden representations during ordinary preference training?

// how it works

The method

Decompose the reward model into two pieces: a feature extractor φ (the LM decoder itself, producing a hidden representation per token) and a small reward head r′ (a linear layer or MLP) that reads the last token's representation and outputs the scalar reward. Training keeps the usual preference-ranking loss on that scalar:

𝓛R = −𝔼(x, yw, yℓ) log σ( r(x, yw) − r(x, yℓ) ) // standard Bradley-Terry loss — push the preferred completion y_w above the dispreferred y_ℓ

The new idea is a second, contrastive goal-conditioned loss applied to φ's hidden states. Borrowing from goal-conditioned RL, the authors treat the cosine similarity between a "state-action" representation and a future "goal-state" representation as an approximate Q-function — a proxy for the expected reward of continuing toward that goal. They want representations early in a sequence to already look like the representations of good endings, and to look unlike the representations of bad endings:

𝓛C = log σ( f(y≤t, yt+1, yg+) ) + log( 1 − σ( f(y≤t, yt+1, yg−) ) ) // f = cosine similarity between a (state, action) representation and a goal-state representation

In practice — what the paper calls the Single Goal State (SGS) loss — for each preference pair you sample two indices i < j from the preferred completion: hi is the source state and hj is a positive goal state (similarity to be maximized). For the negative goal state you take a token from the dispreferred completion (similarity to be minimized). No extra annotation is required beyond the sequence-level preferences you already have. The two objectives are trained jointly:

𝓛 = 𝓛R + λ · 𝓛C // λ trades off reward ranking against the goal-conditioned representation objective

Reading out Q-values at inference

Once trained, the same representations give you a token-level Q-value for free. At inference there are no per-sample future tokens to compare against, so the authors fix a single global goal state hg — the mean last-hidden representation of all preferred completions in the training set — and define the Q-value at token t as its cosine similarity to that goal:

Q(yt) = cos( ht, hg ),   hg = 1N Σyw ∈ D h(yw) // goal state = mean representation of preferred completions; no new labels needed

That single readout powers three uses: (1) the reward score itself improves because r′ now sits on top of goal-aware features; (2) the Q-value trace can flag trajectories likely to end "incorrect" — filter them out, or pinpoint the step where the Q-value collapses; and (3) at decode time you can swap the global goal for a prototype (the mean of 20 examples scored highly on helpfulness/correctness/coherence/complexity in HelpSteer) and use cosine similarity to that prototype to steer beam search via guided decoding.

// what they found

The results

The math experiments train a reward model from OpenMath-CodeLlama-7b on OpenMathInstruct-1, pairing execution-verified correct solutions (preferred) against incorrect ones (dispreferred). The baseline — the same backbone trained with preference ranking only — is the "CodeLlama 7b Reward"; the goal-conditioned version is the "Q-Function 7b Reward". Measured as reward-score AUROC (how well the reward separates correct from incorrect completions), the contrastive loss wins on every benchmark, in- and out-of-distribution.

Because the loss is enforced at intermediate steps, the Q-Function model also reads partial solutions better. Shown only the first x% of a generation, its AUROC rises more smoothly with information and dips less than the noisy baseline (Figure 3) — direct evidence the representations track progress toward the goal mid-sequence.

Q-values are directly useful

Filtering majority-vote samples by their Q-value (discard any generation whose Q-value goes negative) beats plain majority@50, and the gains are t-test significant. On MATH it lifts accuracy 55.6 → 59.6% while discarding 65.6% of generations and saving 55.6% of tokens; on GSM8K, 84.8 → 86.0%. The Q-value trace also pinpoints the exact line where a Python solution's logic breaks.

Used as the reward in PPO, the Q-Function RM raises policy accuracy by a benchmark-size-weighted +1.7% on average. It edges the standard-RM PPO and ties DPO in-distribution (GSM8K 80.5 vs DPO 80.1; MATH 45.1 vs 44.8) — but pulls clearly ahead of DPO out-of-distribution, where DPO is known to degrade (algebra222 70.9 vs 64.8; svamp 81.2 vs 76.6; mawps 93.6 vs 90.3).

Beyond math: alignment & steering

Switching the backbone to Llama-3-8B-Instruct and training on Helpful-Harmless, the same loss raises RM accuracy from 68.2 → 70.5% (+2.3). Using the learned representations to steer guided decoding via a HelpSteer prototype improves GPT-4-judged win-rate over an SFT baseline across every axis — most of all on complexity.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

The core claim — that a contrastive goal-conditioned auxiliary loss buys a reward model an approximate Q-function for free — is supported, and the headline numbers are reported carefully (95% CIs on the partial-completion curves, t-test significance on the filtering table, four PPO seeds). But the gains are concentrated exactly where they are easiest to get, and they thin out as you move from "reward discrimination" toward "better policy."

Start with the AUROC story. The largest improvement (MATH, +0.09) lands on the weakest baseline — MATH's preference-only AUROC is 0.618, barely above chance, so there is the most headroom there. On the already-strong benchmarks the lift compresses to +0.01–0.05. And the whole math setup uses execution-verified correct-vs-incorrect pairs: a crisp, near-noise-free goal signal, which is the friendliest possible regime for a method built around "distance to a correct goal state." The genuinely subjective case (Helpful-Harmless) gets a more modest +2.3% accuracy.

The downstream effect is the honest part of the paper. A reward model whose AUROC jumps by up to 0.09 only moves the PPO policy by a weighted +1.7%, and the authors flag the likely culprit themselves: the preference data are off-policy Mixtral-8x7B generations, so the policy gain is bottlenecked by distribution shift rather than RM quality. That gap between RM-metric and policy-metric is the usual reward-modeling reality check.

The steering result deserves the most skepticism. It is a win-rate judged by a single LLM (GPT-4), and the biggest "win" is complexity (+21.6%) — but the prototype is explicitly built to score high on complexity, and GPT-4 is documented to prefer longer, more elaborate answers. So part of that number may be the judge and the method optimizing the same axis, not human-perceived quality. There is no human evaluation, and win-rate can't tell you whether harmlessness (the floor) was preserved.

baselines

Fair within scope: the control is the same backbone trained with the standard Bradley-Terry loss, and DPO is included as a strong PPO comparator. But only one model size per regime (7B math, 8B NL), and no comparison to annotation-based process/step reward models — the obvious alternative that also delivers fine-grained signal.

contamination

Low-stakes here. GSM8K/MATH are heavily contaminated, but the headline metric is reward-AUROC on generations, not raw accuracy, so leakage matters less than usual. "OOD" (algebra222/Asdiv/mawps/svamp) means distribution-shifted classic sets, not truly unseen data.

what's missed

The win-rate + GPT-4 judge can reward length/complexity — the very axis the prototype optimizes; RM-AUROC gains up to 0.09 shrink to +1.7% policy accuracy and +2.3% HH accuracy; no human eval; no scaling beyond 7–8B; λ sensitivity is left to the appendix.

would raise confidence

Scale to 70B+; on-policy iterative RM/policy retraining (their own next step); a subjective-preference RM benchmark (e.g. RewardBench) to show the gain isn't specific to verifiable-answer tasks; and a human eval of the steered generations.

// bottom line

A clean, low-cost idea that earns its keep: a contrastive goal-conditioned auxiliary loss gives a reward model an approximate Q-function with no new labels, and the byproducts — token-level error localization, trajectory filtering, decode-time steering — are genuinely useful. The effects are real and statistically tested, but largest in the friendliest setting (verifiable math, a forgiving LLM-judge win-rate) and smaller once they have to survive the trip into the policy (+1.7%). A promising representation-learning direction for RMs; not yet shown to scale or to clearly improve subjective-preference modeling.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. A reward model's hidden states can be shaped into an approximate Q-function with a contrastive goal-conditioned auxiliary loss — maximize representation similarity along preferred trajectories, minimize it toward dispreferred goal states — trained jointly with the ordinary Bradley-Terry loss and no extra annotation.
  2. It improves reward-score AUROC by up to 0.09 on math (GSM8K/MATH), +0.04 on average OOD, and +2.3% on Helpful-Harmless — and the resulting Q-values pinpoint the exact reasoning step where a solution goes wrong.
  3. The Q-values are directly actionable: filter low-Q trajectories to lift majority-vote accuracy while saving up to ~55% of tokens, and steer Llama-3 at decode time toward more helpful generations (+9.6% to +21.6% GPT-4 win-rate vs SFT).
  4. Caveat the gains: they are largest on verifiable-answer tasks and a single LLM judge, the downstream PPO improvement is a smaller +1.7%, and scaling, human evaluation, and subjective-preference benchmarks remain open.
