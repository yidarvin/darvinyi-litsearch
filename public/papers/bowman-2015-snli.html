A large annotated corpus for learning natural language inference

The Stanford Natural Language Inference corpus — 570,152 human-written sentence pairs labeled entailment, contradiction, or neutral — made supervised neural inference viable for the first time and became the field's default entailment benchmark.

Samuel R. Bowman · Gabor Angeli · Christopher Potts · Christopher D. Manning

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

Natural language inference (NLI) — deciding whether one sentence entails, contradicts, or is neutral with respect to another — had long been pitched as a clean test bed for whether a system actually understands meaning. The problem in 2015 was not the task but the data. The standard resources were tiny: the paper notes the PASCAL RTE challenge sets had fewer than a thousand examples each, and the SemEval-2014 SICK corpus was a step up to only about 4,500 training pairs. That is far too little to train the data-hungry distributed-representation models that were beginning to dominate NLP.

Worse, the existing corpora had structural problems. Many pairs were algorithmically generated rather than written by people, and most were "beset with indeterminacies of event and entity coreference" — when two independently sourced sentences mention "a man," it is genuinely unclear whether they refer to the same man, which makes the entailment label ambiguous and annotation noisy. In the SICK SemEval task, the strongest systems leaned on hand-built lexical resources and reasoning components, so the field could not actually answer its motivating question: can learned representations be trained to do inference at all? No corpus was simultaneously large enough, naturalistic enough, and clean enough to find out. SNLI was built to remove that bottleneck.

// how it works

The method

The core idea is to ground the labeling task in image captioning so that the "world" each sentence pair describes is fixed and shared. About 2,500 Amazon Mechanical Turk workers were each shown a single premise — a caption drawn from the Flickr30k corpus (~160k captions over ~30k images) — but crucially not the photograph it described. For that premise, a worker wrote three brand-new sentences: one that is definitely true of the pictured scene (entailment), one that might be true (neutral), and one that is definitely false (contradiction).

Two design choices fall out of this. Because every premise yields exactly one of each label, the three classes are balanced by construction. And because workers reason about a described scene rather than two unrelated sentences, the coreference indeterminacy that plagued earlier corpora is greatly mitigated — given the premise "A dog is lying in the grass," a worker can safely treat the dog as the one prominent dog and write contradictions about it. Hiding the image (showing only the caption) is meant to ensure the gold label is recoverable from text alone.

Validation and the gold label

To measure quality and build trustworthy evaluation sets, roughly 10% of the data (56,951 pairs) went through a second pass. Each such pair was shown to four additional annotators, giving five labels in total once the original author's label is included. The gold label is the one chosen by at least three of the five annotators; if no label clears that bar — about 2% of cases — the pair is marked with a placeholder "–" and excluded from training and evaluation. (After catching some random guessing early, validation was locked to about 30 trusted workers.)

gold(p, h) = argmaxℓ ∈ {E, N, C} votes(ℓ)   if maxℓ votes(ℓ) ≥ 3 of 5,  else "–" // gold label = a 3-of-5 annotator majority over the five labels (author + four validators); no-consensus pairs are dropped

The corpus ships with a fixed 550,152 / 10,000 / 10,000 train / dev / test split, where every original Flickr caption appears in only one of the three sets (so premises never leak across splits) and every dev and test pair has been validated. Parses from the Stanford PCFG parser are bundled in.

Models tested on the new corpus

The paper then runs three families of models that could scale to the corpus: (i) two off-the-shelf RTE systems from the Excitement Open Platform (an edit-distance algorithm and a classifier, with and without lexical resources); (ii) a simple lexicalized feature classifier with six feature types — BLEU of hypothesis vs. premise, length difference, word overlap, unigram/bigram indicators, and sparse cross-unigram and cross-bigram features over POS-matched word pairs; and (iii) neural sentence-embedding models. In the neural setup each sentence is encoded independently into a 100-dimensional vector (a sum-of-words baseline, a plain RNN, or an LSTM), the two vectors are concatenated, and the result is passed through three stacked 200d tanh layers into a 3-way softmax (Figure 3). Word vectors are initialized from 300d GloVe and fine-tuned; training uses AdaDelta with dropout and L2 regularization.

// what they found

The results

The headline is scale: at 570,152 pairs, SNLI is roughly two orders of magnitude larger than any comparable NLI resource, with a 37,026-word vocabulary. Premises average 14.1 tokens and hypotheses 8.3 — workers wrote the shortest sentence that still settled the judgment, clustering hypotheses around seven words. And the corpus is clean: validation gave an overall Fleiss κ = 0.70 (0.77 for contradiction, 0.72 for entailment, 0.60 for the fuzzier neutral class), with 91.2% of gold labels matching the original author's label and only 2.0% of validated pairs failing to reach a 3-of-5 consensus.

On modeling, scale did exactly what it was supposed to. The corpus was large enough to train neural networks that were competitive on NLI for the first time: the 100d LSTM reached 77.6% 3-class test accuracy. But the quieter result is more interesting — the authors' own bag-of-features lexicalized classifier hit 78.2%, essentially tying the LSTM (a 0.6-point gap the paper notes sits within its ~0.5% run-to-run variance), while a plain sum-of-words model got 75.3% and a stripped-down unlexicalized classifier only 50.4%. So in 2015, on SNLI, a neural net became viable but did not pull ahead of a simple feature classifier.

Finally, the representations transfer. Taking the LSTM trained on SNLI and fine-tuning it on the small SICK entailment task reached 80.8% SICK test accuracy, versus 71.3% for training on SICK alone — early evidence that a model forced to encode SNLI's scene descriptions learns something reusable. (Tested on SICK with no fine-tuning, the SNLI model managed only 46.7%, a first hint of how domain-specific the corpus is.)

// does it hold up?

The eval read

SNLI's dataset and crowdsourcing recipe have aged far better than its 2015 model scores — and the corpus is now as famous for what it taught the field about benchmark validity as for the benchmark itself.

The defining issue, discovered three years later, is annotation artifacts. Because workers generated hypotheses under a fixed protocol, they left systematic, label-correlated lexical traces: negation and "sleeping/nobody"-type words cue contradiction; vague superordinate words ("outdoors," "animal," "person") cue entailment; specific numbers and modifiers cue neutral. Gururangan et al. (2018) and Poliak et al. (2018) showed that a classifier given only the hypothesis, with the premise hidden, scores around two-thirds of SNLI test pairs correctly — roughly double the ~34% you would get from the majority class. A large fraction of the benchmark, in other words, is solvable without doing inference at all. The 2015 paper brushes against the same vulnerability without naming it: with just 100 training examples a cross-bigram classifier already beats the unlexicalized one, and its top-weighted features are shallow lexical shortcuts the authors call high-precision entailments (premise playing → hypothesis outside; a banana → person eating). Those are cross-premise/hypothesis features, not the premise-blind cues Gururangan and Poliak later isolated — but they point at the same fact: much of the task is solvable from surface lexical correlation rather than reasoning. The broader lesson — crowd-elicited labels can quietly encode the elicitation protocol — reshaped how benchmarks are built and audited (hypothesis-only controls, adversarial filtering, contrast sets).

Two narrower reads. First, deep learning did not win here: the headline is that neural NLI became viable, but the LSTM (77.6%) did not beat the simple lexicalized classifier (78.2%), whose near-perfect 99.7% train accuracy also shows it was largely memorizing. The story was scale, not architecture. Second, construct validity is narrow: premises are Flickr30k photo captions, so "contradiction" means "false of the same pictured scene," not logical contradiction, and temporal, modal, quantifier, and abstract reasoning are mostly out of scope. The 46.7% zero-shot transfer to SICK makes the domain gap concrete — which is precisely why MultiNLI (Williams et al., 2018) followed with multi-genre text.

baselines

Reasonable for 2015 but uneven: the Excitement Open Platform RTE systems were run only in 2-class mode and were never built for SNLI's scale, so Table 4 mainly shows task difficulty (the 2-class most-frequent baseline is 66%) rather than a fair head-to-head. The strongest non-neural comparison is the authors' own lexicalized classifier — which the LSTM ties (77.6 vs 78.2) but does not beat.

contamination

Premise splits are clean — each Flickr caption sits in only one of train/dev/test, and every dev/test pair is validated. But all hypotheses come from one pool of ~2,500 workers, so the artifact cues (and worker style) are shared across splits; part of test accuracy reflects fitting the elicitation method, not inference.

what's missed

Aggregate accuracy hides that ~two-thirds of test pairs are solvable from the hypothesis alone (Gururangan et al., 2018; Poliak et al., 2018); that "contradiction" is photo-grounded rather than logical; that neutral is the noisiest class (κ 0.60); and that the domain is a narrow slice of English (46.7% transfer to SICK).

would raise confidence

A hypothesis-only control reported next to the headline number; harder, artifact-filtered test splits; explicit cross-domain evaluation (the gap MultiNLI later filled); and per-class, per-phenomenon error analysis instead of a single accuracy figure.

// bottom line

SNLI's lasting contribution is the corpus and the image-caption elicitation recipe, not its model leaderboard. It made supervised neural NLI trainable and seeded a decade of entailment benchmarks — and then became the textbook case that scale plus crowdsourcing can manufacture a benchmark that strong models partly game through annotation artifacts. Both halves of that legacy are load-bearing.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. SNLI turned NLI from a boutique task into a deep-learning benchmark: 570,152 human-written, photo-caption-grounded sentence pairs — two orders of magnitude bigger than anything before — with a balanced-by-construction 3-class scheme and a validated dev/test split (Fleiss κ 0.70).
  2. The image-captioning elicitation was both the clever part and the flaw. Fixing the "world" to a described photo tamed coreference and let workers produce fluent, diverse hypotheses fast — but the fixed protocol stamped systematic lexical artifacts into the hypotheses, later shown to let premise-blind models score about two-thirds.
  3. On SNLI itself, a plain lexicalized classifier (78.2%) tied the best neural model (LSTM 77.6%), and SNLI-pretrained representations transferred to SICK (80.8% vs 71.3%). The 2015 story was that scale made neural NLI viable and transferable — not that deep learning had won.
