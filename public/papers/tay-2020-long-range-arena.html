Long Range Arena: A Benchmark for Efficient Transformers

A wave of "efficient Transformers" each claimed near-vanilla quality at sub-quadratic cost — but every paper measured itself on a different task. LRA is the fix: six tasks spanning 1K–16K tokens, one fixed no-pretraining protocol, ten architectures, side by side.

Yi Tay, Mostafa Dehghani, Samira Abnar, Yikang Shen, Dara Bahri, Philip Pham, Jinfeng Rao, Liu Yang, Sebastian Ruder, Donald Metzler — Google Research / DeepMind

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

Self-attention is quadratic in sequence length, so a Transformer that comfortably handles 512 tokens chokes on 16K. By late 2020 a "dizzying number" of efficient Transformers — the authors call them xformers — had been proposed to break that quadratic wall: Reformer, Linformer, Performer, Longformer, BigBird, Sparse Transformers, Sinkhorn, Synthesizer, Linear Transformers, and more. Almost every one claimed quality "comparable or superior" to vanilla attention at a fraction of the memory.

The problem was that none of these claims were comparable to each other. The authors lay out three specific failures of the literature. First, there was no agreed test bed — every model was evaluated on a different set of tasks and datasets, so relative strengths and weaknesses were invisible. Second, the benchmarks that were used were chosen arbitrarily, often without asking whether the task actually requires long-range reasoning at all. Third, papers routinely conflated the architecture's inductive bias with the benefits of pretraining — and since pretraining is expensive, that conflation both obscured the true value of the architecture and locked the subfield out for researchers without industrial compute.

LRA's goal is narrow and deliberate: a single, lightweight, capability-probing benchmark that isolates how well an architecture models long context, from scratch — no pretraining, no per-task data augmentation, no cross-attention escape hatches — so that ten architectures can finally be ranked on the same axis.

// how it works

The method

LRA (pronounced "el-ra") is a suite of six tasks whose inputs run from 1K to 16K tokens and deliberately span modalities — natural text, synthetic and natural images rendered as pixel sequences, and mathematical expressions — to probe similarity, hierarchical, and visual-spatial reasoning. The design follows six stated desiderata: generality (every task is encoding-only, since not all xformers can decode autoregressively), simplicity (no data augmentation, pretraining explicitly out of scope), difficulty, long inputs, probing diverse aspects, and accessibility (lightweight enough to run without industry-grade compute).

The six tasks

  • ListOps (≤2K). Hierarchically nested operators (MAX, MEAN, MEDIAN, SUM_MOD) over digits in brackets; a 10-way classification of the result. Tests parsing of long, hierarchically structured sequences — the model must attend to all tokens to resolve the nesting.
  • Byte-level text classification (4K). IMDb sentiment, but fed as raw characters rather than word tokens, which lengthens the sequence and forces the model to compose characters → words → phrases. Binary accuracy.
  • Byte-level document retrieval (8K). Two ACL Anthology Network documents (4K bytes each, 8K total) are encoded separately in a "two-tower" setup and matched — cross-attention is deliberately forbidden, so the test is whether a model can compress a long document into a representation good enough for similarity scoring. Binary.
  • Image classification (1K). CIFAR-10 images flattened to a 1D sequence of 1024 grayscale pixels (8-bit, vocabulary 256). The model must recover 2D spatial structure from a 1D stream, with no convolutional stem allowed. 10-way.
  • Pathfinder (1K). A 32×32 image (1024 pixels) of dashed curves and two circles; decide whether the circles are joined by a path. A classic long-range spatial-dependency probe. Binary.
  • Path-X (16K). The same task scaled to 128×128 images = 16K-token sequences. An aspirational stress test of extreme length.

Required attention span

To justify that these tasks really do demand long-range reasoning, the authors define a diagnostic called required attention span. For a trained attention model, it is the mean distance between a query token and the tokens it attends to, weighted by the attention weights, then averaged over all attention modules and over 1K random validation samples:

span = meani Σj aij · |i − j| // aij = attention weight from query i to key j; large span ⇒ the task forces attention far down the sequence

Measured on the best vanilla Transformer, the spans are large across the board (Figure 2): Retrieval demands the longest reach (≈1.3K), followed by ListOps (≈0.8K), Pathfinder (≈0.55K), then Image and Text. In other words, these aren't tasks a model can solve by peeking at nearby context — they genuinely exercise the property xformers claim to provide.

All ten xformers plus a vanilla Transformer baseline are trained from scratch under a shared, fixed hyperparameter configuration (the authors note they implemented each model "to the best of our abilities" and often consulted the original developers). Everything is written in JAX/FLAX; efficiency is benchmarked at batch size 32 on 4×4 TPU v3 chips.

// what they found

The results

The headline is "no one-size-fits-all." No architecture wins everywhere, and — pointedly — the vanilla Transformer remains a very strong baseline, landing second overall on average score despite being the thing every xformer was built to replace. On the five-task average (Path-X excluded, see below), BigBird leads at 55.01 and the vanilla Transformer is right behind at 54.39; most of the rest cluster between 50.5 and 53.5, with Local Attention trailing the pack at 46.06.

But the average hides the real story, which is that per-task champions are all different models. Reformer wins ListOps (37.27); the Linear Transformer wins byte-level Text (65.90); the Sparse Transformer wins both Retrieval (59.59) and Image (44.24); and the Performer wins Pathfinder (77.05). The hierarchical ListOps task is especially discriminating: kernel-based models (Performer 18.01, Linear Transformer 16.13) and Local Attention (15.82) collapse to barely above the 10-way random baseline, suggesting their efficiency-driven inductive biases struggle with hierarchical structure, while roughly half the field clears 30% (the fixed-sparse Sparse Transformer is a notable exception, scoring just 17.07).

Every model fails Path-X. At 16K tokens — the same Pathfinder task, just longer — no model scores above chance (≈50% on a binary task), so the paper marks them all FAIL and excludes Path-X from the average entirely, "as it has no impact on relative performance." That a sequence-length increase alone flips a solvable task into an unlearnable one is one of the paper's most cited observations.

The efficiency frontier

On the efficiency side (measured on the byte-level text task at lengths 1K–4K, batch 32, TPU v3), the low-rank and kernel-based models dominate. The Performer is fastest at 5.7× the vanilla Transformer's throughput at 4K (8.0 vs. 1.4 steps/sec), with the Linear Transformer (5.6×) and Linformer (5.5×) close behind. On memory, the Linformer is smallest at 0.99 GB per TPU device versus 9.48 GB for vanilla attention at 4K — roughly a 10× reduction. The slowest model is the Reformer, at about 0.8× vanilla speed at 4K and half its speed at 1K.

Putting quality and speed on the same plot (Figure 3) makes the trade-off explicit: the high-quality cluster (BigBird, vanilla Transformer, Synthesizer) sits slow on the left, while the fast, memory-light cluster (Performer, Linformer, Linear Transformer) earns ~5× speed and ~10× memory savings at the cost of a few points of LRA score. The authors conclude the kernel-based variants make the best overall speed–quality trade-off — but there is no point in the top-right corner. No model is both best and fastest.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

LRA's enduring value is the protocol, not the leaderboard — and the authors are unusually candid about that. They state plainly that all models share a single fixed hyperparameter configuration, that "the best performance and relative order of the models may change if we aggressively tune," and that the paper is "not meant to be a final authoritative document on which xformer is the best." Read with that caveat, several of the rankings are within noise: Sinkhorn (51.39), Linformer (51.36), and Sparse Transformer (51.24) are separated by less than two-tenths of a point on a five-task average whose per-task standard deviations run from 1.8 (Image) to 9.7 (ListOps), and no error bars over seeds are reported. The single "Avg" column also compresses five very different tasks into one number, the same averaging move critiqued by "AI and the Everything in the Whole Wide World Benchmark" — a paper LRA's own readers should hold it to.

The deeper limitation is the no-pretraining design. Decoupling architecture from pretraining is exactly what makes LRA a clean inductive-bias probe, but it also means the benchmark measures these models in a regime almost no one deploys them in. A model that is weak from scratch may shine after pretraining (and vice versa), so an LRA rank is not a downstream-quality rank. The Image task underlines the risk: the authors note models "overfit the training set" with a large train→test gap, meaning some scores reflect regularization and optimization luck as much as long-range modeling. And four of the six tasks (ListOps, pixel Image, Pathfinder, Path-X) are synthetic probes; only Text and Retrieval touch real data, and both sit in a low-signal regime — Retrieval averages 55% and Image 41%, close enough to chance for several models that it is hard to separate genuine modeling from noise. Path-X is the extreme case: a task on which every model scores at chance yields zero ranking signal, which is why it is excluded from the average and kept only as an aspirational target.

Finally, the efficiency numbers are hardware- and implementation-bound. They come from the authors' JAX/FLAX implementations on 4×4 TPU v3 at batch 32, and the paper warns results are "largely conditioned on hardware and implementation details." Speed and memory rankings need not transfer to GPUs or to optimized kernels — and within two years FlashAttention (which cites LRA) showed that exact attention can be made IO-efficient and fast, quietly undercutting the premise that approximate attention is necessary at all and shifting the very frontier LRA was built to chart.

baselines

Strong: ten xformers plus vanilla Transformer, all trained from scratch under one shared config. But no per-model tuning — by the authors' own admission the order can shift with aggressive tuning, so treat sub-point gaps as ties.

contamination

Low risk: tasks are synthetic or use standard splits (IMDb, ACL Anthology, CIFAR-10) with no pretraining corpus to leak. The real confound is optimization/regularization — the large Image train→test gap — not data contamination.

what's missed

From-scratch ≠ pretrained deployment; 4 of 6 tasks are synthetic; low absolute scores (Retrieval 55%, Image 41%) compress signal; Path-X is non-discriminative; efficiency is tied to TPU/JAX and predates IO-aware exact attention.

would raise confidence

Per-model tuning budgets and seed error bars; a pretraining track alongside from-scratch; GPU timings; more real long-document tasks; difficulty recalibrated so a task like Path-X yields signal instead of uniform failure.

// bottom line

LRA gave the efficient-Transformer field its first apples-to-apples test bed and a durable verdict: no architecture wins on both quality and speed, vanilla attention is a stubbornly strong baseline, and kernel/low-rank methods buy ~5× speed and ~10× memory for a few points of accuracy. Read it as a controlled architecture probe, not a downstream leaderboard — the fixed-config, no-pretraining protocol that makes it fair is exactly what limits how far its rankings generalize.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. One protocol, six tasks, 1K–16K tokens. LRA standardized how "efficient Transformers" get compared — ListOps, byte-level Text, byte-level Retrieval, pixel Image, Pathfinder, and Path-X — all trained from scratch with no pretraining, so the score reflects the architecture's inductive bias, not its data.
  2. No free lunch. Across ten xformers, BigBird (55.01) barely edges vanilla attention (54.39), per-task winners are all different models, and the fast kernel/low-rank models (Performer, Linformer, Linear Transformer) trade ~3 LRA points for ~5× speed and ~10× less memory. The top-right "fast and accurate" corner is empty.
  3. Extreme length is the open frontier — and the rankings are provisional. Every model fails Path-X (16K) at chance, and the authors stress that with tuning or pretraining the order could change. LRA is a fair probe of long-range inductive bias, not the last word on which efficient Transformer is "best."
