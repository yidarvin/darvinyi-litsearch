MultiLoKo: a multilingual local knowledge benchmark for LLMs spanning 31 languages

A 31-language benchmark of locally-sourced Wikipedia knowledge — made fully parallel with both human and machine translations — that separates a model's language proficiency from its knowledge, and shows that translate-from-English benchmarks quietly distort which languages look hard.

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

As LLMs ship worldwide, evaluating them in languages other than English matters more and more — yet the authors argue the current state of multilingual evaluation is, bluntly, insufficient. The benchmarks in common use fall into two flawed families.

The first are older multilingual NLU sets — PAWS-X, XNLI, XCOPA. They were built for classification, fit awkwardly onto autoregressive models, cover relatively few languages, and are rarely run on modern systems. The second, and now dominant, family are English benchmarks translated into many languages: MGSM (translated GSM8K), MMMLU (translated MMLU), Belebele. These get wide language coverage cheaply, but their content is English in origin. Translated data is known to differ systematically from native text, and translating a benchmark bakes in an English-centric bias: the result is multilingual on the surface, not in content.

A few datasets source questions separately per language (MLQA, TyDiQA), but none deliberately selects content that is locally relevant to each language, and their coverage is small. EXAMS is locally sourced per language but — for reasons the authors find puzzling — was essentially never adopted for a prominent model release. So two things are missing at once: a wide-coverage benchmark whose questions probe knowledge that is locally salient to each language, and a controlled way to measure how much the translate-from-English shortcut distorts the conclusions we draw about which languages and models are good.

// how it works

The method

MultiLoKo is built around a deliberately local sourcing protocol, modelled on SQuAD's Wikipedia-and-questions recipe but re-run independently for each of 31 languages (30 plus English). For every language the authors take the ~6K most-visited Wikipedia pages over 2016–2021, sample ~3,000-character paragraphs, and have annotators rate each topic's local relevance on a 1–5 scale — 1 being specific to the language (a Swedish singer unknown abroad), 5 being globally famous (“YouTube”). Everything scoring above 3 is discarded, keeping only locally-salient topics.

Native speakers then write closed-form questions with a single short answer (500 per language). Two further annotator passes prune the set: a separate reviewer checks each question against the guidelines, and two new annotators independently answer it — anything either annotator gets “wrong” or finds ambiguous is dropped, with only semantically-equivalent answer variants added (“four” for “4”). Finally, every item is made fully parallel: all non-English questions are translated to English and all English questions into the other 30 languages, by both human translators and Google Translate. That yields a locally-sourced main partition of 15,500 items plus four translated partitions (human / machine × to / from English).

Each partition is split into a public dev set and a blind, out-of-distribution test set (250 items/language each) — split not randomly but by page-visit frequency, so the most-visited topics sit in dev and the long tail in test. Test scores are obtainable only through an external submission service, to resist overfitting and inadvertent contamination. Eleven models marketed as multilingual are evaluated (4 base: Llama 3.1 70B/405B, Mixtral 8×22B, Qwen2.5 72B; 7 chat including Gemini 2.0 Flash, GPT-4o, Claude 3.5 Sonnet and the chat variants) at temperature 0, base models 5-shot and chat models 0-shot, each with language-specific prompts.

Four metrics, two of them parallel-data tricks

Scoring is Exact Match (EM): the fraction of answers that verbatim match a gold answer after light post-processing. Gap — the difference between a model's best and worst language — measures parity, and is only informative read alongside average EM (a small gap can mean uniform excellence or uniform failure). The 2×2 parallel design then enables two diagnostic metrics. The Mother Tongue Effect (MTE) isolates knowledge transfer by asking the same question in its native language versus in English:

MTE(ℓ) = EMlocal(ℓ asked in ℓ) − EM(same questions asked in English) // positive ⇒ the knowledge is more accessible in the native language than in English

The Locality Effect (LE) measures the cost of the translate-from-English shortcut — how different a language looks when probed with locally-sourced questions versus English questions translated into it:

LE(ℓ) = EM(locally-sourced ℓ) − EM(English-sourced, translated into ℓ) // positive ⇒ a translated benchmark would underestimate this language (sign per Table 1 / Fig 5)

// what they found

The results

The first finding is sobering: none of the eleven models is good at this. The best average EM barely tops 30 — Gemini 2.0 Flash 34.4, Llama 3.1 405B 34.3, GPT-4o 34.0 — and even the easiest languages cap below 50 EM. Bottom of the table, base Qwen2.5 72B falls to 19.7 and the chat models collapse further: Mixtral 8×22B-it 10.1, Qwen2.5 72B instruct 2.5. Strikingly, base models generally beat their own chat versions, largely because the chat models false-refuse or break the answer format. Parity is poor across the board: the best-to-worst-language Gap runs from 34.8 (Gemini) to 48.8 (GPT-4o) over the full 31 languages, and even restricting to the five best non-English languages, top models already lose more than five points relative to English.

Knowledge doesn't transfer between languages

For most languages the Mother Tongue Effect is positive — a question is answered better in the language it is locally relevant to than in English — which means models cannot move that local knowledge into English. Most of the languages with negative or near-zero MTE (Bengali, Khmer, Urdu, Farsi) are floored in both settings, so there is no room to drop; Hindi is the called-out exception — it has reasonable native performance yet still answers better in English. A blunter transfer probe, consistency (the share of “correct-in-either-language” questions that are correct in both), confirms it: even the best models sit below 50% overlap — an EM of ~30 in two languages does not mean they got the same questions right. (Claude 3.5 Sonnet could not be coaxed into a parsable English answer — its English EM was an artefactual 4.8 — inflating its MTE to 24.18; it is excluded from all transfer analyses.)

Local knowledge ≠ translated knowledge — the meta-finding

The paper runs two distinct comparisons here. The first separates a model's proficiency in a language from the difficulty of its questions: take a language's own locally-sourced questions and ask them in English instead. Difficulty rankings correlate only 0.79 between the two, and a handful of languages move dramatically — Bengali leaps from the 26th-hardest to the 4th-easiest, Urdu 26→12, Hindi 14→5 — meaning the model plainly has the knowledge but lacks proficiency in the language; Russian moves the other way (7→21).

The second, more practical comparison is what happens when you build a benchmark the cheap way — write questions in English and translate them into each language. The Locality Effect (locally-sourced minus translated-from-English) shows this can move a single language's score by up to 22 points (GPT-4o ranges from −22 to +14; Gemini −21 to +15; Llama 3.1 405B −13 to +17), roughly 15 points on average, and reshuffles which languages look hard (difficulty rank correlation 0.78). Yet model rankings barely change — so translated data is fine for comparing models, but misleading for deciding which languages to invest in.

Machine translation adds its own distortion

Using Google-Translate instead of human translations systematically lowers every model's score — not a single model has a negative average drop — by between 2% and 34% of the human-translated score, and shifts some languages' apparent difficulty by up to 12 points. Yet, as with locality, the effect on model ranking is minimal (rank correlation R = 0.97, only local swaps among statistically-tied models). The split itself behaves as designed: the frequency-based test set is genuinely harder than dev (average EM 21 vs 24), implying it holds more tail knowledge — and hinting that Wikipedia is not the primary place models learned this from.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

MultiLoKo's construction is the strong part — locality filtering, multi-pass human QC, a held-out frequency-split test set, and a genuinely useful 2×2 parallel design. The weak part is what the headline EM numbers can actually bear, because they fold together at least three things the benchmark cannot cleanly separate: a model's knowledge, its language proficiency, and its instruction-following.

The most consequential confound is instruction-following. The eye-catching lows — Qwen2.5 72B instruct at 2.54, Claude 3.5 Sonnet's English EM of 4.8, base-beating-chat across the board — are partly artefacts of format adherence under a brittle exact-match parser rather than measurements of knowledge: the authors call base-beating-chat “partly” a matter of false refusals and poor instruction-following, and note the chat models sometimes simply give a qualitatively different answer. The authors are candid about this: they discovered a post-processing bug that mis-scored Urdu for GPT-4o and Gemini, had to special-case English and Japanese, and concede that evaluating “chatty” LLMs independently of their instruction-following remains unsolved. So the absolute scores — and especially the chat-model floors — read better as lower bounds than as a multilingual leaderboard.

The meta-findings are the more durable contribution, but they too come with caveats: the “up to 22 points” locality swing is a per-language extreme, while the average Locality Effect across languages is near zero because positive and negative effects cancel — which is exactly why “rankings unchanged” and “scary 22-point swing” can both be true at once. The LE/MTE headline numbers are also computed on the dev split only, and on an uneven model set (Claude dropped). And uncontrolled question difficulty across languages is a real confound — the authors concede Tagalog likely lands as the 2nd-easiest language simply because its annotators wrote easier questions.

baselines

A reasonable early-2025 slate (frontier closed + open base/chat), but already dated and missing reasoning-tuned models. More importantly the base-vs-chat comparison is confounded by instruction-following, so the rank order partly measures format adherence, not multilingual knowledge.

contamination

Thoughtfully mitigated — a blind, frequency-split OOD test set held by an external provider, and locality filtering that avoids globally-famous topics. But every item is from Wikipedia, which sits in all of these models' pretraining; the dev set is public; and the OOD split only shifts toward less-visited pages (dev 24 vs test 21 EM is a modest gap), so leakage of popular-page knowledge is plausible.

what's missed

EM cannot enumerate every correct surface form (the authors flag false negatives, tried BLEU/ChrF/“contains” and judges, and kept EM as the “least biased” option). Difficulty conflates knowledge, proficiency and transfer; the English-translation probe that separates proficiency from data difficulty itself “conflates data difficulty and transfer.” LE's sign convention is even stated in opposite directions in §3.3 vs Table 1/Figure 5.

would raise confidence

Report the headline LE/MTE on the held-out test split, not just dev; control or calibrate question difficulty across languages (the answerer-agreement data already collected could help); add a judged, non-EM metric to de-confound instruction-following; widen the model set to current frontier + reasoning models; and directly quantify Wikipedia-overlap leakage.

// bottom line

MultiLoKo is one of the cleaner attempts to make “multilingual” mean multilingual in content, not just on the surface, and its central caution — that a translate-from-English benchmark can move a single language's apparent score by 20+ points while leaving model rankings intact — is genuinely useful for anyone leaning on MMMLU/MGSM-style evals. Treat it as a sharp diagnostic of where translated evals mislead and where knowledge fails to transfer, not as a final multilingual scoreboard: the absolute numbers are softened by EM brittleness and instruction-following confounds, and the strongest claims currently live on the dev split.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. “Multilingual” benchmarks built by translating English are multilingual on the surface only. MultiLoKo instead sources 500 locally-relevant Wikipedia questions per language for 31 languages, then makes everything parallel via human and machine translation so the translate-from-English shortcut can be measured directly.
  2. No frontier model is evenly multilingual: best average EM barely clears 30, best-to-worst-language gaps run 35–49 points, and even top models share under 50% of their correct answers across languages — local knowledge does not transfer.
  3. Swapping locally-sourced for English-translated questions can shift one language's score by 20+ points and reshuffle which languages look hard (rank corr ≈0.78), yet leaves model rankings intact — so translated evals are acceptable for ranking models but misleading for language prioritization, and machine translation lowers every model's score on top of that.
