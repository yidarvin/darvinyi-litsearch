BrowseComp: A Simple Yet Challenging Benchmark for Browsing Agents

OpenAI's browsing benchmark — 1,266 short-answer questions engineered to be trivial to verify but brutally hard to find. Plain chatbots score near zero; an early Deep Research agent solves 51.5%, scaling smoothly with test-time compute.

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

For most of the last decade, "can a model find information on the web?" was measured by benchmarks like TriviaQA, HotpotQA, FEVER, Wizard of Wikipedia, ELI5, and GAIA. They share a property the authors flag explicitly: they focus on information that a person could find quickly — a single hop or two of search — and recent language models have largely saturated them. As AI moves from chatbots to reasoners to genuine browsing agents (OpenAI Deep Research and Operator, Gemini Deep Research, Perplexity, Grok), there was no hard, reliable yardstick for the capability those agents are actually built for: persistently hunting down obscure facts.

The framing is almost philosophical. Human web navigation is "clunky" for three reasons — our memory and world knowledge are limited, we get distracted and fatigued, and we cannot parallelize attention. A sufficiently capable machine should overcome all three: it should, in principle, retrieve any well-specified piece of information from the open web, even if doing so requires browsing thousands of pages. BrowseComp is designed to probe exactly that frontier.

The hard part of building such a benchmark is the tension between difficulty and ease of grading. Genuinely hard information-seeking usually produces long, messy answers that are a nightmare to score; easy-to-grade short answers are usually easy to find. BrowseComp threads the needle with one idea — make the answer a short string that is trivial to verify but extremely hard to locate — and then turns the difficulty up until even the people who wrote the questions mostly can't solve them.

// how it works

The method

BrowseComp was collected purely by human trainers, following the instructions of SimpleQA (Wei et al., 2024): fact-seeking questions with single, indisputable, short answers that don't change over time and are supported by evidence. The signature move is the inverted question. A trainer starts from a "seed" — a person, event, or artifact — gathers several of its characteristics that each span a large search space, and composes a question from their conjunction. The canonical example the authors gave trainers:

// the inverted-question recipe

"What's the title of the scientific paper published in the EMNLP conference between 2018–2023 where the first author did their undergrad at Dartmouth College and the fourth author did their undergrad at University of Pennsylvania?" (Answer: Frequency Effects on Syntactic Rule Learning in Transformers.) A few searches verify it instantly — but a brute-force search would mean examining thousands of papers and checking each author's background.

To force difficulty, trainers had to pass three checks: (1) the question had to stump GPT-4o (with and without browsing), OpenAI o1, and an early version of the Deep Research model; (2) five plain Google searches must not surface the answer on any first page of results; and (3) another person should not be able to solve it within ten minutes. The last was soft, but tasks a second trainer cracked more than 40% of the time were sent back for revision.

There is one honest limitation baked into the construction: answer uniqueness is "likely but not guaranteed." Because you can verify the reference answer is correct but can't cheaply prove no other answer fits, the authors lean on trainers' domain familiarity (e.g., "Dartmouth is a small school"), ask them to add constraints when unsure, and discard a question if a second trainer finds a different valid answer within ten minutes.

The final set is 1,266 questions (down from an original 1,287 after 21 were removed for bad ground truth). Topics were assigned post-hoc by a prompted ChatGPT and span ten categories, deliberately reflecting trainers' personal interests for higher-quality data.

Grading is deliberately simple: because every reference answer is a single short string, an AI model judges whether a predicted answer is semantically equivalent to the reference, using the same grader prompt as Humanity's Last Exam (Phan et al., 2025). Models are also asked to emit a confidence score from 0–100% alongside each answer, which enables the calibration and aggregation analyses below.

a* = arg max i∈{1…N} conf(ai) // best-of-N: with N samples per question, keep the answer the model is most confident in — the aggregation strategy that worked best

To resist training-set contamination, BrowseComp ships a canary string (browsecomp:26b5c67b-…) so the data can be filtered out of training corpora, and the authors ask researchers not to post examples online in plain text or images.

// what they found

The results

The headline is the gap between models that merely have web access and the one agent trained to browse. GPT-4o (0.6%) and GPT-4.5 (0.9%) are essentially at zero. Bolting browsing onto GPT-4o barely moves it — to 1.9% — which is the paper's sharpest point: tool access alone is not the capability. OpenAI o1, which can't browse at all but reasons well, reaches 9.9%, showing some answers can be surfaced from internal knowledge. Only OpenAI Deep Research — an agent explicitly trained for persistent browsing — clears the bar, solving 51.5% by autonomously searching, synthesizing across sources, and pivoting its strategy.

Because BrowseComp questions require trawling many websites, performance behaves like an agentic scaling law: accuracy climbs smoothly as you spend more test-time compute (browsing effort), sweeping an early Deep Research from roughly 9% to 52% as the budget grows.

The second compute lever is sampling more answers and voting. Generating 64 outputs per question and aggregating lifts accuracy by 15–25% over a single attempt. Best-of-N — keep the single highest-confidence sample — wins, reaching about 78%. The interpretation matters: even though the model's confidence is poorly calibrated in an absolute sense, best-of-N working so well means the model "frequently knows when it's right." Verifying is genuinely easier than finding — exactly the asymmetry BrowseComp is built on.

One reliability wrinkle cuts the other way: browsing makes models more overconfident. The agents with web access show the worst calibration error — Deep Research at 91% and GPT-4o-with-browsing at 82%, versus 65% for o1 — so access to tools appears to inflate confidence in wrong answers.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

BrowseComp is a genuinely clever benchmark, and its difficulty is real — but the headline table invites a misread. The footnote on Deep Research is doing a lot of work: the model "is trained on data that specifically teach the model to be good at BrowseComp tasks." So the 51.5%-vs-9.9% chasm between Deep Research and o1 is not a clean capability comparison; part of it is the gap between a model trained on this distribution and one that wasn't. That's fine as a demonstration that browsing agents can be built, but it undercuts using these exact numbers to rank "reasoning vs browsing."

The comparison set is also entirely in-house. Every model evaluated is OpenAI's own, even though the introduction names Gemini, Perplexity, Grok, and Claude as the target class. There's no cross-lab leaderboard here, so the paper establishes a benchmark and a single vendor's place on it, not the field's.

On grading, the "likely but not guaranteed" uniqueness of answers is a soft spot the authors are honest about. An LLM grader checks equivalence to a single reference string, so a genuinely correct alternative answer would be scored wrong. The strongest evidence this isn't hypothetical is internal: of the 118 tasks where Deep Research scored 0%, the authors found 21 with mismatched, ambiguous, or incorrect ground truth and removed them. That's a meaningful label-error rate surfaced only by auditing the hardest tail — the rest of the dataset wasn't audited the same way.

Finally, contamination defense is social, not enforced — a canary string plus a request not to post examples. Since answers are short, web-findable strings, any leaked question is trivially gameable. And by the authors' own framing this is "an incomplete but useful benchmark," a CodeForces-style proxy that deliberately sidesteps the real user-query distribution (long answers, ambiguity). The pass-rate distribution below reinforces that it measures a narrow, heterogeneous slice of "find the hard fact."

baselines

OpenAI-only (GPT-4o, GPT-4.5, o1, Deep Research); no Gemini / Claude / Perplexity / Grok despite being the named target class. Deep Research is trained on BrowseComp-like data, so its lead is not an apples-to-apples capability gap.

contamination

Defended by a canary string and a "please don't post examples" request — a social, unenforced safeguard. Short, verifiable answers make any leaked item trivially gameable.

what's missed

Single-reference LLM grading can mark valid alternative answers wrong (21/1,287 bad labels found in just the zero-pass tail). Calibration is poor and worsens with browsing (91% error for Deep Research). By design it ignores long answers and query ambiguity.

would raise confidence

External, non-OpenAI agents on the same eval; an independent audit of answer uniqueness across the full set; a Deep-Research run without task-specific training; and reporting calibration next to accuracy on any leaderboard.

// bottom line

BrowseComp nails the design problem — short, cheap-to-grade answers that are agonizing to find — and produces a benchmark that is hard for frontier agents and for the humans who wrote it. Read it as a clean measure of persistence and creative search on hard-to-find facts, not as a vendor-neutral ranking or a proxy for general browsing usefulness, and mind that its toughest data point (Deep Research) had a home-field training advantage.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. The inverted-question trick — start from a fact, build a question whose answer is easy to verify but hard to find — yields a benchmark that is simultaneously brutally hard (humans gave up on 70.8% after two hours) and dirt-cheap to grade via short-string equivalence.
  2. Browsing ≠ reasoning, and you need both. GPT-4o with browsing manages just 1.9%; non-browsing o1 reaches 9.9%; only Deep Research, which reasons and browses, hits 51.5% — and accuracy scales smoothly with test-time compute, climbing to ~78% with best-of-N over 64 samples.
  3. Treat it as a useful but narrow proxy: OpenAI-only baselines, a training-data advantage for the leader, social-only contamination defense, and worsening calibration with browsing (91% error) all bound what the score actually tells you.
