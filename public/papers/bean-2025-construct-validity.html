Measuring what Matters: Construct Validity in Large Language Model Benchmarks

Twenty-nine expert reviewers code 445 LLM benchmarks from ICML, ICLR, NeurIPS, ACL, NAACL, and EMNLP against a construct-validity codebook — finding that barely half justify why their score means what it claims to, and only 16% run any statistical test at all — then distill the findings into an eight-point, actionable checklist.

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

Building an LLM benchmark means turning something abstract — "reasoning," "safety," "alignment" — into a concrete task and a scoring rule you can actually run. Psychometrics has a name and a decades-old theory for whether that translation is any good: construct validity, "the degree to which a benchmark score provides evidence for making claims about the target phenomenon." If a benchmark has high construct validity for "intelligence," a model that scores well really is, in some real sense, more intelligent; if construct validity is low, a high score can be irrelevant or actively misleading. The authors' complaint is that the field builds and publishes benchmarks constantly, but almost never checks this property directly.

The paper is explicit about how it differs from adjacent audits. McIntosh et al.'s critique of 23 benchmarks raises adjacent "functionality and integrity" concerns without construct-validity language or grounding in the psychometric literature, and Reuel et al.'s BetterBench (a 46-criteria audit of 24 benchmarks' design/implementation/documentation/maintenance lifecycle) explicitly scopes construct validity out of its own checklist, writing that assessing it "would presumably require in-depth analysis by domain experts in the respective fields, which is beyond the scope of this assessment." Statistical-rigor gaps (error bars, significance testing) and reproducibility gaps have each gotten their own dedicated papers. What hadn't been done at any real scale was a systematic, expert-coded review of construct validity itself — whether benchmarks define their target phenomenon clearly, sample task items that actually represent it, and score them in a way that supports the claims authors go on to make.

// how it works

The method

The corpus starts from 46,114 articles: every ICML, ICLR, and NeurIPS paper from 2018–2024, plus every ACL, NAACL, and EMNLP paper from 2020–2024. A keyword filter — title or abstract containing "benchmark" and either "LLM" or "language model" — narrows that to 2,189 candidates. Rather than have humans triage all 2,189, the team first ran GPT-4o mini as an automated screener against three of their four inclusion criteria (does the paper concern LLM capabilities? does it introduce an empirical benchmark with reported performance? is it compatible with text/vision models?), validating the model against 50 human-labeled articles and reporting an F1 of 84%. That cut the set to 522 articles, which 29 reviewers — matched to their own areas of expertise — then triaged by hand against all four inclusion criteria (adding: is the benchmark genuinely novel, not a repackaging of prior work?), landing on 445 included benchmark articles.

Each of the 445 was then coded against a construct-validity codebook built around four elements, following Raji et al.'s definition of a benchmark: a phenomenon (the abstract thing being measured, e.g. "multi-step mathematical reasoning"), a task that operationalizes it (e.g. grade-school mathematics word problems), a metric that scores it (e.g. exact-match), and the claims the authors make from the resulting score. The codebook's individual items are drawn from classical validity theory — face, content, ecological, predictive, convergent, discriminant, and criterion validity — applied to each of those four elements. Every article was coded by a primary reviewer; a second reviewer then mapped those answers onto a simplified set of categories for statistics, with the primary reviewer verifying the mapping. To check reliability, 46 of the 445 papers (about 10%) were coded independently by two reviewers, yielding a mean Brennan–Prediger Kappa of .524 across 30 categorical questions.

// what they found

The results

The 445 benchmarks cluster into three phenomenon families — general capabilities (led by natural-language understanding, logical reasoning, and long-context handling), general applications (code generation, summarization, document retrieval), and specific applications (physical science, medicine, law) — and the number of included benchmarks rises sharply every year through 2024, with the share that even discusses construct validity rising alongside it but never catching up to the total (Fig. 2). Reasoning was the single most common target phenomenon (18.5% of benchmarks), followed by alignment (8.1%) and code generation (5.7%).

On definitions: 78.2% of benchmarks defined their target phenomenon at all, but of those definitions, only 52.2% were "widely-agreed" — the rest were contested or had no real consensus definition in the field to begin with. 61.2% described the phenomenon as a composite of sub-elements (e.g. "agentic capability" spanning intent recognition, alignment, and structured output) rather than a single unified construct. On sampling, only 17.1% of benchmarks used random or stratified sampling to build their task set, while 12.3% relied exclusively on convenience sampling (using whatever data was easiest to obtain) and another 27.0% used it partially. 38.2% of benchmarks reused data from an existing benchmark or human exam rather than building fresh material, and just 7.7% sourced their tasks solely from another benchmark.

On metrics, exact-match scoring dominates: used at least partially by 81.3% of benchmarks and exclusively by 40.7%, versus 17.1% using LLM-as-a-judge (partially) and 13.0% using human ratings (partially). Only 16.0% of the 445 benchmarks used any uncertainty estimate or statistical test to compare models at all. On claims, 53.4% of articles present some evidence for their benchmark's construct validity — comparing to other benchmarks of similar phenomena (35.2%), to a human baseline (32.4%), or to a more realistic setting (31.2%) — meaning close to half offer no such justification whatsoever.

The paper grounds all of this in a single worked example: GSM8K, the grade-school-math benchmark. It scores well on several checklist items (fresh, from-scratch dataset; large, justified sample; multi-run statistical comparison) but is marked down for not isolating arithmetic-execution error from reading-comprehension and reasoning error, and for having no formal error-taxonomy analysis of its failure modes — concrete illustrations of how even a widely-trusted, heavily-cited benchmark can fail specific construct-validity checks.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

This is a meta-evaluation paper, so the right question is whether its own measurement process is as rigorous as the one it's asking the field to adopt — and on transparency, it clearly is: a full appendix (D) reports inter-rater agreement broken out field by field, something almost no comparable benchmark-audit paper does. That granularity is exactly what complicates the paper's own headline number. The main text reports a single mean Brennan–Prediger Kappa of .524 across 30 categorical questions — "moderate" on the standard Landis–Koch scale — but Appendix D's Table 9 shows that single mean conceals enormous field-to-field variance. Two of the fields closest to the paper's central thesis are among its least reliable: phenomenon_defined — whether a paper defines its target phenomenon at all, the field behind the headline "78.2% provide a definition" statistic — has a kappa of just 0.073, barely above chance, with raw percent agreement of only 53.7%; and results_author_validity — whether authors present their own construct-validity evidence, the field behind "53.4% present evidence for construct validity" — comes in at 0.305, in the "fair" band, well under the reported mean. In other words, the two specific judgment calls closest to the paper's own thesis are among the least reliably coded items in the entire codebook.

The corpus-construction pipeline is also more precisely documented than the main text alone suggests, and worth reading past the abstract for: Appendix C reports that on the 50-article validation sample, the GPT-4o-mini screening step achieved 80% precision and 89% recall (not just the combined 84% F1 quoted up front) — meaning roughly one in nine genuinely qualifying benchmark papers was likely dropped by the automated filter before any of the 29 human reviewers ever saw it, out of 2,189 original candidates. That's a candid, quantified estimate of exactly the risk a reader would otherwise have to guess at — and the paper reports it only in an appendix, not the abstract or main results.

The corpus scope itself is a second, larger limitation, and one the authors also name directly: restricting the review to ICML, ICLR, NeurIPS, ACL, NAACL, and EMNLP "may systematically exclude certain types of impactful benchmarks... developed and released by industry labs without formal peer review." That's a real gap, and an ironic one given the batch this page ships alongside: McIntosh et al.'s companion critique specifically flags that most heavily-used LLM benchmarks in practice are unrefereed preprints — which is exactly the population Bean et al.'s peer-reviewed-venue filter is built to exclude. A construct-validity audit of the six proceedings above is genuinely valuable, but it is not, and doesn't claim to be, an audit of the benchmarks practitioners reach for most.

Finally, the checklist's real-world payoff is demonstrated on exactly one benchmark (GSM8K) as a worked example, not validated at scale — there's no evidence in the paper that a benchmark scoring well on the checklist actually produces more stable, more replicable, or less-gameable leaderboard results than one scoring poorly. The checklist is plausible and well-grounded in classical validity theory, but its predictive value for real-world benchmark reliability is asserted rather than measured here.

baselines

No model baselines — the comparison point is prior benchmark-audit papers (McIntosh et al.'s 23-benchmark critique, Reuel et al.'s BetterBench), none of which coded construct validity directly at this scale; this is the first to do so with 445 benchmarks and 29 reviewers.

contamination

Not applicable in the model-training sense; the analogous risk is measurement contamination — the reported mean kappa (.524) hides that the two fields driving the paper's own headline statistics (phenomenon_defined, results_author_validity) have kappas of 0.073 and 0.305, respectively (Appendix D, Table 9).

what's missed

The peer-reviewed-venues-only corpus excludes industry-released, never-peer-reviewed benchmarks (e.g. HumanEval) by construction — precisely the segment a companion critique in this batch (McIntosh et al. 2024) argues dominates actual LLM evaluation practice.

would raise confidence

Re-running (or discarding) the lowest-agreement codebook fields rather than folding them into an unweighted mean, and evidence that the checklist's scores predict something observable — e.g. that low-scoring benchmarks show less stable model rankings than high-scoring ones.

// bottom line

The most rigorous, most transparently self-documented construct-validity audit of LLM benchmarks to date — but its own per-field appendix shows the two codebook items closest to its headline claims are among its least reliably coded, and its peer-reviewed-venue filter selectively favors academic benchmarks over the unrefereed, industry-released ones that arguably need the scrutiny most.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. A 29-reviewer systematic review codes 445 LLM benchmarks from ICML, ICLR, NeurIPS, ACL, NAACL, and EMNLP (2018–2024) against a construct-validity codebook — finding that only 53.4% justify their construct validity at all, only 16.0% use any statistical test, and just 17.1% use random or stratified task sampling.
  2. The findings distill into eight actionable recommendations and an operational checklist, worked through end-to-end on GSM8K as a concrete example — showing even a widely-trusted benchmark can pass some construct-validity checks (fresh data, statistical comparisons) while failing others (no error-mode analysis, unresolved reasoning/arithmetic confound).
  3. The audit's own reported mean inter-rater kappa (.524) hides that the two codebook fields feeding its own headline statistics — whether a phenomenon is defined at all, and whether authors justify construct validity — have kappas of just 0.073 and 0.305 (Appendix D); and its six-venue, peer-reviewed-only corpus by design excludes the unrefereed, industry-released benchmarks this batch's companion critique argues dominate actual practice.
