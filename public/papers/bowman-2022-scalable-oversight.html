Measuring Progress on Scalable Oversight for Large Language Models

Scalable oversight — supervising a model that may know more than you do — has mostly been a theory problem. This paper turns Ajeya Cotra's sandwiching proposal into an actual experiment: non-expert humans try to answer hard exam and reading-comprehension questions with help from an unreliable 52B RLHF dialog model, and the human–model team beats both the model alone and the humans' own unaided guesses by wide margins.

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

Scalable oversight — the ability to supervise a system once it starts outperforming its supervisors on the skills relevant to a task — is one of the core stated problems of AI safety, going back to Amodei et al.'s 2016 "Concrete Problems." By 2022 there were several concrete technique proposals for it: debate, market-making, recursive reward modeling, and more. What there wasn't was empirical evidence for any of them, because of an apparently circular obstacle: to test whether an oversight technique works on a model that broadly exceeds human ability, you need a model that broadly exceeds human ability, and none existed. So how do you get empirical traction on a problem defined by a capability regime that hasn't arrived yet?

This paper operationalizes an answer proposed by Ajeya Cotra in 2021 — sandwiching — into an actual measurable experimental design, and then runs a first proof-of-concept version of it with present-day models. The goal isn't to solve scalable oversight; it's to establish that the sandwiching paradigm is usable today, on tasks and models that already exist, in a way that plausibly transfers evidence to the future regime it's meant to address.

// how it works

The method

Sandwiching places a model's capability level between two groups of humans on the same task. Expert evaluators have all the skill or knowledge needed to do the task well — their judgment is the upper-bound reference, used only to grade the outcome. The model also has most of the relevant skill or knowledge (from pretraining) but is not reliably aligned to use it well when asked directly. Non-expert participants understand the task and are well-intentioned, but are missing key knowledge the model has — so their job is to use some oversight technique to reliably extract correct, trustworthy answers from the model, without any help from the experts. If the non-experts can pull this off, that's evidence the technique could, in principle, work on a future model that similarly exceeds its supervisors.

This paper's proof-of-concept experiment applies two relaxations to keep a first study tractable. Static model: participants interact with the model only through dialog — no fine-tuning or inspection — so the question becomes how much a human can extract through conversation alone. Labels in place of experts: rather than convening live expert evaluators, the experiment uses two existing labeled multiple-choice datasets and scores against the ground-truth answer key, which sidesteps the cost of expert panels at the price of a less faithful sandwich.

The tasks and the model

Two question-answering tasks were chosen to satisfy the sandwiching constraints with a present-day model: MMLU (Hendrycks et al., 2020), multiple-choice exam questions drawing on specialized knowledge most people won't have, and time-limited QuALITY (Pang et al., 2022), multiple-choice reading-comprehension questions over ~5,000-word short stories, artificially made hard for humans by imposing a 5-minute time limit that rules out reading the story in full. The model under study was a 52-billion-parameter dialog assistant, fine-tuned to be helpful via the RLHF technique from Bai et al. (2022) — trained for helpfulness only, not helpfulness-and-harmlessness.

Participants and conditions

Two groups of five participants each (10 total) were hired through Surge AI, one group per task, each answering the same 100 randomly-sampled validation-set questions. One outlier participant per task was excluded from the reported results after scoring far above the rest in the unassisted condition (84% vs. 57% average for the other four on MMLU; 89% vs. 49% on QuALITY) and beating the model outright — a deliberate but consequential trim to satisfy the assumption that non-experts lack task-relevant knowledge the model has. Five conditions were compared: Unassisted Human (no model, no outside resources); Model (zero-shot); Model (5-shot) (MMLU only, since QuALITY passages don't fit multiple examples in context); Model (best-of-20 chain-of-thought) (20 sampled reasoning chains, reranked by the RLHF preference model); and Human + Model, the main target condition, where participants freely chat with the model to reach an answer.

// what they found

The results

Human–model teams substantially outperformed both humans alone and the model alone on both tasks. Comparing individual accuracy, the best model conditions beat unassisted humans by a wide margin (66% vs. 57% on MMLU, 67% vs. 49% on QuALITY) — and model-assisted humans then beat the best model conditions by a similarly wide margin (75% vs. 66% on MMLU, 77% vs. 67% on QuALITY). Every participant exceeded 71% individual accuracy in the model-assisted condition on both tasks.

Looking at weighted-majority-vote aggregates across the (four) participants per task, the model outperforms humans substantially only on QuALITY (66% vs. 66% on MMLU is roughly tied; 67% vs. 50% on QuALITY favors the model), while model-assisted human teams beat the best model result by large margins on both (78% vs. 66% on MMLU, 86% vs. 67% on QuALITY). That 86% vs. 50% comparison on QuALITY is a 36-point improvement over the humans' own unassisted weighted-vote performance — the paper's largest headline gain. Model-assisted human performance still falls short of the (task-specific) expert ceiling on both: 78% vs. 90% on MMLU, 86% vs. 94% on QuALITY.

Calibration error (CE, lower is better, computed over five confidence bins) tells a more mixed story: on MMLU, unaided humans and the model alone are reasonably well-calibrated (CE ≤ 10), but human-model teams and chain-of-thought prompting are markedly worse (CE up to 18). On QuALITY, unaided humans calibrate poorly (CE 17) while human-model teams calibrate much better (CE 7). Restricting to the most confident weighted-majority answers (99% confidence) raises accuracy to 88% on QuALITY (N=89) and 81% on MMLU (N=84). Access to the model measurably raised participants' own confidence too — from 54% to 80% average confidence on MMLU, and 38% to 75% on QuALITY. Dialogs averaged 10.9 turns and 2.0 resets on MMLU versus 6.3 turns and 0.1 resets on QuALITY (the time limit discourages resetting). A learning-effect check — comparing accuracy in the first versus second half of each participant's work — found only modest improvement (an average of 0.7%, with just 2 of 4 MMLU and 3 of 4 QuALITY annotators improving), suggesting the gains aren't primarily explained by participants learning the test material from the after-each-question answer reveal.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

This paper is unusually forthright about its own limitations — it dedicates an entire subsection to six reasons its results shouldn't be over-read, which is worth crediting up front. Still, several of those caveats matter enough to shape how much weight the headline numbers should carry.

The two "expert ceiling" rows in Table 1 are not apples-to-apples. QuALITY's 93.5% expert figure is a genuinely measured untimed human-team result reported by Pang et al. (2022); MMLU's 90.0% is explicitly labeled a hypothesized committee-of-experts estimate from Hendrycks et al. — nobody actually measured expert performance on these MMLU questions. Reading both as equivalent "ceilings" overstates how far model-assisted humans (78% on MMLU) still have to close a gap that, on the MMLU side, is partly speculative.

The selection effect from dropping two participants is real and openly acknowledged, but worth dwelling on: excluding the two people who beat the model unassisted (84% and 89% individual accuracy) is necessary to satisfy sandwiching's core assumption, but it also mechanically removes exactly the participants most likely to reveal cases where model assistance fails to help — the paper's own words are that this "makes it harder for us to observe cases where model assistance fails to help our participants at all." The reported uplift is therefore an uplift measured on a population pre-filtered to need the model's help.

The answer-reveal-after-each-question design is a real divergence from the sandwiching protocol it's meant to approximate, and the paper says so directly: revealing the correct answer immediately "removes one of the major points of difficulty in scalable oversight... the need for participants to identify when they have developed a method that is reliable enough to deploy." In a genuine oversight setting, you don't get instant ground truth on the exact question you're trying to oversee — that's the whole problem. This experiment's task is closer to calibrated trial-and-error learning than to oversight of a genuinely unknown answer.

baselines

Model-alone baselines (zero-shot, 5-shot, best-of-20 CoT) are, in the authors' own words, "relatively weak at showing that our model is misaligned" — leaving open how much of the human+model uplift reflects a genuinely weak model-alone baseline vs. genuine elicitation skill. MMLU's expert ceiling (90.0) is a hypothesized estimate, not a measured score, unlike QuALITY's (93.5).

contamination

Not classic train/test leakage, but a related concern: MMLU and QuALITY are pre-existing public benchmarks the model plausibly saw supervised-style signal for during training/RLHF — the paper's own limitation #6 flags that this may not stress-test oversight the way a genuinely novel task would.

what's missed

No adversarial/deceptive-model condition — everything here uses a merely unreliable, not actively misleading, assistant, so it doesn't test the harder oversight regime debate/market-making are meant for. Calibration error for human-model teams on MMLU (CE up to 18) is worse than either humans or the model alone, undercutting the "you can tell when to trust it" promise the technique needs.

would raise confidence

Measured (not hypothesized) expert baselines on both tasks, testing across multiple model scales and assistants, removing the answer-reveal shortcut, and a genuinely adversarial/deceptive model condition rather than a merely unreliable one.

// bottom line

As a first empirical operationalization of sandwiching, this paper does what it sets out to do: it shows the paradigm is runnable today and that a trivial dialog-based technique already produces a large, real uplift over either humans or a present-day model alone. But the specific numbers rest on a pre-filtered participant pool, an answer-reveal shortcut that removes the hardest part of real oversight, and one hypothesized (not measured) expert ceiling — so the paper is best read as "sandwiching is viable and worth building on," not as a validated measurement of how well plain dialog assistance would hold up as an oversight technique for a genuinely superhuman or deceptive model.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. This paper operationalizes Ajeya Cotra's sandwiching proposal — a model's capability placed between non-expert participants and expert evaluators — into a runnable experimental design, then tests it with a proof-of-concept on MMLU and time-limited QuALITY using a 52B RLHF dialog assistant.
  2. Human–model teams substantially beat both unassisted humans and the model alone: weighted-majority-vote accuracy reached 78% on MMLU (vs. 66% for the best model result) and 86% on QuALITY (vs. 67% for the best model result, and a 36-point gain over the humans' own unassisted 50%).
  3. The authors are candid about six limitations that temper the headline: a hypothesized (not measured) MMLU expert ceiling, a selection effect from excluding two high-performing participants, an answer-reveal shortcut that removes real oversight's core difficulty, and no test against a genuinely deceptive (rather than merely unreliable) model.
