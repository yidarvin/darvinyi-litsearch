MedXpertQA: Benchmarking Expert-Level Medical Reasoning and Understanding

4,460 questions built from medical-licensing and specialty-board exams — not auto-generated image captions — open a 51-to-71-point gap against near-saturated prior benchmarks (MMLU Medical, MedQA, MedMCQA), and even OpenAI's o1, the best model overall at 49.89% average, only narrowly clears the paper's own pre-licensed-human baseline of 43.92%.

Yuxin Zuo, Shang Qu, Yifei Li, Zhangren Chen, Xuekai Zhu, Ermo Hua, Kaiyan Zhang, Ning Ding, Bowen Zhou

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

MedQA, MedMCQA, PubMedQA, and the medical slice of MMLU have been the field's default medical benchmarks for years, and they are running out of headroom: o1-preview already scores 96% on MedQA-USMLE and 99% on MMLU Medical Genetics (per Nori et al., 2024, cited in the paper's introduction). A benchmark that every frontier model has effectively solved can't distinguish anything.

The multimodal side has a different problem. VQA-Rad, Path-VQA, Slake, PMC-VQA, and OmniMedVQA are all built by automatically pairing a medical image with a caption-derived question, so they mostly test basic visual perception — "what organ is this?" — rather than the multi-step differential-diagnosis reasoning a physician actually performs, and none of them wraps the image in the patient history, lab values, and exam findings that define a real clinical encounter. MMMU's health-and-medicine slice adds exam-style questions, but it isn't medicine-specific, tops out at university-course difficulty, and still lacks that clinical context.

MedXpertQA's authors argue the field needs a benchmark that is (1) hard enough that current models don't already saturate it, (2) sourced from real medical-licensing and specialty-board exams for both its text and multimodal questions rather than templated auto-generation, and (3) actively defended against the contamination that made MedQA and MMLU easy targets for models trained on scraped web text.

// how it works

The method

MedXpertQA starts from 37,543 candidate questions (Table 1) harvested from USMLE and COMLEX-USA (general medical licensing), 17 American specialty-board exams spanning fields like internal medicine, psychiatry & neurology, surgery, preventive medicine, and family medicine, plus three image-rich sources — the American College of Radiology's DXIT/TXIT exams, the European Board of Radiology's exams, and the New England Journal of Medicine's Image Challenge. That's 26,675 candidates for the Text subset and 10,868 for the multimodal MM subset.

From there a five-stage filtering-and-augmentation funnel does the real work, per Table 1's phase-by-phase counts (the paper's own prose numbers its two difficulty-based steps in the reverse order from the table, so the exact execution sequence is ambiguous in the text — the counts themselves are not). Human Expert Filtering keeps only questions whose aggregated human-response distribution clears an adaptive threshold on the Brier score — a proper scoring rule over how humans actually answered — calibrated to expert-annotated difficulty tiers (max threshold at the 25th percentile of scores, min at the 3rd; roughly 16.78% of candidates lack response data and skip this step), taking the pool to 10,146. AI Expert Filtering runs 8 models (open models in the 7-11B range as "basic" experts — Qwen2.5-7B, InternLM2.5-7B, LLaMA-3.1-8B for Text; Qwen2-VL-7B, InternVL2-8B, LLaMA-3.2-11B-Vision for MM — plus GPT-4o and Claude-3.5-Sonnet as "advanced" experts) four times each per question: anything a basic model always answers correctly is discarded as too easy, anything every expert gets wrong is kept, cutting the pool further to 4,737.

B = 1N Σi=1N (yi − ŷi)² // the Brier score used for Human Expert Filtering — yᵢ is the true label for option i, ŷᵢ is the fraction of human respondents who picked it; lower B = an easier question

Edit-Distance and Semantic-Similarity Filtering come next — the latter uses a MedCPT sentence encoder plus IQR-based outlier detection on pairwise cosine similarity to strip near-duplicate questions that would otherwise let a model exploit template shortcuts — taking the pool to 4,713, then 4,683. Finally, Question and Option Augmentation has GPT-4o-2024-11-20 and Claude-3.5-Sonnet-20241022 rephrase every surviving question and expand its option count — to 10 options for Text, 5 for MM — specifically to raise the guessing floor and reduce the odds a model has simply memorized the original exam item. A final multi-round physician-licensed expert review corrects (rather than just deletes) flagged errors, landing at 2,455 Text and 2,005 MM questions — 2,450 Text and 2,000 MM once each subset's 5-question few-shot dev split is set aside, 4,460 total across 17 specialties and 11 body systems.

The MM subset's images span 10 categories — radiology (X-ray, CT, MRI, PET, ultrasound), pathology, vital signs (ECG, blood pressure), retinal images, photos, diagrams, documents, charts, and tables — and total 2,839 images across the full 2,005-question MM set (2,852 images across just the 2,000-question test split, per Table 2). That yields an image-to-question ratio of 1.43 and an average question length of 149.35 tokens (Table 2) — both well above prior multimodal medical benchmarks: OmniMedVQA's ratio is 0.92 at 42.40 tokens, and even MMMU-Pro (Health & Medicine)'s is 1.25 at 107.08 tokens.

One more construction step feeds the paper's second contribution: GPT-4o-2024-11-20 auto-labels each question's core task (Diagnosis, Treatment Planning, or Basic Medicine) and, separately, whether it demands complex multi-step reasoning ("Reasoning") versus more direct recall or perception ("Understanding") — meant to isolate a subset suited to evaluating o1-like reasoning models. A 10%-sample human audit (490 Text + 400 MM questions) found 28 and 11 disagreements respectively, an ≈4.3% label-error rate against the auto-labeling.

// what they found

The results

The saturation problem becomes concrete the moment you put MedXpertQA next to the benchmarks it's replacing. Across four representative models, Figure 1 labels the point-drop from existing benchmarks (MMLU Medical, MedQA, MedMCQA Dev) to MedXpertQA Text as 70.7 for Qwen2.5-72B, 61.5 for GPT-4o, 51.3 for o1 (evaluated on a 10% sample due to cost), and 56.3 for DeepSeek-R1 — every one of the three comparison benchmarks sits far closer to its own ceiling than MedXpertQA does.

Across the full 18-model evaluation (Tables 4-5), o1 leads overall at 49.89% average (44.67% Text, 56.28% MM) — only modestly ahead of the paper's own "Expert (Pre-Licensed)" human baseline of 43.92% overall (42.60% Text, 45.53% MM), and it clears that baseline comfortably on MM while running close to even on Text. DeepSeek-R1 is the strongest reasoning-scaled LLM on Text at 37.76% average, "most notably on the Reasoning set, where it substantially outperforms other models" per the paper. Every vanilla (non-reasoning-scaled) model trails badly: the best vanilla LMM, GPT-4o, averages 30.37% on Text and 42.80% on MM; the best vanilla LLM, DeepSeek-V3, averages 24.16% on Text.

Reasoning-scaled models systematically narrow — sometimes flip — the usual Reasoning-vs-Understanding gap. Vanilla models score worse on Reasoning than Understanding across the board, but the paper's inference-time-scaling ablation (three matched pairs: DeepSeek-R1 vs. DeepSeek-V3, QwQ-32B-Preview vs. Qwen2.5-32B-Instruct, QVQ-72B-Preview vs. Qwen2-VL-72B) shows accuracy gains from extra test-time compute concentrate specifically in the Reasoning subset for all three pairs. o1 on MM is the most dramatic instance — 52.78% Reasoning vs. 65.45% Understanding, the opposite direction from every vanilla model.

A system-level breakdown of GPT-4o's accuracy surfaces a real asymmetry: GPT-4o is comparatively strong on Integumentary questions (skin conditions) — its share of the correctly-answered set rises well above its share of the full question set — and comparatively weak on Cardiovascular, which (together with Nervous) is tied for the second-largest body-system slice of the benchmark at 13.7% each, just behind Skeletal at 19.8% (Figure 3).

The paper's own data-leakage check (Table 6) reports GPT-4o output perplexity rising from 1.03×10²¹⁸ pre-augmentation to 1.35×10²⁴⁷ post-augmentation, ROUGE-L similarity to the reference explanation falling from 0.1893 to 0.1664, and edit-distance similarity falling from 0.2691 to 0.2416 — all three move in the direction of less apparent memorization after the rephrase-and-augment step, which the authors read as evidence the augmentation reduces leakage risk relative to the un-augmented source items.

An Appendix error-type analysis (200 sampled wrong answers per model, labeled by gpt-4o-2024-11-20) breaks down how GPT-4o, Claude-3.5-Sonnet, and Gemini-1.5-Pro fail. Reading the chart's segment boundaries against its gridlines, Reasoning Process Error is the largest single category for all three models on both subsets — on the order of 45-60% of Text errors, versus roughly a third of MM errors — while Perceptual Error (misreading the image itself) is a close second specifically on MM, at a comparable order of magnitude to Reasoning Process there. The paper's own text confirms the qualitative shape: "the high percentage of Reasoning Process Errors on both MedXpertQA Text and MedXpertQA MM reflects the difficulty of our benchmark... Perceptual Errors are also prominent in MedXpertQA MM."

// does it hold up?

The eval read

MedXpertQA's construction pipeline — Brier-filtered human difficulty, adversarial AI-expert filtering, rephrase-based leakage mitigation, physician review — is more careful than most medical benchmarks, and the saturation problem it targets is real (o1-preview at 96-99% on MedQA/MMLU Medical is not a useful measurement anymore). But three specific choices weaken how much confidence you should place in the headline numbers.

First, the option-count asymmetry is a real confound that the paper never normalizes for. MedXpertQA Text uses 10 answer options; MMLU (Medical), MedQA, and MedMCQA Dev all use 4. A pure-guessing floor is 10% for Text vs. 20-25% for the comparison benchmarks, so part of the reported 51-71-point drop in Figure 1 is mechanically produced by widening the answer space, not purely by deeper reasoning difficulty. The paper reports no guessing-baseline-adjusted score, nor a 4-option-matched subset, that would let a reader separate "genuinely harder" from "structurally lower ceiling."

Second, the leakage audit (Table 6) is narrow: perplexity, ROUGE-L, and edit-distance are computed only against GPT-4o's outputs — "since it is the most effective among all vanilla LMMs, possibly reflecting a higher risk of data leakage," per the paper's own justification — and none of the other 17 evaluated models (Claude, Gemini, DeepSeek, o1, the Qwen family) gets the same check, even though they were plausibly trained on different web scrapes with different overlap risk. The metrics themselves are also indirect: perplexity-of-output and similarity-to-a-paraphrased-explanation are proxies for memorization, not a direct verbatim-recall probe.

Third, the benchmark's reproducibility is structurally limited by design: Appendix A's "Leakage Prevention Statement" explicitly withholds the original data sources and asks readers not to share MedXpertQA examples publicly. That's a defensible contamination defense, but it also means no outside party can re-verify item quality, audit the expert-review corrections, or independently confirm the Reasoning/Understanding labels beyond the paper's own 10%-sample check (the ≈4.3% disagreement rate noted above is a spot-check, not full verification, of the paper's second headline contribution).

Finally, the human anchor is worth reading carefully: "Expert (Pre-Licensed)" is explicitly defined as upper-year medical students or trainees preparing for licensing exams — not board-certified physicians — yet the paper's framing throughout (title, abstract: "expert-level") implies a genuine expert ceiling. o1 already exceeds this baseline on MM (56.28% vs. 45.53%) and nearly matches it on Text (44.67% vs. 42.60%), which complicates the "models still lag far behind experts" framing for at least the MM arm.

baselines

MMLU/MedQA/MedMCQA comparisons are fair evidence those benchmarks are saturated, but MedXpertQA Text's 10-option format vs. their 4-option format isn't normalized for, so part of the reported point-drop reflects a structurally lower guessing floor rather than purely deeper reasoning difficulty.

contamination

The Table 6 leakage check is real but narrow — perplexity/ROUGE-L/edit-distance are computed only for GPT-4o's outputs, not the other 17 evaluated models, using indirect proxies rather than a direct verbatim-recall test.

what's missed

No guessing-baseline-adjusted score across the differing option counts; no cross-model leakage audit; the Reasoning/Understanding split (the paper's second headline contribution) is GPT-4o-labeled with only a 10%-sample human check (≈4.3% disagreement).

would raise confidence

An option-count-adjusted comparison, leakage tests run on 2-3 more model families, larger-sample or full human verification of the Reasoning/Understanding labels, and a board-certified-physician baseline alongside the current pre-licensed-trainee one.

// bottom line

MedXpertQA earns its place as the harder successor to MedQA and MMLU-Medical — nothing evaluated comes close to saturating it, and the construction pipeline is genuinely more rigorous than most medical benchmarks. But the headline 51-to-71-point gap (Figure 1) conflates two different effects — real clinical-reasoning difficulty, and a mechanically wider 10-option answer space — and the checks that would let a reader fully trust the score (leakage, label quality) lean on a single model and a 10% audit rather than exhaustive verification.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. MedXpertQA opens a 51-to-71-point gap against near-saturated MMLU (Medical)/MedQA/MedMCQA (Figure 1), and even o1 — the best model overall at 49.89% average — only narrowly clears the paper's own pre-licensed-human baseline of 43.92%, with the real separation concentrated on the MM subset (56.28% vs. 45.53%).
  2. Part of that gap is a confound, not just difficulty: MedXpertQA Text uses 10 answer options against 4 for MMLU/MedQA/MedMCQA, so a chunk of the headline point-drop reflects a lower guessing floor — a normalization the paper never reports.
  3. Inference-time-scaled models (o1, DeepSeek-R1, o3-mini, QwQ, QVQ) systematically outperform their own vanilla backbones specifically on the Reasoning subset, and GPT-4o's error profile is dominated by Reasoning-Process errors on Text plus a mix of Reasoning-Process and Perceptual errors on MM — real evidence that multi-step clinical reasoning and image interpretation are separate, still-unsolved bottlenecks.
