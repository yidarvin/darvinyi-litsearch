HarmBench: A Standardized Evaluation Framework for Automated Red Teaming and Robust Refusal

Before HarmBench, every red-teaming paper used its own behaviors, models, and success metric — and a single hidden knob (how many tokens you let the model generate) could swing reported attack success by up to 30%. HarmBench fixes the whole pipeline: 510 harmful behaviors, 18 attacks, 33 models, and one open classifier that matches GPT-4. The headline findings: no attack or defense is uniformly effective, and bigger models are not safer.

Mantas Mazeika, Long Phan, Xuwang Yin, Andy Zou, Zifan Wang, Norman Mu, Elham Sakhaee, Nathaniel Li, Steven Basart, Bo Li, David Forsyth, Dan Hendrycks

What problem is this solving?

Automated red teaming — programs that automatically search for prompts which make an LLM produce a forbidden behavior — was a fast-moving field with no common yardstick. Each new attack paper picked its own set of harmful behaviors, its own target models, and its own definition of "success," so the promising numbers they reported couldn't be lined up against each other. The field was accumulating results that didn't compose.

The authors argue prior evaluations were missing three properties, and built HarmBench to satisfy all three:

  • Breadth. Common test sets were small and narrow — AdvBench, the de-facto standard, is essentially a refusal-detection set. They were also single-modality and skewed toward the same handful of harm types. A benchmark that only probes a thin slice of behavior overstates how robust a model really is.
  • Comparability. The killer detail: the number of tokens the target model is allowed to generate during evaluation drastically changes the measured attack success rate — by up to 30% (Figure 1 below) — because a substring-matching judge has more text to find a "harmful" snippet in. This parameter was never standardized, which the paper bluntly calls renderings of cross-paper comparisons "effectively meaningless." Worse, several prior works evaluate on the very metric their method optimizes, which invites gaming.
  • Robust metrics. Success was usually scored by substring matching (does the output contain "Sure, here is…" and not "I'm sorry"?) or by ad-hoc, undocumented GPT-4 judges — both noisy, both gameable, neither reproducible.
// how it works

The method

HarmBench standardizes the entire evaluation pipeline (Figure 2). A fixed catalogue of behaviors goes in; an attack turns each behavior into a concrete test case (an adversarial prompt); the target model + any defense generates a completion, capped at the standardized 512 tokens; a classifier labels each completion success or failure; and the attack success rate is the fraction of behaviors successfully elicited. The three desiderata map onto the three stages — breadth in the behaviors, comparability in the generation step, robust metrics in the classifier.

ASR(y, g, f) = 1N Σi c( fT(xi), y ) // share of N test cases for behavior y, from attack g against model f, that the classifier c labels successful

510 behaviors, deliberately curated

HarmBench contains 510 behaviors split into four functional categories — 200 standard, 100 copyright, 100 contextual, and 110 multimodal — and seven semantic categories: cybercrime & unauthorized intrusion, chemical & biological weapons/drugs, copyright violations, misinformation & disinformation, harassment & bullying, illegal activities, and general harm. Contextual behaviors pair a harmful request with a block of context (e.g. a persona to write targeted disinformation for); multimodal behaviors attach an image the prompt refers to (Figure 3). Two design choices sharpen the signal: they exclude dual-intent behaviors (writing an encryption function is benign for a developer and malicious for a ransomware author, so it is left out) to keep every behavior unambiguously harmful, and they score only whether the model attempts the behavior, which separates robustness from raw capability. A canonical validation/test split — with separate classifiers for each — means methods can be tuned without touching the test set.

An open classifier that matches GPT-4

The arbiter of "success" can't be substring matching (noisy) or a closed GPT-4 endpoint (expensive, irreproducible, drifting). For non-copyright behaviors HarmBench fine-tunes Llama 2 13B chat on data distilled from GPT-4-0613; for copyright it uses a hash-based classifier that checks directly for reproduced copyrighted text. On a manually labeled validation set, the test classifier reaches 93.2% agreement with human labels (94.5% on standard, 90.5% on contextual) — slightly above GPT-4's 88.4% and well above AdvBench substring matching (69.9%) and the GPTFuzz RoBERTa judge (75.4%). It's cheap, open, and reproducible.

R2D2 — a fast adversarial-training defense

To show the benchmark drives co-development of attacks and defenses, the authors introduce R2D2 (Robust Refusal Dynamic Defense). They adversarially train against GCG — the strongest attack on robust models — but GCG is brutally slow (≈20 minutes to craft one test case on a 7B model with an A100). Borrowing persistent test cases from the fast-adversarial-training literature, R2D2 maintains a dynamic pool of GCG test cases that is continually updated, and trains with three terms: an away loss that opposes the GCG target, a toward loss that pushes the model to emit a fixed refusal string, and a standard SFT loss on instruction data to preserve utility. Every L updates, K% of the pool is reset to keep the adversary fresh. They fine-tune Mistral 7B base with this method (via the Zephyr codebase) into Zephyr 7B + R2D2, with the released Zephyr 7B as the natural comparison.

The results

Using HarmBench, the authors run the largest like-for-like red-teaming comparison to date: 18 attacks drawn from 12 papers against 33 target LLMs and defenses, open and closed. Two structural findings fall out.

No attack or defense is uniformly effective. Ranking by average ASR (Figure 4), the strongest attacks are AutoDAN, GCG, TAP, PAIR, and GCG-Transfer, and the most robust open models cluster around the Llama 2 family and Zephyr + R2D2 — but no single attack breaks every model, and no model resists every attack. There is no free lunch on either side.

Robustness is largely independent of model size. Within a model family, ASR stays roughly flat from 7B to 13B to 70B (Figure 5, left) — scaling up parameters does not buy safety. This held across Llama 2, Qwen, Orca 2, Mistral, Vicuna, and Baichuan 2. The lone exception the paper flags is copyright behaviors, where ASR rises with model size — bigger models are simply more capable of reproducing copyrighted text. ASR is also higher on contextual behaviors than standard ones, and lowest on copyright (the hash-based classifier is deliberately strict).

R2D2 sets the bar for GCG robustness. Against the GCG attack, Zephyr + R2D2 is the most robust model by a wide margin (Figure 5, right): single-attack GCG succeeds 69.5% of the time on base Zephyr and 32.5% on Llama 2 7B Chat, but only 5.5% on Zephyr + R2D2. Averaged across the three GCG variants (GCG, GCG-Multi, GCG-Transfer) — the metric Figure 5's bar chart plots — the paper reports R2D2 as 4× lower than Llama 2 13B, the second-most-robust model. Figure 6 shows why it holds up: across 500 GCG optimization steps, R2D2 keeps the GCG loss high (the attacker can't drive it down) and ASR pinned near 5%, while base Zephyr's loss collapses toward zero and its ASR sits around 70%. Crucially, utility is preserved — Zephyr + R2D2 scores 6.0 on MT-Bench versus 6.5 for Mistral 7B Instruct v0.2.

The per-attack numbers (below, from the paper's all-behaviors appendix table) also tell an honest story about what R2D2 actually buys. It demolishes the optimization attacks it was hardened against — GCG drops to 5.5% and GCG-Transfer to 0.0% — but its response to a plain Direct Request (just asking, no attack) is 14.2%, higher than Llama 2 7B Chat's 0.8%. R2D2 hardens Zephyr against adversarial optimization without giving it Llama 2's blanket caution toward naive harmful asks.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

As a measurement standard, HarmBench is unusually careful, and its two structural findings are well-supported. The place to read skeptically is the seam between the benchmark (a public good) and R2D2 (a result the benchmark is used to advertise).

The classifier is the load-bearing component, and its provenance is slightly circular: it's a Llama-2-13B model distilled from GPT-4-0613, then scored against human labels on the authors' own validation set. "Comparable to GPT-4" is therefore partly definitional — it was trained to imitate GPT-4 — and 93.2% agreement still means roughly one judgment in fourteen disagrees with a human, with errors that aren't uniformly spread across behavior types. Every ASR in the paper inherits that one model's notion of "harmful." To their credit, the separate validation/test classifiers and behavior splits genuinely defuse the tune-on-test failure mode they criticize in prior work.

The R2D2 headline is in-distribution to its own adversary. The claim is "state-of-the-art robustness against the GCG attack" — and GCG is exactly the attack R2D2 trains on, so the 5.5% number is partly a measure of resisting the thing it was optimized to resist. The per-attack table makes the shape of the win concrete: R2D2 zeroes out GCG-Transfer (0.0%) but its Direct-Request ASR (14.2%) is actually higher than Llama 2's — it hardens against optimization, not against being asked nicely. The paper does show R2D2 is among the most robust open models overall (Figure 4), but the eye-catching 4× figure should be read as a GCG-specific, partly in-distribution result rather than a guarantee against novel attacks.

Finally, ASR measures attempt, not real-world harm. By design the classifier asks whether the model tried to comply, not whether the output is actually dangerous, useful, or correct — a model that confidently emits wrong "bioweapon instructions" scores as successfully attacked. Combined with the deliberate exclusion of dual-intent behaviors, HarmBench is a clean but slightly idealized proxy: it under-samples the messy dual-use middle where most real misuse actually lives. And like any static benchmark with public behaviors, it will erode as models train on it.

baselines

Strong and current for early 2024: 18 attacks from 12 papers, plus a head-to-head of five "success" judges (substring, GPTFuzz, ChatGLM, Llama-Guard, GPT-4). R2D2's defense comparison is narrower and centers on GCG.

contamination

Classifier distilled from GPT-4 and scored on the authors' own labels (mildly circular). Static, public behaviors invite future train-on-test leakage; mitigated by canonical val/test classifier+behavior splits.

what's missed

ASR = attempt, not actual harm/usefulness/correctness. Dual-intent behaviors excluded by design, so the dual-use middle is under-sampled. The 512-token cap fixes one comparability knob but the metric still depends on the choice.

would raise confidence

Report R2D2 against attacks fully held out from its training; human-rate a sample of "successful" completions for real harmfulness; track ASR drift as models age into the benchmark.

// bottom line

HarmBench's lasting contribution is the standard, not the defense. It turned a pile of incomparable per-paper numbers into a shared benchmark — fixed behaviors, a 512-token pipeline, and a cheap open classifier that matches GPT-4 — and established two durable facts: no attack or defense dominates, and safety doesn't scale with model size. R2D2 is a real and clever result, but its headline robustness is partly in-distribution to the GCG attack it trains on.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. Standardization was the contribution. A single hidden knob — the token budget — could swing reported ASR by up to 30%, so pre-HarmBench red-teaming papers simply weren't comparable. Fixing the behaviors (510), the pipeline, and the success classifier made the numbers mean the same thing across papers.
  2. Two facts that reshaped safety intuitions: no attack or defense is uniformly effective, and robustness is roughly independent of model size — scaling parameters up does not make a model safer.
  3. An open judge that matches GPT-4. A fine-tuned Llama-2-13B classifier distilled from GPT-4 hit 93.2% human agreement (vs GPT-4's 88.4% and substring matching's 69.9%) — cheap, reproducible, and now widely reused — at the cost of baking one model's harm boundary into every score.
  4. R2D2 works, but read the GCG number carefully. Efficient GCG adversarial training cut GCG attack success on Zephyr from 69.5% to 5.5% (the paper's 4× robustness edge over Llama 2 13B) while keeping MT-Bench at 6.0 — though it's optimized against the very attack it's evaluated on, and its plain Direct-Request ASR (14.2%) is no better than the base model's.
