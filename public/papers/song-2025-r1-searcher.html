R1-Searcher: Incentivizing the Search Capability in LLMs via Reinforcement Learning

A two-stage, purely outcome-based RL recipe: first reward the model just for invoking retrieval, then switch to rewarding correct answers — no supervised cold start, no process reward — and the resulting policy generalizes zero-shot from a local Wikipedia index to live Google search.

Huatong Song, Jinhao Jiang, Yingqian Min, Jie Chen, Zhipeng Chen, Wayne Xin Zhao, Lei Fang, Ji-Rong Wen · Renmin University of China, DataCanvas Alaya NeW

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

Large Reasoning Models like OpenAI-o1, DeepSeek-R1, and Kimi k1.5 had just shown that RL with outcome rewards can teach strong step-by-step reasoning. But that reasoning draws entirely on parametric knowledge — it has nothing to offer for time-sensitive facts, private/local information, or anything the model simply never saw, and it hallucinates instead of admitting it doesn't know.

The existing fixes each have a real cost. Prompt-engineered RAG pipelines — iterative decomposition, query generation, sub-question answering — work but lean on closed-source LLMs for their best results. Distilling that behavior into smaller models via supervised fine-tuning (SFT) tends to make the model memorize solution paths rather than learn the underlying skill, which hurts generalization to novel questions. And test-time scaling methods that use Monte Carlo Tree Search to explore retrieval strategies at inference (e.g. ReARTeR, CR-Planner) buy accuracy at the cost of heavy per-query retrieval overhead, since the search happens fresh every time rather than being baked into the model. The gap R1-Searcher targets: incentivize the search behavior itself, during training, purely through outcome-based RL — no distillation, no SFT cold start, no process supervision — so the resulting policy is cheap at inference and (ideally) generalizes past the training distribution.

// how it works

The method

R1-Searcher trains Qwen-2.5-7B-Base or Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct with a two-stage, outcome-only RL curriculum built on a modified Reinforce++, using training data drawn only from HotpotQA and 2WikiMultiHopQA and bucketed by difficulty (easy / medium / difficult, measured by how many rollouts a prompted Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct needs to answer correctly).

Stage 1 — learn to invoke retrieval at all

The model doesn't yet know it can search. Stage 1 rewards it purely for the act of retrieving, ignoring answer correctness entirely: a retrieval reward of 0.5 for making at least one retrieval call, plus a 0.5 format reward for correctly wrapping the query in <|begin_of_query|>...<|end_of_query|> and its answer in <think>/<answer> tags. This is a small, targeted warm-up (350 examples) whose only job is to get the retrieval-invocation format to stick.

Stage 2 — learn to use retrieval to answer correctly

The retrieval reward is dropped. Now the format penalty flips harsh (−2 for malformed output) and an F1-based answer reward — word-overlap between predicted and gold answer — drives the model to actually use what it retrieves:

Ranswer = 2 · INPN + RN // IN = intersection word count, PN/RN = predicted/reference word counts — F1 over the answer text, not process supervision

8,148 examples (4,561 from HotpotQA, 3,587 from 2WikiMultiHopQA, mixing medium and hard difficulty) drive Stage 2, each with 16 rollouts per sample.

Two changes to Reinforce++ for retrieval

RAG-based rollout: generation pauses at <end_of_query>, the query is extracted and sent to the retriever, and the results come back wrapped in <begin_of_documents>...<end_of_documents> — reasoning then resumes with the new evidence in context. Retrieval mask-based loss calculation: the retrieved-document span is marked as special tokens and excluded from the RL loss, so the model isn't trained to generate the retrieved text itself, only to reason over and respond to it — the same masking idea SEARCH-R1 (posted five days later) independently converges on.

// what they found

The results

Across four multi-hop benchmarks — HotpotQA and 2WikiMultiHopQA (in-domain), Musique and Bamboogle (out-of-domain) — scored by both Cover Exact Match (ACCR) and GPT-4o-mini LLM-as-Judge (ACCL), running on Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct, R1-Searcher beats the strongest baseline (ReARTeR, primarily a GPT-4o-mini MCTS test-time-scaling method) by 48.2% on HotpotQA and 21.7% on 2WikiMultiHopQA under LLM-as-Judge. Trained from a base model with no cold start at all, the Qwen-2.5-7B-Base variant does even better — best or near-best on every dataset, surpassing closed-source GPT-4o-mini-based pipelines despite using an open, far smaller backbone.

Generalization: RL beats SFT, and the policy transfers to the open web

An RL-vs-SFT ablation on matched data finds RL clearly ahead on both in-domain and out-of-domain sets — the case study traces this to SFT models leaning on unreliable internal knowledge instead of querying, where RL-trained models search when uncertain instead of guessing. And despite training exclusively against a local dense-retrieval Wikipedia index, the Qwen-2.5-7B-RL policy transfers zero-shot to live Google search on Bamboogle: switching from local to online retrieval yields an 18.2% relative gain with the identical backbone, and the online variant beats Search-o1 (a 32B-parameter online-search reasoning model) by 11.4% — evidence the model learned a transferable search skill rather than memorizing the shape of its training corpus.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

The two-stage curriculum and the zero-shot online-search transfer are the strongest parts of this paper — but the baseline set, judge choice, and dataset scope all narrow how far the headline numbers travel.

Every baseline is evaluated under a different harness than R1-Searcher's training. All comparison methods run through the FlashRAG toolkit with a shared 2019 KILT Wikipedia corpus and BGE-large-en-v1.5 retriever, while R1-Searcher itself is trained and evaluated with its own retrieval pipeline. The paper doesn't report that R1-Searcher was re-run inside the exact same FlashRAG harness as the baselines, so some of the delta could reflect retriever/corpus differences rather than the RL recipe alone — a tighter test would hold the retrieval stack fixed and vary only the policy.

LLM-as-Judge is the headline metric, and it's GPT-4o-mini grading against GPT-4o-mini-based baselines. ACCL uses GPT-4o-mini itself as the judge — the same model family several strong baselines (ReARTeR, CR-Planner, Marco-o1's variants) are built on. The paper never checks the judge against human labels or reports agreement with the stricter ACCR (Cover Exact Match) metric it also computes; where the two might disagree isn't discussed, even though ACCR is available in every table.

Training and evaluation both live entirely inside HotpotQA/2WikiMultiHopQA-style multi-hop QA. Four datasets total, two of them (HotpotQA, 2Wiki) partially in-distribution by construction. There's no contamination screen analogous to DeepResearcher's pass@k filter to check whether Qwen-2.5-7B-Base or Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct could already answer some training questions from parametric memory before ever calling retrieval — a gap this paper shares with Search-R1, its closest contemporaneous sibling.

baselines

Broad and relevant (branching, summarization, adaptive-retrieval, RAG-CoT, test-time-scaling, reasoning-model families) but run through a shared FlashRAG/KILT harness distinct from R1-Searcher's own pipeline — not a fully matched apples-to-apples setup.

contamination

Not addressed. HotpotQA and 2WikiMultiHopQA are heavily-published multi-hop sets; no pass@k or memorization filter distinguishes "learned to search" from "already knew the answer."

what's missed

The headline LLM-as-Judge metric (GPT-4o-mini) is never validated against human labels or reconciled with the paper's own stricter ACC_R numbers; only four multi-hop datasets total, two partly in-domain.

would raise confidence

Re-running R1-Searcher inside the same FlashRAG/KILT harness as the baselines; a human-validated or model-agnostic judge; a contamination/pass@k screen; single-hop and non-Wikipedia-answer benchmarks beyond Bamboogle to stress the generalization claim further.

// bottom line

A genuinely elegant training recipe — a warm-up reward that just teaches "you can search," followed by an outcome-only answer reward, with no SFT cold start — and the zero-shot jump from local Wikipedia retrieval to live Google search is real evidence of a transferable skill rather than a memorized corpus shape. Read the specific margins over GPT-4o-mini baselines cautiously: the evaluation harness isn't fully matched and the judge is unvalidated, so the ranking is more trustworthy than the exact percentage gaps.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. A two-stage reward curriculum, not a single reward function. Stage 1 rewards the mere act of retrieving (0.5 for ≥1 call + a format reward); Stage 2 drops that and switches to an F1 answer reward — separating "learn the mechanic" from "learn to use it well."
  2. No SFT cold start, no process reward, no distillation. The entire capability comes from outcome-based RL on a modified Reinforce++ with retrieval-token loss masking — the same masking idea SEARCH-R1 arrives at independently five days later.
  3. The generalization result is the paper's best evidence. A policy trained only against a local Wikipedia index transfers zero-shot to live Google search, beating a 32B online-search reasoning model (Search-o1) by 11.4% on Bamboogle — a real out-of-distribution stress test, not just a held-out split.
  4. The evaluation leans on an unvalidated GPT-4o-mini judge inside a not-fully-matched harness. Baselines run through a shared FlashRAG/KILT pipeline distinct from R1-Searcher's own, and the headline LLM-as-Judge metric is never checked against human labels or against the paper's own stricter Cover-Exact-Match numbers.
