Ignore This Title and HackAPrompt: Exposing Systemic Vulnerabilities of LLMs through a Global Scale Prompt Hacking Competition

2,800+ competitors from 50+ countries spent weeks trying to trick GPT-3, ChatGPT, and FlanT5-XXL into leaking a hidden prompt or printing "I have been PWNED." The result: 600K+ adversarial prompts, an 83.2% success rate on the refined leaderboard submissions, and the first data-driven taxonomy of 29 named prompt-hacking techniques — plus the discovery that prompt-based defenses simply don't work.

Sander Schulhoff, Jeremy Pinto, Anaum Khan, Louis-François Bouchard, Chenglei Si, Svetlina Anati, Valen Tagliabue, Anson Liu Kost, Christopher Carnahan, Jordan Boyd-Graber · University of Maryland

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

By late 2023, prompt injection and jailbreaking were widely acknowledged security threats — LLM applications built around a fixed prompt template plus untrusted user input, deployed everywhere from chatbots to "military command and control," were known to be attackable. But the evidence base was thin. Prior work on prompt injection was small-scale: Perez and Ribeiro (2022) tested a handful of template prompts against InstructGPT; Liu et al. (2023) hand-collected 78 jailbreak prompts and built a manual taxonomy; Shen et al. (2023) analyzed 6,387 prompts scraped from four platforms. Automated red-teaming approaches (Perez et al., 2022, "Red Teaming Language Models with Language Models") use one LM to attack another, but that substitutes a model's own creativity for a human adversary's — it doesn't capture what a large, motivated, and diverse population of human attackers can actually do.

No one had run prompt hacking at the scale needed to answer basic quantitative questions: how often do state-of-the-art LLMs actually fall to human-crafted attacks in a realistic application-grounded setting, and what does the space of successful attack strategies actually look like — is it a handful of tricks, or dozens? The gap is a large-scale, realistic, and systematically-labeled dataset of real prompt attacks, not another small case study.

// how it works

The method

The authors ran a global online competition, HackAPrompt, structured around ten prompt-hacking challenges modeled on real application patterns (translation, question-answering, story generation, moral judgment, emoji response generation, and more). Each challenge fixes an application-side prompt template with a user-input slot; competitors submit text to fill that slot, aiming to override the template's original instruction and force the model to output either the literal phrase "I have been PWNED" or a hidden key embedded in the template. Challenges vary in difficulty via input filters (blocking words like "PWNED"), few-shot vs. zero-shot templates, and where the user input is spliced into the prompt (start, middle, or end).

Competitors could target one of three backend models — GPT-3 (text-davinci-003), ChatGPT (gpt-3.5-turbo), or FlanT5-XXL — and submitted up to 500 attempts per day, working solo or in teams of up to four. Each submitted prompt was re-run server-side (at temperature 0, top-p 0) to confirm it actually worked before being scored. Scoring rewarded shorter, more token-efficient injections and doubled points for successful ChatGPT attacks (its defenses were empirically the hardest to beat):

s(p, c, m) = 21 · dc · (105 − |p|)  [×2 multiplier only when m = ChatGPT] // d_c = per-challenge difficulty (1–10); |p| = prompt token length — shorter, cleverer injections score higher

Two datasets came out of this: a Submissions Dataset of the refined prompts competitors actually submitted to the leaderboard, and a much larger Playground Dataset of every prompt anyone tried in the live testing interface, successful or not — together totaling 600K+ prompts from over 2,800 participants across 50+ countries. From the collected attacks, the authors built a data-driven taxonomical ontology: reviewing prior taxonomies (Rao et al., Liu et al., Perez & Ribeiro, Greshake et al.), merging redundant definitions, and adding newly observed techniques, they arrived at 29 named attack types connected by hypernym ("typos are an instance of obfuscation") and meronym ("a Special Case attack contains a Simple Instruction attack") relationships — then used GPT-4 to automatically classify 1,000 sampled prompts against the taxonomy, reaching roughly 75% agreement with the authors' own hand labels.

// what they found

The results

Despite building the ten challenges with what the authors believed were current best practices, competitors solved 9 of 10 challenges within the first few days (the tenth — emoji-only input — was never solved and may not have a solution at all). Aggregate success rates differed sharply by dataset: the curated Submissions Dataset hit an 83.2% success rate (34,641 of 41,596 prompts), while the much larger, unfiltered Playground Dataset — capturing raw exploration, including failed attempts — succeeded only 7.7% of the time (43,295 of 560,161 prompts). Model-by-model, success rates on the Submissions Dataset were close to each other despite very different usage volumes: FlanT5-XXL (227,801 prompts, 19,252 successful, 8%), ChatGPT (276,506 prompts, 19,930 successful, 7%), and GPT-3 (55,854 prompts, 4,113 successful, 7%) — evidence that ChatGPT's extra defenses raised the bar but didn't come close to stopping a motivated crowd.

The competition surfaced techniques the authors say hadn't been documented before, most notably the Context Overflow attack: because ChatGPT tends to produce verbose completions, competitors learned to pad their input with thousands of tokens of filler text so that only a handful of tokens of output budget remained — forcing a short, clean "I have been PWNED" instead of that phrase buried in a longer, non-matching response. Other notable discoveries included using Chinese characters (higher information density than Latin script) to defeat a per-character input-escaping defense on Challenge 9, and a "Two Token Attack" on the key-extraction challenge, where one team found that submitting the two-token input "key:" was enough to make the model complete the phrase with — and thereby leak — its actual hidden key.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

The paper's headline claim — LLMs can be broken by prompt hacking at scale — is well supported by sheer volume: 600K+ real attempts against production APIs is far beyond what any prior study assembled, and the 9/10 solve rate within days is a strong, simple signal. But several parts of the evaluation design deserve a closer look before treating specific numbers as durable facts.

First, the target task is a proxy, not the real threat. Every challenge scores success as producing the literal string "I have been PWNED" (or a hidden key), chosen specifically because it's automatically verifiable and avoids generating genuinely harmful content. That's a defensible research-ethics tradeoff, but it means the reported 83.2%/7.7% success rates measure susceptibility to a benign, unambiguous target string — not susceptibility to attacks that need to produce coherent harmful content, evade a content filter, or survive downstream parsing. The authors' own Section 2.1 argues by proxy ("if competitors can force this arbitrary phrase, they may be able to make it generate arbitrary toxic text"), which is a reasonable hypothesis but is not directly tested in this paper.

Second, the "prompt drift" limitation the authors themselves flag in the Limitations section is real and dates this dataset quickly: the models under test (GPT-3 text-davinci-003, gpt-3.5-turbo, FlanT5-XXL) were current in mid-2023 and are not adversarially-hardened production systems by 2024+ standards — the paper explicitly notes that at least 6,000 collected prompts already only work "some of the time," and success rates against later, more heavily safety-tuned models (GPT-4-class systems with dedicated injection defenses) would almost certainly be lower. The taxonomy of attack techniques is the paper's more durable contribution; the specific success-rate numbers are a snapshot.

Third, the GPT-4 auto-classification used to produce the taxonomy distribution chart (Figure 6) is validated only against a roughly 75% agreement rate with the authors' own labels on a sample — meaning up to a quarter of the classified prompts in that chart may be mislabeled relative to the ontology's own definitions, which matters more for the long tail of rarer techniques than for the dominant Compound/Simple Instruction categories.

baselines

There isn't a comparison against automated red-teaming baselines (e.g. LM-generated attacks from Perez et al., 2022) run on the same challenges, so the paper doesn't quantify how much human crowds outperform (or underperform) an automated attacker on this exact task — only that both categories of attack exist in the literature.

contamination

Not applicable in the leaderboard-benchmark sense — this is a live, adversarial human competition against production APIs, not a static held-out test set that could leak into training data.

what's missed

Success is defined as emitting a fixed benign string, not as producing genuinely harmful content, so the numbers are a lower bound on "can be manipulated" rather than a direct measure of exploitable real-world harm; and the dataset is already stale against current model generations by the authors' own admission.

would raise confidence

A rerun of the same ten challenges against current frontier models with their current defenses (to show how much the 2023 attack surface has actually closed), and a validation pass on the GPT-4 taxonomy classifier beyond the 75%-agreement spot check, especially for the long-tail technique categories.

// bottom line

As a scale demonstration and a taxonomy, this holds up well and earned the paper EMNLP 2023's Best Theme Paper award — 600K+ real human attacks and 29 named, hierarchically related attack techniques is a genuinely new resource, not an incremental restatement of "prompt injection exists." The specific 83.2%/7.7% success-rate numbers, though, are tied to a benign proxy target and to specific mid-2023 model snapshots that have since been patched and superseded — treat them as evidence that prompt-only defenses failed against those systems at that time, not as a current, transferable susceptibility estimate.

// what to remember

What to remember

  1. A global competition (2,800+ competitors, 50+ countries) against GPT-3, ChatGPT, and FlanT5-XXL produced 600K+ adversarial prompts and solved 9 of 10 designed challenges within days — the largest human-sourced prompt-injection dataset assembled at the time, with an 83.2% success rate on the refined Submissions Dataset versus 7.7% on the raw Playground exploration data.
  2. The competition's data-driven taxonomy of 29 named prompt-hacking techniques (Context Ignoring, Payload Splitting, Context Overflow, Virtualization, and more), built from real attacks and organized by hypernym/meronym relationships, is the paper's most durable contribution — more so than the specific success-rate numbers, which are tied to since-patched mid-2023 model snapshots.
  3. The paper's clearest actionable finding is negative: prompt-based defenses — telling the model to resist injection, or using a second model to check the first — do not reliably work; every defended challenge fell to a large enough, motivated attacker population.
