The future of employment: How susceptible are jobs to computerisation?

A Gaussian process classifier trained on 70 hand-labelled O*NET occupations puts 47% of US employment at high risk of computerisation — the occupation-level automation-exposure baseline that a decade of task-level critiques, and the LLM-era exposure literature, are built against.

Carl Benedikt Frey, Michael A. Osborne

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

By 2013, the labour-economics literature on computerisation had a well-worn but increasingly outdated frame: Autor, Levy and Murnane's (2003) "task model" showed computers substitute for routine tasks — well-defined, rule-based procedures — while complementing non-routine cognitive work, and Autor and Dorn (2013) used that lens to explain the hollowing-out of middle-income jobs. The trouble is that the frame was calibrated to the Computer Revolution of the 1980s–2000s, and by the authors' own account "the scope of what computers do has recently expanded, and will inevitably continue to do so." Advances the authors group under Machine Learning and Mobile Robotics — big-data pattern recognition, better sensors, cheaper actuators — were starting to touch tasks the task model called non-routine: driving, sorting, even some legal and clerical judgment.

The only existing quantitative playbook for ranking occupations by exposure to a technology came from the offshoring literature, and it had two competing, equally awkward methodologies: Blinder (2009) hand-eyeballed O*NET job descriptions to assign a subjective offshorability index (concluding 22–29% of US jobs were offshorable), while Jensen and Kletzer (2005) built a purely objective ranking from standardised O*NET variables — a method Blinder himself showed could rank lawyers and judges as highly tradable while calling data-entry keyers and telephone operators nearly impossible to move offshore. Neither approach was built to measure automatability (as opposed to offshorability), and no study had yet quantified, occupation by occupation, what the specific technical bottlenecks to full automation were, or how many jobs across the whole US labour market sat on the wrong side of them. That is the gap this paper set out to fill: a discrete list of engineering bottlenecks to computerisation, mapped onto measurable occupational data, and turned into a probability of computerisation for every detailed occupation in the economy.

// how it works

The method

The authors first derive, from a workshop held with ML researchers at Oxford's Engineering Science Department, three categories of engineering bottleneck — task properties that current ML and robotics still can't touch: perception and manipulation (handling irregular objects in unstructured environments), creative intelligence (originality that can't yet be specified as an objective function), and social intelligence (negotiation, persuasion, care). They then map each bottleneck onto measurable O*NET "level" variables — nine in total: Finger Dexterity, Manual Dexterity and Cramped Work Space for perception/manipulation; Originality and Fine Arts for creative intelligence; Social Perceptiveness, Negotiation, Persuasion and Assisting/Caring for Others for social intelligence. Every one of the 702 detailed occupations in their O*NET-derived dataset (903 raw O*NET titles, aggregated to the 2010 six-digit SOC classification, minus 4.63 million jobs in "All Other" titles with no O*NET record — about 3% of the 138.44 million covered) becomes a 9-dimensional feature vector.

Ground truth comes from the authors themselves: together with "a group of ML researchers," they hand-labelled 70 of the 702 occupations (10%) with a binary tag — 1 if "the tasks of this job [can] be sufficiently specified, conditional on the availability of big data, to be performed by state of the art computer-controlled equipment," 0 otherwise — choosing only the occupations they felt most confident about. That 70×9 labelled set becomes training data for a Gaussian process classifier: a non-parametric probability distribution over functions f mapping the 9 O*NET variables to a continuous "automatability" score, squashed through a logistic link to give a class probability.

P(y* = 1 | f*) = 11 + exp(−f*) // logistic link turning the GP's latent discriminant f* into a computerisation probability

They test three covariance kernels for the GP — exponentiated quadratic, rational quadratic, and a linear kernel (equivalent to logistic regression) — using the GPML toolbox, validating each with 100 random 50/50 splits of the 70 labels. The exponentiated-quadratic kernel narrowly wins (AUC 0.894, log-likelihood −163.3) over the rational quadratic (0.893, −163.7), both comfortably ahead of plain logistic regression (0.827, −205.0). With that kernel fixed, they retrain on all 70 labels and use the posterior mean probability P(z* = 1 | X*, D) — inference done via Expectation Propagation, since the logistic likelihood isn't conjugate to the Gaussian prior — to score the remaining 632 unlabelled occupations. The result is a continuous "probability of computerisation" for all 702 occupations, illustrated schematically below for six representative jobs against each bottleneck dimension.

// what they found

The results

Weighting the 702 occupation-level probabilities by 2010 BLS employment and thresholding at 0.7 and 0.3, the authors report 47% of total US employment in the "high risk" category (P > 0.7), 19% "medium risk" (0.3–0.7), and 33% "low risk" (P < 0.3). At the extremes of the full 702-occupation ranking: Recreational Therapists come out least computerisable (P = 0.0028), and Telemarketers most computerisable (P = 0.99) — neither of which was part of the 70 hand-labelled training occupations, so both are genuine held-out predictions.

The paper frames the high-risk category as a "first wave": transportation and logistics, the bulk of office and administrative support, and most of production are predicted to be substituted first, which the authors call unsurprising given contemporaneous developments (Google's driverless cars, warehouse robotics, big-data-driven document processing). The more striking finding is that large swaths of services, sales, and construction also land in the high-risk band — cashiers, counter and rental clerks, and telemarketers among them — despite involving customer interaction, because that interaction turns out not to demand much actual social intelligence. The "medium risk" band is described as a technological plateau: Table III of the paper shows manual dexterity, finger dexterity, and cramped-work-space scores are all elevated there relative to the low-risk band, meaning these jobs (installation, maintenance, and repair occupations feature heavily) are gated on perception-and-manipulation bottlenecks that the authors expect to erode only gradually.

Finally, the paper checks its predictions against wages and education: both the average median wage and the share of workers with a bachelor's degree or higher fall sharply and roughly monotonically as computerisation probability rises. High-probability occupations cluster around $30k median wages and under 20% bachelor's-attainment; low-probability occupations sit near $60–65k and 55–60%+ bachelor's-attainment. The authors read this as a truncation of labour-market polarisation: rather than continuing to hollow out middle-income jobs (the pattern of the prior three decades), they predict computerisation will mainly substitute for low-skill, low-wage work going forward, while high-skill, high-wage occupations stay comparatively protected.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

The single biggest methodological choice in this paper — scoring whole occupations, not the individual tasks that make them up — turned out to be the thing the rest of the field pushed back on hardest. Three years later, the OECD's Arntz, Gregory and Zierahn (2016) redid the exercise using PIAAC survey data that captures the actual task composition within each occupation, rather than treating an occupation as a monolithic bundle, and found only about 9% of jobs at high risk of automation across OECD countries — a five-fold reduction from this paper's 47%. Their argument is exactly what Frey and Osborne's own model structure invites: an occupation labelled "high risk" because its O*NET profile resembles automatable training examples can still contain a large share of individually non-automatable tasks, and the binary occupation-level GP score simply can't see that distinction. The authors partially anticipate this themselves in their limitations section — "we do not capture any within-occupation variation resulting from the computerisation of tasks that simply free-up time for human labour to perform other tasks" — but never quantify how much of the 47% that caveat might eat into.

There is also a subtler circularity worth flagging in the paper's own most-quoted exhibit. Figure I's six illustrative occupations are meant to build intuition for how the model's predictions track common sense. But checking them against the appendix's full ranking table shows several are not held-out predictions at all: Dishwashers (label = 1), "Meeting, Convention, and Event Planners" (label = 0), Zoologists and Wildlife Biologists (label = 0), Fashion Designers (label = 0), and Physicians and Surgeons (label = 0) are all part of the 70 hand-labelled training occupations — meaning the model recovering the "obvious" answer for them is close to true by construction, not evidence of generalisation. Only Telemarketer, Boilermaker, and (very likely) the Court/Municipal Clerk occupation in that figure are genuine out-of-sample predictions.

baselines

The paper discusses both prior O*NET-based ranking methods at length — Blinder's (2009) subjective offshorability index and Jensen and Kletzer's (2005) objective ranking — as the direct methodological precedents, but never actually benchmarks its own GP ranking against either one on a shared set of occupations. We're told both predecessors have "obvious drawbacks"; we're never shown, quantitatively, that the new method ranks occupations better.

contamination

Not leakage in the train/test-split sense the GP validation (AUC 0.894, 100 random 50/50 splits) actually guards against reasonably well — but a related circularity in the narrative: several of the paper's own illustrative "look, it matches intuition" examples (Figure I's dishwasher, biologist, fashion designer, physicians/surgeons) are drawn from the 70 hand-labelled training occupations rather than the 632 the model had to generalise to.

what's missed

The Gaussian process machinery computes a full posterior predictive variance V(f*|x*,D) for every occupation (Eq. 7 in the paper) — but every table and figure reports only the posterior mean probability. A confidently-estimated 0.83 and a coin-flip 0.83 sitting between two dissimilar training profiles are presented identically; no per-occupation uncertainty is ever surfaced.

would raise confidence

An external audit of the 70 training labels with multiple independent raters and a disclosed agreement rate; published per-occupation credible intervals from the GP's own posterior variance; and — the test the paper's limitations section invites but nobody has run at matching granularity — a retrospective comparison of the high/medium/low risk categories against how US occupational employment actually moved over the following decade.

// bottom line

The 47% figure is a careful, internally validated estimate of occupation-level technical automatability under a specific 2013 reading of ML and robotics — not a forecast of job losses, not a task-level estimate, and, by the paper's own flagship illustrative figure, partly a restatement of the authors' own training labels. Its real importance is as the paper the rest of the automation-exposure literature reacts to: the OECD's task-level re-estimate cut the number to 9%, and a decade later Eloundou et al.'s (2023) LLM-exposure rubric inverts this paper's central wage/education finding — exposure now rises, rather than falls, with wages and educational attainment — because the automating technology changed from pattern-recognition robotics to language models. Which occupations are "at risk" turns out to depend entirely on which technology you assume is doing the automating.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. A Gaussian process classifier, trained on 70 hand-labelled O*NET occupations and validated on the rest of the 702, puts 47% of 2010 US employment in the "high risk" (P > 0.7) computerisation band — transportation/logistics, office/admin support and production first, plus a surprising share of services, sales and construction — against 33% low risk and 19% medium.
  2. Both average wage and educational attainment fall sharply as computerisation probability rises in this paper's occupation-level, robotics/pattern-recognition-era framing — exactly the gradient the LLM-exposure literature (Eloundou et al., 2023, GPTs are GPTs) later inverts once the automating technology is language rather than sensors and actuators.
  3. The paper's occupation-level design was directly challenged within three years by the OECD's task-level re-estimate (Arntz, Gregory & Zierahn, 2016: ~9% high-risk, versus this paper's 47%), and several of its own headline illustrative examples turn out to be training-set labels rather than held-out predictions — worth reading this paper alongside both correctives whenever the "47%" figure gets cited. The paper itself circulated as an Oxford Martin School working paper from September 2013 (the version most people cite), reaching print in Technological Forecasting and Social Change only in 2017.
