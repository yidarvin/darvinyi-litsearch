Direct Preference Optimization: Your Language Model is Secretly a Reward Model

RLHF without the RL. A change of variables shows the KL-constrained reward-maximization objective has a closed-form optimum — so you can skip the reward model and the PPO loop entirely and fit preferences with a single binary cross-entropy loss. The policy is the reward model.

Rafael Rafailov, Archit Sharma, Eric Mitchell, Stefano Ermon, Christopher D. Manning, Chelsea Finn — Stanford University
venue NeurIPS 2023 citations 9,201 topic Post-training & RL source ↗

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

By mid-2023, reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF) was the dominant way to turn a raw pretrained language model into something steerable — the recipe behind InstructGPT and the Anthropic assistant. But the recipe is a three-stage contraption: (1) supervised fine-tune a base model on demonstrations to get πSFT; (2) collect pairwise human preferences and fit a separate reward model under a Bradley–Terry likelihood; (3) optimize the policy against that frozen reward with on-policy RL — almost always PPO — under a KL penalty that keeps it near πSFT.

Stage 3 is where the pain lives. You have to sample from the policy inside the training loop, hold four networks in memory at once (policy, reward, value/critic, and a frozen reference), and PPO is famously fiddly — sensitive to its clipping range, KL target, learning rate, and reward normalization. Worse, the reward model is a lossy proxy that the policy learns to over-optimize: push hard enough on a learned reward and quality collapses even as the score climbs. The whole apparatus is dramatically more complex and less stable than the supervised fine-tuning that preceded it.

So the question the paper asks is sharp: the RLHF objective is just reward maximization with a KL leash — do we actually need RL, or even an explicit reward model, to optimize it? If preference data could be consumed with the simplicity and stability of a supervised classification loss, alignment would get a lot cheaper and a lot more reproducible. That is the gap DPO closes.

// how it works

The method

1 · The objective everyone is already optimizing

Start from the standard RLHF fine-tuning objective: maximize expected reward while staying close (in KL) to the reference policy.

maxπθ  𝔼x∼D, y∼πθ[ r(x,y) ] − β·DKL( πθ(y|x) ‖ πref(y|x) ) // Eq. 3 — β tunes the KL leash; πref is the frozen SFT model

This objective is not new — it is exactly what PPO is used to maximize. The DPO insight is that it doesn't need PPO, because its optimum is known in closed form.

2 · The KL-constrained optimum has a closed form

For any reward r, the policy that maximizes Eq. 3 is the reference policy re-weighted by the exponentiated reward:

πr(y|x) = 1Z(x) πref(y|x) exp( 1β r(x,y) ) // Eq. 4 — Z(x)=Σy πref(y|x)·exp(r(x,y)/β) is the partition function, intractable to compute

This is a known result, and on its own it's useless: the partition function Z(x) sums over every possible completion. But take logs and rearrange to solve for the reward in terms of the policy:

r(x,y) = β·log πr(y|x)πref(y|x) + β·log Z(x) // Eq. 5 — the reward is recoverable from a policy, up to the prompt-only term log Z(x)

3 · The partition function cancels

Now plug Eq. 5 into the Bradley–Terry preference model — the assumption that the probability one completion is preferred is a logistic function of the difference in their rewards. Because only the difference appears, the intractable β·log Z(x) term — which depends on the prompt but not the completion — is identical for both responses and cancels exactly. The human-preference probability collapses to an expression in the optimal policy and the reference alone, and its maximum-likelihood loss becomes:

ℒDPO = −𝔼(x,yw,yl)∼D log σ( β·log πθ(yw|x)πref(yw|x) − β·log πθ(yl|x)πref(yl|x) ) // Eq. 7 — a binary cross-entropy on preference pairs; σ is the logistic function, yw≻yl

That's the whole method. There is no reward model, no sampling from the policy during training, no RL — just batches of (prompt, chosen, rejected) triples fed through a logistic loss. The title's claim is literal: the policy carries an implicit reward,

r̂θ(x,y) = β·log πθ(y|x)πref(y|x) // your language model is secretly a reward model — its log-ratio to the reference

and the paper proves this reparameterization loses no generality: every reward class consistent with Bradley–Terry can be written this way (Theorem 1).

4 · Why the gradient doesn't degenerate

The gradient of ℒDPO pushes up the log-probability of the preferred completion yw and down that of the dispreferred yl — but each example is scaled by σ(r̂θ(x,yl) − r̂θ(x,yw)), a dynamic per-example weight that is large exactly when the implicit reward ranks the pair backwards. The model spends its gradient on the pairs it currently gets wrong. The authors show this weighting is load-bearing: a naïve version that just maximizes P(yw) and minimizes P(yl) without it causes the model to degenerate (Appendix Table 3). In practice you set πref=πSFT; when no SFT model exists, πref is obtained by maximum likelihood on the preferred completions to keep the reference in-distribution.

// what they found

The results

The experiments are deliberately modest in scale — models up to 6B parameters across three open-ended generation tasks — but they are designed to isolate the one question that matters: does dropping RL cost you anything?

1 · DPO dominates the reward–KL frontier

In a controlled IMDb sentiment setting where the ground-truth reward is a known sentiment classifier (so the true reward–KL trade-off is measurable), DPO produces by far the most efficient frontier: the highest achievable reward at every level of KL divergence from the reference. Crucially, DPO and PPO optimize the same objective, yet DPO's curve strictly dominates PPO's — and it even beats PPO-GT, an oracle PPO that is handed the ground-truth reward function. Doing RL without RL is not just simpler here; it's more efficient. The sweep covered 22 training runs (varying β, target KL, and the unlikelihood coefficient).

2 · It scales to real preference data

On Reddit TL;DR summarization (GPT-J 6B as the SFT model, win rate vs. human-written references judged by GPT-4), DPO reaches a win rate of ≈61% at temperature 0.0, exceeding PPO's 57% at its best temperature — and DPO also clears the Best-of-128 baseline's peak. DPO is far more robust to sampling temperature: PPO's win rate degrades toward the base GPT-J model at high temperature, while DPO stays high. In a direct human head-to-head, DPO samples (temp 0.25) were preferred 58% of the time over PPO samples (temp 0).

On Anthropic-HH single-turn dialogue (Pythia-2.8B), DPO is the only computationally efficient method that improves over the dataset's own preferred ("chosen") responses, matching or beating the expensive Best-of-128 baseline. And it gets there fast: win rate is stable across training and across sampling temperatures.

3 · Out-of-distribution and the GPT-4 judge

Transferring the TL;DR-trained policies to CNN/DailyMail news articles (a distribution shift), DPO continued to beat PPO by a clear margin, suggesting the implicit-reward policy generalizes at least as well as an explicit-reward one. And because every real-task number rides on GPT-4-as-judge, the authors ran a human study to validate it (below): humans agreed with GPT-4 roughly as often as they agreed with each other.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

The derivation is airtight and the simplicity claim is real — but in 2023 the empirical case for "DPO ≥ PPO" was thinner than the algorithm's eventual reputation, and worth reading carefully.

First, scale and scope. Everything here runs at ≤6B parameters on three narrow, single-turn tasks: sentiment, summarization, and one-step HH dialogue. There is no instruction-following at scale, no multi-turn, no harmlessness evaluation, and no frontier-size model. The paper is candid that scaling DPO "orders of magnitude larger" is future work — so the headline equivalence with PPO is established only in a small regime.

Second, the headline metric is a GPT-4 judge with a known length bias. The authors had to abandon their simple judge prompt and switch to a "concise" prompt precisely because GPT-4 prefers longer, more repetitive summaries than humans do — and that prompt choice alone moves DPO's reported win rate from 47% to 54%. The validating human study is small (272 / 122 / 199 raters across the three comparisons, with multiple human labels collected only for the DPO and PPO-1 pairs), and everything is scored against a single anchor (PPO at temperature 0) rather than a full pairwise matrix. The judge sits inside the human-agreement band, but it is the same kind of LLM-judge that later work (and several neighbours in this atlas) shows is gameable by verbosity and self-resemblance.

Third, and most consequential, the PPO baseline may be undertuned — which would flatter DPO. On Anthropic-HH the authors "are unable to find a prompt or sampling temperature" where the public PPO checkpoint beats the base Pythia-2.8B, so they substitute Best-of-128 as a "rough proxy for PPO-level performance." That is an assumption standing in for a measurement: the dialogue claim that DPO matches PPO rests on a proxy, not a head-to-head. Given how implementation-sensitive PPO is, a weak PPO is the most charitable reading of these curves for DPO.

Fourth, DPO is offline and off-policy by construction. It learns from a fixed preference set sampled from πref and never explores on-policy the way reward-model RL can — the very property that Christiano et al. showed matters for avoiding reward hacking. The paper itself flags that the small dip in Figure 3-right "may be an instance of" reward over-optimization but doesn't resolve it. How DPO behaves under distribution shift and over-optimization was left open here — and became exactly the seam that the next wave of work (online/iterative DPO, IPO, KTO, and the RLVR line) pried at.

baselines

Reasonable spread — PPO (own + TRLX impl), oracle PPO-GT, Best-of-N, Preferred-FT, Unlikelihood, SFT, 0/2-shot prompting. But the load-bearing dialogue comparison swaps real PPO for a Best-of-128 proxy, and the public PPO checkpoint underperforms even base Pythia.

contamination

Not a real risk here — DPO is a training method, evaluated by win rate on held-out test prompts; IMDb labels come from a sentiment classifier, not leaked gold answers.

what's missed

Scale (≤6B), multi-turn and instruction-following, safety at scale, on-policy exploration, and the over-optimization behaviour of the implicit reward. GPT-4-judge verbosity bias is acknowledged but not neutralized.

would raise confidence

A fully-tuned PPO head-to-head at frontier scale, larger models, a full pairwise human matrix, and explicit reward-over-optimization curves for DPO's implicit reward across more than one distribution shift.

// bottom line

DPO is the rare result that is both theoretically clean and immediately practical: it collapses the reward-model-plus-PPO stack into one supervised loss and matches PPO in everything it tested. The 2023 evidence was small-scale and leaned on a biased GPT-4 judge and a Best-of-128 stand-in for PPO, so the paper promised more than it proved. Then the field ran the large-scale experiment for it — DPO became the default preference-tuning recipe (Zephyr, the Llama-2-era chat models, TÜLU 3) — which is about the strongest vindication a method can get, even as later work charted where its offline, off-policy nature bites.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. The trick is a change of variables: the KL-constrained RLHF optimum has a closed form, and because Bradley–Terry only sees reward differences, the intractable partition function cancels — turning "fit a reward model, then run PPO" into a single binary cross-entropy loss on preference pairs.
  2. Your LM is secretly a reward model: the implicit reward is r̂(x,y)=β·log[πθ(y|x)/πref(y|x)]. The per-example weight σ(r̂l−r̂w) concentrates gradient on mis-ranked pairs and is what keeps the update from degenerating.
  3. At ≤6B parameters DPO matched or beat PPO on sentiment, summarization, and dialogue with no RL loop, no reward model, and almost no tuning — then scaled into the default alignment recipe, even as its offline/off-policy limits drew the next generation of preference-optimization research.
