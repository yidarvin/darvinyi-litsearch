ReAct: Synergizing Reasoning and Acting in Language Models

Let a frozen LLM interleave free-form thoughts with grounded actions — reason to plan, act to fetch facts — and a handful of in-context examples turns it into an agent. The template under almost every modern reasoning-and-acting scaffold.

Shunyu Yao, Jeffrey Zhao, Dian Yu, Nan Du, Izhak Shafran, Karthik Narasimhan, Yuan Cao

What problem is this solving?

By late 2022 large language models could do two things impressively — but only one at a time. Chain-of-thought prompting (Wei et al., 2022) showed they could reason their way through arithmetic, commonsense, and symbolic tasks by narrating intermediate steps. Separately, a line of work used LLMs to act — generating plans or domain-specific actions for embodied and web agents (SayCan, WebGPT, Gato, Inner Monologue). The two literatures barely touched.

That split has a cost. Chain-of-thought is, in the authors' phrase, a "static black box": the model reasons purely from its own frozen internal representations, with no way to consult the world. So it hallucinates facts and propagates errors down a reasoning chain it can't check. Action-only agents have the opposite problem: they react to observations but never reason abstractly about high-level goals or hold a working memory, so they lose the plot on long-horizon tasks — failing to decompose a goal into subgoals, or forgetting what they've already tried.

Humans don't work either way in isolation. We weave inner speech through our actions — "now that everything is cut, I should heat the pot"; "I'm out of salt, use soy sauce instead." That tight loop between acting and reasoning is what lets us plan, handle exceptions, and decide when we need to go look something up. The open question ReAct asks: can you get an LLM to do the same in one interleaved trace, and does the combination beat either half alone?

// how it works

The method

The idea is almost embarrassingly simple. An agent normally picks an action at from an action space A given its context ct (the history of observations and actions). ReAct augments the action space with language itself: Â = A ∪ L. An action drawn from the language space L is a thought — a reasoning trace. A thought changes nothing in the environment and returns no observation; it only updates the context the model carries forward.

Â = A ∪ L      ât ∈ L : ct+1 = (ct, ât) // a "thought" is an action in language space — it yields no observation, it just rewrites the context for the next step

So a trajectory becomes a stream of thought → action → observation steps. Thoughts do real work: they decompose a goal ("I need to search x, find y, then z"), inject commonsense ("desklamps are usually on desks or dressers"), extract the relevant bit from a noisy observation, track progress, reformulate a failed search, and handle exceptions. Crucially, none of this requires new training — the whole thing runs on a frozen PaLM-540B prompted with a few human-written exemplars (1–6, depending on the task). Annotating a ReAct prompt is just typing your thoughts on top of the actions you'd take.

The thought schedule is tuned to the task. For knowledge-intensive reasoning (HotpotQA multi-hop QA, Fever fact verification) thoughts are dense — one before nearly every action — and the action space is a deliberately weak Wikipedia API: search[entity] (first five sentences of a page, or top-5 lookalikes), lookup[string] (the Ctrl-F next match), and finish[answer]. For decision-making tasks with long action sequences (ALFWorld text games, WebShop online shopping) thoughts are sparse — the model decides for itself when a thought is worth emitting.

Because ReAct and CoT have complementary failure modes (more on that below), the authors also propose letting the model switch between them: ReAct → CoT-SC falls back to chain-of-thought with self-consistency when ReAct can't finish within 7 (HotpotQA) or 5 (Fever) steps; CoT-SC → ReAct falls back to grounded retrieval when the self-consistency vote is weak (the majority answer wins fewer than n/2 samples, i.e. internal knowledge is shaky). Finally, they show ReAct isn't prompt-only: bootstrapping 3,000 ReAct trajectories that reached correct answers and fine-tuning smaller PaLM-8B/62B models on them flips the small-model story entirely.

// what they found

The results

On the two knowledge tasks, the headline is nuanced. With PaLM-540B, ReAct beats action-only prompting on both HotpotQA (27.4 vs 25.7 EM) and Fever (60.9 vs 58.9 accuracy) — reasoning helps acting. ReAct also beats CoT on Fever (60.9 vs 56.3), where verifying a claim demands an up-to-date fact. But on HotpotQA, plain ReAct actually trails both CoT (29.4) and even Standard prompting (28.7). The wins come from combining: ReAct→CoT-SC reaches 35.1 EM on HotpotQA and CoT-SC→ReAct reaches 64.6 accuracy on Fever — the best prompting numbers in the paper, though all of them sit far below the supervised state of the art (67.5 / 89.5).

A hand-labeled error study (50 trajectories each from ReAct and CoT, correct and incorrect, 200 total) explains why. Hallucination is CoT's signature failure: it accounts for 56% of CoT's errors and gives it a 14% false-positive rate among "successes," versus 0% and 6% for ReAct. Grounding in retrieved text largely cures that. The trade is flexibility: ReAct's rigid thought-act-observation structure produces more reasoning errors (47% vs CoT's 16%), including a distinctive failure where the model loops, regenerating the same thought and action. And 23% of ReAct's failures are simply uninformative searches it can't recover from.

The decision-making results are where ReAct made its name. On ALFWorld, the best of 6 prompt permutations reaches a 71% average success rate — beating the best Act-only prompt (45%), the imitation-learned BUTLER baseline (37%), and an Inner-Monologue-style ablation (ReAct-IM, 53%). Even ReAct's worst trial (48%) beats everyone else's best, and the ReAct-over-Act advantage holds across all six trials (relative gains of 33–90%, averaging 62%). On WebShop, one- to two-shot ReAct scores 66.6 with a 40.0% success rate, versus 59.9 / 29.1% for imitation learning and 62.4 / 28.7% for IL+RL — an absolute +10% success rate over methods trained on 10³–10⁴ trajectories, though still well short of expert humans (82.1 / 59.6%).

An appendix robustness check swaps the base model: GPT-3 (text-davinci-002) consistently beats PaLM-540B with the same ReAct prompts — 30.8 vs 29.4 EM on HotpotQA and 78.4 vs 70.9% on ALFWorld — suggesting the paradigm, not the particular model, is doing the work.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

ReAct's influence is enormous and earned — but the original evidence is thinner than the legend, and worth reading carefully. Start with the marquee claim. The abstract advertises an absolute +34% success rate on ALFWorld, but that is the best of 6 prompt permutations (71%) measured against the imitation baseline (37%). ReAct's average success rate is 57%, so the fair gap over BUTLER is closer to ~20 points. Reporting best-of-k as the headline, while the average lives in the body, flatters the result.

Second, on its own flagship QA benchmark the flagship method loses. Plain ReAct scores 27.4 EM on HotpotQA — below CoT (29.4) and below even Standard prompting (28.7). The "synergy" only appears once you graft ReAct onto CoT with self-consistency. That's a legitimate and useful finding, but it means the clean story ("reasoning + acting beats either alone") doesn't hold for the headline task in its pure form; it needs a hand-designed fallback heuristic to win.

Third, the retriever is a toy by design. The Wikipedia API only fetches the first five sentences by exact title, which the authors concede is "significantly weaker than state-of-the-art lexical or neural retrievers." Roughly a quarter of ReAct's failures are uninformative searches. So the absolute QA numbers are bottlenecked by the retrieval interface, not just the reasoning — and the gap to supervised systems (67.5 EM) stays cavernous. Finally, leakage cuts the other way: HotpotQA and Fever are 2018 datasets in PaLM's training window, so CoT's strong closed-book showing partly reflects memorization (the paper even flags stale gold labels on HotpotQA), and exact-match undercounts correct answers — 28–29% of cases are "label ambiguity," right answer, wrong string.

baselines

Strong and well-ablated within each task (Standard / CoT / CoT-SC / Act / IM-style), but a single base model (PaLM-540B) carries all main results; GPT-3 appears only as an appendix robustness check.

contamination

HotpotQA & Fever (2018) predate PaLM's crawl, so the closed-book CoT baseline benefits from memorization; the paper itself notes outdated HotpotQA gold labels.

what's missed

Headline ALFWorld/WebShop gains are best-of-6 prompt selection; the average is materially lower. Eval sets are small (134 ALFWorld games; 200 hand-labeled QA trajectories; 500 sampled HotpotQA questions for GPT-3).

would raise confidence

More base models, a real retriever to disentangle reasoning from retrieval, contamination-controlled QA, larger human-eval samples, and reporting average rather than best-of-k for the decision-task headlines.

// bottom line

ReAct's durable contribution is the paradigm, not its 2022 scores. Interleaving free-form thoughts with grounded, environment-changing actions became the default way to build an LLM agent — every tool-use scaffold, every "think then act" loop descends from it. The benchmark numbers that introduced it are modest, single-model, and partly inflated by best-of-k selection; judge the paper by the idea it seeded, which the field then validated far beyond these four tasks.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. One trick: actions in language space. Augment the action space with "thoughts" that change nothing but the model's own context (Â = A ∪ L), and interleave them with real actions. No fine-tuning required — a frozen LLM and a few exemplars. This is the scaffold modern agents inherited.
  2. Grounding kills hallucination but costs flexibility. Retrieving facts drops CoT's 56%-of-failures hallucination problem to ~0, but the rigid thought-act-observation loop raises reasoning errors (47% vs 16%) and can get stuck looping. The best results combine ReAct with CoT self-consistency rather than picking one.
  3. A few examples beat trained agents — read the asterisks. One-to-two-shot ReAct outperforms imitation/RL agents trained on 10³–10⁵ trajectories (+34% ALFWorld, +10% WebShop), but those headline gains are best-of-6 prompt selection on a single base model over small eval sets. The paradigm generalized; the original numbers were a modest proof of concept.
