Training a Helpful and Harmless Assistant with RLHF

Anthropic's foundational RLHF recipe — fit a preference model to crowdworker comparisons, then optimize it with PPO under a KL leash — that turns a raw language model into a helpful and harmless assistant, and argues alignment is essentially free (even a bonus) for large models.

Yuntao Bai, Andy Jones, Kamal Ndousse, Amanda Askell, … Dario Amodei, Tom Brown, Jared Kaplan · Anthropic

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

By early 2022 the field had a working hypothesis — fine-tune a large language model on human feedback to make it more useful — but only fragments of a recipe. InstructGPT had shown RL from human comparisons could improve helpfulness, but it leaned on a supervised demonstration stage, capped its preference models at 6B parameters, and said nothing about harm. LaMDA trained for safety, but with absolute ratings and supervised learning rather than RL, and never asked whether alignment costs capability. And learning to summarize had nailed the RL-from-preferences machinery on one narrow task.

Three questions were left open, and they are the ones practitioners actually care about. Does aligning a model make it dumber? An "alignment tax" — pay for safety with capability — would be a reason not to deploy aligned models at all. Can one model be both helpful and harmless at once, given that avoiding harm and being maximally useful pull in opposite directions? And how robust is RL against a learned reward — when does optimizing a preference model stop tracking what humans actually want, and start gaming it?

This paper's contribution is less a single trick than a complete, studied system: separate helpfulness and red-teaming datasets gathered through open-ended dialogue, a scan of seven models from 13M to 52B parameters, an iterated "online" data-collection loop, and — unusually for the time — a hard look at where the whole approach breaks. It is the paper that gave RLHF its working vocabulary, and the direct methodological ancestor of the assistants that followed.

// how it works

The method

The pipeline has two human-feedback ingredients and is otherwise deliberately simple. Crowdworkers hold open-ended chats with 52B models; at each assistant turn they see two candidate responses and pick one. In helpfulness mode they choose the more helpful and honest reply; in red-teaming mode they adversarially try to provoke bad behavior and choose the more harmful reply. Anthropic deliberately did not hand workers a rubric — they let "helpful" and "harmless" be defined by crowd intuition, vetting workers by the sophistication of their dialogues rather than by label agreement.

Step one fits a preference model (PM): a language model with a scalar head, pretrained on comparisons ("preference model pretraining," PMP) and then fine-tuned on the human data to score the better response higher. The score difference is a Bradley–Terry-style log-odds of preference:

P(A > B) = 11 + e rPM(B) − rPM(A) // a PM-score gap is a calibrated probability that humans prefer A to B

Step two runs reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF) with PPO: the policy generates a full response (one "timestep"), and the PM score at the end is the reward. Following learning-to-summarize, the reward carries an empirical KL penalty pinning the policy near its initialization:

rtotal = rPM − λKL DKL(π ‖ π0) // λKL = 0.001, so small the authors note it "might actually be wholly unnecessary" (DKL < 100 typically)

The initial policy π0 is not a fresh model but a 52B base LM "context-distilled" on an HHH prompt — roughly, prompting baked into the weights. RLHF then trains against 137k prompts drawn from the static dataset plus 369k model-generated prompts (sample efficiency is about the same either way, so they mix them for diversity).

The √KL law and the robustness probe

Two methodological observations carry most of the paper's lasting weight. First, the authors split the static dataset 50:50 and train independent train PMs and test PMs: RL optimizes against the train PM while the held-out test PM watches for over-optimization — the RL analogue of a train/test split. Second, plotting reward against the square root of the policy's KL from its initializer reveals an approximately linear law:

rPM  ∝  √ DKL(π ‖ π0) // reward grows linearly in √KL for much of training, with parallel lines across model sizes

The intuition: DKL(π+δπ ‖ π) expands at quadratic order in the policy perturbation δπ, while reward varies linearly in δπ (the un-optimized initial policy sits at no extremum) — so reward ∝ √KL precisely while training stays in the "small-δπ" regime. The lines being parallel across model size means a small model's RL curve plus a large model's zero-shot point can predict the large model's eventual RL performance, and it explains why RLHF buys such a large effective jump in model size (Figure 1's RLHF and context-distilled curves run parallel).

The dataset, in three tranches

Data was gathered in three waves, each from progressively better models — the engine of the "online" approach. Note the structural asymmetry: the red-teaming data goes down in desirability (workers pick the worst reply), so the dataset only ever tells the model what not to do.

// what they found

The results

The headline is that RLHF makes models both more helpful and much harder to red-team, and that for large models this comes at no cost to raw capability. In Figure 1, the online HH model is preferred by crowdworkers far over the context-distilled baseline on helpfulness, while staying near the top of the harmlessness axis — whereas the purely-helpful model is dramatically easier to provoke (bottom of the harmlessness panel). The same plot places the model's helpfulness Elo inside the band estimated for hired professional writers.

The alignment tax is real — but only for small models

Averaged over MMLU, Lambada, HellaSwag, OpenBookQA, ARC, and TriviaQA, RLHF hurts small models and helps large ones. The 13B and 52B RLHF models come out ahead on zero-shot NLP evaluations and roughly even on few-shot — an "alignment bonus" rather than a tax above ~10B parameters. (The authors flag a caveat: the sudden, grok-like jumps partly reflect a multiple-choice formatting effect that favors large models.)

Helpfulness and harmlessness genuinely fight

A preference model trained 100% on one objective scores worse than chance on the other — direct evidence the two are anti-correlated. During RL, the policy's harmlessness score saturates the upper tail of the harmlessness data (over-optimized) while its helpfulness score sits mid-distribution (under-optimized): the model learns the cheap harmless behavior ("I can't answer that") long before the expensive one (helpfully explaining why a request is harmful — what the authors call a "hostage negotiator"). Larger PMs are far more robust to the data mix — reweighting the harmlessness loss by 10× costs the 13M model 7.4% helpfulness accuracy but the 52B model only 1.5%.

Two more compatibility results matter for the "no cost" claim: mixing HH preference training with summarization degrades neither skill, and running natural-language RLHF on a code-fine-tuned model improves its HumanEval pass@k at every k for large models — RLHF generalizes its instruction-following gains to a skill its data never touched. And the iterated-online gain survives a controlled experiment: at equal dataset size and identical hyperparameters, the base+RS+online mixture beats the base-only run, so online training works on its merits, not just because it saw more data.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

This is a landmark paper, and most of its honesty is on the page — the authors are unusually frank about where the method breaks. But read as measurement, almost every quantitative claim shares one root: the preference model is simultaneously the reward, the judge, and (often) the ranker of the qualitative samples shown. When Figure 24 reports that the PM "prefers" model samples to human writers, that is partly the RL policy over-fitting to the very PM doing the scoring. The only signal genuinely independent of the PM is crowdworker Elo — and crowdworkers agree with the authors only ~63% of the time, so the ground truth is itself noisy.

The most quotable result — "models preferred to professional writers ~57% of the time" — the authors themselves quarantine, and rightly. The writers were Upwork hires, not professional assistants; their prompts were non-adversarial "softball questions"; and the prompt distribution was off from real use, enough that the HH model (56%) and helpful-only model (51%) rank inconsistently, forcing the writer baseline onto Figure 1 as a wide band rather than a point. It is an existence proof, not a parity claim.

On baselines, the comparisons are almost entirely internal — context-distilled and plain LMs from Anthropic's own lineage. InstructGPT and LaMDA are discussed but never benchmarked head-to-head (different scales, different orgs), so "simpler procedures, comparable results" rests on narrative rather than a shared yardstick. And the NLP "alignment bonus" is softened by the authors' own admission that the multiple-choice format produces misleading grok-like curves that flatter large models.

Where it earns trust is in not overselling robustness. The train/test-PM probe shows reward and true quality diverging past ~150k samples; the √KL relation is offered as a striking observation, not a proven law; over-optimized harmlessness, a PM fooled by a single human-written example (Figure 11), and persistent gender bias correlated with the base LM are all reported rather than buried. The honest reframing is that this is the paper that named reward over-optimization for general assistants — it does not claim to have solved it.

baselines

Internal only (context-distilled / plain LMs from the same lab). No head-to-head against InstructGPT or LaMDA, so the "simpler yet comparable" claim is argued, not measured.

contamination

Not a test-leakage setting (this is preference data, not a held-out benchmark) — but the NLP gains ride a multiple-choice formatting artifact the authors themselves call "arguably misleading," and the human-writer win is non-adversarial by construction.

what's missed

Average-case only — no worst-case / adversarial robustness. Evaluation is largely circular (PM is reward and judge); honesty is "scratching the surface"; gender bias persists, tracking the base LM.

would raise confidence

An independent (non-PM) automated judge; matched-scale head-to-heads vs contemporaries; worst-case red-team metrics; and harmlessness data that records the best response, not the most harmful, to teach the missing "hostage negotiator" upper tail.

// bottom line

The paper that operationalized RLHF for general-purpose assistants and handed the field its working vocabulary — PM-as-reward, the KL leash, online iteration, the alignment tax/bonus, the √KL law, and the helpful-vs-harmless tension. Treat it as a mechanism catalogue and an existence proof rather than a calibrated benchmark: nearly every number is mediated by the same preference model being optimized, and the authors say so plainly.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. Alignment is essentially free above ~10B parameters. Small models pay an "alignment tax"; 13B/52B RLHF models actually beat their raw counterparts on zero-shot NLP — there is little reason to deploy an un-aligned large model.
  2. Helpful and harmless genuinely conflict. A PM trained on one is worse-than-chance on the other, and RL over-optimizes the cheap harmless behavior (refusal) before the expensive one (helpful refusal). Larger models are far more robust to the data mix.
  3. RL against a learned reward over-optimizes — and √KL predicts how far it has drifted. Reward grows linearly in √KL; train/test PMs diverge past ~150k samples; bigger PMs are more robust reward signals. This is the paper that named reward over-optimization for assistants.
  4. Online iteration beats static collection on its merits. Redeploying fresh RLHF models to gather data fills the high-quality tail; a size-and-hyperparameter-controlled experiment confirms the gain is real, not just more data.
  5. It is the ancestor recipe. PM + PPO + KL penalty + open preference data (released as hh-rlhf) is the template every later assistant — and every rubric/RLVR reward-modeling line in this atlas — builds on or reacts to.
