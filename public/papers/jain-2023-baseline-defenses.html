Baseline Defenses for Adversarial Attacks Against Aligned Language Models

A systematic transplant of computer vision's detection, preprocessing, and adversarial-training defense playbook onto LLM jailbreaks. Against the leading optimizer attack of the day (GCG), a perplexity filter catches every adversarial suffix and paraphrasing crushes attack success — a reversal of the vision literature, where such simple defenses are routinely broken.

Neel Jain, Avi Schwarzschild, Yuxin Wen, Gowthami Somepalli, John Kirchenbauer, Ping-yeh Chiang, Micah Goldblum, Aniruddha Saha, Jonas Geiping, Tom Goldstein

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

A few weeks before this paper, Zou et al. (2023) showed that Greedy Coordinate Gradient (GCG) could automatically discover a suffix of gibberish tokens that, appended to any harmful request, reliably flips an aligned chatbot from refusal into compliance — and that the suffix transfers from open models to closed commercial ones. That result reframed LLM jailbreaking as exactly the kind of problem computer vision had been fighting for a decade: an optimization-based adversarial attack. But nobody had yet asked the obvious next question — do any of vision's known defenses actually work here?

The computer-vision literature offers three broad defense families: detection (flag suspicious inputs before they reach the model), preprocessing (transform the input to destroy the attack while preserving benign content), and robust/adversarial training (bake resistance into the model's weights). In vision, this arms race has run for years and detection defenses in particular have a poor track record — Carlini & Wagner (2017) showed ten different detectors could all be bypassed by an adaptive white-box attacker, and theory suggests a strong detector should be about as hard to build as a robust classifier in the first place.

The gap this paper targets is narrow and concrete: nobody had systematically tested whether that same arms-race outcome holds for language. Text is discrete rather than continuous, GCG-style attacks cost 5–6 orders of magnitude more model evaluations than an image attack, and there was no established threat model for what "white-box" or "gray-box" even means for a chatbot API. The authors don't claim to invent new defenses — they explicitly frame the work as baselines, representative of each CV defense category, applied as directly as possible to the LLM setting, with both a non-adaptive and a white-box adaptive attacker for each one.

// how it works

The method

The paper first re-derives a threat model suited to text. Classical adversarial ML bounds an attacker by an ℓp-norm perturbation size; that constraint is meaningless for LLMs, since inputs aren't checked by humans for "looking normal" and context windows are effectively unbounded. Instead, the authors propose bounding the attacker by computational budget — number of model evaluations — which is already the natural constraint GCG faces (its suffix costs 513,000 model evaluations spread over two models to optimize). They also argue the field should focus on gray-box robustness (defense internals — the detector, the paraphraser's weights — are secret, but the target LLM's behavior can be probed) rather than the traditional, arguably unrealistic, full white-box standard, since commercial APIs like ChatGPT keep their weights secret by design.

Every defense is tested as a testbed against GCG specifically: a 20-token adversarial suffix, optimized for 500 steps against an ensemble of Vicuna-7B-v1.1 and Guanaco-7B, then evaluated for transfer against five 7B-class chat models (Vicuna, Falcon-7B-Instruct, Guanaco, ChatGLM-6B, MPT-7B-Chat) on 100 AdvBench harmful-behavior prompts. AlpacaEval measures the cost each defense imposes on benign generation quality. Three defense categories are tested:

1. Detection — self-perplexity filtering. GCG's optimized suffixes are, by construction, low-likelihood token sequences, so they spike a language model's own perplexity. The authors test both a plain perplexity threshold and a windowed variant (flag if any 10-token window exceeds a threshold), with the threshold set to the maximum perplexity observed across the AdvBench harmful-prompt dataset itself — so, by design, no harmful prompt without an attack should ever trip the filter. For the white-box adaptive attacker, they add a perplexity term directly into GCG's loss:

Ltrigger = (1 − αppl) Ltarget + αppl Lppl // α_ppl ∈ {0, 0.05, 0.1, 0.2, 0.6, 1.0} — the attacker's own perplexity penalty weight

2. Preprocessing — paraphrasing and retokenization. Paraphrasing feeds the (possibly adversarial) prompt through ChatGPT (gpt-3.5-turbo, temperature 0.7, "paraphrase the following sentences:") before it reaches the target model, hoping the paraphraser reproduces the harmful request but drops the fragile adversarial tokens. Retokenization instead applies BPE-dropout — randomly declining some byte-pair-encoding merges so "studying" might tokenize as "study" + "ing" instead of one token — on the theory that GCG's suffix depends on a very specific tokenization that dropout disrupts.

3. Robust optimization — adversarial training via instruction finetuning. Rather than crafting fresh GCG attacks at every training step (computationally infeasible at LLM scale — a single suffix already takes hours on multiple GPUs), the authors mix human-written harmful prompts from Ganguli et al.'s (2022) Anthropic red-teaming dataset into ordinary instruction finetuning data at a rate β, training the model to refuse them (and, in one variant, also to take an explicit "ascent" step away from a harmful completion).

// what they found

The results

Without any defense, GCG's suffix is potent but uneven across targets: attack success rate (ASR) is 0.79 on Vicuna-7B, 0.70 on Falcon-7B-Instruct, and 0.96 on Guanaco-7B, but only 0.04 on ChatGLM-6B and 0.12 on MPT-7B-Chat. Against that attack, the plain perplexity filter is close to a knockout: every model's PPL Passed and PPL Window Passed rate is 0.00 (one exception, ChatGLM at 0.01) — the filter catches essentially 100% of GCG's gibberish suffixes while, by construction, letting every unattacked AdvBench prompt through.

Under the white-box adaptive attack (a perplexity term folded into GCG's own loss), the defense still holds up surprisingly well. At αppl = 0.1 — the largest weight before the attack stops working at all — only 20% of attacks slip past the windowed filter, which the paper notes is "approximately the same effectiveness as when the attack is not present" (Figure 2). Shortening the suffix to fight perplexity doesn't help much either: a 10-token suffix reaches a 52% average ASR against a 10-token window versus 32% (20 tokens) and 34% (5 tokens) — the best of a bad set of options for the attacker. The cost side is real, though: across the five models, an average of 93.71% of clean AlpacaEval prompts pass the plain filter and 91.13% pass the windowed one — meaning roughly one benign prompt in ten gets falsely flagged, which the authors call "heavy-handed" as a standalone deployment.

On Vicuna, the confusion matrix in Figure 4 shows successful attacks dropping from 74 to just 5 once paraphrasing is applied, and — notably — paraphrasing never converts a previously-failed attack into a success. The cost: about a 10% drop in AlpacaEval performance for Vicuna and Alpaca, and 15% for Guanaco. This defense is not unconditionally robust, though: in a genuine white-box setting, the authors show GCG itself can be re-purposed to attack the paraphraser — optimizing a prompt against a third model (LLaMA-2-7B-chat as a stand-in paraphraser) that, when paraphrased, faithfully reproduces all 10 tokens of the original adversarial string. In the more realistic gray-box case, where the paraphraser's weights are unknown, they report this attack is substantially harder and leave it as future work.

Retokenization is the murkiest result. At the optimal 0.4 dropout rate, Vicuna's AlpacaEval win rate falls from 54.41 to 47.64 and Guanaco's from 53.23 to 48.94 — a real quality cost — while the adaptive white-box attack (a character-level suffix) still drives Vicuna's ASR back down to 0.11 at 0.4 dropout, but on Falcon the adaptive attack actually outperforms the original GCG attack (0.81 vs 0.78 ASR at 0.4 dropout), suggesting the defense's benefit is model-dependent and not uniformly positive.

Adversarial training is the one category that doesn't transfer from vision. Mixing harmful red-teaming prompts into instruction finetuning (β = 0.2) nudges the unattacked refusal rate down only slightly and moves the GCG-attacked success rate by less than 1% — it essentially does not touch the actual attack it's meant to defend against. The obvious fix backfired: because the β = 0.2 ascent-step model degenerated into repeating "cannot"/"harm" for most instructions, the authors tried lowering the mixing rate to 0.1 and 0.05 — but that made things worse, not better. Even at those lower rates the model degenerated further, repeating the same token for almost every prompt, and the authors chose not to report robustness numbers for that setting because the model was "practically unusable."

// does it hold up?

The eval read

The paper is unusually candid about its own scope, and that candor is worth taking at face value — but it also means the "defenses work" headline needs qualifying more than it usually gets in downstream citations. Every defense is tuned and tested against exactly one attack family: GCG, run with one specific configuration (20-token suffix, 500 steps, two-model ensemble). That's a fair choice given GCG was the SOTA optimizer at the time, and the adaptive-attack sections do genuinely stress-test each defense against an attacker aware of it. But it leaves open whether these same filters hold against manually-crafted jailbreaks (DAN-style prompts), against the black-box iterative-refinement attacks that arrived soon after (PAIR, TAP), or against a stronger future optimizer — a limitation the authors themselves flag directly in Section 5.2, noting "This weakness of text optimizers may rapidly change in the future. Or it may not."

The perplexity filter's threshold is calibrated on the same distribution it's evaluated against: T is set to the maximum perplexity of any prompt in the AdvBench harmful-prompts dataset, and then AdvBench prompts are exactly what's used to measure the false-negative rate. That's not train/test leakage in the usual sense — the filter never sees the attack strings during calibration — but it is a form of self-referential tuning: the threshold is picked to make AdvBench pass by construction, so the reported "0% of harmful prompts trigger the filter" result is closer to a sanity check than independent evidence. The false-positive analysis (AlpacaEval, ≈1-in-10 flagged) is a genuinely independent test, but AlpacaEval's instruction-following prompts are a narrow proxy for "benign" — it says little about, say, legitimate security-research questions or violent fiction that might also spike perplexity.

Every target model tops out at 7B parameters (Vicuna, Falcon-Instruct, Guanaco, ChatGLM, MPT-Chat) — there is no evaluation against GPT-3.5, GPT-4, or Claude, despite the paper's own motivating framing being about "commercial models." This matters because perplexity filtering requires access to the target's own token log-probabilities, which most commercial chat APIs simply don't expose; the paper doesn't discuss this deployability gap. Sample sizes are also thin in places — ASR is measured on 100 AdvBench test prompts, and only the BPE-dropout results report any variance (averaged over 4 runs); a <1% shift in adversarial-training ASR is being read as "no effect" without a confidence interval to back that reading.

baselines

Fair for its era — GCG was the genuine state-of-the-art optimizer attack when this was written, and each defense gets both a static and a white-box adaptive evaluation. But it's the only attack family tested; no manual jailbreaks, no black-box iterative methods.

contamination

Not data leakage, but the perplexity threshold is calibrated on the exact AdvBench set it's then evaluated against — the reported 0% false-negative rate on harmful prompts is closer to a self-consistency check than independent validation.

what's missed

No commercial/closed models (GPT-3.5/4, Claude) despite motivating the work around them; no discussion that perplexity filtering needs API-exposed logprobs most chat APIs don't provide; thin sample sizes with almost no variance reporting.

would raise confidence

Test against black-box attacks beyond GCG (PAIR/TAP-style), evaluate on closed commercial models, calibrate the perplexity threshold on a held-out benign corpus distinct from the eval set, and report confidence intervals on the small ASR deltas.

// bottom line

As an honest first look at whether CV's defense playbook survives the jump to language, this holds up well — the authors are careful to frame every result as a baseline against one specific 2023-era attack, and they report their own negative result (adversarial training) without spin. The risk is in how it gets cited downstream: "perplexity filtering stops jailbreaks" is a much stronger claim than what's actually shown here, which is narrower — it stops this specific optimizer's gibberish suffixes on open 7B chat models, evaluated against a threshold tuned on the same benchmark.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. Simple filters punch above their weight against optimizer attacks. A perplexity threshold — nothing more than "does this text look statistically weird to the model itself" — catches essentially all of GCG's gibberish suffixes, and even a white-box attacker trying to fold perplexity into its own loss can't recover much: past αppl ≈ 0.2, attack success collapses to the unattacked baseline.
  2. Paraphrasing is the strongest defense here, but not unconditionally. It cuts Vicuna's ASR from 0.79 to 0.05 with successful attacks on Vicuna dropping from 74 to 5, at a real ≈10–15% cost to benign generation quality — and a genuine white-box attacker can retarget the paraphraser itself, though the harder gray-box case (paraphraser weights unknown) remains open.
  3. Vision's arms race doesn't repeat here — yet. Where CV detectors and preprocessing defenses have a long history of being broken by adaptive attacks, the bottleneck for LLMs is that today's discrete-text optimizers are 5–6 orders of magnitude more expensive than image attacks and can't easily satisfy a combined jailbreak-plus-stealth objective. Whether that gap survives better optimizers is the paper's own open question — and the one that later black-box methods like PAIR and TAP began to press on.
