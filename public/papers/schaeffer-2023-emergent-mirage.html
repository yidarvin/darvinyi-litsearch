Are Emergent Abilities of Large Language Models a Mirage?

The headline “emergence” of large language models — skills that seem to switch on abruptly at some scale — is largely an artifact of how we score them. Swap a harsh all-or-nothing metric for a smooth one and the cliff flattens into a ramp.

Rylan Schaeffer, Brando Miranda, Sanmi Koyejo · Stanford

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

In 2022 a striking claim swept through the field: large language models possess emergent abilities — skills “not present in smaller-scale models but present in large-scale models,” ones you could not have predicted by extrapolating the trend from smaller models (Wei et al.; Ganguli et al.; and the GPT-3 report). What made the claim so arresting was two-fold: sharpness — performance leaping from chance to competence seemingly instantaneously — and unpredictability — the leap arriving at a scale no one could foresee. The idea borrowed its mystique from physics: P. W. Anderson’s “More Is Different,” where new properties materialize as a system grows.

The stakes were not academic. If capabilities can switch on without warning, then a sufficiently large model might one day, unannounced, acquire mastery of something dangerous — which is exactly why emergence became a load-bearing premise in AI-safety and alignment arguments. Yet nobody had asked the most basic measurement question: is the discontinuity in the model, or in the ruler we hold up to it? Schaeffer, Miranda, and Koyejo noticed that the alleged jumps showed up almost exclusively under two unforgiving metrics — Multiple Choice Grade (right answer gets all the probability mass, or it doesn’t) and Exact String Match (the output string matches the target exactly, or it doesn’t). That coincidence is the crack this paper pries open.

// how it works

The method

The alternative explanation is almost embarrassingly simple. Suppose that within a model family the per-token cross-entropy loss falls smoothly with scale — the unremarkable, repeatedly-observed behaviour of neural scaling laws. Model it as a power law in the parameter count N:

LCE(N) = (N / c)α // per-token cross-entropy, with constants c > 0, α < 0 — it decreases smoothly with scale

A smoothly falling per-token loss means the probability of getting any single token right rises smoothly toward 1:

p(single token correct) = exp(−LCE(N)) = exp(−(N / c)α)

Now let the researcher pick a metric that only pays out when a whole sequence of L tokens is correct — exact-match accuracy on, say, an L-digit arithmetic answer. If per-token correctness is roughly independent, the chance of scoring a 1 is the per-token probability raised to the L-th power. That exponent is the whole trick: it turns a smooth curve into one that hugs zero and then whips upward.

Accuracy(N) ≈ p(single token correct)L = exp(−(N / c)α·L) // nonlinear in the per-token error — looks like a sharp, “emergent” cliff on a log-x plot

Keep the model outputs fixed and instead score them with a metric that scales roughly linearly with the per-token error — Token Edit Distance, which just counts how many tokens are wrong — and the same family improves smoothly, continuously, predictably:

Token Edit Distance(N) ≈ L·(1 − exp(−(N / c)α)) // quasi-linear in the per-token error — no cliff, just a ramp

The same swap works for the discontinuous case: Multiple Choice Grade is a step function (a tiny shift in probability mass flips the score from 0 to 1), so it manufactures a discontinuity; replace it with the continuous, strictly-proper Brier Score and the step dissolves. The paper distills the illusion into three interpretable ingredients: (1) a metric that nonlinearly or discontinuously deforms the per-token error rate; (2) too little test data to resolve small-model performance — at resolution 1 / (test-set size), a small model that is merely bad reads as a flat zero; and (3) too few large models sampled to see the smooth trend. None of the three is a fact about the model’s mind; all three are facts about the experiment.

// what they found

The results

Schaeffer et al. test the hypothesis three ways, each a step harder to dismiss.

1 · Query a real model family and change only the metric

They took the publicly-queryable InstructGPT/GPT-3 family — four sizes (350M, 1.3B, 6.7B, 175B parameters, as of March 2023) — on two tasks prominently cited as emergent: 2-shot multiplication of two 2-digit integers and 2-shot addition of two 4-digit integers. Under Accuracy, the family shows the textbook emergent cliff once the target reaches 4–5 digits. Holding those exact outputs fixed and switching to Token Edit Distance, performance instead improves smoothly, continuously, and predictably with scale (Fig. 3). A second prediction also lands: give yourself better statistics by generating more test examples, and the small models turn out to sit above chance all along, with accuracy decaying approximately geometrically in target length — exactly the shape the math predicts for an exponentiated metric.

2 · Meta-analyze the benchmark where emergence was “found”

On BIG-Bench, of the 39 preferred metrics, at most 5 exhibit any emergence at all; on the hand-annotated set of claimed emergent abilities, just 4 / 39 metrics account for them, and a remarkable > 92% of all claimed emergent abilities appear under only two metrics — Multiple Choice Grade (76.1%, discontinuous) and Exact String Match (16.4%, nonlinear) (Fig. 5). If emergence were a property of the model on the task, it would show up across reasonable metrics; instead it clusters on the harsh ones. As a clean confirmation, the LaMDA family’s emergent abilities under Multiple Choice Grade vanish when the same tasks are re-scored with the continuous Brier Score.

3 · Manufacture emergence on demand

The clincher: if emergence is the researcher’s doing, you should be able to induce it where none has ever been reported. They do — in vision, across fully-connected, convolutional, and self-attentional architectures. Shallow autoencoders on CIFAR-100 reduce reconstruction error smoothly, but define a discontinuous “reconstruction” metric (squared error below a threshold c) and a sharp ability springs into being. Autoregressive Transformers classifying Omniglot characters improve smoothly — until you demand that all of a length-L sequence be correct, at which point a brand-new “emergent” classification ability appears (Fig. 8). Same models, same training, manufactured cliff.

The whole result set reduces to one table: on a single, fixed set of model outputs, whether you see “emergence” is decided entirely by the metric you pick to score them.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

This is itself a critique paper, so the eval read has to be turned on the critique. It is an unusually clean piece of measurement science — the control is airtight, because it re-scores the identical model outputs two ways, so nothing about the model changes between “emergent” and “not.” But three things keep “mirage” from being the last word.

Smooth ≠ predictable where it counts. Per-token cross-entropy being smooth does not make the capability you actually ship predictable. A user of an arithmetic system cares whether the answer is exactly right — that is the harsh, nonlinear metric. If the metric you deploy against has a cliff, the surprise is real to you even though the per-token loss was sliding down a ramp the whole time. The paper relabels the discontinuity as living in the metric rather than the model, but for anyone making a go/no-go decision on a downstream task, the planning problem doesn’t dissolve.

The cleanest evidence is the most decomposable task. The mechanism leans on a capability factoring into L roughly-independent tokens, which arithmetic exact-match obeys beautifully — and the authors candidly flag that the independence assumption is false and the match is only qualitative. It is far less obvious that the same decomposition tames every claimed emergent ability: multi-step chain-of-thought reasoning, in-context learning of a genuinely novel task, or instruction following don’t obviously reduce to “get L tokens each independently right.” The strongest direct test touches one model family (GPT-3, only four sizes) on arithmetic; LaMDA and the rest arrive via meta-analysis of published numbers, not fresh re-evaluation. And the paper itself concedes that up to 5 of 39 BIG-Bench metrics still show some emergence — a residue it doesn’t fully chase down.

baselines

Not a head-to-head benchmark but a metric ablation — and the control is the paper’s great strength: the same fixed model outputs are scored two ways, so only the ruler changes. Weakness: only the GPT-3 family (4 sizes) is directly queried; LaMDA and BIG-Bench come from published numbers, not re-runs.

contamination

Leaderboard contamination isn’t the risk here; the relevant analogue is multiple comparisons. BIG-Bench alone is ≥220 tasks × ~40 metrics × ~10 model families ≈ 106 task–metric–model triplets, so some “emergence” is expected by chance — a caution the paper raises against itself and against the original claims.

what's missed

That a smooth per-token loss can still cash out as a genuinely abrupt deployed capability. And whether non-decomposable abilities — CoT reasoning, in-context learning, instruction following — survive the smooth-metric swap, since the mechanism is demonstrated mainly on exact-match arithmetic.

would raise confidence

Re-running the linear/continuous-metric ablation on non-arithmetic emergent tasks and on open frontier model families; and showing the smooth-metric curve actually forecasts the harsh-metric jump out-of-sample — predicting the cliff in advance, not just smoothing it in hindsight.

// bottom line

The paper decisively establishes that a large share of “emergence” is a measurement artifact: a task and its metric are separate design choices, and a nonlinear or discontinuous metric can conjure a cliff out of a smooth trend — a discipline every benchmark builder should now internalize. But “mirage” oversells the dissolution. If the capability you ship is graded by the harsh metric, the surprise is real to your users even though per-token loss was smooth all along. The lasting contribution is methodological hygiene — report graded metrics, resolve small-model performance with enough data, and correct for multiple comparisons — not a proof that scaling never buys anything that matters abruptly.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. Emergence is largely metric-made. A smooth per-token improvement, scored by a metric that demands many tokens all-correct (exact match) or crosses a hard threshold (multiple-choice grade), reads as a sudden cliff — and > 92% of BIG-Bench’s claimed emergent abilities sit under just those two metric types.
  2. The fix is mechanical. Hold the model outputs fixed and swap to a linear (Token Edit Distance) or continuous (Brier Score) metric, or simply collect enough test data to resolve small-model performance, and the cliff becomes a predictable ramp — shown on the GPT-3 family and even manufactured on demand in vision networks.
  3. But smooth isn’t the same as predictable-where-it-counts. The metric you actually deploy is often the harsh one, so emergence can still be a real surprise downstream. The durable lesson is to treat task and metric as distinct choices and to report graded metrics with adequate statistics.
