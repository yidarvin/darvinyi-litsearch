HealthBench: Evaluating Large Language Models Towards Improved Human Health

OpenAI built health's answer to a rubric benchmark: 5,000 realistic, multi-turn conversations graded against 48,562 criteria written by 262 physicians from 60 countries. Open-ended responses, not multiple choice. o3 tops out at 0.60 (HealthBench Hard: 0.32), GPT-4.1 nano beats 2024's GPT-4o at 1/25th the cost, frontier models now out-write unassisted physicians — and a GPT-4.1 grader agrees with doctors about as well as doctors agree with each other.

Rahul K. Arora, Jason Wei, Rebecca Soskin Hicks, Preston Bowman, … Alex Beutel, Johannes Heidecke, Karan Singhal · OpenAI

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

People already ask LLMs about their symptoms, their lab results, and their loved ones' diagnoses — millions of times a day, often in places where a clinician is hours or oceans away. That is exactly the use case where a confidently wrong answer can do the most damage, and exactly the use case existing health benchmarks measured worst. The dominant evals were medical-licensing-style multiple choice (USMLE-derived sets, MedQA, PubMedQA): a model picks A, B, C, or D, and we call it a doctor. Real health conversations are open-ended, multi-turn, and underspecified — a user who buries the emergency in the third message, a nurse asking for a discharge note, a parent in a low-resource setting with a feverish baby.

HealthBench frames the gap as three failures of prior evaluations. Meaningful: multiple-choice scores don't reflect real-world impact, because the task isn't multiple choice. Trustworthy: most health evals were never validated against actual physician judgment, so a high score is an article of faith. Unsaturated: the canonical exams are effectively solved — when GPT-4-class models score in the high 80s or 90s, the benchmark stops telling you which model is safer to deploy. A benchmark that is none of these three can still produce a leaderboard; it just can't ground a decision about whether to put a model in front of a patient.

The hard part is grading. Once you let the model write a paragraph instead of picking a letter, exact-match scoring dies and you're left with weakly-correlated NLG metrics or slow human review. HealthBench's bet is that the right unit of ground truth is not a gold answer but a physician-written rubric — a per-conversation checklist of specific, objective things a good response should and shouldn't do — and that a carefully validated model grader can apply those rubrics at scale.

// how it works

The method

HealthBench is 5,000 conversations between a user and a model, each ending on a user message the model must answer. Conversations average 2.6 turns and 668 characters (ranging up to 19 turns and 9,853 characters). Most are synthetic: physicians enumerated the situations that matter — "a user reveals over several turns that they're having an emergency," "a clinician asks for a note summary," "a query so underspecified it needs clarification" — and a language-model pipeline turned each situation into a realistic dialogue, filtered for realism with o1-preview. The rest comes from physician red-teaming and from rewrites of Google's HealthSearchQA queries.

Every conversation carries its own rubric: a set of self-contained criteria, each worth positive or negative points, written by a physician for that specific exchange. A criterion might read "states that this presentation warrants immediate emergency care" (+points) or "fails to ask about the duration of chest pain" or "recommends an unproven remedy" (−points). The median example has 11 criteria; examples range from 2 to 48. In total HealthBench grades against 48,562 unique criteria. A model-based grader reads the conversation, the response, and one criterion at a time and decides, met or not met — full points or none.

An example's score is the points it earned divided by the maximum points available on its rubric — and because negative criteria exist, a single response can score below zero. The overall HealthBench score is the mean of per-example scores, clipped to [0, 1].

scoreexample = Σi ∈ met wiΣi ∈ positive wi   ·   HealthBench = clip[0,1]( meanexamples scoreexample ) // wi are signed criterion weights; met negative criteria subtract from the numerator

Two orthogonal taxonomies make the single number diagnosable. Themes (seven of them) group conversations by real-world task; axes (five) classify what each criterion is testing. The split below explains a lot of the later results — nearly 40% of all criteria are about completeness, so the overall score leans heavily on whether a response includes everything it should.

Two variants sharpen the tool. HealthBench Consensus pulls out 34 pre-written criteria (e.g., "promptly tells the user to seek emergency care") that are assigned to a conversation only when two or more physicians agree they apply — appearing 8,053 times across the dataset — giving low-noise, safety-critical sub-scores plus physician grades for meta-evaluation. HealthBench Hard is the 1,000 hardest examples, where many current models score zero. The whole cohort effort ran 11 months across 262 physicians (winnowed from 1,021 applicants), spanning 26 specialties and 49 languages.

// what they found

The results

HealthBench is far from saturated and it moves fast. Overall scores run from 0.16 (GPT-3.5 Turbo) to 0.60 (o3), with GPT-4o (Aug 2024) at 0.32 in between. The frontier's most recent jump — roughly GPT-4.1's 0.48 to o3's 0.60 — is a bigger step for safety and performance than the entire GPT-3.5-to-GPT-4o transition that preceded it. Crucially, headroom remains everywhere: on HealthBench Hard the top model, o3, manages only 0.32.

The cross-vendor picture (Figure 5) puts o3 first at 0.60, then Grok 3 (0.54), Gemini 2.5 Pro / Mar 2025 (0.52), GPT-4.1 (0.48), and o1 (0.42), with Claude 3.7 Sonnet w/ extended thinking (0.35), GPT-4o (0.32), and Llama 4 Maverick (0.25) trailing. Performance is uneven by theme: models do best on emergency referrals and expertise-tailored communication and worst on context-seeking, health data tasks, and global health — i.e., they answer well but are reluctant to ask the clarifying question a real clinician would. By axis, the weak spots are completeness and context awareness; since completeness is ~40% of criteria, the model that wins is usually the one that omits the least.

Three secondary findings matter for anyone shipping a product. Cost collapsed: GPT-4.1 nano outperforms August 2024's GPT-4o while being 25× cheaper, and the April 2025 models (o3, o4-mini, GPT-4.1) define a new Pareto frontier of score-per-dollar — the most relevant axis for low-resource health settings. Reliability still lags the average: under "worst-at-k" (the minimum score over k samples), o3's worst-at-16 is cut by a third relative to its 0.60 mean, even as it more than doubles GPT-4o's worst-case — one bad answer in a safety-critical domain can outweigh many good ones. And the gains are not just verbosity: length explains little within a model (Pearson r = −0.053 for GPT-4o, 0.123 for o3), and o3 still beats GPT-4.1 63.7% of the time even when only comparing responses of similar length (vs 72.9% uncontrolled). Run-to-run variance is tiny — standard deviation ≈0.002 across 16 full runs.

Models vs. physicians

As a human baseline, physicians wrote their own ideal responses to HealthBench tasks under three conditions. Unassisted ("de novo"), they scored just 0.13 — below every frontier model — though the authors caution this partly reflects an unnatural task (doctors don't normally compose long chat replies) and the brevity of what they wrote. The more interesting result is whether physicians could improve a model's draft when shown four references. For the older September 2024 models they could: physician edits scored 0.31 vs the references' 0.28, improving more often than worsening (56.2% vs 39.8%). For the April 2025 models they could not — physician responses (0.48) essentially matched the references (0.49), and were as likely to make them worse as better (47.7% vs 46.8%).

Is the grader trustworthy?

The whole benchmark rests on whether the model grader matches physician judgment, so HealthBench meta-evaluates it on the 34 consensus criteria, for which physicians supplied their own met/not-met grades — over 60,896 grading instances (avg 1,791 per criterion). Scoring each grader the way a physician is scored against peers, GPT-4.1 reaches Macro-F1 0.709, ahead of o4-mini (0.692), o3 (0.681), GPT-4.1 mini (0.661) and nano (0.580), all above the random baseline of 0.50. Against the physician cohort, GPT-4.1 beats the average physician in 5 of 7 themes, sits in the upper half in 6 of 7, and clears the 33rd percentile in all 7. Because it hits physician-level agreement without a reasoning model's cost, GPT-4.1 is adopted as the default grader. The honest caveat is in the same data: physician-physician agreement itself only ranges 55–75%, so "expert ground truth" here is genuinely noisy.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

HealthBench is the strongest open health eval to date, and it earns that on craft: a real physician cohort, a sensible meaning/trust/unsaturated framing, an explicit meta-evaluation, a canary string and private held-out set, and a clipped-mean metric whose variance they actually measured. The places to push are mostly about who built and grades it, and about what a rubric mean can and can't say.

The sharpest concern is a closed loop: an OpenAI benchmark, graded by an OpenAI model (GPT-4.1), on which OpenAI models lead. The meta-evaluation is the right instinct, but it validates the grader only on the 34 consensus criteria — 14% of grading instances, deliberately pre-written to be "objectively gradable." The other 86% of scoring runs on example-specific criteria authored by a single physician and, by the paper's own admission, not cross-validated. So grader fidelity is demonstrated precisely where grading is easiest and unmeasured where most of the score is made. A non-OpenAI grader cross-check would do a lot to rule out same-family bias — its absence is the single biggest open question.

The headline metric also flatters recall. With completeness at ~40% of criteria and accuracy at 33%, the overall score is dominated by "did you include all the right facts," which is why the authors had to spend a whole section showing length isn't the driver. They make that case reasonably (length-controlled win rates stay high), but a clipped criteria-mean still rewards the long, hedge-everything answer over the crisp, correct one — the opposite of good clinical communication, and arguably why GPT-style models that "say more" do well. The "models beat physicians" line should be read narrowly: unassisted physicians scored 0.13 on a task they never do, writing deliberately short answers, under a metric tilted toward completeness. The authors flag this themselves; downstream coverage usually doesn't.

On contamination they did the responsible things, but synthetic generation cuts both ways: conversations produced by a language-model pipeline may encode what LLMs think patient messages look like, subtly favoring models from similar lineages, and may under-sample the genuinely messy, code-switched, low-literacy queries the global-health framing cares most about. And the benchmark scores single responses to multi-turn chats — it measures neither full clinical workflows nor real health outcomes, which the paper is careful to say is future work. A 0.60 is a measure of rubric coverage, not of whether anyone got better care.

baselines

Current and fair across vendors (o3, GPT-4.1, Claude 3.7 Sonnet w/ extended thinking, Gemini 2.5 Pro, Grok 3, Llama 4 Maverick). But the OpenAI family both leads and supplies the grader; no non-OpenAI grader is reported.

contamination

Handled well — canary string, private held-out set, mostly synthetic data, request not to post examples. Residual risk: LM-generated dialogues may bias toward LM-like phrasing and similar model families.

what's missed

Grader fidelity is proven only on 14% of criteria (the consensus set); the 86% example-specific criteria are single-author, uncross-validated. Score is completeness-weighted (≈40%), rewarding longer answers; single-response, not workflow or outcome.

would raise confidence

A non-OpenAI cross-grader; meta-evaluation on example-specific criteria; reporting grader variance off the consensus set; and real-world workflow/outcome studies tied back to rubric scores.

// bottom line

A genuinely better way to measure health LLMs — open, expert-grounded, unsaturated, and meta-evaluated — that has rightly become a default. Just remember the score is a physician-rubric coverage average graded by GPT-4.1, validated on its easiest 14%: trust the relative trajectory and the headroom (o3 at 0.60 overall, 0.32 Hard) more than any single absolute number, and treat "beats physicians" as a statement about a task physicians don't normally do.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. Rubrics, not multiple choice. 5,000 open-ended health conversations graded against 48,562 physician-written criteria — the template for meaningful, unsaturated evaluation of long-form answers, and a direct ancestor of the rubric-benchmark wave (PRBench, ResearchRubrics, TutorBench, SciPredict).
  2. Fast progress, lots of headroom. Overall scores climbed 0.16 → 0.60 (GPT-3.5 Turbo → o3), GPT-4.1 nano beats 2024's GPT-4o at 1/25th the cost, yet HealthBench Hard still tops out at 0.32 and worst-at-k reliability lags the mean.
  3. The model grader is the load-bearing assumption. GPT-4.1 matches physician agreement (Macro-F1 0.709, beating average physicians in 5/7 themes) — but only validated on the 34 consensus criteria, while 86% of scoring runs on un-cross-validated example-specific rubrics graded by an OpenAI model on a benchmark OpenAI models top.
