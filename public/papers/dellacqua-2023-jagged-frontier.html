Navigating the Jagged Technological Frontier: Field Experimental Evidence of the Effects of AI on Knowledge Worker Productivity and Quality

A pre-registered field experiment with 758 Boston Consulting Group consultants, randomized to no AI, GPT-4, or GPT-4 plus a prompt-engineering overview. On realistic tasks judged to sit inside GPT-4's capability frontier, AI-assisted consultants completed 12.2% more subtasks, worked 25.1% faster, and scored 40%+ higher on quality. On a task engineered to sit outside that frontier, correct answers fell from 84.5% (no AI) to 60–70.6% (with AI) — the same tool, in the same workflow, both a booster and a disruptor depending on which side of an invisible line the task falls on.

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

By mid-2023, early field evidence — including Peng et al.'s GitHub Copilot trial and Noy & Zhang's writing-task experiment — had shown that large language models could meaningfully speed up narrow, well-defined tasks. What remained unclear was how AI performs across a full, realistic knowledge-work workflow, done by genuinely elite professionals, where some subtasks are obviously well-suited to a language model and others are not. Prior debates about AI and expertise were fought largely in the abstract, or on older, narrower forms of machine learning that made discrete predictions from structured data — not on general-purpose LLMs that can attempt almost anything, well or badly, without any obvious warning sign to the user.

The authors frame the core problem as a "jagged technological frontier": AI capability doesn't form a clean boundary that neatly separates "hard for humans, easy for AI" from the reverse. Two tasks that look equally difficult to a human professional can sit on opposite sides of what GPT-4 can actually do well, and — critically — that boundary isn't visible to the person using the tool. If professionals can't tell which side of the frontier they're standing on, then the practical question isn't "is AI good or bad for knowledge work" but "what happens when skilled people can't see the line, and how much does that cost them when they get it wrong." Testing that required a task realistic and consequential enough that getting it wrong would actually look like getting it wrong — which is why the authors partnered with a real management consultancy rather than running the study on crowdworkers or students.

// how it works

Two pre-registered experiments inside one consulting firm

The authors partnered with Boston Consulting Group to design and run two pre-registered (OSF, IRB23-0392), randomized field experiments with 758 individual-contributor consultants — about 7% of BCG's global cohort at that level — who volunteered to spend roughly 5 hours on the study, with performance incentives tied to their annual review. Everyone first completed a baseline "assessment task" without AI, giving the authors a within-subject skill measure; participants were then randomly assigned to one of three conditions: no AI, GPT-4 only, or GPT-4 plus a prompt-engineering overview (extra training on how to use it well). All AI conditions used the same GPT-4 snapshot, accessed through a custom company platform, "as available at the end of April, 2023."

The "jagged frontier" itself is illustrated conceptually in Figure 1 below: the blue curve is GPT-4's actual capability across a spectrum of tasks, the dashed line is human-perceived task difficulty, and the point is that the two don't track each other — a task inside the frontier (AI does it well) can sit right next to one outside it (AI does it badly), even though a human would judge them similarly hard.

385 consultants were assigned to the "inside the frontier" experiment: a creative product-innovation exercise (invent and develop a footwear concept for a niche market), designed with an executive from a global footwear company and structured as 18 realistic subtasks spanning creativity, analytical thinking, writing, and persuasiveness. Two human graders scored every response (BCG staff or top-program MBA students), and GPT-4 independently scored the same responses as a second measure.

The other 373 consultants were assigned to an "outside the frontier" experiment, deliberately built to be hard for GPT-4: a business case using a spreadsheet of channel/brand performance data plus interview transcripts, where the correct recommendation depended on subtle details buried in the interviews rather than the headline numbers — a task type BCG itself uses to screen job candidates. The authors write plainly that they iterated to "design a task where consultants would excel, but AI would struggle without extensive guidance."

// what they found

The results — a booster inside the frontier, a disruptor outside it

Inside the frontier (the footwear-idea task), every AI condition beat the control group on every outcome. Task completion rose from an 82% control baseline to about 93% (GPT+Overview) and 91% (GPT Only) — a 12.2% average increase. Timing improved too: the GPT+Overview group finished the first 17 questions 1,129 seconds faster (18.8 minutes, 22.5% faster than control) and GPT Only 1,388 seconds faster (23.13 minutes, 27.63% faster). On quality, GPT+Overview raised human-graded scores by 1.75 points over a control mean of 4.1 — a 42.5% increase — and GPT Only by 1.56 points (38%); an independent GPT-4-graded score showed the same pattern at a smaller magnitude (16.8–18.6% higher). Figure 2 shows the full picture: the control group's score distribution sits almost entirely below both AI-assisted distributions, which are nearly on top of each other.

The gains were not evenly spread by skill, but they didn't concentrate at the top either: splitting participants by their pre-task assessment score, bottom-half performers improved 43% using AI versus their own baseline, while top-half performers improved 17% — a pattern the authors note is consistent with Noy & Zhang (2023) and Choi & Schwarcz (2023). A separate, more cautionary finding: semantic-similarity analysis of the brainstormed shoe ideas (using Google's Universal Sentence Encoder) found AI-assisted responses were more similar to each other than non-AI responses — higher individual quality came with less variety across the group.

Outside the frontier (the channel/brand business case), the pattern reversed. The control group answered correctly 84.5% of the time; the AI-assisted groups scored 60.0% (GPT+Overview) and 70.6% (GPT Only) — a roughly 19-percentage-point average drop, and a larger drop for the group that got more AI training (24pp) than the group that got less (13pp). AI-assisted consultants were also faster here — 30% less time for GPT+Overview, 18% less for GPT Only — meaning they moved briskly toward the wrong answer more often. Recommendation-quality scores (a separate 1–10 rubric, independent of correctness) still rose for AI users regardless of whether their answer was right (5.95→7.21, +21%) or wrong (5.33→6.28, +18%) — AI-assisted write-ups read as more polished even when the underlying recommendation was incorrect.

Digging into how successful outside-the-frontier users worked, the authors describe two recurring behavioral patterns: "Centaurs," who cleanly divide labor between themselves and the AI task-by-task, and "Cyborgs," who interleave their own work with the AI's at a much finer grain (finishing the AI's sentences, iterating line by line). This typology is qualitative — drawn from interviews and session logs in an appendix — rather than a headline quantitative result.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

This is a genuinely strong field-experiment design: pre-registered on OSF before data collection, IRB-approved, randomized within a real professional population doing tasks close to their actual job (some drawn from BCG's own hiring case interviews), with dual human-and-GPT-4 grading and a within-subject skill baseline to check heterogeneity claims against. That's a much stronger evidentiary bar than most "AI helps at work" studies clear.

The headline "outside the frontier" number deserves the most scrutiny, because of how it was constructed. The authors are explicit that they engineered the outside-frontier task by iterating until they found a case AI would struggle with — "after several iterations, we settled on a task based on an existing business case..." That's a reasonable way to demonstrate the frontier exists at all, but it means the 84.5%→60–70.6% correctness drop is not a random draw from "tasks knowledge workers happen to encounter outside AI's frontier" — it's closer to a stress test the authors built specifically to fail AI, which they succeeded at. The paper's real, more durable claim is the existence of the jagged boundary and its invisibility to users, not that a fixed ~15–24 point accuracy penalty is what professionals should expect to hit on some fraction of their day-to-day work.

The quality-scoring result outside the frontier is worth a second look too: recommendation quality rose for AI-assisted work regardless of whether the recommendation was correct. That's either a genuinely interesting finding about AI making even wrong answers more persuasively written — or a sign that the human graders were, to some degree, rewarding fluent prose over substance, which would matter a great deal for any organization using "looks like a good memo" as a proxy for "is a good decision." The paper doesn't fully disentangle these two readings.

On independence: BCG is a co-author (three of nine authors, including the Henderson Institute) and supplied the graders, the tasks, and the subjects, which is a tighter industry partnership than an arm's-length replication would be — though the design and pre-registration were led by the Harvard/Wharton/MIT/Warwick academic team, and BCG's commercial incentive here (a splashy AI-and-consulting result) cuts in a direction that's at least plausible rather than obviously self-serving. Generalizability is otherwise narrow by the authors' own account: one firm, one profession, one GPT-4 snapshot from April 2023 — a model whose capabilities have since moved considerably, on a frontier the paper's own thesis says keeps shifting. And the appendix finding that the mode of AI-response "retainment" (near-verbatim copy-paste of GPT-4 output) sits around 0.87 — the single most common value, not the average — is a real caveat on the "Centaur/Cyborg thoughtful collaboration" framing: a lot of the sample may simply have been pasting.

baselines

Real RCT, pre-registered, IRB-approved, with a within-subject skill baseline and dual human/GPT-4 grading — one of the more rigorous field designs in this literature.

contamination

Not model contamination, but task-selection bias: the "outside the frontier" task was iteratively engineered by the authors specifically to make GPT-4 fail, so its ~19pp accuracy penalty is a demonstration case, not a random sample of real workplace tasks.

what's missed

Whether "higher quality" outside the frontier reflects genuine improvement or graders rewarding fluent-but-wrong prose; how much of the effect is one BCG-co-authored study of one firm and one April-2023 GPT-4 snapshot; the ~0.87 modal copy-paste "retainment" rate, which sits uneasily with the Centaur/Cyborg narrative of thoughtful collaboration.

would raise confidence

A random (not adversarially constructed) sample of outside-the-frontier tasks; independent replication outside BCG; grading rubrics that explicitly separate fluency from correctness; and a later-model replication to see whether the specific frontier has simply moved rather than flattened.

// bottom line

One of the most rigorously designed field experiments on LLMs and elite knowledge work to date, and the source of the now-standard "jagged frontier" framing. The qualitative claim — AI can be a large booster on some tasks and a real disruptor on others, and professionals can't always tell which is which — is well supported. The specific magnitude of the "outside the frontier" penalty is less generalizable, since that task was deliberately built to make GPT-4 fail rather than sampled at random from real work.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. The jagged frontier, demonstrated with real consultants. 758 BCG consultants, pre-registered RCT: inside GPT-4's capability frontier, AI-assisted work was 12.2% more complete, 25.1% faster, and 40%+ higher quality than control.
  2. Same tool, opposite effect outside the frontier. On a business case engineered to sit outside GPT-4's capability, correctness fell from 84.5% (no AI) to 60.0–70.6% (with AI) — and AI users got there faster, meaning they moved confidently toward wrong answers.
  3. Gains concentrated at the bottom, but the "outside" number is a stress test. Bottom-half-skill performers gained the most (+43% vs. own baseline, vs. +17% for top performers) — but the headline "outside the frontier" accuracy drop comes from a task the authors iteratively engineered specifically to defeat GPT-4, not a random sample of everyday work.
