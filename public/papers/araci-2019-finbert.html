FinBERT: Financial Sentiment Analysis with Pre-trained Language Models

A University of Amsterdam master's thesis that further pre-trains BERT on 29M words of Reuters financial news and fine-tunes it with ULMFit-style anti-forgetting tricks — the first application of BERT to finance, beating ELMo, ULMFit, and every prior baseline on the Financial PhraseBank and FiQA sentiment datasets.

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

Financial sentiment analysis — classifying a sentence as positive, negative, or neutral toward a company's outlook — is a narrower and harder problem than general sentiment analysis. Financial text has "specialized language" and "a tendency to use vague expressions instead of easily-identified negative/positive words," and labeled data is scarce because annotation requires financial expertise. Two families of prior methods each fall short: word-counting lexicons such as Loughran & McDonald's finance dictionary encode domain knowledge but "come short in analyzing deeper semantic meaning," while neural sequence models are "data-hungry" and financial datasets are too small to train them well from scratch.

By mid-2019, transfer learning had started to close this gap in general NLP — ELMo's contextualized embeddings and ULMFit's whole-model fine-tuning both showed that pre-training on unlabeled text lets a model reach strong performance with little labeled data. BERT pushed this further, but "the specifics of fine-tuning BERT for text classification" were still under-explored, and nobody had yet adapted BERT to the financial domain specifically. FinBERT sets out to close both gaps at once: apply BERT to finance, and work out a fine-tuning recipe that survives the small-data, high-vocabulary-shift regime that domain adaptation implies.

// how it works

The method

FinBERT starts from BERT-base (12 Transformer encoder layers, 768 hidden size, 12 attention heads, 110M parameters, pre-trained on BookCorpus + English Wikipedia) and adds a domain-adaptation step: further pre-training on TRC2-financial, a subset of Reuters' TRC2 corpus (1.8M news articles, 2008–2010) filtered down to financial keywords — 46,143 documents, more than 29M words, nearly 400K sentences. The further-pre-training objective is unchanged from BERT itself: masked-token prediction plus next-sentence prediction. The paper tries two variants of this step — pre-training on the large TRC2-financial corpus (FinBERT-domain) versus pre-training only on the sentences from the small classification training set itself (FinBERT-task) — to see whether a bigger but less targeted corpus beats a smaller but on-distribution one.

For the downstream task, a dense layer is added on top of the final hidden state of the [CLS] token and trained on labeled sentiment data — cross-entropy loss (weighted by inverse-square-root class frequency to offset label imbalance) for classification, mean-squared error for the regression variant used on the continuous-valued FiQA dataset.

The paper's second contribution is a fine-tuning recipe borrowed and adapted from ULMFit to fight catastrophic forgetting — the tendency of aggressive fine-tuning to erase the language knowledge learned during pre-training. Three techniques are combined: slanted triangular learning rates (the learning rate ramps up then decays across training), discriminative fine-tuning (each layer trains at a lower rate than the one above it, αl-1 = θ·αl with discrimination rate θ = 0.85, on the theory that lower layers hold more general language information), and gradual unfreezing (training starts with only the classifier head unfrozen, then one additional encoder layer is unfrozen after every third of a training epoch, working down from the top). Implementation details: dropout p = 0.1, warm-up proportion 0.2, max sequence length 64 tokens, learning rate 2e-5, batch size 64, 6 epochs, trained on a single AWS p2.xlarge instance (one NVIDIA K80 GPU).

Evaluation uses two datasets. Financial PhraseBank (Malo et al., 2014) has 4,845 English sentences drawn from LexisNexis financial news, each labeled positive/negative/neutral by 16 annotators with finance/business backgrounds, based on how the sentence's information would likely move the mentioned company's stock price; the dataset ships with per-sentence annotator-agreement levels, and the paper reports results both on the full set and on the subset where all annotators agreed. 20% of all sentences are held out as test, then 20% of the remainder as validation, leaving 3,101 training examples (about 64% of the total). FiQA Task 1 (from the WWW '18 financial-opinion-mining challenge) supplies 1,174 financial news headlines and tweets with continuous sentiment scores in [-1, 1], evaluated with 10-fold cross-validation since the official held-out test set wasn't available to the author.

On the Financial PhraseBank, FinBERT is the best model on every reported metric, on both the full dataset and the 100%-agreement subset:

On FiQA sentiment scoring, FinBERT again reports the best numbers: MSE 0.07 / R² 0.55, against Yang et al. (2018) at MSE 0.08 / R² 0.40 and Piao & Breslin (2018) at MSE 0.09 / R² 0.41 — though, as the paper itself flags, those two prior results are measured on FiQA's official held-out test set while FinBERT's numbers come from 10-fold cross-validation on the public training data, since the author didn't have access to the official test set.

The ablations are where the paper earns its keep. Further pre-training on the financial domain corpus (FinBERT-domain) edges out vanilla BERT and FinBERT-task, but only barely — loss 0.37 vs 0.38 vs 0.39, accuracy 0.86 vs 0.85 vs 0.86, F1 0.84/0.84/0.85. The author's own explanation is that vanilla BERT is already close to a ceiling: on the full-agreement subset, vanilla BERT alone hits 0.96 accuracy, leaving little room for domain adaptation to move the needle.

For catastrophic-forgetting mitigation, combining all three techniques gives the best test loss (0.37) and ties for the best accuracy (0.86, matched only by STL+gradual-unfreezing); gradual unfreezing is singled out as the most important individual technique — slanted triangular learning rate alone reaches only 0.81 accuracy, and adding discriminative fine-tuning without gradual unfreezing actually makes accuracy worse (0.79) than the learning-rate schedule alone.

Layer-choice and partial-fine-tuning experiments show the last encoder layer is the best single layer to classify from (loss 0.37 / accuracy 0.86 / F1 0.84, versus loss 0.65 / accuracy 0.76 / F1 0.77 for layer 1), and that fine-tuning can be truncated for speed: starting fine-tuning from layer 9 onward keeps accuracy at 0.84 while cutting training time from 332s (all layers, from the embeddings) to 172s; fine-tuning only the classification layer collapses to 0.52 accuracy in 119s. The confusion matrix on the full-agreement test subset (Figure 4) shows the model's remaining errors cluster where humans themselves disagree most — the paper cites inter-annotator agreement of 98.7% for positive/negative, 94.2% for negative/neutral, but only 75.2% for positive/neutral.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

This is a master's thesis, and it reads like a careful one — the ablations (further-pretraining variant, forgetting-mitigation technique, layer choice, partial fine-tuning) go well beyond what the headline "we beat SOTA" result requires, and the author is explicit about several of the paper's own limitations. But the eval has real gaps that a reader relying on this as a benchmark should know about.

The comparison set is uneven. FinBERT is checked against three baselines the author implemented directly (LSTM+GloVe, LSTM+ELMo, ULMFit) and three from the literature (LPS, HSC, FinSSLX) whose numbers are simply copied from their original papers rather than re-run under the same split. The paper says so itself: "these baseline methods are not experimented with as thoroughly as we did with BERT. Therefore the results should not be interpreted as definitive conclusions of one method being better." That's an honest caveat, but it also means the table's ranking is not a controlled comparison.

The FiQA table is a bigger issue: Yang et al. (2018) and Piao & Breslin (2018) evaluate on FiQA's official held-out test set, while FinBERT's MSE/R² come from 10-fold cross-validation on the public training data — a materially easier setting, since the model gets to see nine-tenths of the same distribution it's tested on rather than a genuinely unseen split. The paper discloses this in prose immediately after the table, but the table itself presents all three rows side by side as if directly comparable.

There's also a protocol ambiguity on the main dataset: Section 4.2.2 defines a fixed test/validation/train split for Financial PhraseBank (20% test, 20% of the remainder validation, ~64% train), but the results table for the author's own models (Table 2) is captioned "for the models implemented by us, we report 10-fold cross validation results" — the paper never fully reconciles whether the reported numbers come from the fixed held-out test set, from CV over the training partition, or some mix. Finally, the conclusion's claim that "we increased the state-of-the art by 15% in accuracy" is percentage points (0.86 − 0.71, FinBERT vs. LPS/HSC on the full dataset), not a 15% relative improvement — a common but real source of reader confusion that a more careful write-up would disambiguate.

baselines

Self-implemented (LSTM, ELMo, ULMFit) but explicitly "not experimented with as thoroughly" as FinBERT; external baselines (LPS, HSC, FinSSLX) are numbers quoted from their own papers, not re-run under FinBERT's split.

contamination

Not discussed; BERT's Wikipedia/BookCorpus pretraining predates both eval datasets by construction, and no explicit train/test leakage check is reported for TRC2-financial pretraining vs. the Financial PhraseBank/FiQA test sentences.

what's missed

No variance across seeds/runs anywhere — every number is a single point estimate on modest-sized test partitions (test set ≈969 sentences on the full split). The FiQA comparison quietly swaps evaluation protocols (CV vs. official test set) between FinBERT and its baselines.

would raise confidence

Multi-seed results with variance bars; a run on FiQA's actual official test set; and one clear statement of exactly which split (fixed hold-out vs. k-fold) produced each reported number in Table 2.

// bottom line

FinBERT's engineering — the anti-catastrophic-forgetting recipe, the layer-choice and partial-fine-tuning ablations — is genuinely useful and holds up as a domain-adaptation playbook; it's also why this thesis became the widely cited starting point for finance-BERT variants. The headline "beats SOTA" numbers are real but should be read as a demonstration, not a tightly controlled benchmark: baselines weren't tuned as hard as FinBERT, the FiQA table mixes evaluation protocols, and Financial PhraseBank's own results move between a fixed split and cross-validation without a single clear account of which produced Table 2.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. FinBERT further pre-trains BERT-base on 29M words of Reuters financial news (TRC2-financial) and fine-tunes it with ULMFit-style slanted triangular learning rates, discriminative fine-tuning, and gradual unfreezing — the first application of BERT to finance — reaching 0.86 accuracy / 0.84 F1 on the Financial PhraseBank and MSE 0.07 / R² 0.55 on FiQA sentiment, ahead of ELMo, ULMFit, and all reported prior baselines.
  2. Domain-adaptive further pre-training barely moved the needle here: FinBERT-domain beat vanilla BERT by only 0.01 in accuracy, because vanilla BERT was already close to the label-agreement ceiling (0.96 accuracy on the fully-agreed subset) before any financial-corpus adaptation.
  3. Fine-tuning cheaply works almost as well as fine-tuning everything: gradual unfreezing was the single most load-bearing anti-forgetting technique, and starting fine-tuning from layer 9 of 12 kept accuracy at 0.84 while cutting training time roughly in half versus fine-tuning the whole model.
