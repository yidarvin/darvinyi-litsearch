Microsoft COCO Captions: Data Collection and Evaluation Server

Five (and sometimes forty) independent human captions per COCO image, plus a hidden-test-set evaluation server, turned image captioning from a pile of incomparable leaderboards into a single reproducible benchmark.

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

By late 2014 a wave of neural image-captioning models had landed on arXiv within months of each other — Show and Tell, Karpathy & Fei-Fei's Deep Visual-Semantic Alignments, LRCN, Kiros et al.'s multimodal embeddings, Fang et al.'s "From captions to visual concepts and back" — all racing to caption the newly released Microsoft COCO images. But COCO itself shipped as an object-detection and segmentation dataset: it had no official caption annotations and no shared evaluation protocol. Every captioning paper was collecting its own held-out captions (often on the older, much smaller Flickr8k and Flickr30k sets), computing BLEU/METEOR/ROUGE/CIDEr with its own re-implementation, and reporting numbers that were not directly comparable to any other paper's. The authors state the problem plainly: "numerous evaluation metrics exist... [and] the implementations of these metrics often differ." Nothing stopped a system from being tuned directly against a public reference set it would then be scored on, and no one had systematically asked how much of the score gap between two captioning systems was signal versus metric noise. This paper is the fix: a shared caption corpus over all of COCO's official train/val/test splits, plus a server that computes every metric the same way for everyone and never reveals the test references.

// how it works

The method

Two things had to be built: the caption corpus itself, and the evaluation harness that scores submissions against it. For the corpus, the authors reused COCO's existing train/validation/test image splits (images gathered from Flickr by searching pairs of 80 object categories and scene types) and collected captions on Amazon Mechanical Turk, following the protocol pioneered by Hodosh et al. and Young et al. on Flickr images. Each AMT worker saw one image and a fixed instruction set — describe all the important parts of the scene; don't start with "There is"; don't describe unimportant details, past/future events, or what a person might say; never invent proper names; write at least 8 words — designed to keep captions literal, present-tense, and comparably scoped across workers (Figure 1 shows the resulting style). Two caption sets were produced: MS COCO c5, five independent reference captions for every image in train/val/test, and MS COCO c40, forty reference captions for a randomly chosen 5,000-image slice of the test set — built specifically because prior work (the CIDEr paper) had shown automatic metrics correlate better with human judgment as the number of references grows, so c40 lets the community measure metrics under a much richer reference pool than the field's usual five.

The evaluation server is the half that made the corpus load-bearing. A submitter gets the validation and test images and, for validation only, the human reference captions — test-set references are kept private specifically "to reduce the risk of overfitting." Submissions are two JSON files of {"image_id": int, "caption": str} objects, uploaded through a CodaLab-hosted server (reference code is also released so validation-set scoring can be run locally). Before scoring, every candidate and reference caption is tokenized with the Stanford PTBTokenizer (CoreNLP 3.4.1) and stripped of punctuation, so every team's captions pass through an identical text-normalization pipeline before any metric sees them — closing off the "different tokenizer, different score" failure mode the paper calls out explicitly. The server then reports BLEU-1 through BLEU-4, ROUGE-L, METEOR, and CIDEr-D on both c5 and c40.

CIDEr-D is the one metric this paper actively modifies rather than just re-implements. Plain CIDEr (from the companion Vedantam et al. paper) scores a candidate by TF-IDF-weighted n-gram cosine similarity against the references, but is exploitable: a low-quality sentence engineered to repeat high-IDF n-grams can score artificially well. CIDEr-D adds a length-based Gaussian penalty and clips per-n-gram matches so repetition stops helping:

CIDEr-Dn(ci,Si) = 10m Σj e−(l(ci)−l(sij))²∕2σ² · min(gn(ci), gn(sij)) · gn(sij)‖gn(ci)‖ ‖gn(sij)‖ // σ = 6, a ×10 scale factor keeps CIDEr-D numerically comparable to BLEU/METEOR/ROUGE; the min(·,·) clip and Gaussian length penalty are the anti-gaming additions over plain CIDEr
// what they found

The results

By the time of writing, AMT had produced 413,915 captions over 82,783 training images, 202,520 captions over 40,504 validation images, and 379,249 captions over 40,775 testing images (179,189 of those for MS COCO c5, 200,060 for MS COCO c40) — 1,026,459 captions in total, with the paper explicitly framing the eventual full release (over 1.5 million captions, 330,000+ images) as still in progress once the 2015 COCO image expansion landed. Rather than reporting any model's score, the paper's own headline experiment scores humans against humans: hold out one extra reference caption per test image, treat it as the "candidate," and run it through the evaluation server against the other references. Table 1 gives the result — the ceiling every automatic metric can realistically claim against real human captions, not synthetic ones.

Two things jump out. First, going from 5 to 40 references lifts every metric substantially (BLEU-4 more than doubles, 0.217 → 0.471), confirming the CIDEr paper's claim that automatic metrics need many references to track human judgment well — which is exactly why c40 exists, even though almost every real captioning paper only ever gets scored on c5. Second, at the five-reference setting the field actually uses, BLEU-4 and METEOR score a genuine human caption as agreeing with other humans only 21.7% and 25.2% of the time by their own arithmetic — CIDEr-D is the strongest of the seven at 0.854, which is exactly why the server adopts it as the primary metric, but even CIDEr-D is well short of 1.0 against gold-standard human text.

The paper then pushes the same human-agreement lens down to individual words. Treating "does a caption mention word w" as a binary event, they compute human precision and recall for a held-out caption's word choices against the other k=4 references, for every noun, verb, and adjective in the vocabulary (Figure 3). A simple two-parameter model — p = P(a subject uses w given the object is present) — reproduces the empirical precision-recall curve well: high-p object nouns like "elephant" or "tennis" sit at high recall and high precision (rare, unambiguous objects that everyone mentions the same way), while attributes like "bright" or "huge" sit at low recall (many equally valid ways to describe the same visual property, so no two annotators converge on the same word). This directly motivates a proposed new metric, Precision at Human Recall (PHR): since human recall for a given word is a near-fixed property of the concept, comparing a vision system's precision at that same recall level against the human precision benchmark gives a cleaner read on system quality than raw precision/recall alone.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

Hiding the test-set references is a real defense against the crudest failure mode — a submitter literally reading the answer key — but it does not defend against the failure mode that actually came to dominate the field: optimizing directly against a known, public scoring function. The paper is explicit that CIDEr-D was chosen specifically because plain CIDEr is gameable and CIDEr-D adds clipping and a length penalty to resist it, which is the right instinct. But the formula itself, clipping and all, is public, differentiable-adjacent, and stable across submissions — nothing stops (and within two years, plenty did stop) a system from training a reward model directly on CIDEr-D and running reinforcement learning against it. Self-critical sequence training and its descendants did exactly that, and the community narrative around that era was that CIDEr-optimized captions could be repetitive or oddly generic in ways that gained score without gaining descriptive quality — the server closes the "peeked at the test labels" door while leaving the "reverse-engineered the grader" door open, which is a narrower guarantee than "evaluation server" tends to imply. The paper also, admirably, does not claim otherwise: it only reports human-vs-human agreement, never runs a single captioning model through the server, and states the goal of augmenting these numbers with human evaluation "on an annual basis" — a forecast that undersold how fast the field would move and how much weight would end up placed on the automatic numbers alone before that human-evaluation layer matured.

The human-agreement numbers are also the paper's own strongest indictment of the metrics it standardizes. At the five-reference setting essentially every captioning paper for the next several years actually used, BLEU-4 (0.217) and METEOR (0.252) score one genuine human caption as agreeing with other genuine human captions only about a quarter of the time — numbers low enough that a multi-point BLEU-4 gap between two competing systems is hard to read as evidence of one being meaningfully better-described, rather than noise in which n-grams two different (but equally valid) descriptions happened to share. A decade on, CLIPScore (2021) and later learned/embedding-based and reference-free metrics exist precisely because this paper's own Table 1 all but predicted that n-gram-overlap metrics would plateau in how well they track human judgment — CLIPScore's whole pitch is scoring semantic alignment between image and text directly, sidestepping the "did you happen to use the same words as my five annotators" problem this paper documents so clearly. Whether CLIPScore itself is a superior training target rather than just a better held-out metric is a separate, still-open question, and CLIPScore introduces its own failure modes (sensitivity to prompt phrasing, insensitivity to some hallucinated specifics) that a 2015-era reader had no way to anticipate.

baselines

None run — this paper reports zero model scores. It calibrates the metrics against human-vs-human agreement only, which is honest but means the "is a score gap real" question is answered here only in the abstract, not for any actual submitted system.

contamination

Hiding test references blocks direct answer-copying, not reward-hacking the public CIDEr-D/BLEU/METEOR formulas themselves — and the captions, images, and this very paper have now sat on the open web for a decade, so any model pretrained on broad web/code corpora has almost certainly seen COCO captions verbatim, a contamination risk the 2015 design had no way to anticipate.

what's missed

No error analysis of what kinds of caption mistakes (hallucinated objects, wrong counts, wrong spatial relations) the metrics fail to penalize — the paper measures agreement in the aggregate but never characterizes which failure types slip through BLEU/METEOR/ROUGE/CIDEr-D unpunished.

would raise confidence

Periodic real human-judgment studies on actual submitted systems (which the paper promises but this paper itself doesn't deliver), a refreshed or rotating hidden test set to blunt a decade of web-scale contamination, and reporting metric deltas alongside a same-scale human-agreement ceiling so a reader can tell whether a 2-point CIDEr-D gap between two systems is inside or outside the metric's own noise floor.

// bottom line

The dataset and hidden-test-server design were exactly what a fragmented field needed in 2015, and the honesty of reporting human-vs-human agreement instead of a single flattering model score is unusual and commendable. But "evaluation server" and "gaming-resistant metric" turned out to mean "resistant to looking at the answers," not "resistant to being directly optimized" — and the paper's own numbers (BLEU-4 at 0.217 human agreement) were an early, clear signal that the field's chosen scoreboard would eventually need the reference-free and learned metrics that arrived years later.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. MS COCO Captions gave image captioning what it lacked in 2015: one shared corpus (five references per image via MS COCO c5, forty for a 5,000-image slice via MS COCO c40 — 1,026,459 captions total across 164,062 train/val/test images at time of writing) and one hidden-test evaluation server, so BLEU/METEOR/ROUGE-L/CIDEr-D scores finally meant the same thing across papers.
  2. The paper's own human-vs-human agreement numbers are its most important finding: BLEU-4 (0.217) and METEOR (0.252) barely track human judgment at the five-reference setting virtually every paper actually used, while the anti-gaming CIDEr-D (0.854) fares best but still falls well short of perfect agreement — an early, self-documented case for the reference-free and learned metrics (CLIPScore and beyond) that followed years later.
  3. Hiding test-set references stops a submitter from copying answers, but does not stop a submitter from training directly against the public CIDEr-D formula — a gap the field later exploited via CIDEr-optimized reinforcement learning, which is the more instructive lesson for anyone designing an "evaluation server" today.
