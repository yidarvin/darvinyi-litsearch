DeepSeek-V3 Technical Report

A 671B-parameter Mixture-of-Experts model — only 37B active per token — that matches GPT-4o and Claude-3.5-Sonnet on hard reasoning, code, and math, trained for $5.576M across 2.788M H800 GPU-hours: roughly an order of magnitude under a comparable dense frontier run, with no irrecoverable loss spikes or rollbacks.

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

By late 2024 open-weight models had nearly closed the quality gap with the closed frontier — but the remaining gap had quietly become an economics and engineering problem. Llama-3.1-405B reached the frontier as a dense 405-billion-parameter model trained on tens of millions of GPU-hours; every token paid for every parameter. The open question was whether you could hit GPT-4o / Claude-3.5-Sonnet parity on the hardest reasoning, coding, and math benchmarks while training for a small fraction of that compute — and ship the weights.

Mixture-of-Experts is the obvious lever: activate only a slice of the network per token. But MoE at this scale drags in three nasty problems the paper has to solve at once. (1) Load balancing — the usual fix is an auxiliary loss that pushes tokens to under-used experts, but that loss fights the language-modeling objective and dents quality. (2) Communication — routing tokens to experts spread across hundreds of GPUs means heavy cross-node all-to-all traffic that leaves accelerators idle. (3) Numerics — FP8 training would roughly halve memory and bandwidth, but nobody had shown it works on a model this large without diverging. DeepSeek-V3 is, as much as anything, a demonstration that all three can be solved together.

// how it works

The method

DeepSeek-V3 is 61 Transformer layers, hidden dimension 7168, 671B total parameters with 37B activated per token. Two architectural pillars carry over from DeepSeek-V2 and two new training strategies sit on top.

MLA + DeepSeekMoE

Multi-head Latent Attention (MLA) compresses the keys and values into a low-rank latent vector that is cached at inference, instead of storing full per-head K/V. With 128 heads at per-head dimension 128, this collapses the KV cache that normally dominates long-context inference memory. DeepSeekMoE replaces most feed-forward blocks with a fine-grained expert layer: 1 shared expert + 256 routed experts (each of intermediate width 2048), of which the top-8 routed experts fire per token, capped at 4 nodes per token to bound communication. The shared expert always runs and absorbs common patterns; the routed experts specialize.

Auxiliary-loss-free load balancing

The first headline contribution. Instead of adding a balancing loss (which injects gradients that degrade the model), DeepSeek-V3 keeps a per-expert bias term bi that is added to the routing affinity only when selecting the top-K experts. The bias is nudged up for under-loaded experts and down for over-loaded ones between steps. Crucially, the gating value that actually multiplies the expert's output still uses the raw affinity — so balancing steers routing without distorting the forward signal.

g′i,t = si,t,  if  (si,t + bi) ∈ TopK( {sj,t + bj}, Kr )0,  otherwise // the bias bi only gates selection; the kept gating value is the unbiased affinity si,t (paper Eq. 16)

Multi-Token Prediction (MTP)

The second contribution. Rather than predicting only the next token, DeepSeek-V3 trains lightweight sequential MTP modules that each predict one more future token while preserving the full causal chain; the embedding layer and output head are shared across the main model and the MTP modules. This densifies the training signal (each position supervises several future tokens) and the modules can later be repurposed for speculative decoding. In the released model the prediction depth is D = 1 — each token also predicts the token after next.

The systems story: FP8, DualPipe, and $5.576M

The efficiency headline is an infrastructure achievement. DeepSeek-V3 is — by the authors' claim — the first validation of FP8 mixed-precision training on an extremely large-scale model, using fine-grained tile/block-wise quantization to tame activation outliers while keeping the sensitive operations in higher precision. The DualPipe schedule overlaps forward/backward computation with the cross-node all-to-all expert communication, nearly eliminating pipeline bubbles so the GPUs stay busy. Pre-training runs on 14.8T tokens (4K sequence length), followed by a two-stage context extension to 128K, then SFT and RL. The full bill, at an assumed $2 per H800 GPU-hour:

Post-training is almost free in compute terms (5K hours), and includes a novel step: distilling long chain-of-thought reasoning from DeepSeek-R1 into the standard V3 model — the reasoning teacher that V3-Base would, in turn, become the foundation for.

// what they found

The results

DeepSeek-V3-Base is reported as the strongest open-source base model available at release, and the chat model reaches parity with the closed frontier on aggregate — with the wins concentrated in math and competitive code. On MATH-500 it scores 90.2 (vs Claude-3.5-Sonnet 78.3, GPT-4o 74.6); on AIME 2024, 39.2 (Claude 16.0, GPT-4o 9.3); on the Codeforces percentile, 51.6 (GPT-4o 23.6, Claude 20.3); and it tops LiveCodeBench (40.5 Pass@1-CoT). On general knowledge it is even with the field — MMLU 88.5 — and it is the first open model to clear 85% on Arena-Hard (85.5), with a 70.0 length-controlled win rate on AlpacaEval 2.0.

It does not win everywhere. The picture is honest about where the closed models still lead:

So V3 trails Claude on the graduate-science GPQA-Diamond (59.1 vs 65.0), MMLU-Pro, and agentic SWE-bench Verified, and trails GPT-4o sharply on English factual recall (SimpleQA 24.9 vs 38.2) — while dominating on Chinese (C-Eval 86.5, C-SimpleQA 64.8). The R1-distillation ablation is striking: feeding DeepSeek-V2.5 reasoning data from R1 lifts LiveCodeBench Pass@1 from 31.1 → 37.4 and MATH-500 from 74.6 → 83.2, though average answer length grows with it.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

As a systems paper the result is very strong and largely self-evident: the model exists, the weights are public, and the training was demonstrably stable. The claims that deserve scrutiny are the two marketing-adjacent ones — "comparable to the closed frontier" and "$5.576M" — and the question of how much of the math/code dominance is real capability versus contamination and teacher distillation.

"Comparable" is true in aggregate but lopsided in composition. V3's biggest margins are on math (MATH-500, AIME, CNMO) and competitive programming (Codeforces) — precisely the domains where (a) problems circulate widely on the web, and (b) distilling a long-CoT reasoning teacher (R1) buys the most. Where the test is fresh graduate science (GPQA-Diamond), broad professional knowledge (MMLU-Pro), agentic software engineering (SWE-bench Verified), or English factual recall (SimpleQA), the closed models still lead. The eval is broad and reports these losses plainly, which is to its credit — but a reader should not read "MATH-500 90.2" as "beats GPT-4o overall."

On cost: the $5.576M is explicitly the marginal cost of the final, successful run, and the paper says so — it "does not include the costs associated with prior research and ablation experiments on architectures, algorithms, or data." It is not a from-scratch reproduction price. Two eval-setup details also shape the headline numbers: SWE-bench Verified is run with the Agentless scaffold (not a full agent), and AIME / CNMO are averaged over 16 samples at temperature 0.7 — so those "Pass@1" figures are mean accuracies, not single-shot, and ship without variance bars.

baselines

Fair and current for Dec 2024 — pinned snapshots (Claude-3.5-Sonnet-1022, GPT-4o-0513) plus Qwen2.5-72B and Llama-3.1-405B. Closed baselines are opaque (no params/compute), so "comparable" is benchmark-conditional, not architectural.

contamination

The dominant wins (MATH-500, AIME 2024, Codeforces) are the most leakage-prone categories. LiveCodeBench is windowed (Aug–Nov 2024), which helps; AIME/Codeforces problems are widely circulated. Decontamination is asserted but the methodology isn't released.

what's missed

$5.576M is the final-run marginal cost, excluding all R&D/ablations. SWE-bench uses Agentless, not an agent. Math evals are 16-run averages with no CIs. R1 distillation inflates length along with accuracy.

would raise confidence

Post-cutoff fresh math/code problems; an apples-to-apples agentic SWE-bench run; published decontamination details; variance/CIs on sampled evals; and total (not marginal) compute. The internal ablations on auxiliary-loss-free balancing (Table 5, expert-load figures) are the most convincing piece.

// bottom line

A landmark efficiency-and-systems paper: FP8 + fine-grained MoE + DualPipe + reasoning distillation reach GPT-4o / Claude-3.5 parity at roughly a tenth of the compute, with public weights and a remarkably stable run. The model is the real artifact; the caveats are about what "comparable" and "$5.576M" quietly leave out, not about invented wins.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. A 671B MoE with only 37B active per token reaches GPT-4o / Claude-3.5-Sonnet parity for $5.576M / 2.788M H800-hours — recasting the open-vs-closed frontier as a training-efficiency and engineering problem.
  2. Two clean modeling ideas — auxiliary-loss-free load balancing (a routing bias, not a loss) and multi-token prediction — plus the first large-scale FP8 training and the DualPipe compute/communication overlap that make the economics work.
  3. Distilling long-CoT reasoning from DeepSeek-R1 is what lifts math and code; the wins cluster in math / competitive code / Chinese, while Claude still leads on GPQA, MMLU-Pro and SWE-bench and GPT-4o leads on English factual recall. V3-Base went on to become the foundation for R1 itself.
