MMBench: Is Your Multi-modal Model an All-around Player?

A bilingual, 3,217-question multiple-choice benchmark built around a 20-leaf ability taxonomy and a CircularEval protocol that only credits a model once it survives every reordering of a question's choices — plus GPT-4 standing in for a model that won't say "A."

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

By mid-2023, vision-language models (GPT-4v, LLaVA, MiniGPT-4, Flamingo-style architectures) were multiplying faster than anyone could compare them. The evaluation options were both broken in complementary ways. Objective datasets like VQAv2, COCO Caption, GQA, and OK-VQA give you a number, but that number is corrupted by two problems: exact-match scoring produces false negatives whenever a model says "bike" and the reference says "bicycle," and the tasks are coarse — one accuracy score tells you nothing about whether a model is bad at OCR, spatial reasoning, or celebrity recognition specifically. Subjective, human-in-the-loop protocols like OwlEval (82 hand-built open-ended questions) and LVLM-eHub's pairwise arena fix the matching problem — a human can tell "bike" means "bicycle" — but introduce their own: they're expensive to scale, hard to reproduce with a different annotator pool, and small evaluation sets are statistically noisy.

A second, less obvious problem sits underneath both: most VLMs at the time had weak instruction-following. Asked a 4-choice question, many wouldn't output a clean "A"/"B"/"C"/"D" — they'd restate the answer in prose, refuse, or wander. Scoring that with rule-based string matching silently penalizes models for a formatting failure rather than a reasoning failure, and conflates the two. And even for models that do comply, plain multiple-choice has a structural weakness: a 4-way question has a ~25% random-guessing floor, and the paper's own analysis (Fig. 4) shows VLMs additionally exhibit systematic positional bias — e.g. one model predicts "B" 42.1% of the time against a near-uniform ~25%-per-letter ground truth — meaning some of a model's measured accuracy is just a favored-letter habit, not understanding. MMBench's premise is that a benchmark needs to solve fine-grained ability coverage, free-form-answer parsing, and guessing/positional bias simultaneously, or the leaderboard numbers it produces aren't trustworthy.

// how it works

The method

MMBench attacks the coverage problem first, with a hierarchical ability taxonomy. At the top sit two Level-1 abilities every model needs, Perception (gathering information from sensory input) and Reasoning (drawing conclusions from it). These split into six Level-2 groups — Coarse Perception, Fine-grained Perception (single-instance and cross-instance), Attribute Reasoning, Logical Reasoning, and Relation Reasoning — which split again into 20 Level-3 leaf abilities: things like Object Localization, OCR, Celebrity Recognition, Image Quality, Spatial Relationship, Physical Relation Reasoning, and Structuralized Image-Text Understanding. Every leaf ability is stocked with at least 125 questions, hand-collected by undergraduate/graduate volunteers (over 80% sourced from the internet, the rest reusing public-dataset images with self-written questions), for a total of 3,217 multiple-choice questions, each a (question, 2–4 choices, image, answer) quadruple. The whole set is also translated into Chinese (MMBench-CN, GPT-4-assisted with human verification) for apples-to-apples bilingual comparison, and split dev:test at 4:6 — dev ships with public answers, test answers stay server-side.

Quality control runs two automatic filters before any human touches the data (Fig. 3a). A text-only majority-vote filter feeds each question, image withheld, to a panel of SOTA LLMs (GPT-4, Gemini-Pro, etc.); if most of them answer correctly from text alone, the question isn't actually testing multimodal understanding and gets manually reviewed for removal. A wrong-sample filter runs the question past a panel of SOTA VLMs with the image; if every VLM fails, the question is flagged as potentially broken (bad choices, wrong labeled answer) and manually checked.

The taxonomy alone doesn't fix free-form answers or guessing bias, so MMBench adds two evaluation-protocol pieces. First, LLM-assisted choice extraction: a heuristic pass tries to pull a bare label (A/B/C/D) out of the model's output; if that fails, gpt-4-0125 is shown the question, choices, and the model's raw prediction, and asked to output the matching label (or a pseudo-label "Z" if nothing matches). This was validated against ~420 human-annotated hard cases (10% of the unparseable set): GPT-4 hits 91.5% alignment with human choice extraction, the best of the LLMs tested (GPT-3.5-Turbo and Qwen-Max both landed around 85%; the strongest open-source option, InternLM2-7B, reached 87%). Table 1 in the paper shows just how much this matters — VisualGLM-6B's heuristic match rate is only 64.8%, and LLM-based extraction adds +23.2 points to its measured accuracy purely by rescuing answers the string-matcher couldn't parse.

Second, and the paper's real methodological contribution: CircularEval. Instead of asking a question once, MMBench asks it N times (N = number of choices), circularly shifting the choice-and-answer mapping each pass, and only counts the model correct if it gets every pass right.

correct(q) = ANDi = 1..N [ model( shifti(q) ) = shifti(answer) ] // N = number of choices; a question only counts if every circularly-shifted pass is answered correctly — one failed pass ends the trial early, so real cost is sub-N×

The results

The CircularEval-vs-VanillaEval comparison, run on the MMBench-dev split, is the paper's headline finding: switching from a single pass to circular passes drags accuracy down hard, and the drop size itself is diagnostic. OpenFlamingo v2 — top of the instruction-following table but a weak model overall — collapses from 36.7% (Vanilla) to just 2.6% under CircularEval, a −34.1-point fall to essentially random. Even the strongest models aren't spared: GPT-4v drops 10.8 points to 74.3%, Qwen-VL-Max drops 8.7 points to 76.4%, and InternLM-XComposer2 — the smallest drop among strong models — still falls 4.7 points to 79.1%. CircularEval also widens the gaps that actually separate models: LLaVA-v1.5-13B beats its own 7B version by 2.1 points under Vanilla but by 4.7 points under Circular.

On the final MMBench-test leaderboard (CircularEval, GPT-4-assisted extraction), a text-only GPT-4-Turbo baseline scores 2.9% overall — near the 25%-ish random floor's lower tail once you require all circular passes, confirming the quality-control filters removed most of the text-answerable leakage. Among open-source models, InternLM-XComposer2 tops the board at 78.1%, ahead of every other open-source and even proprietary model "by a large margin." Among proprietary models: Qwen-VL-Max 75.4%, GPT-4v 74.3%, Gemini-Pro-V 70.2%. MiniCPM-V, at ≤3B parameters, still clears 61.4% overall — evidence that scale isn't the only lever. At the bottom, MiniGPT-4, IDEFICS, VisualGLM, and InstructBLIP all sit in the 30s–40s%, and OpenFlamingo v2 is at random-level 2.3%. Backbone LLM choice matters independently of vision components: holding the LLaVA vision stack and training corpus fixed, swapping Vicuna-v1.5 for InternLM2-20B lifts every L-2 ability (reasoning most of all); going from the 7B to 13B variant of the same LLM family adds +8.3 points for MiniGPT, +1.5 for InstructBLIP, and +3.5 for LLaVA-v1.5.

Two secondary findings temper the leaderboard's face value. Bilingual gap: most models score lower on MMBench-CN than English, but strong bilingual models barely notice — InternLM-XComposer2's EN→CN drop is under 1 point, and the largest gap among all 70%+ models is only 2 points. Content moderation: the three proprietary VLMs all refuse some questions outright — GPT-4v refuses 1.8% of CircularEval passes (74% of its refusals are celebrity-recognition questions), Gemini-Pro-V 1.6%, Qwen-VL-Max 0.1%. Assuming perfect answers on every refusal (an upper bound), scores move by at most 2.4 points (GPT-4v 74.3→76.2, Gemini-Pro-V 70.2→72.6, Qwen-VL-Max 75.4→75.5) — a real but modest effect. Finally, the fine-grained breakdown (Fig. 9) shows the proprietary/open-source gap isn't uniform: it concentrates almost entirely in Structuralized Image-Text Understanding (tables, diagrams, layouts) and knowledge-heavy leaves (celebrity recognition, physical property, natural relation), while elsewhere open models are competitive. Even the best models plateau on specific leaves: max accuracy across every evaluated VLM is only 61.3% on Image Quality, 61.5% on Structuralized Image-Text Understanding, 64.0% on Physical Relation Reasoning, and 68.0% on Spatial Relationship (Fig. 10) — low-level visual judgments, structured-document parsing, and inter-object spatial reasoning are where every model in the study still struggles.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

CircularEval is a genuine improvement, but it's a bar-raiser, not a guessing-immune protocol. Requiring all N circular passes to succeed does suppress lucky single-shot guessing and exposes positional bias (the -34.1-point OpenFlamingo v2 collapse is the proof), but a model with a consistent — rather than random — wrong prior can still pass: if a model always maps "the object that looks biggest" to whatever slot happens to hold the right answer under a specific shift pattern, or if it has learned a shift-invariant shortcut correlated with the true answer's content rather than its label, CircularEval won't catch it. The paper never reports a lower-bound "adversarial" baseline (e.g., a model that always answers based on image-independent priors) run through CircularEval to show the floor is actually near 0%; it only shows the floor is much lower than VanillaEval's ~25–37%, which is a weaker claim than "guessing is now impossible."

The GPT-4-assisted choice extractor is the other load-bearing piece, and it inherits GPT-4's own biases into the scoring pipeline. The reported 91.5% human-alignment rate is measured on a 10%, ~420-sample subset of the already-hard (heuristic-match-failed) cases — the paper doesn't report whether extraction errors are randomly distributed across models or systematically favor/disfavor particular answer-phrasing styles (e.g., verbose vs. terse models, or models that hedge). An 8.5% misalignment rate applied unevenly across 20+ models on a leaderboard that separates models by single-digit points is not obviously negligible, and using a single frozen GPT-4 checkpoint (gpt-4-0125) as arbiter means the whole leaderboard is only as reproducible as that checkpoint's future availability — the paper doesn't discuss what happens to comparability once the model is deprecated.

On construction itself: MMBench's own quality-control loop uses SOTA LLMs/VLMs (GPT-4, Gemini-Pro, and the same VLM families later benchmarked) to decide which questions survive — a design that risks tautologically favoring whatever those particular models are already good at, and disfavoring genuinely hard questions that happen to trip every current model (which get flagged as "potentially wrong" rather than "hard"). The paper reports this loop transparently, but doesn't stress-test whether it introduces a systematic skew.

baselines

Broad and contemporaneous for 2023–24 (21+ open-source and proprietary VLMs across architectures/scales), and the text-only GPT-4-Turbo control (2.9% overall) is a smart, cheap contamination sanity check. But there's no adversarial/shortcut baseline run through CircularEval to establish a true guessing floor, and the open-source group is dominated by LLaVA-family derivatives, which may understate how differently a genuinely distinct architecture would fare.

contamination

The text-only-majority-vote filter is a real, if incomplete, contamination defense — it screens for image-independent leakage from pretraining, not for the test split's images/questions having leaked into a later model's training data (a live risk given the benchmark's popularity and the internet-sourced provenance of >80% of its images). MMBench-test answers are held server-side, which helps, but the widely-distributed dev split is not similarly protected.

what's missed

Multiple-choice format, even with CircularEval, only measures whether a model can select among given options — it says nothing about free-generation quality, calibration on "none of the above," or open-ended visual reasoning, which is a large share of what VLMs are actually used for downstream.

would raise confidence

A published inter-annotator/inter-LLM agreement breakdown by model family (not just an aggregate 91.5%) for the choice extractor; an explicit adversarial-baseline run through CircularEval; and a periodic contamination re-audit as models increasingly train on data that could include MMBench's public dev split and leaderboard discussions of it.

// bottom line

MMBench correctly diagnosed that 2023-era VLM evaluation was contaminated by guessing, positional bias, and instruction-following noise, and CircularEval + LLM-assisted extraction measurably improve on naive multiple-choice — the 2,288 citations and its adoption as a default eval in VLMEvalKit reflect real, earned utility. But "harder to game" is not "impossible to game," and the paper's own validation of its extractor and quality-control loop is thinner than the confidence the resulting leaderboard numbers project.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. CircularEval reframes multiple-choice robustness as an "all-passes-must-succeed" requirement: a question is fed N times with circularly shifted choices, and a model must get every shift right — turning a ~25%-guessable format into one where the weakest models (OpenFlamingo v2: 36.7%→2.6%) collapse to near-random and the strongest ones (GPT-4v, Qwen-VL-Max, InternLM-XComposer2) still lose 4.7–10.8 points.
  2. A 20-leaf, 2-level ability taxonomy over 3,217 questions turns one accuracy number into a diagnosis: the same evaluation exposes that every model tested — including the 78.1%-overall leader InternLM-XComposer2 — plateaus at only 61–68% max accuracy on Image Quality, Structuralized Image-Text Understanding, Physical Relation Reasoning, and Spatial Relationship specifically.
  3. GPT-4-as-choice-extractor (91.5% human alignment) closes the instruction-following gap without penalizing models for prose instead of bare labels, but it makes the leaderboard dependent on a single, eventually-deprecated model checkpoint (gpt-4-0125) as final arbiter — a dependency the paper documents but doesn't fully stress-test for bias.
