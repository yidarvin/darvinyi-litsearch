Do ImageNet Classifiers Generalize to ImageNet?

Rebuild the ImageNet and CIFAR-10 test sets from scratch, following the original recipe as closely as possible — and every classifier from AlexNet to NASNet loses 11–14% accuracy. The twist: it isn't overfitting to the old test set. The models simply can't handle images that are a hair harder.

Benjamin Recht, Rebecca Roelofs, Ludwig Schmidt, Vaishaal Shankar · UC Berkeley
venue ICML 2019 citations 2,200 topic Benchmarks & Evals source ↗

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

The entire empirical edifice of machine learning rests on one quiet assumption: a low error on a held-out test set predicts a low error on fresh data from the same source. We report a number on the ImageNet validation set, call it "accuracy," and treat it as a property of the model. But for almost a decade, the community has hammered the same CIFAR-10 and ImageNet test images — thousands of papers, public leaderboards, and uncounted hyperparameter sweeps, all peeking at the same held-out set.

Conventional statistical wisdom says this should rot the benchmark. Repeatedly selecting models by their test-set score is a form of training on the test set; over years it should produce adaptive overfitting — models tuned to the idiosyncrasies of those specific images, whose reported gains don't reflect real progress. The worry is concrete: maybe a chunk of the last decade's "SOTA" is an artifact of test-set reuse.

The clean way to check would be to evaluate every model on a fresh, truly i.i.d. test set from the original distribution. The problem is that no such set exists — the authors of CIFAR-10 and ImageNet never hid a second test split for later use. So the most basic sanity question about our headline metric has gone unanswered: if a model scores 95% on the test set, does it score 95% on a new test set drawn the same way? This paper builds the missing test sets and finds out.

// how it works

The method

Lacking a hidden i.i.d. split, the authors do the next-hardest thing: they replicate the dataset-creation process. For CIFAR-10 they draw new images from the same Tiny Images source; for ImageNet they go back to Flickr, restricting to uploads from a similar time window, and reproduce the original sub-class keyword and query distributions. They reuse the original labeling instructions, the Amazon Mechanical Turk task format (a 48-image grid per class, ≥10 workers per image), and the cleaning pipeline. The new test sets are deliberately small — 2,000 images for CIFAR-10.1 and 10,000 for ImageNetV2 (≈3% of CIFAR-10, <1% of ImageNet) — so they don't skim only the "leftover hard" images out of the source pool.

Then they evaluate a testbed spanning a decade of progress: the seminal AlexNet, the VGG/ResNet/DenseNet/Inception workhorses, and the 2018 state-of-the-art NASNet and PNASNet, plus pre-deep baselines (random features on CIFAR-10, Fisher vectors on ImageNet). Pre-trained weights are used where available; otherwise the published training commands are re-run untouched. Crucially, none of these models ever saw the new test sets, so the new sets are statistically independent of every model in the testbed.

Decomposing the drop

The conceptual core is a decomposition. The observed gap between original accuracy LS and new accuracy LS′ splits into three terms, each with a different cause:

LS − LS′ = (LS − LD) + (LD − LD′) + (LD′ − LS′) adaptivity gap  ·  distribution gap  ·  generalization gap // S, S′ = old / new test sets · D, D′ = old / new underlying distributions

The generalization gap (LD′ − LS′) is ordinary sampling noise. With 10,000 points it is tiny: a 95% Clopper–Pearson interval is at most ±1% (±2% even at 99.99% confidence), and it shrinks further at the high accuracies that matter. Random chance cannot explain a double-digit drop. The adaptivity gap (LS − LD) is the dangerous one — the inflation from tuning on the reused test set. The distribution gap (LD − LD′) is any systematic difference between the original and replicated distributions.

For a single model you can't separate adaptivity from distribution shift. The trick is to use many models from across the decade. If reuse caused adaptive overfitting, later (more heavily tuned) models should show diminishing returns on fresh data — their test scores keep climbing while their true accuracy plateaus. So the shape of the original-vs-new scatter becomes a diagnostic for which mechanism is at work.

// what they found

The results

Every model drops, a lot. The losses range 3%–15% on CIFAR-10 and 11%–14% top-1 on ImageNet. The 2018 state-of-the-art falls 11 points on ImageNet (83% → 72% top-1) and 6 points top-5 (96% → 90%). To calibrate that 6-point top-5 drop: the best model in ILSVRC 2013 hit 89% top-5 and the 2014 winner 93%, so the drop wipes out roughly five years of progress in one of the most active periods in ML history.

But the second finding is the one that matters more, and it is reassuring: the relative order of models is almost perfectly preserved. Original accuracy is highly predictive of new accuracy, and the relationship is strikingly linear — with a slope greater than one:

accnew = 1.11 · accorig − 20.2%  (ImageNet, top-1)
accnew = 1.69 · accorig − 72.7%  (CIFAR-10) // bootstrap 95% CI on the slope: [1.07, 1.19] for ImageNet, [1.63, 1.76] for CIFAR-10

A slope above 1 means later, higher-accuracy models drop less, not more — there are no diminishing returns. That is the exact opposite of the adaptive-overfitting signature. The authors read it as strong evidence that a decade of leaderboard competition produced real gains: progress on the old test set transfers, and then some, to brand-new data.

The labeling knob moves the whole benchmark

If adaptivity isn't the cause, what is? Section 4 is the smoking gun. From the same Flickr candidate pool, the authors build three ImageNet test sets that differ only in how they use the MTurk selection frequency — the fraction of workers who tagged an image as belonging to its class. All three are correctly labeled and average above 0.7 selection frequency, yet model accuracy swings by 14 points top-1 across them.

For reference, the original ImageNet validation set has a mean selection frequency of 0.71 in their experiments — so the authors report MatchedFrequency (0.73, the closest match) as the headline ImageNetV2, which is exactly the one with the biggest drop. The takeaway is unsettling: the 14-point spread induced by the labeling criterion alone is larger than the entire accuracy spread among every post-AlexNet convolutional network they tested. "ImageNet accuracy" is not a physical constant; it is a function of a subjective annotation step.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

This paper is a model of reproducibility hygiene — confidence intervals on every number, 100,000-sample bootstraps on the fits, a decade-wide testbed, and public release of both new test sets and all code. The rank-preservation result is robust and, in hindsight, the durable contribution. But the headline — "11–14% gone" — does not fully survive scrutiny, and the reason is baked into the paper's own Section 4.

The decisive follow-up is Engstrom, Ilyas, Santurkar, Tsipras, Steinhardt & Mądry, "Identifying Statistical Bias in Dataset Replication" (ICML 2020). Their argument: MatchedFrequency is built by matching per-class selection-frequency distributions, but those frequencies are estimated from a handful of noisy worker votes. That estimation noise is not symmetric in its effect — it systematically biases the matched sample toward harder images, manufacturing part of the very gap the paper reports. After re-collecting annotations and correcting for the bias, only 3.6% ± 1.5% of the original 11.7% ± 1.0% drop remains unaccounted for. Roughly two-thirds of the ImageNet headline was an artifact of how the replication test set was constructed, not genuine distribution shift.

To the authors' real credit, the effect does not vanish — a ~3–4 point residual drop is real — and Recht et al. themselves surface the exact lever Engstrom et al. later formalize: their own Table 2 shows accuracy is hypersensitive to the selection-frequency knob, swinging from −11.8% to +2.1%. The paper hands you the rope. What it doesn't do is treat "distribution gap" as anything more than a residual bucket: they explicitly concede "it is unclear what properties of our new images cause the accuracy drops." Everything they couldn't control — object pose, Flickr-era drift, and, it turns out, statistical bias in the matching itself — lands in that bucket and gets narrated as "models can't generalize to harder images."

Two more caveats worth keeping. First, the anti-adaptivity claim is narrower than it's usually quoted: they rule out diminishing returns, but openly admit they cannot rule out a roughly constant adaptivity offset that hits all models equally. Second, this is two late-2000s computer-vision benchmarks; the "benchmarks are broken" reading got over-extrapolated to NLP and LLM evals, where the replication mechanics are entirely different.

baselines

Strong. A testbed from AlexNet through PNASNet plus pre-deep baselines (random features, Fisher vectors) — broad and current for 2019. The limit is scope (two old CV benchmarks), not staleness.

contamination

The study is about test-set reuse, so leakage is the subject, not a flaw. But the default ImageNetV2 (MatchedFrequency) is itself statistically biased toward hard images (Engstrom et al. 2020), inflating the headline drop.

what's missed

No characterization of what makes new images harder; "distribution gap" absorbs every uncontrolled variable, including the replication's own imperfection. The human-accuracy check covers only CIFAR-10, and only on the model-hard subset.

would raise confidence

A bias-corrected selection-frequency re-analysis (Engstrom et al. later supplied it: ~3.6pp residual); a genuinely i.i.d. hidden test split; replication on non-vision benchmarks.

// bottom line

The provocative number was about half construction artifact — correct the replication's statistical bias and the genuine ImageNet drop is ~3.6 points, not 11–14. But the paper's lasting result survives intact and is arguably more important: across a decade of models, rankings and per-point gains transfer almost perfectly to fresh data (slope > 1), so benchmark progress is real rather than adaptive overfitting. The linear "accuracy on the line" relationship became a load-bearing fact of the entire distribution-shift literature.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. Reuse didn't break the leaderboard. Across a decade of models, rank order and per-point gains carried over to brand-new test sets (slope > 1), so competitive progress on CIFAR-10/ImageNet reflects real improvement — not adaptive overfitting to stale test images. This quietly rehabilitated leaderboards.
  2. A benchmark number is a function of its labeling knob. Three test sets from the same Flickr pool, all correctly labeled and >70% selected, span 14 points of top-1 accuracy just by changing the selection-frequency criterion — more than the spread across every post-AlexNet conv net. "ImageNet accuracy" is not a single constant.
  3. Replication is statistical, and the statistics bite back. The headline 11–14% drop shrank to ~3.6 points once Engstrom et al. (2020) corrected the selection-frequency estimation bias — a lasting lesson that building a "matched" test set is itself an inference problem that can manufacture the effect it sets out to measure.
