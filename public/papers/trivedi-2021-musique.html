MuSiQue: Multihop Questions via Single-hop Question Composition

Existing multihop QA datasets are known to be solvable via shortcuts — a model can often answer the final hop without ever using the answer to the intermediate hop. MuSiQue builds multihop questions bottom-up, by composing pairs of single-hop questions and explicitly filtering out any pair a strong model can answer without the dependency. Relative to prior multihop datasets, the result has 3x the human-machine gap, and a single-hop shortcut model's F1 drops 30 points when evaluated on it.

What problem is this solving?

Multihop QA datasets exist to force models to chain multiple reasoning steps together. But by 2021 a run of analysis papers — Min et al. (2019a), Chen and Durrett (2019), and the authors' own prior work, Trivedi et al. (2020)'s DiRe (Disconnected Reasoning) study — had shown that HotpotQA and similar datasets don't actually require this. Models routinely land on the right answer while skipping one of the intended hops entirely, exploiting over-specific sub-questions, train-test leakage, and thin distractor sets.

The paper's own worked example makes the failure mode concrete. Take the HotpotQA-style question "Armageddon in Retrospect was written by the author who was best known for what novel?" A model never has to resolve who wrote Armageddon in Retrospect (Kurt Vonnegut) — it can scan the context, notice only one person is described as "best known for a satirical novel," and return Slaughterhouse-Five directly. The question is disconnected: the second hop is answerable without the first. Rephrase it as "...best known for what 1969 satire novel?" and now three people in the context are each "best known for" some novel — the model must resolve the first hop to disambiguate which one. That's a connected question, and MuSiQue's entire construction pipeline exists to manufacture only the second kind.

The paper formalizes this as the MuSiQue condition: for every edge (qj, qi) in a question's reasoning-step DAG, a strong model must fail to answer qi when the answer to its predecessor qj is masked out of it — and must fail to answer any step at all without the context. HotpotQA and 2WikiMultihopQA were never optimized against this condition; MuSiQue is built to satisfy it by construction rather than by post-hoc filtering.

// how it works

The method

Rather than crowdsourcing multihop questions end-to-end (which is how HotpotQA and 2WikiMultihopQA's hand-authored templates were built), MuSiQue composes them bottom-up out of a huge pool of existing single-hop questions drawn from five Wikipedia-based RC datasets: SQuAD, Natural Questions, MLQA (en-en), T-REx, and Zero-Shot RE. The construction pipeline runs eight stages:

  • S1 — Find good single-hop questions. Drop questions that five trained QA models (two seeds each of RoBERTa-large and Longformer-Large, plus UnifiedQA) can't answer at all, plus ones with malformed spans or contexts outside a 20–300 word range.
  • S2 — Find composable pairs. Pair (q1, a1) with (q2, a2) when a1 is a named entity mentioned in q2, using SpaCy entity typing, a Wikipedia-search identity check, and a wikification model to confirm the two mentions denote the same entity.
  • S3 — Filter disconnected pairs (Disconnection Filtering, "DF"). Train two Longformer-Large models per 5-fold split and reject head questions the models solve too easily, plus reject tail questions that stay answerable once the bridge entity is masked out of them — enforcing the MuSiQue condition (Eqn. 2) directly during construction rather than checking for it after the fact. This is the paper's central mechanism.
  • S4 — Build 2–4 hop chains from the surviving connected 2-hop edges, across six reasoning-graph shapes (linear chains, "collect-then-combine" fan-ins, etc.), capping entity/question reuse for diversity.
  • S5 — Minimize train/test leakage ("RL") by greedily splitting on overlapping single-hop questions, answers, and supporting paragraphs — not just the composed questions themselves.
  • S6 — Build 20-paragraph contexts per question, mixing the true supporting paragraphs with positive distractors — retrieved from the pool of other filtered single-hop gold paragraphs rather than raw Wikipedia — specifically so a model can't tell distractors apart from evidence by corpus statistics alone.
  • S7 — Crowdsource the actual question text on Mechanical Turk: 17 qualified workers phrase each DAG of single-hop questions into one natural, coherent multihop question, producing MuSiQue-Ans (∼25K 2–4 hop questions, 24,814 after final counting in Table 2).
  • S8 — Add unanswerable variants by making one randomly chosen sub-question's answer absent from the context, doubling the set into MuSiQue-Full (∼50K instances) with a paired answerability-classification task layered on top.

The results

Compared against HQ-20K and 2W-20K (HotpotQA and 2WikiMultihopQA, both subsampled to the same 20K-instance training size as MuSiQue-Ans for a fair comparison), MuSiQue-Ans has a "∼27 pt gap in answer F1, whereas HQ and 2W have a gap of only 10 and 5, respectively," between human performance (78.0 answer F1) and the best multihop model tested. Support-paragraph identification tells the same story even more sharply: on HQ and 2W, automated support identification actually surpasses human performance, while on MuSiQue-Ans it trails humans by 14 points.

The paper's cheatability metric — the DiRe score, which measures how well an "artifact" model can score using only disconnected shortcuts — drops from 68.8 (HQ) and 63.4 (2W) to just 37.8 on MuSiQue-Ans. A concrete illustration: a 1-Para model, given only the single paragraph most likely to contain the answer (no genuine multihop reasoning possible), scores 64.8 F1 on HotpotQA and 60.1 on 2WikiMultihopQA — but only 32.0 on MuSiQue-Ans. MuSiQue-Full, which pairs every answerable instance with a near-identical unanswerable twin, is harder still: the strongest multihop model's combined answer+sufficiency score drops 14–17 points versus MuSiQue-Ans, and every artifact-based model collapses to near-zero.

Two ablations (Table 6) show which pipeline stages are actually load-bearing. Removing the Disconnection Filter (\ DF) lets the End2End model's answer F1 jump from 42.3 to 60.6 and the 1-Para artifact model jump from 32.0 to 59.2. Removing the leakage-minimizing split (\ RL) is even more dramatic: End2End answer F1 jumps to 87.3, and even the context-only artifact model (which never sees the question) reaches 69.5 F1 — evidence that naive random splitting alone would have let models memorize their way to a high score.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

MuSiQue is unusually self-aware for a benchmark paper — it runs its own cheatability audit (DiRe scoring, the 1-Para/C-only/Q-only artifact battery, the \DF and \RL ablations) rather than asserting difficulty from a single leaderboard number. That's a real methodological strength. But the guarantee it delivers is narrower than "models can't shortcut this dataset" — it's "models resembling Longformer-Large and RoBERTa-large, circa 2020, can't shortcut this dataset," and that adversary is now five years stale.

baselines

Fair for its era: EE, SA, and EX variants are all Transformer-based (Longformer-Large / RoBERTa-large / BART-large) and the paper even re-verifies its own RNN reimplementation against the original HotpotQA baseline (64.0 vs. the original paper's reported 58.3 F1) before using it. But every filtering and evaluation model predates in-context few-shot prompting; the paper never tests a prompted LLM against its own disconnection filter.

contamination

The pipeline explicitly minimizes train-test leakage at the single-hop level (step S5) — a genuine strength most benchmark papers skip. But the five source datasets (SQuAD, Natural Questions, MLQA, T-REx, Zero-Shot RE) have been public training-corpus staples since 2016–2019; a model that has memorized those underlying single-hop (question, answer) pairs from pretraining could re-derive shortcuts MuSiQue's leakage control never anticipated, since it only controls leakage within the MuSiQue splits, not exposure to the source datasets during pretraining.

what's missed

The DiRe/disconnection-filtering apparatus is a property of the specific adversary model used to build it (Longformer-Large). The \DF ablation proves the filter mattered against 2021 Transformers; it says nothing about whether a 2024-era in-context reasoner would find the "connected" survivors just as easy to shortcut through memorized world knowledge or different heuristics the filter was never calibrated to catch.

would raise confidence

Re-running the DiRe/1-Para/C-only artifact battery with a modern few-shot-prompted LLM as the "adversary," rather than only fine-tuned 2020-era Transformers, to check whether the connectedness guarantee still holds against the class of model the dataset is now mostly used to evaluate (as IRCoT and its successors do).

// bottom line

MuSiQue's bottom-up composition plus explicit disconnection filtering is a genuinely more rigorous construction than HotpotQA's or 2WikiMultihopQA's, and the ablations back that up with real numbers rather than just design intent. The open question the paper can't answer about itself is whether "connected reasoning" as certified against Longformer-Large in 2021 still means the same thing against the LLMs the dataset is used to benchmark today.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. Composing multihop questions bottom-up from filtered, connected single-hop pairs — rather than crowdsourcing them end-to-end — is what actually suppressed the shortcuts that made HotpotQA and 2WikiMultihopQA gameable: DiRe cheatability score drops from 68.8/63.4 to 37.8.
  2. Train-test leakage control matters as much as question design: removing it (the \RL ablation) let a context-only model that never even sees the question reach 69.5 answer F1, nearly matching the full multihop model's 87.3.
  3. MuSiQue (both -Ans and its unanswerable-augmented -Full sibling) became a standard stress test for the retrieval-augmented reasoning systems that followed it — including IRCoT, which evaluates directly against MuSiQue's open-domain variant.
