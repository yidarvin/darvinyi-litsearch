"Why Should I Trust You?": Explaining the Predictions of Any Classifier

LIME explains one prediction at a time: fit a sparse, locally faithful surrogate model around a single instance, in a representation any human can read, regardless of what the underlying black-box classifier actually is.

Marco Tulio Ribeiro, Sameer Singh, Carlos Guestrin — University of Washington

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

By 2016, deploying a classifier meant trusting a single scalar: held-out accuracy. The paper opens with a demonstration of why that number can be actively misleading. The authors train an SVM with an RBF kernel to separate "Christianity" from "Atheism" on a 20 Newsgroups subset; it reaches 94% held-out accuracy — a number that would normally end the conversation. But when they explain individual predictions, the model turns out to be keying on words like "Posting," "Host," and "Re" — leftover email headers that happen to correlate with the label in this particular split, not anything about the text's content. ("Posting" appears in 22% of training examples, 99% of those labeled "Atheism.") A test-set metric that looks excellent is hiding a classifier that has learned essentially nothing about the task.

The paper's second running example makes the same point visually: a deliberately miscalibrated image classifier labels a photograph of a husky as a "wolf" — not because it has learned canine morphology, but because every wolf photo in its small training set happened to have a snowy background. Both anecdotes share a structure: the model is behaving reasonably by the metric it was optimized on, and unreasonably by any metric a human actually cares about, and nothing about the accuracy number reveals this.

Existing tools didn't close the gap. Systems like Modeltracker and Gestalt help practitioners browse individual predictions, but they're navigation aids — they don't explain why a model made a specific call. Other work anticipated failure modes for vision tasks specifically, but needed extra annotations and didn't generalize past images. And any approach that buys interpretability by restricting model choice up front (sparse linear models, small decision trees, rule lists) forecloses using whatever performs best — which by 2016 was increasingly an ensemble, a kernel method, or a deep network, architectures whose internal decision logic resists any single global description. What was missing was a way to answer, instance by instance and for any model: is this specific prediction trustworthy, and why?

// how it works

The method

LIME (Local Interpretable Model-agnostic Explanations) sidesteps the trade-off between interpretability and model choice by not trying to explain the whole model at once. For a black-box classifier f and an instance x, it asks a narrower question: what's a simple, human-readable model g that faithfully mimics f in x's immediate neighborhood?

ξ(x) = argming∈G L(f, g, πx) + Ω(g) // G = interpretable model class (sparse linear, here); L = locally-weighted fidelity loss; Ω = complexity penalty (a hard cap of K non-zero terms)

The trick that makes this work for literally any f is operating in an interpretable representation rather than the model's native feature space. A document becomes a binary vector over present/absent words; an image is segmented into superpixels and represented as present/absent superpixels, grayed out when "absent." LIME perturbs this binary representation — randomly toggling components to sample z′ around x′ — maps each sample back to the original representation, queries the black box for f(z), and weights the sample by a proximity kernel πx(z) = exp(−D(x,z)²/σ²). Algorithm 1 fits this weighted sample set with what the paper calls K-LASSO: select K features via the Lasso regularization path, then refit their weights by least squares.

Because the fitting cost depends only on querying f and the number of perturbed samples N (set to 15,000 via cross-validation for the faithfulness and trust experiments) — never on the size of the training set or the internals of f — the same procedure explains an SVM, a random forest, or a deep network without modification. In a separate runtime illustration the paper reports that explaining a 1,000-tree random forest with N = 5,000 takes under 3 seconds on a laptop with no GPU or parallelization; explaining one prediction of a pretrained Inception network takes about 10 minutes.

A single explanation says something about one prediction, not about whether to trust the model as a whole. The paper's second contribution, SP-LIME (submodular pick), selects a small, non-redundant budget of B instances whose explanations collectively cover the model's global behavior. Given an explanation matrix W (rows = instances, columns = interpretable features, entries = |weight|) and a global importance score Ij = √(Σi Wij) per feature, the pick problem is a maximum-coverage problem — NP-hard in general, but since coverage is monotone submodular, a greedy algorithm that always adds the instance with the highest marginal coverage gain achieves a 1 − 1/e approximation to the optimum (Algorithm 2).

// what they found

The results

The paper runs two simulated-user experiments with an algorithmic ground truth, plus three real-human-subject studies.

Faithfulness (simulated ground truth)

To check that explanations actually reflect what the classifier is doing rather than just looking plausible, the authors train classifiers that are themselves interpretable — sparse logistic regression and decision trees, both capped at 10 features per prediction — so the "true" important features are known exactly, then measure what fraction of those gold features each method's explanation recovers. On the Books sentiment dataset, LIME recovers 92.1% of the truly important features for sparse LR and 97.0% for decision trees, against 72.8% / 78.9% for a Parzen-window gradient baseline, 64.3% / 37.0% for greedy feature ablation, and 17.4% / 20.6% for random selection. The DVDs dataset tells the same story: 90.2% / 97.8% for LIME vs. 60.8% / 80.6% (Parzen), 63.4% / 47.6% (greedy), 19.2% / 17.4% (random).

Simulated trust

The authors then simulate the "should I trust this prediction?" task directly: mark 25% of features as synthetically untrustworthy, define an oracle that labels a prediction "untrustworthy" if it flips when those features are removed, and check whether a simulated user relying on each explanation reaches the oracle's verdict. Averaged over four classifier families (logistic regression, nearest-neighbor, random forest, SVM), LIME's F1 on this task is:

Human subjects

Three studies test whether this translates to real people. First, 100 subjects per condition were shown explanations from two classifiers — one deliberately built to generalize better than its held-out accuracy would suggest — and asked to pick the one that would perform better in the real world. With submodular pick, subjects chose the correct classifier 89.0% of the time using LIME explanations (vs. 75.0% with random pick), and 80.0% vs. 68.0% for the same comparison using a greedy explainer.

Second, in a religion-classification feature-engineering task, crowd workers who could see only LIME's word-level explanations (never the underlying leaked-header dataset itself) removed features they judged spurious over three rounds of interaction, raising real-world accuracy from about 0.57 (no cleaning) to about 0.74 with submodular pick and about 0.69 with random pick, at an average of 3.6 minutes of work per round.

Third, in the paper's flagship demonstration, 27 machine-learning graduate students were shown a deliberately bad husky-vs-wolf classifier's raw prediction, then its LIME explanation:

Before seeing the explanation, 10 of 27 subjects said they trusted the classifier, and 12 of 27 identified "snow" as a feature the model might be using. After seeing the explanation, trust dropped to 3 of 27, and correct identification of the snow artifact rose to 25 of 27.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

LIME's experiments are carefully designed to isolate each claim, but several are more favorable to the method than a fully independent evaluation would be. The clearest case is the faithfulness benchmark: the "gold" important-feature set is defined by training classifiers capped at exactly K = 10 features — the same K the paper uses to configure LIME's own explanation budget. A method whose complexity penalty matches the evaluation's ground-truth cardinality has a structural advantage before a single sample is drawn, and the experiment says nothing about how LIME's recall behaves when the true number of important features is smaller, larger, or unknown — as it always is for the black-box random-forest-on-word2vec classifier the paper calls "impossible to interpret without a technique like LIME" but never scores for faithfulness.

The baseline set is also thin: Parzen-window gradients and greedy single-feature ablation were reasonable 2016 comparisons, but neither is a rigorously optimal alternative explainer, and there's no comparison against a simple global surrogate (one decision tree fit to the whole model), which would have tested whether locality — LIME's central design bet — is actually pulling its weight over a cruder alternative. The human-subject studies are informative but small: the feature-engineering study starts from 10 subjects (250 total interaction paths after three rounds), and the husky/wolf trust reversal — arguably the paper's most-cited single result — rests on 27 graduate students, with no confidence intervals or significance tests reported alongside the percentages.

Most consequential in hindsight: LIME's model-agnosticism comes from querying f only through perturbed samples in the interpretable space, and the paper never checks whether those samples are realistic inputs to f — whether they land anywhere near the manifold the classifier was actually trained on. That gap was unknowable in 2016, but it's exactly what Slack et al.'s 2019/2020 "Fooling LIME and SHAP" exploited: a classifier can behave innocuously on LIME's synthetic, out-of-distribution perturbations while relying on a biased feature everywhere else, defeating the explanation by construction.

baselines

Parzen-window gradients + naive greedy ablation + random — reasonable for 2016 but not rigorously optimal, and no global-surrogate baseline to isolate what locality specifically buys you.

contamination

Not a data-leakage issue in the benchmark sense — the analogous risk is circularity: the synthetic "gold" feature sets are defined by capping classifiers at K=10, the same K used to configure LIME's own explanations.

what's missed

No per-instance fidelity / local-R² reporting, no sensitivity analysis over kernel width σ, sample count N, or K, and no check that perturbation sampling stays in-distribution — later shown exploitable by Slack et al. (2019/2020).

would raise confidence

Per-instance faithfulness distributions rather than aggregate recall on self-defined ground truth, a global-surrogate baseline, larger pre-registered human trials with confidence intervals, and an explicit in-distribution sampling check.

// bottom line

LIME's central bet — trade the global explanation you can't get for a local one that's faithful, sparse, and model-agnostic — has aged remarkably well: it's the direct ancestor of SHAP and Anchors and remains one of the first two or three interpretability methods most practitioners reach for. But the 2016 evaluation is closer to a compelling proof-of-concept than a rigorous stress test — the faithfulness numbers are measured against ground truth tuned to LIME's own hyperparameter, the human trials are small, and the very perturbation-sampling strategy that makes LIME model-agnostic is also its best-documented later weakness.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. LIME reframes "can I trust this model?" as "can I trust this specific prediction?" — fit a sparse, locally-weighted surrogate g around one instance x, rather than trying to explain the whole classifier at once.
  2. It's model-agnostic by construction — it only ever queries the black box on perturbed samples — which is also its most-cited later weakness: nothing in the 2016 evaluation checks whether those perturbed samples resemble real, in-distribution inputs, the exact gap 2019's "Fooling LIME and SHAP" exploited.
  3. Submodular pick (SP-LIME) turns single-prediction explanations into a compact, near-optimal diagnostic tour of a model via greedy maximum-coverage — and every human result in the paper, from choosing the better classifier (89.0% vs. 75.0%) to the husky/wolf trust reversal (10/27 → 3/27), is testing the same underlying claim: seeing why changes what you trust.
