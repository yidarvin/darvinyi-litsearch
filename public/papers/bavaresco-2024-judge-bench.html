LLMs instead of Human Judges? A Large Scale Empirical Study across 20 NLP Evaluation Tasks

Twenty human-annotated NLP datasets, eleven LLM judges, one blunt question: can a model reliably stand in for a human rater? The answer is task-, property-, and expertise-dependent — GPT-4o leads on average but loses outright to open Mixtral models on some tasks, and every judge aligns better with non-expert annotators than with domain experts.

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

Using an LLM to grade another model's output — "LLM-as-a-judge" — had by 2024 become the field's default substitute for human evaluation: it underpins leaderboards, RLHF reward signals, and most rubric-based evals, largely because it is drastically cheaper than paying human raters. But the authors point out that the evidence for trusting it is mixed: some prior work reports LLM judges correlating well with humans (G-Eval, MT-Bench and Chatbot Arena, and several others), while other work finds substantial bias, both against human judgments directly and through purely intrinsic analyses of judge behavior. The authors argue this isn't really a contradiction — it's an artifact of scale: "these discrepancies likely stem from the limitations of this previous work, which typically relies on a few datasets and models, often restricted to closed-source proprietary models."

There's a second problem layered on top: reproducibility. Closed proprietary judges "may be retrained or retired at any time, making subsequent comparisons invalid or impossible" — and the field's growing reliance on LLM judges is compounded by "explicit or implicit data leakage," which the authors flag as undermining any claim that a validation result on one dataset generalizes beyond it.

JUDGE-BENCH is built to answer the scale problem directly: instead of one or two tasks and a handful of models, evaluate 11 current LLMs — both open-weight and proprietary — across 20 datasets with existing human annotations, spanning task type, judged property, judgment format (categorical vs. graded), and annotator expertise (expert vs. non-expert), so that any conclusion about "are LLM judges reliable" has to hold up across genuine variation rather than one favorable setup.

// how it works

The method

JUDGE-BENCH unifies 20 existing NLP datasets under one schema, retaining every individual human annotation rather than just an aggregate score — among them CoLA and its fine-grained CoLA-grammar variant, Switchboard and DailyDialog (dialogue acceptability), Inferential strategies (deductive-reasoning validity), ToxicChat, LLMBar, Persona Chat and Topical Chat, ROSCOE (reasoning-trace quality, evaluated across four source datasets: GSM8K, e-SNLI, DROP, CosmosQA), a recipe-generation planning set, Medical-safety, DICES, WMT 2020 and 2023 machine-translation human evaluations, SummEval, QAGS, and NewsRoom. Together they total over 70,000 test instances with human judgments. Critically, the collection deliberately mixes two kinds of judged content: model-generated text (translations, dialogue responses, generated plans and arguments), where the goal is evaluating an NLP system, and human-generated text (assessed for grammaticality, toxicity, etc.), which lets the authors check whether LLM judges carry a positive bias toward machine-generated output — a bias reported in prior work.

Eleven models are tested: GPT-4o, LLaMA-3.1 (8B and 70B), Gemini-1.5, Mixtral (8x7B and 8x22B), Command R and Command R+, OLMo-7B, Starling-7B, and Mistral-7B — selected for showing strong performance on the Open LLM and Chatbot Arena leaderboards. Wherever a dataset's original human-annotation instructions are available, those instructions are reused verbatim as the model prompt, appended with a short output constraint ("Answer with one of {}. Do not explain your answer.") to keep responses parseable; where the original instructions aren't available, a prompt is constructed from the source paper's task description. The authors also test Chain-of-Thought prompting across the full 20-dataset set (with 9 of the 11 models — Mixtral-8x22B and Command R+ are excluded for computational reasons) and, more narrowly, few-shot and paraphrased prompts on just three datasets the models struggled with (DICES-350-expert and the two WMT 2023 pairs) — but find none of these variants produces a systematic improvement.

Scoring splits along the categorical/graded line: for graded judgments (continuous 0–100 or Likert-style scores) they compute Spearman's correlation (ρ) between model and human scores; for categorical judgments (binary or discrete labels, e.g. grammatical/ungrammatical) they compute Cohen's κ. Because models sometimes refuse or fail to produce a parseable answer, the evaluation protocol makes a specific, consequential choice: "To obtain the same number of judgments across models for a given dataset, we replace invalid LLM responses with judgments randomly sampled from the relevant set of categorical or graded annotations." Finally, where a dataset has multiple individual human raters (typically three), the authors bootstrap an upper-bound (UB) estimate — the average agreement between a single held-out human rater and the aggregate of the others — as a ceiling for how well any judge, human or LLM, could be expected to score.

// what they found

The results

The headline comparison covers the six models with the highest valid-response rates — GPT-4o, Llama-3.1-70B, Mixtral-8x22B, Gemini-1.5, Mixtral-8x7B, and Command R+. Averaged across categorical datasets, GPT-4o and Llama-3.1-70B tie for the best mean Cohen's κ at 0.28 (Mixtral-8x22B 0.24, Gemini-1.5 0.22, Mixtral-8x7B 0.21, Command R+ 0.10); on graded datasets GPT-4o leads mean Spearman's ρ at 0.50, ahead of Mixtral-8x22B (0.44), Llama-3.1-70B (0.43), Gemini-1.5 (0.43), Mixtral-8x7B (0.38), and Command R+ (0.30). But "GPT-4o leads on average" hides real per-task reversals: on CoLA (sentence acceptability) Mixtral-8x7B scores 0.55 and Mixtral-8x22B scores 0.54 against GPT-4o's 0.34, and on SummEval (graded summary quality) Mixtral-8x22B reaches 0.54 versus GPT-4o's 0.35 — the open models outright beat the proprietary leader on specific properties.

Toxicity and safety judgments are the weakest category across every model. On DICES-990, all six headline models score a negative Cohen's κ (GPT-4o −0.24, Llama-3.1-70B −0.17, Mixtral-8x22B −0.16, Gemini-1.5 −0.12, Mixtral-8x7B −0.20, Command R+ −0.09) — worse than chance agreement. The authors trace part of this to safety guardrails: models facing a medical or safety-sensitive prompt often generate an explanation or a refusal instead of a judgment, and on DICES specifically they tend to over-select "Unsure" or "Unsafe" rather than ever calling something "Safe."

Two broader patterns hold across nearly every model. First, on datasets with graded annotations every model correlates more strongly with non-expert human judges than with domain-expert judges — the authors offer a speculative explanation (non-experts may rely on surface-level features that happen to align with what LLMs are most sensitive to) but are explicit that "this remains speculative and calls for further investigation." Second (Figure 4), every model aligns better with human-written text than with machine-generated text, for both categorical and graded annotations alike — consistent with prior reports that LLMs carry a bias toward their own kind of output.

Per-property results (Figure 3) show no single model dominating across the board: GPT-4o and Gemini-1.5 top acceptability and verbosity, the two Mixtral models take coherence and consistency, and every model's correlation on "engagingness" stays low no matter which judge is asked. Finally, elicitation tricks don't reliably help: Chain-of-Thought prompting sometimes helps (e.g., Llama-3.1-8B's ρ on WMT 2023 En-De rises from 0.08 to 0.18) and sometimes hurts (the same model's κ on DICES-350-expert falls from 0.01 to −0.07), and the paper's own conclusion is that CoT "does not reliably improve agreement."

// does it hold up?

The eval read

JUDGE-BENCH's scale — 20 datasets, 11 models, expert/non-expert and human/machine splits — is exactly the kind of breadth the paper's own gap analysis says prior work lacked, and the result (no model wins everywhere, alignment varies enormously by property) is a genuinely more honest picture than a single headline correlation number. But two choices baked into the evaluation protocol deserve more scrutiny than the paper gives them.

The first is the invalid-response substitution rule. Valid-response rates vary hugely across the 11 models — from 0.70 for Starling-7B and 0.84 for OLMo-7B up to ~1.00 for the strongest models (Appendix Figure 5) — and every non-answer for a low-compliance model gets swapped for "judgments randomly sampled from the relevant set of categorical or graded annotations." That keeps sample sizes equal across models, but it also means roughly 30% of Starling-7B's reported score is, mechanically, agreement with random guessing rather than agreement with the model's own judgment. The paper reports this design choice plainly but never separately reports what each model's score would look like scored as a failure instead of backfilled with a random label — so a reader can't tell how much of the low-compliance models' weak scores are "the model is a bad judge" versus "the model often refuses to answer, and refusals get randomized."

The second is contamination, which the paper raises as a concern for the field in its introduction but then doesn't audit in its own benchmark. JUDGE-BENCH's own Table 2 flags CoLA and CoLA-grammar as "Leaked" per Balloccu et al. (2024) — i.e., known or suspected to already be present in the pretraining data of the very models being tested on them — and the Limitations section concedes that "employing existing datasets could arguably lead to potential risks and shortcomings, such as data leakage." No decontamination pass or leakage-excluded re-analysis is run, so CoLA's contribution to the headline averages is left exactly as-is.

baselines

Genuinely broader than prior single-dataset judge studies — 11 models spanning open and proprietary, several sizes, and CoT re-run across all 20 datasets with 9 of them. But only one frontier proprietary judge (GPT-4o) is included; there's no Claude, no GPT-4-class reasoning model, and the few-shot/paraphrase robustness checks are run on only 3 of the 20 datasets and 3 models, so "elicitation strategies don't help" leans partly on that narrower slice too.

contamination

Explicitly acknowledged as a field-wide risk in the introduction and flagged for two of the paper's own 20 datasets (CoLA, CoLA-grammar) in its own dataset table — but never controlled for. Headline averages include the flagged-leaked datasets without adjustment or a leakage-excluded comparison.

what's missed

Pairwise/preference-style judging is explicitly out of scope (deferred to future work, with PairEval and JudgeBench cited as the relevant alternative paradigm), the study is almost entirely English-language, and correlation-with-humans is measured — not correctness — a limitation the authors themselves note: humans and LLMs could look "aligned" while sharing the same underlying bias.

would raise confidence

Report scores both ways for low-valid-response models (as-failed vs. randomly-backfilled); exclude or separately flag the Balloccu-identified leaked datasets in headline numbers; extend the few-shot/paraphrase sweep beyond its current 3 datasets; and re-run with frontier reasoning-class judges now that over a year has passed since the study's models were current.

// bottom line

The paper's central message — validate an LLM judge against task-specific human data before deploying it, because reliability swings wildly by task, property, and annotator expertise — is well supported and genuinely useful. Treat the specific per-model leaderboard numbers more cautiously: they bake in a random-substitution rule for unreliable judges and include at least two self-flagged, potentially contaminated datasets without adjustment.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. JUDGE-BENCH unifies 20 pre-existing human-annotated NLP datasets (70,000+ instances) under one schema specifically to stress-test whether 11 current LLMs can replicate human judgments — scored with Cohen's κ for categorical labels and Spearman's ρ for graded scores, with a bootstrapped human upper bound for context.
  2. No model wins everywhere: GPT-4o and Llama-3.1-70B tie for the best average categorical alignment (κ 0.28) and GPT-4o leads graded alignment (ρ 0.50), but open Mixtral models outright beat GPT-4o on CoLA and SummEval, every model aligns better with non-expert than expert human annotators, and toxicity/safety judgments go negative for all six headline models on DICES-990.
  3. Read the leaderboard, not just the headline: unreliable judges have their refused/invalid answers silently backfilled with random human labels rather than scored as wrong, and the paper's own dataset table flags CoLA and CoLA-grammar as known-leaked — neither caveat is factored into the reported per-model averages.
