ContractNLI: A Dataset for Document-level Natural Language Inference for Contracts

Reframe contract review as natural language inference: pose a policy as a hypothesis ("Receiving Party shall not use Confidential Information for any purpose other than the stated purposes"), hand a model a whole NDA, and ask it to decide entailed / contradicting / not-mentioned — and to point at the evidence spans. 607 annotated contracts, a [SPAN]-token model for long documents, and a hard lesson about where it breaks.

Yuta Koreeda, Christopher D. Manning · Hitachi America / Stanford

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

Contract review is slow, expensive, and unevenly distributed. The paper opens with an industry figure (Exigent Group, 2019): 60–80% of all business-to-business transactions are governed by some written agreement, and a typical Fortune 1000 company keeps 20,000 to 40,000 active contracts at any time. That review is done by hand by professionals — which means smaller companies and individuals often sign without it, a fairness problem as much as a cost problem.

The contract-NLP that existed in 2021 — lease-clause extraction (Leivaditi et al., 2020) and CUAD (Hendrycks et al., 2021) — framed review as information extraction: find where a topic is discussed and highlight the span. CUAD answers "is there a restriction on soliciting or hiring employees?" by extracting the relevant text. Useful, but it tells you only that a clause exists, not what it decides. You still have to read the span to know whether it violates your policy.

ContractNLI's reframing: treat review as natural language inference. Fix a set of policy statements as hypotheses, and ask whether a whole contract entails, contradicts, or doesn't mention each one — plus surface the evidence. Now a system can flag clauses that conflict with your policy without you reading every extracted term.

Why this isn't just NLI with longer text: standard NLI (SNLI/MNLI) judges a hypothesis against a single short premise sentence. Here the premise is an entire document — on average 2,254 tokens, with 86% of documents exceeding BERT's 512-token limit — and the task additionally demands evidence identification, an open-domain-QA-style retrieval problem that NLI rarely couples with. That combination is the novel, hard part.

The dataset

The authors collected 607 non-disclosure agreements (NDAs) — a single contract type, chosen because NDAs are easy to gather and a fixed type lets you write finer-grained hypotheses. Documents came from Google search (across seven English-speaking-country domains) and SEC EDGAR exhibits; EDGAR yielded just 236 NDAs from 28,780 screened candidates. They wrote 17 hypotheses (reviewed by paralegals), each a single sentence such as "Some obligations of Agreement may survive termination.", and held that same closed set fixed across train, dev, and test.

The labels are imbalanced by design: a document averages 77.8 candidate spans, most entailed/contradicting hypotheses have only one or two evidence spans (a few up to nine), and CONTRADICTION is rare across most hypotheses (see the distribution in the results below). That scarcity foreshadows where the models struggle.

// how it works

The method: Span NLI BERT

Each example is a (contract, hypothesis) pair with two coupled outputs: a document-level 3-way NLI label {ENTAILMENT, CONTRADICTION, NOTMENTIONED}, and evidence identification — which spans justify the decision (defined only when the label is ENTAILMENT or CONTRADICTION). A span is a whole sentence or a list item within a sentence, on purpose: a lawyer needs to read evidence with comprehensible context, unlike factoid QA where a token span suffices.

Prior Transformer approaches did evidence finding by predicting start and end tokens and scaled to long documents with a static window and stride. The authors argue both choices are wrong here. Start/end prediction forces the model to solve span-boundary detection and evidence identification at once, even though the spans are already defined by sentence structure; and a static window can split a span across contexts or starve it of surrounding context. Span NLI BERT fixes both:

  • Evidence as multi-label classification over predefined spans. Insert a special [SPAN] token before each span; put an MLP + sigmoid on top of each [SPAN] token to predict whether that span is evidence. Boundaries are fixed, so the model only decides relevance — straightforward multi-label binary classification.
  • Dynamic context segmentation (Algorithm 1). Split a long document into overlapping windows with a dynamic stride, guaranteeing that every span appears un-split, with at least n surrounding tokens, in at least one window.

The NLI head sits on the [CLS] token (MLP + softmax over the three labels). The two heads train jointly with a multi-task loss, where NLI loss is zeroed for any context that happens to contain no evidence span — otherwise an ENTAILMENT/CONTRADICTION teacher signal would contradict what that window actually shows.

ℓ = ℓspan + λ · ℓNLI // per-context multi-task loss; ℓNLI = 0 for contexts with no evidence span

Because a document is scored as several overlapping windows, predictions are aggregated: span probabilities are averaged, and the document NLI prediction is a weighted average of per-window NLI probabilities, weighted by each window's summed span probability — so windows that actually contain evidence count more toward the verdict.

// what they found

The results

Span NLI BERT beats every baseline on both sub-tasks. Evidence identification is scored with mAP and P@R80 (precision at 80% recall, the same coverage-oriented metric CUAD used); NLI is scored with accuracy plus per-label F1 for Contradiction and Entailment, macro-averaged so the label imbalance can't flatter the numbers.

Two comparisons make the architecture's case. First, a SQuAD-style start/end BERT-base (mAP .825) is no better than a TF-IDF + SVM over spans (.836) — predicting boundaries rather than classifying predefined spans throws away the structure. Second, the smaller Span NLI BERT-base (mAP .885) beats the larger SQuAD BERT-large (.869): the architectural change buys more than scaling the backbone. Scaling still helps within Span NLI BERT — DeBERTa-v2-xlarge reaches the top, and transfer-fine-tuning it on CUAD's span-identification task is the single best model (NLI accuracy .892). Legal-domain pretraining was a wash, though: a from-scratch case-law BERT (Zheng et al., 2021) didn't help, and LEGAL-BERT (Chalkidis et al., 2020) helped evidence but hurt NLI (accuracy .794 vs .838).

The headline number hides the real story, which is in the Contradiction column. Even the best Span NLI BERT-large posts F1(C) of just .357 against F1(E) of .834 — the rare CONTRADICTION class is where everything falls apart. The dataset's imbalance is the proximate cause:

Two diagnostic experiments sharpen the picture. Evidence identification still has headroom to lift NLI. In a binary entailment-vs-contradiction probe, feeding the model oracle ground-truth evidence spans lifts contradiction F1 from .490 to .657 (BERT-base); the paper reads this as showing there is “much room for improvement on NLI just by improving evidence identification.” That oracle ceiling of .657 is still well short of perfect, so contradiction reasoning remains hard in its own right. And the hypothesis text is under-used: replacing each hypothesis's words with a meaningless per-hypothesis symbol consistently lowers scores — but only narrowly — implying the model leans on shallow cues rather than truly reading the hypothesis.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

ContractNLI's framing is genuinely useful and its long-document recipe was ahead of its time. But as a benchmark — a yardstick others will trust — it has structural soft spots that the aggregate mAP hides.

The gold labels are not attorney-verified, and no agreement is reported. The 17 hypotheses were reviewed by paralegals, but the per-document labels come from a single primary annotator (a computational-linguistics researcher, not a lawyer), assisted by two hand-picked, consistently-strong MTurk workers who redundantly marked candidate spans for coverage. The primary annotator alone judged the NLI labels and consolidated span boundaries, and most of dev and some of test were annotated solely by that one person. No inter-annotator agreement (κ) is reported anywhere. For a legal-reasoning gold standard that is a real reliability gap — and a sharp contrast with CUAD, whose labels came from legal experts and were triple-verified.

The hypothesis set is closed and shared across splits. The same 17 hypotheses appear in train and test. So the benchmark measures generalization to new contracts but never to new policy questions — exactly the axis a deployed contract-review tool would have to handle. The authors even note the task "could also be addressed by building a text classifier for each hypothesis": it is close to 17-way multi-task classification dressed as NLI, and a model can simply memorize the 17 prompts.

The metric that matters is the weakest and noisiest. Evidence mAP of .92 reads as "solved," but the binding constraint is contradiction F1 (.357, with a ±.039 std over the three best-of-ten hyperparameter runs, on a 123-document test set) and minority-label accuracy under negation by exception — a general rule later flipped by an exception ("...shall not disclose... except to its employees"), sometimes pages away. On hypotheses exhibiting non-local negation by exception, the model's minority-label accuracy collapses to .00 (weighted .45 vs .85 without); the local case drops it to .40 (vs .77). The system fails precisely on the adversarial-drafting patterns that contract review most needs to catch.

Scope and provenance are narrow. One contract type (NDAs), scraped from public web search and EDGAR exhibits, 607 documents total. Generalization to other contract types is untested (acknowledged as future work), and the small scale forces a 10-configuration hyperparameter search (averaging the three best-on-dev models) just to stabilize the numbers.

baselines

Fair for 2021: beats the incumbent start/end-token SQuAD-style model and TF-IDF SVMs, and ablates backbone scale and legal-domain pretraining. No generative-LLM baseline — it predates them — so the strongest comparison is encoder-only.

contamination

The contracts are public (EDGAR + web) and the labeled set is on GitHub. A non-issue for the 2021 encoders tested, but any LLM-era score on ContractNLI must treat document and label leakage as live.

what's missed

Aggregate evidence mAP (.92) flatters the system. The real signal is contradiction F1 (.36) and minority-label accuracy under negation-by-exception (→ .00 non-locally) — both buried under the headline.

would raise confidence

Held-out hypotheses (not just held-out contracts); multiple independent expert annotators with a reported κ; more contract types; and phenomenon-level breakdowns (negation, discontinuous spans, definition references) reported as the primary metric.

// bottom line

The lasting contribution is the framing — contract review as document-level NLI with evidence — and the [SPAN]-token + dynamic-segmentation recipe for long documents that later legal benchmarks such as LegalBench cite. But it is a small, single-type, fixed-hypothesis benchmark whose gold labels rest on one primary annotator with no reported agreement, and whose own numbers show models still fail on the contradiction and exception cases that contract review most needs to get right.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. ContractNLI reframes contract review as document-level NLI plus evidence identification: 17 fixed policy hypotheses × a whole NDA → {entailed, contradicting, not-mentioned} with supporting spans. It was the first NLI-for-contracts dataset and, at 607 NDAs, the largest annotated contract corpus of its time.
  2. Span NLI BERT's two ideas — multi-label classification over predefined [SPAN] tokens (instead of start/end prediction) and dynamic context segmentation so each span gets enough context — let a BERT-base model beat a SQuAD-style BERT-large. Architecture beat scale; DeBERTa-xlarge with CUAD transfer was best overall (NLI accuracy .892).
  3. The benchmark's own analysis is the real lesson: evidence finding is nearly solved (mAP .92), but document-level legal reasoning is not. Contradiction F1 stays low (.357 for BERT-large), and "negation by exception" crushes minority-label accuracy to .00 non-locally — the cases that matter most are the ones models miss.
