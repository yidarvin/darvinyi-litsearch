DeepSeek-V2: A Strong, Economical, and Efficient Mixture-of-Experts Language Model

236B total parameters, 21B activated per token — DeepSeek-V2 pairs a new low-rank compressed attention mechanism with a fine-grained Mixture-of-Experts design to cut training cost 42.5%, shrink the KV cache 93.3%, and lift inference throughput 5.76× over its dense predecessor, while landing top-tier among open models on English, code, math, and Chinese benchmarks.

Shao, Dai, Guo, Wang, Xin, et al. (DeepSeek-AI)

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

By 2024 the standard recipe for a stronger open LLM was simply more parameters, and both halves of that recipe had become a serving and training liability. Dense models — including DeepSeek's own 67B predecessor — scale their per-token compute and their Key-Value (KV) cache linearly with size: every token generated at inference time needs a cached key and value vector for every attention head in every layer, and for a 67B dense model with standard Multi-Head Attention (MHA) that cache alone can dwarf the model's own weights in GPU memory once batch size or context length grow, capping how many requests a single node can serve concurrently. The fixes already on the table for this — Multi-Query Attention (MQA), which collapses all query heads onto one shared key/value head, and Grouped-Query Attention (GQA), which shares across small groups instead of one — do shrink the cache, but each does it by literally deleting information the attention mechanism could otherwise use, and both concede measurable quality relative to full MHA.

On the training side, Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) architectures such as GShard promised to decouple total model capacity from activated compute per token, but coarse-grained routing over a handful of large experts limits how specialized any single expert can become, and expert-parallel training introduces its own cross-device communication tax. DeepSeek-V2 targets both bottlenecks at once: an attention mechanism with a small cache that doesn't trade away quality, and an MoE design with finer-grained expert specialization that trains more economically than the dense alternative — rather than picking one axis to optimize and accepting a cost on the other.

// how it works

The method

DeepSeek-V2 keeps the Transformer skeleton but replaces both of its major sub-layers. Standard MHA must cache the full key and value vectors for every one of nh attention heads at every layer — 2·nh·dh·l elements per token, where dh is the per-head dimension and l the layer count. Multi-head Latent Attention (MLA) instead down-projects the input hidden state into a single low-rank latent vector cKV ∈ ℝdc (with dc ≪ nh·dh), then up-projects that one latent back into per-head keys and values only when needed. Because the up-projection matrices for keys and values can be algebraically absorbed into the query and output projection matrices at inference time, the model never has to materialize per-head keys or values at all — it only ever caches the compressed latent. The one wrinkle is rotary position embeddings (RoPE): RoPE is position-sensitive, so applying it directly to the compressed keys would break the matrix-absorption trick. DeepSeek-V2's fix is a small decoupled RoPE path — extra low-dimensional queries and a single shared key that carry positional information separately from the compressed content path — so the model still needs to cache that decoupled key alongside the latent.

KV cache / token = (dc + dRh)·l ≈ 92 dh·l // with DeepSeek-V2's dc = 4dh, dRh = dh/2 — equal to GQA with only 2.25 groups, but (per the paper's own Appendix D.2 ablation) MHA-level or better quality, not a compromise

For the feed-forward half of each block, DeepSeek-V2 adopts the DeepSeekMoE design from the team's earlier companion paper: every layer holds 2 shared experts (always active, absorbing the "common knowledge" every token needs so routed experts don't have to re-learn it) plus 160 fine-grained routed experts, of which the router activates only 6 per token. Cutting experts into many small pieces instead of a handful of large ones is a deliberate bet that narrower experts specialize better. Because those 160 routed experts are scattered across 8 devices under expert parallelism, and a token's chosen experts could in principle land anywhere, DeepSeek-V2 adds device-limited routing — each token is confined to at most M=3 devices, selected from among the devices holding its highest-affinity experts — plus three separate auxiliary losses (expert-level, device-level, and communication-level balance) to keep compute and network traffic from collapsing onto a few popular experts or devices. Under this configuration (60 transformer layers, hidden dimension 5,120, 128 attention heads), DeepSeek-V2 totals 236B parameters, of which 21B are activated per token, and supports a 128K context window via a YaRN-based extension from a 4K pretraining length.

// what they found

The results

Pretrained on 8.1T bilingual tokens (roughly 12% more Chinese tokens than English), DeepSeek-V2 turns its architectural savings into concrete numbers against its own dense predecessor, DeepSeek 67B: training cost on their H800 cluster drops from 300.6K to 172.8K GPU-hours per trillion tokens — a 42.5% reduction — the KV cache per token falls by 93.3%, and once deployed with FP8 weights and 6-bit KV-cache quantization on a single node of 8 H800 GPUs, maximum generation throughput rises past 50,000 tokens/second — 5.76× DeepSeek 67B's ceiling — with prompt-processing throughput above 100,000 tokens/second.

Despite activating only 21B of its 236B parameters, DeepSeek-V2's base model beats DeepSeek 67B on nearly every benchmark and is competitive with dense 70B-class open models trained on substantially more data:

The pattern holds across the comparisons the paper draws one-by-one: against Mixtral 8x22B (which activates nearly twice as many parameters, 39B), DeepSeek-V2 is ahead on MMLU and Chinese tasks and comparable on code/math; against LLaMA 3 70B, trained on "fewer than a quarter" of its English tokens, DeepSeek-V2 still lands within a couple of points on code and math while pulling well ahead on Chinese benchmarks. The 128K context extension (via YaRN, target length 160K) holds up cleanly on "Needle In A Haystack" retrieval tests across the full window. After Supervised Fine-Tuning (1.5M instruction examples: 1.2M helpfulness + 0.3M safety) and a two-stage GRPO reinforcement-learning pass — first a reasoning-specific reward model on code/math, then a multi-reward helpfulness + safety + rule-based stage — DeepSeek-V2 Chat (RL) reaches 38.9 length-controlled win rate on AlpacaEval 2.0, 8.97 on MT-Bench, and 7.91 on the Chinese AlignBench leaderboard, ranking behind only GPT-4-1106-Preview (8.01) among all models scored and ahead of GPT-4-0613 (7.53) and ERNIEBot-4.0 (7.89).

// does it hold up?

The eval read

The architecture claims are the most independently verifiable part of this paper: MLA's cache-size arithmetic is a closed-form consequence of the projection dimensions the authors chose, and it doesn't depend on DeepSeek's own evaluation harness to be true. But several of the paper's other claims deserve a more skeptical read.

Every open-source competitor in Table 2 and Table 3 (Qwen1.5 72B, Mixtral 8x22B, LLaMA3 70B) is scored through DeepSeek's own "internal evaluation framework," not each lab's self-reported numbers — a defensible choice for holding shot-count and prompt format constant across models, but it also means every number a reader compares DeepSeek-V2 against was produced by the paper's own authors, with no third-party reproduction reported. The authors themselves flag one consequence of this: for TriviaQA and NaturalQuestions, "chat models, such as LLaMA3 70B Instruct, might not strictly adhere to the format constraints typically specified in the few-shot setting," which can underestimate a competitor purely on formatting grounds — an honest disclosure, but also a reminder that at least two of the paper's own head-to-head numbers may not reflect those models' true capability.

The headline efficiency numbers — 42.5% cheaper training, 93.3% smaller KV cache, 5.76× throughput — are all measured against one baseline: DeepSeek 67B, a dense model from the same team on the same tokenizer and the same H800 training stack. That is a clean, confound-free architecture comparison, but it is not a comparison against the field: the paper never reports DeepSeek-V2's KV-cache-per-token or GPU-hours-per-token next to Mixtral 8x22B's own MoE or LLaMA 3 70B's GQA, so "far cheaper and faster than a dense model" is demonstrated, while "far cheaper and faster than other efficient contemporaries" is only implied.

There's also a confound baked into the quality comparison specifically: DeepSeek-V2 changes architecture (MLA + DeepSeekMoE), data (8.1T tokens vs. DeepSeek 67B's smaller, less Chinese-heavy corpus), and scale (236B/21B vs. 67B dense) all at once relative to its predecessor. The 42.5%/93.3%/5.76× deltas are legitimate architecture-only comparisons because they're measured at matched training configuration, but the benchmark-score uplift over DeepSeek 67B is not architecture-isolated — some unknown share of it comes from simply training on far more, better-filtered data. The one architecture-only ablation the paper does run (MHA vs. GQA vs. MQA vs. MLA, Appendix D) is on a smaller proxy setup, not at full 236B scale with data held fixed. Finally, the 8.1T-token corpus's contamination controls are described only as generic "quality-based filtering" — there's no reported n-gram-overlap or decontamination number against the benchmark suite the paper evaluates on, which matters more, not less, given that one of the very benchmarks it uses (LiveCodeBench) exists specifically because contamination is a known live problem for models of exactly this era.

baselines

Every open competitor is scored through DeepSeek's own internal harness, not third-party numbers — consistent shots/format, but no independent reproduction, and the authors themselves flag a format-sensitivity caveat for TriviaQA/NaturalQuestions that can underestimate chat-model competitors.

contamination

No decontamination methodology or overlap number is reported for the 8.1T-token corpus beyond generic "quality-based filtering" — notable given the paper's own inclusion of LiveCodeBench, a benchmark built specifically to route around contamination.

what's missed

Efficiency deltas (42.5%/93.3%/5.76×) are only ever measured against dense DeepSeek 67B, never against other efficient contemporaries (Mixtral's MoE, LLaMA 3's GQA); the quality uplift over 67B entangles the new architecture with a much larger, cleaner training corpus.

would raise confidence

A same-data, full-scale ablation isolating MLA+DeepSeekMoE's quality effect from the data/scale change; a disclosed contamination audit; and head-to-head cache-size/training-cost numbers against Mixtral 8x22B and LLaMA 3 70B rather than only DeepSeek 67B.

// bottom line

MLA's cache-compression math and DeepSeekMoE's expert-specialization argument both hold up on their own terms — solidly enough that DeepSeek-V3 (671B/37B activated) and DeepSeek-R1 later inherited MLA wholesale rather than revisiting the design. But the paper's most quotable numbers are DeepSeek-V2-vs-its-own-predecessor deltas under a simultaneous data and scale change, not DeepSeek-V2-vs-the-field deltas — treat "MLA + DeepSeekMoE alone" and "MLA + DeepSeekMoE + a 4×-larger, cleaner corpus" as two different claims this single paper bundles into one set of headline percentages.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. Multi-head Latent Attention caches a single low-rank latent per token instead of per-head keys and values, cutting DeepSeek-V2's KV cache 93.3% versus dense DeepSeek 67B — equivalent to GQA with only ~2.25 groups — while the paper's own ablations show it beating full MHA on quality rather than merely tying it.
  2. DeepSeekMoE's fine-grained routing (160 routed experts + 2 always-on shared experts, 6 activated per token) lets 21B active parameters out of 236B total match or beat dense 70B-class models on most English, code, math, and Chinese benchmarks — the same recipe DeepSeek-V3 later scaled to 671B total / 37B activated.
  3. The reported 42.5% training-cost cut, 93.3% KV-cache cut, and 5.76× throughput gain are all measured against DeepSeek-V2's own dense predecessor on identical infrastructure — a clean architecture-only comparison, but not evidence of an advantage over other efficient contemporaries, which the paper never benchmarks head-to-head on cost or cache size.
