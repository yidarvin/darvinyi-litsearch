ChatEval: Towards Better LLM-based Evaluators through Multi-Agent Debate

Single-LLM judges are the default for automatic evaluation, but human evaluation itself relies on panels of annotators discussing a decision, not one person's snap judgment. ChatEval builds a multi-agent "referee team" — each agent a distinct persona (Critic, Psychologist, Scientist, ...) — that debates which of two responses is better before voting, and shows the debate itself, not just more compute, is what improves alignment with human judgment.

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

By mid-2023, "LLM-as-a-judge" — prompting a single strong model like GPT-4 to score or compare responses — had become the default replacement for expensive human evaluation (MT-Bench, G-Eval, FairEval). It works better than n-gram metrics like ROUGE and BLEU, which the authors note "have been shown to exhibit a relatively weak correlation with human judgments," but a single LLM call is still just one perspective, and the paper's framing is explicit: "relying on a single perspective can introduce bias and instability in the results," which is exactly why real human evaluation "best practices often involve multiple human annotators collaborating in the evaluation." Meanwhile, separate work on multi-agent LLM debate (Du et al.'s factuality/reasoning debate, Liang et al.'s divergent-thinking debate) had shown that multiple LLMs cross-checking each other improves task performance and mitigates the "Degeneration-of-Thought" problem where a single model gets stuck defending its first answer — but nobody had yet applied that debate mechanism specifically to the evaluation problem itself.

The authors' guiding metaphor, stated directly in the paper: "There are a thousand Hamlets in a thousand people's eyes" — every evaluator brings a different lens, and a single LLM judge collapses that diversity into one opinion. The gap ChatEval targets is narrow and concrete: does giving LLM evaluators distinct personas and letting them discuss before deciding — mirroring how a human evaluation panel actually works — produce judgments that track human preference better than one LLM judging alone?

// how it works

The method

ChatEval has three components. Debater agents: each participating LLM is treated as an independent agent; every other agent's response becomes chat history injected into its prompt template, and the whole debate runs with no human intervention. Diverse role specification: rather than giving every debater the same generic instruction, each agent gets a distinct persona prompt — the paper's five examples are General Public ("you are interested in the story and looking for updates on the investigation"), Critic ("you will check fluent writing, clear sentences, and good wording... question others judgment to make sure their judgment is well-considered"), News Author, Psychologist, and Scientist — inspired by the role-prompting approach in Wu et al. (2023). Communication strategy: how the shared chat history is maintained and passed between agents. The paper defines and compares three strategies (Figure 2): One-by-One, where debaters take turns in a fixed order each round and each new turn sees everything said so far; Simultaneous-Talk, where all agents respond in parallel each round (nullifying speaking-order effects) and their responses are broadcast together; and Simultaneous-Talk-with-Summarizer, which adds a dedicated LLM that condenses each round's messages before broadcasting the summary to every debater.

Unlike Du et al. (2023), ChatEval does not force debaters toward an explicit consensus. When the task is a pairwise comparison, the final verdict is a majority vote across the debaters' individual judgments; when the task calls for a direct score, the final result is the average of the debaters' scores. The default experimental configuration throughout the paper is the one-by-one strategy, 2 debater agents, and 2 discussion turns, with position calibration applied to both the single-agent and multi-agent conditions to control for order bias. All debate groups in the paper are homogeneous — either every agent is GPT-4 or every agent is ChatGPT (GPT-3.5-turbo), both run at temperature 0 for reproducibility; mixing strong and weak models in one debate is explicitly left to future work. ChatEval is evaluated on two benchmarks: FairEval, 80 open-ended questions (Chiang et al. 2023) each with two candidate responses (Vicuna-13B vs. ChatGPT) and human majority-vote preference labels from three annotators (via Wu et al.'s annotations); and Topical-Chat dialogue response generation, using Mehri & Eskenazi's (2020) human ratings across 60 dialogue contexts and 6 response systems on four dimensions — naturalness, coherence, engagingness, groundedness.

// what they found

The results

On FairEval, human inter-annotator accuracy against the majority label averages 71.7% (individual annotators score 68.8%, 76.3%, and 70% with Kappa 0.5, 0.62, and 0.5 respectively) — the paper's own reminder that even human evaluation disagrees with itself a fair amount. Against that backdrop: FairEval's own best method (MEC+BPC) reaches 58.7% accuracy / 0.31 Kappa with ChatGPT and 62.5% / 0.37 with GPT-4. ChatEval's single-agent baseline scores 53.8% / 0.27 (ChatGPT) and 61.3% / 0.36 (GPT-4); moving to multi-agent debate lifts these to 60.0% / 0.33 for ChatGPT (+6.2 accuracy points over single-agent) and 63.8% / 0.40 for GPT-4 (+2.5 points) — surpassing FairEval's best reported numbers for both backbones.

On Topical-Chat, evaluated as turn-level Spearman (ρ) and Kendall-Tau (τ) correlation with human ratings averaged across the four dialogue-quality dimensions: G-Eval-4 (the prior best LLM-based metric) reaches ρ=0.588, τ=0.575. ChatEval's GPT-4 multi-agent setup reaches ρ=0.684, τ=0.632 — the authors report this as improving average Spearman by 0.096 (16.3%) and Kendall-Tau by 0.057 (10.0%) over G-Eval. Multi-agent also beats single-agent for the GPT-4 backbone (single-agent: ρ=0.658, τ=0.611); for the ChatGPT backbone the multi-agent gain is smaller and mixed across dimensions — engagingness rises from 0.599 to 0.664 while naturalness actually drops slightly, from 0.474 to 0.441.

Three ablations isolate what's actually driving the improvement. Diverse roles are load-bearing: replacing every agent's persona with the same generic "you are now an Annotator" prompt (still multi-agent, still 2 agents / 2 turns / one-by-one) scores 53.8% accuracy / 0.25 Kappa — statistically indistinguishable from the 53.8% / 0.27 single-agent baseline, i.e. debate with identical personas buys nothing. Communication strategy matters: with 3 agents / 2 turns and diverse roles, one-by-one reaches 60% / 0.33, versus 55% / 0.28 for simultaneous-talk and 55% / 0.27 for simultaneous-talk-with-summarizer — all three still beat the single-agent baseline, but one-by-one is clearly best for this ChatGPT setup. More agents and more turns are not free (Figure 3): accuracy climbs with role count up to 3–4 roles (peaking at 62.5%) then drops to 58.8% at 5 roles, while Kappa keeps rising through 4 roles before falling; discussion turns show no consistent upward trend at all, peaking around turn 2 and degrading thereafter — which the authors attribute to growing context length diluting the discussion, consistent with similar findings in Liang et al. and Du et al.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

The mechanism claim is well isolated: the same-role-prompt ablation is the paper's strongest piece of evidence, cleanly separating "debate helps" from "diverse personas specifically help," and it shows the latter is doing essentially all the work (53.8%→53.8% with identical roles vs. 53.8%→60.0% with diverse roles). That's a real, controlled result. Several other parts of the evidence base are thinner than the headline percentages suggest, though.

The evaluation sets are small. FairEval is 80 questions with exactly one response pair each (Vicuna-13B vs. ChatGPT only), and Topical-Chat is 60 dialogue contexts × 6 systems. No confidence intervals or significance tests are reported anywhere in the paper for the accuracy/Kappa/correlation deltas — so it's not possible from the paper alone to know whether, say, GPT-4's +2.5-accuracy-point gain (61.3%→63.8% on n=80) would survive a different random sample of questions. The FairEval comparison is also cross-paper: FairEval's 58.7%/62.5% numbers are "the best results from their paper," not a live re-run under ChatEval's exact prompting and sampling setup, so the head-to-head, while informative, isn't a fully controlled replication.

Every debate group tested is homogeneous — all-GPT-4 or all-ChatGPT — so the practically interesting question (does debate help most when mixing a strong and a weak model, or when combining different model families entirely) is explicitly left untested; the paper flags this itself as future work. The communication-strategy ranking (one-by-one beats the other two) is established on a single backbone (ChatGPT) and a single benchmark (FairEval) — it's not verified to hold for GPT-4 or for Topical-Chat. And the discussion-turn ablation shows real performance degradation with more rounds, but the paper only observes this pattern; it doesn't diagnose whether the cause is context dilution, sycophantic convergence toward an early speaker's opinion, or something else — Alice's "Stance Maintenance" behavior described in the qualitative case study (Table 5) is presented as a virtue, but an agent anchoring on its first stance is also a textbook mechanism for exactly this kind of debate-quality decay.

Finally, cost is never discussed: multi-agent debate multiplies inference calls by roughly (number of agents × number of turns) relative to single-agent judging, yet the paper reports zero cost/latency accounting alongside the accuracy gains — a reader can't tell from this paper alone whether a 2.5–6.2 point accuracy improvement is worth 4× (2 agents × 2 turns) or more the judging cost.

baselines

Single-agent baseline uses the identical prompt template with chat-history/role slots stripped, and position calibration is applied to both conditions — a fair, apples-to-apples comparison for the core debate-vs-no-debate claim.

contamination

Not directly applicable (this is an evaluator methodology, not a trained model or a released benchmark), but the responses being judged are real ChatGPT/Vicuna outputs scored by same-family GPT-4/ChatGPT judges — a self-enhancement-bias risk (a GPT-family judge favoring GPT-family outputs) that the paper never separately measures or controls for.

what's missed

No significance testing on small-n deltas (80 and 60-context test sets), no heterogeneous-model debate teams, and no inference-cost accounting against the accuracy gains reported.

would raise confidence

Report confidence intervals on the accuracy/correlation deltas; test whether the one-by-one strategy's advantage generalizes across backbones and tasks; run mixed-capability debate teams; and report the compute multiplier alongside each accuracy gain.

// bottom line

ChatEval's central mechanism — diverse personas plus structured debate beats a single LLM judge — is convincingly isolated by the same-role-prompt ablation and holds across both benchmarks tested. The magnitude of the improvement (2.5–6.2 accuracy points, +16.3% Spearman correlation) is measured on small test sets without significance testing, on homogeneous debate teams only, and with no accounting for the multiplied inference cost — a genuine proof of concept for multi-agent LLM judging, with the generalization and cost questions still open.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. Diverse personas are load-bearing, not decorative: giving debaters distinct roles (Critic, Psychologist, Scientist, ...) rather than a shared generic "Annotator" prompt is what makes debate work — same-role multi-agent debate (53.8% / 0.25) performs no better than a single agent (53.8% / 0.27).
  2. Multi-agent debate beat single-agent LLM judging on both benchmarks tested: +6.2 accuracy points (ChatGPT) and +2.5 points (GPT-4) on FairEval; +16.3% average Spearman correlation over G-Eval on Topical-Chat with the GPT-4 backbone.
  3. More debate is not strictly better: accuracy peaks around 3–4 personas and 2 discussion turns, then degrades — a context-length/stagnation effect the paper observes but doesn't fully diagnose, not a free lunch from adding more agents or rounds.
