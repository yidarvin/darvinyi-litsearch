ContractEval: Benchmarking LLMs for Clause-Level Legal Risk Identification in Commercial Contracts

Can a model you run on your own GPU read a contract as well as GPT-4.1? ContractEval is the first benchmark to test 19 proprietary and open-source LLMs on CUAD's clause-level risk-extraction task — scoring not just correctness but conciseness and "laziness."

What problem is this solving?

When one company buys another, the buyer's lawyers comb the seller's contracts for hidden liabilities — who owns the IP, when an agreement auto-renews, whether liability is capped. The grunt work falls to junior legal assistants who manually pull out the relevant clauses, a process the paper notes can run into the hundreds of thousands of dollars for a single deal, often just to confirm there is no deal-breaker. It is the obvious place to point an LLM.

But contract review is a different shape of problem from the legal NLP that has been studied most. Case retrieval, judgment prediction, and legal text generation are document-level or generative tasks; contract review is span-level extraction — the model has to point at the exact sentence(s) inside a document that can be over 300,000 characters long. The two datasets closest to this task, CUAD and ContractNLI, were built around 2021 and mostly benchmarked earlier models like BERT. No one had a current, systematic read on how 2025-era LLMs handle it.

There is a second, practical wrinkle that makes the question sharper. Law firms are bound by strict client-confidentiality rules, so many are wary of shipping sensitive contracts to a proprietary API. That pushes the field toward locally-deployed, open-source models that keep the data in-house. So the real question ContractEval asks is not just "are LLMs good at this?" but: can an open model you run yourself match a proprietary one on clause-level legal risk — without the data ever leaving the building?

// how it works

The method

ContractEval is built on the test split of CUAD (the Contract Understanding Atticus Dataset): 4,128 (contract, question) pairs spanning 41 clause categories drawn from 102 unique contracts, with context lengths ranging from 0.6k to 301k characters. The labels were created by law students with 70–100 hours of training under lawyer supervision. Critically, the data is heavily imbalanced — about 30% positive / 70% negative — because for most (contract, question) pairs there simply is no matching clause.

The task framing is deliberately simple, mirroring what a junior assistant does: the model is handed the full contract as context plus a targeted question ("On what date is the contract effective?") and must return only the sentence(s) that answer it — or the string "no related clause" if none exists. Its output is compared against CUAD's human-highlighted span.

The model fleet

They evaluate 19 models: 4 proprietary (GPT-4.1, GPT-4.1 mini, Gemini 2.5 Pro Preview, Claude Sonnet 4) and 15 open-source across the DeepSeek, Llama, Gemma, and Qwen3 families — including Qwen3 at 4B/8B/14B with "thinking" mode toggled on and off, plus FP8- and AWQ-quantized Qwen3 8B variants. A model had to support a ≥128k-token context window to even qualify (the longest contract exceeds 300k characters, which is why a legal-specialized model like SaulLM-7B, capped at 32k, was excluded). To anchor the economics: the paper estimates running GPT-4.1 over the CUAD test set costs roughly $50 at $2 / $8 per million input / output tokens — whereas a local open model has no per-token fee.

Three lenses, not one

The signature design choice is that ContractEval refuses to collapse "good at contract review" into a single number. It scores three different things a senior lawyer actually cares about:

1) Correctness via F1 and F2. A true positive requires the prediction to fully cover the labeled span; a true negative is correctly saying "no related clause" when the label is empty. F2 is reported alongside F1 precisely because of the 30/70 imbalance — it weights recall four times as heavily as precision, reflecting that missing a real risk clause is worse than flagging a spurious one.

F2 = 5 · Precision · Recall4 · Precision + Recall // recall-weighted F-score — missing a risk clause costs more than a false flag

2) Output effectiveness / conciseness via the Jaccard similarity between the predicted and ground-truth token sets, computed on positive cases. This catches a model that finds the right clause but buries it in surrounding text — a real cost when senior-lawyer review time runs $2,000–$3,000 an hour.

J(A, B) = |A ∩ B||A ∪ B| // token overlap of predicted span A and ground-truth span B

3) "Laziness" via the false "no related clause" rate — the fraction of cases where the model bails with "no related clause" even though a real clause exists. It is a subset of the false negatives, isolated to flag models that are overly conservative or low-confidence rather than wrong-but-trying.

// what they found

The results

On correctness, the proprietary models lead. GPT-4.1 mini tops the benchmark at F1 0.644 / F2 0.678, essentially tied with GPT-4.1 (0.641 / 0.672); Claude Sonnet 4 (F1 0.523) and Gemini 2.5 Pro (F1 0.497) trail them. The best open model is Qwen3 8B in thinking mode at F1 0.540 / F2 0.512 — competitive, but still about 16% below GPT-4.1. At the bottom, DeepSeek R1 Distill Qwen 7B (F1 0.071) and Qwen3 4B in thinking mode (F1 0.075) score under 0.1, a near-total failure on the task.

But correctness isn't the whole picture, and that is the point of the three-lens design. On Jaccard conciseness, Gemini 2.5 Pro is actually highest (0.506), and the open-source Gemma 3 12B reaches 0.446 — edging out GPT-4.1 mini (0.435) and nearly matching GPT-4.1 (0.472). The Qwen3 8B variants that did well on F1 drop to ≤0.391 on Jaccard: they identify the right clause but pad it with surrounding text.

The "laziness" axis

The false "no related clause" rate spreads the field out in a way F1 doesn't. Gemma 3 4B never abstains (0.000); the proprietary models stay low (Gemini 2.5 Pro 0.011, Claude Sonnet 4 0.025, both GPT-4.1 variants ~0.07). But several open-source models in non-thinking mode are alarmingly conservative: Qwen3 8B AWQ tops the field at 30.6%, Qwen3 8B FP8 sits at 28.5%, and even Llama 3.1 8B Instruct misses on over one in five queries (21.4%) — meaning a real risk clause is sitting right there and the model says it found nothing.

Bigger isn't always better; thinking isn't always better

Two counterintuitive findings land squarely on practitioners. First, scaling has diminishing — even negative — returns on correctness: within the Qwen3 family the 8B model (F1 0.530 non-thinking, 0.540 thinking) beats both the smaller 4B and the larger 14B. Output effectiveness (Jaccard) keeps improving with size, but raw correctness plateaus.

Second, "thinking" mode trades correctness for conciseness. Across Qwen3 4B/8B/14B, enabling reasoning raises Jaccard but lowers F1 — and the swing can be enormous: Qwen3 4B falls from F1 0.411 (non-thinking) to 0.075 (thinking). The authors' read is that step-by-step reasoning leads the model to over-explain or pull in irrelevant clauses on what is fundamentally a precise span-extraction task. Quantization tells a similar efficiency-vs-accuracy story: FP8 and AWQ cut GPU cost but shave accuracy, with the degradation more pronounced once thinking is switched on.

The rare-clause cliff

Comparing GPT-4.1 mini against the strongest open model (Gemma 3 12B) across all 41 categories exposes the most operationally important failure. On common clauses — Governing Law, Parties — GPT-4.1 mini approaches or exceeds F1 0.9. But on rare, nuanced, high-risk categories — Uncapped Liability, Joint IP Ownership, Notice Period to Terminate Renewal — both models fall to near-zero F1. These are exactly the clauses whose omission could blow up a deal, and they are where today's LLMs are weakest.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

The model panel is genuinely current — testing GPT-4.1, Claude Sonnet 4, and Gemini 2.5 Pro means the proprietary frontier is fairly represented as of mid-2025. The three-lens scoring is the paper's best idea: separating correctness from conciseness from abstention surfaces failure modes a single F1 would hide, and the per-category breakdown is the kind of fine-grained read this domain needs. The honesty about open models' weaknesses is welcome. But several design choices mean the absolute numbers should be read as a floor, and the headline framing needs a caveat.

The most important one: the open-source fleet is capped at 14B parameters. There is no Llama 3.1 70B, no Qwen3 32B/235B, no full DeepSeek-V3/R1 — the actual frontier of open weights sits an order of magnitude above anything tested. That cap is a defensible deployment constraint (these are the models a small firm can run on one local GPU), but it means the conclusion "proprietary outperforms open-source" partly restates "large outperforms small." The cleaner claim the data supports is frontier-API beats single-GPU-local — not that open weights as a class can't do contract review.

Second, the "fully covers the span" rule for a true positive is binary and strict. A prediction that nails the right clause but clips a few boundary tokens is scored as a complete miss, with no partial credit. This depresses every F1 in the table — even GPT-4.1's 0.64 understates "did it find the clause." Note the rule only penalizes under-coverage: padding extra text around a correct span still "fully covers" it, so over-inclusion is charged to Jaccard, not F1 — which is exactly why the verbose Qwen3 8B variants keep decent F1 but low Jaccard. It is clipping, not verbosity, that costs a true positive. That reframes the dramatic thinking-mode collapse (Qwen3 4B 0.411 → 0.075): part of it could be a measurement artifact if reasoning-mode outputs emit the chain-of-thought instead of the verbatim clause, so the labeled span is no longer reproduced — a possibility the paper neither tests nor rules out, and distinct from a genuine reasoning deficit. Without a parser-robustness check or a span-overlap metric reported next to the binary one, some of "thinking hurts correctness" is hard to separate from "thinking breaks our string match."

Third, and unaddressed in the paper: contamination. CUAD has been public since 2021, sourced from the SEC's EDGAR system and hosted on the open web; every model here was pretrained on web text through 2024–2025. The contracts, and plausibly some annotations, may sit in pretraining data, so part of the proprietary lead could be memorization of EDGAR filings rather than clause-extraction skill. For any 2025 benchmark built on a four-year-old public dataset this is a live confound, and there is no probe for it. Finally, results appear to be single-prompt, single-run with no variance or prompt-sensitivity sweep — even though the authors themselves flag high prompt sensitivity as an open problem. (One caution on interpretation: Gemma 3 4B's 0.000 false rate is not unambiguously "good" — a model that never abstains avoids abstention-errors by construction, which is also why its F1 is just 0.188 on the 70%-negative split.)

baselines

Proprietary frontier (GPT-4.1, Claude Sonnet 4, Gemini 2.5 Pro) is current and fair. But the open side is capped at ≤14B — no 70B+ open model — so "open lags proprietary" partly means "small lags large."

contamination

Not addressed. CUAD is public since 2021 (EDGAR-sourced, on the open web); every 2025 model plausibly saw it in pretraining, so part of the proprietary lead could be memorization rather than skill.

what's missed

The binary "fully covers the span" TP rule gives no partial credit — any clipped span is charged as a full miss, depressing all F1 scores (over-inclusion, by contrast, is penalized only on Jaccard). Combined with single-run, single-prompt evaluation — despite the authors flagging prompt sensitivity — that makes the dramatic thinking-mode F1 collapse hard to read.

would raise confidence

Contamination probes (perturbed/held-out contracts), 70B+ open models to decouple open-vs-closed from small-vs-large, multi-run variance and a prompt sweep, a partial-credit span-overlap metric, and lawyer inter-annotator agreement to set the F1 ceiling.

// bottom line

A useful, honest first map of where mid-2025 LLMs stand on real contract-review extraction: top proprietary models reach roughly junior-legal-assistant quality (F1 ~0.64) and the best single-GPU open model trails by ~16%. But it's a snapshot of a small open-model fleet against a four-year-old public dataset, scored with a strict exact-span rule and no contamination or variance controls — so read the absolute scores as a floor and the gap as "frontier-API vs local-small," not "open weights can't do this."

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. The first head-to-head of modern proprietary vs open LLMs on contract review. 19 models on CUAD's clause-extraction task (41 risk categories, 4,128 contract–question pairs). Top proprietary models reach junior-assistant level (GPT-4.1 mini F1 0.644); the best local open model (Qwen3 8B thinking, F1 0.540) trails by ~16%.
  2. Correctness alone hides the real differences. Open models are often verbose (lower Jaccard despite decent F1), some are "lazy" (Qwen3 8B AWQ misses a real clause >30% of the time by answering "no related clause"), and everyone collapses on rare high-risk clauses — Uncapped Liability and Joint IP Ownership are near-zero F1 for both GPT-4.1 mini and Gemma 3 12B.
  3. Two counterintuitive levers for deployment. Bigger isn't always better — Qwen3 8B beats 14B on F1 — and "thinking" mode trades correctness for conciseness (Qwen3 4B F1 0.411 → 0.075), so reasoning can hurt on precise span extraction. Quantization saves GPU at a small accuracy cost that worsens with thinking.
