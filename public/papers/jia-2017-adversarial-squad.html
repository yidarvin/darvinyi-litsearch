Adversarial Examples for Evaluating Reading Comprehension Systems

One human-approved distractor sentence, appended to a SQuAD paragraph without changing the correct answer, collapses sixteen published readers from 75.4% to 36.4% F1 — while three crowd workers barely notice it’s there.

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

By mid-2017, SQuAD looked like a benchmark the field was rapidly closing out: the best published system reached 84.7% F1 against a human ceiling of 91.2%, and the leaderboard read like a countdown to human parity. Jia and Liang's objection is that average-case test-set accuracy cannot tell a model that has learned to read from a model that has learned to pattern-match. Weissenborn et al. (2017), working concurrently, had already argued that many SQuAD questions are answerable from type- and keyword-matching heuristics alone — find the sentence that shares the most vocabulary with the question, then return the nearest span of the right type. A model can score well on held-out data by exploiting exactly that shortcut, without any of the compositional understanding the benchmark is supposed to certify, and standard accuracy has no way to detect it, because the test distribution never asks the model to prove otherwise.

The paper's opening example makes the failure concrete. A strong 2017 system, BiDAF Ensemble, correctly answers "What is the name of the quarterback who was 38 in Super Bowl XXXIII?" with "John Elway" — the right answer, drawn from a paragraph about Super Bowl 50. Append one sentence to the end of that paragraph: "Quarterback Jeff Dean had jersey number 37 in Champ Bowl XXXIV." Nothing in that sentence contradicts the correct answer, and no human reader would be misled by it — "Champ Bowl XXXIV" is transparently not "Super Bowl XXXIII." Yet the same model now answers "Jeff Dean." The sentence works because it echoes the question's surface vocabulary (quarterback, jersey number, a fabricated "Champ Bowl" that rhymes with "Super Bowl"), not because it has any bearing on the actual question. If a benchmark's headline number can be moved this far by an addition a human would shrug off, that number was never measuring language understanding in the first place — it was measuring something closer to lexical overlap detection, and the paper sets out to build an evaluation scheme that specifically punishes that shortcut.

// how it works

The method

The paper formalizes adversarial evaluation as a drop-in generalization of standard test accuracy. Instead of scoring a model on the original example (p, q, a), score it on an adversary-perturbed example A(p, q, a, f) that an adversary A is allowed to construct (optionally with black-box query access to the model f). For the resulting number to mean anything, A is required to satisfy two constraints: the perturbed example must remain valid (a human would still judge the new answer correct given the new paragraph and question), and it must stay reasonably close to the original.

Adv(f)  =  1|Dtest|  Σ(p,q,a) ∈ Dtest  v( A(p, q, a, f), f ) // adversarial accuracy: same F1 scorer v as standard SQuAD evaluation, applied to adversary-perturbed examples instead of the raw test set

Because vision-style imperceptible perturbations have no clean text analogue — changing even one word can flip a sentence's meaning — the paper builds concatenative adversaries instead: append one new sentence s to the end of the paragraph, leaving the question and the gold answer untouched, subject only to the constraint that s not contradict the correct answer. This targets a different failure mode than image adversarial examples. Vision attacks exploit model oversensitivity to imperceptible noise; these attacks exploit model overstability — the inability to distinguish a sentence that actually answers the question from one that merely shares its vocabulary.

Two concrete attacks instantiate this idea. ADDSENT, the main method, is a four-step pipeline: (1) mutate the question by swapping nouns/adjectives for WordNet antonyms and swapping named entities/numbers for their nearest GloVe neighbor of the same part of speech; (2) generate a fake answer matched to the original answer's type, from a hand-built set of 26 NER/POS categories; (3) convert the mutated question plus fake answer into a declarative sentence, using roughly 50 hand-written rules over CoreNLP constituency parses; (4) send the raw sentence to five Mechanical Turk workers to fix grammar, then three more workers to filter out anything still ungrammatical or incompatible with the answer. The full attack then queries the target model as a black box on every human-approved candidate sentence and keeps whichever one hurts it most. ADDANY drops the grammaticality requirement entirely: it runs a local coordinate search — 6 epochs, greedily updating one word at a time from a candidate pool of common words plus question vocabulary — over sequences of d = 10 words, using the model's predicted answer distribution as the search signal. The result need not be a real sentence at all; the paper's own example converges to the gibberish "tesla move move other george." Two lighter variants isolate specific mechanisms: ADDONESENT attaches a single, already-human-approved sentence with zero access to the target model, testing whether ADDSENT's power actually depends on querying the model; ADDCOMMON restricts ADDANY's vocabulary to the 1,000 most frequent Brown-corpus words, testing whether rare or unnatural tokens are doing the work.

// what they found

The results

The headline number: across sixteen published, independently-trained SQuAD models — spanning BiDAF, Match-LSTM, ReasoNet, Mnemonic Reader, SEDT, jNet, Ruminating Reader, MPCM, RaSOR, DCR, and a logistic-regression baseline, in both single and ensemble form — ADDSENT drops average F1 from 75.4% to 36.4%. Twelve of those sixteen models were never touched during method development; they were run only afterward, as a held-out generality check, and none were robust. Mnemonic Reader Ensemble, the strongest model under attack, still falls from 79.1% to 46.2% F1 — about 6 points ahead of the pack, but nowhere near unaffected.

The four models used during development (Match-LSTM and BiDAF, single and ensemble) show how much harder ADDANY is than ADDSENT: ADDSENT alone takes their average F1 from 75.7% to 31.3%, while the fully unconstrained ADDANY drives the same four models down to 6.7% on average — as low as 2.7% for BiDAF Ensemble and 4.8% for BiDAF Single. Even ADDCOMMON, restricted to only the 1,000 most frequent English words, still cuts average F1 to 46.1%, and the fully model-independent ADDONESENT (one fixed sentence, no querying of the target model at all) still drags BiDAF Ensemble from 80.0% down to 46.9%.

Crucially, the identical distractor sentences barely move humans. Three Mechanical Turk workers scored 92.6% F1 on the original examples and 79.5% under ADDSENT — a 13.1-point drop the authors show is mostly ordinary human error rather than the adversarial sentence itself, since ADDSENT keeps the single worst-case sentence out of up to five human-approved candidates, so at least one of five independently-graded groups is likely to slip regardless. Isolating the effect with ADDONESENT — one fixed sentence, no cherry-picking across candidates — humans drop only 3.4 F1 points (92.6% → 89.2%). Manual inspection of 100 BiDAF-Ensemble failures found only 1 ADDSENT sentence that could plausibly be read as answering the question (0 of 100 for ADDANY): the attacks reliably respect their own validity constraint.

The paper also diagnoses why the shortcut exists. In 96.6% of model failures on ADDSENT, the model's predicted span comes from inside the injected sentence, and success tracks surface overlap directly: 41.5% of BiDAF-Ensemble successes share a 4-gram with the original passage, versus only 21.0% of failures; 32.7% of successes involve questions of 8 words or fewer, versus 11.8% of failures.

Finally, retraining looks like a fix at first: BiDAF trained with raw ADDSENT sentences (Steps 1–3 only, no crowdsourcing) mixed into its training data recovers from 34.8% to 70.4% F1 on ADDSENT test examples. But a mild variant, ADDSENTMOD — a different fake-answer vocabulary, and the sentence prepended rather than appended — still crashes the retrained model to 39.2% F1, barely above the untrained model's 34.3%. The model had learned to distrust the last sentence and reject ADDSENT's specific fake-answer style, not to reason about semantic compatibility in general.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

This paper is unusual among Paper Atlas entries in that it doesn't need a critique bolted on after the fact — it is an eval-rigor critique, aimed at SQuAD and, by extension, at average-case test accuracy as a methodology. So the interesting question isn't "was SQuAD flawed" (yes, demonstrably), it's how well this paper's own diagnostic instrument holds up more than eight years and one full architecture generation later, and how much of "reading comprehension failure" it actually characterizes.

The experimental design itself is careful: four models developed against, twelve more run only afterward as a genuine held-out generalization test, plus a matched human control run on the identical adversarial examples — a rare and valuable inclusion that most later "models are fooled by X" papers skip. The human control is what makes the paper's causal claim (models are overstable, not just noisy) credible rather than merely suggestive. But the finding is squarely a product of its era: every tested model is a 2016–2017 discriminative, span-extraction reader with no pretrained contextual representations — a year before ELMo, roughly fifteen months before BERT. The paper cannot and does not claim the shortcut survives contextualized pretraining, let alone the retrieval-augmented and instruction-tuned readers that came after; that generalization question was left for the field to answer (and it largely has, unevenly — see below).

The most quietly important result in the paper is Table 6, and it is closer to a warning than a fix: training against ADDSENT recovers a huge amount of ADDSENT-specific accuracy (34.8% → 70.4%), but a nearly cosmetic variant of the same attack (ADDSENTMOD) collapses the "robustified" model right back down (39.2%). That is direct evidence that adversarial training here bought a narrow, gameable patch — learn to distrust one position, reject one fake-answer style — rather than general robustness to semantics-altering distractors. It is exactly the shape of failure that later contamination- and benchmark-gaming critiques of LLM evaluation would rediscover in a different setting: a fix that works against the fixed attacker used to build it says little about robustness to a different attacker with the same goal.

baselines

Genuinely careful for its time: four models developed against, twelve more run only afterward as a held-out generalization check, all against a matched human control on identical adversarial examples. But every model is a pre-contextual-embedding, discriminative span-extraction reader — the paper predates ELMo and BERT, so it says nothing yet about whether pretrained representations close (or merely relocate) the shortcut.

contamination

Not leakage in the modern sense — this predates that literature — but a structurally similar problem: ADDSENT's own generator (WordNet antonyms, GloVe nearest-neighbors, ~50 hand-written declarative rules) is a fixed, learnable signature. Table 6 shows a model trained against it doesn't generalize to a near-cosmetic variant (ADDSENTMOD), the paper's own evidence that adversarial training here produced a narrow, gameable patch rather than robustness.

what's missed

Whether closing this one shortcut — overstability to a single concatenated distractor sentence — says anything about "true reading comprehension" more broadly, or just about this one attack surface. The paper itself is careful not to overclaim, but the result is routinely shorthanded in citing literature as "SQuAD models don't understand language," which goes further than the evidence licenses. Also untested here, for lack of existence in 2017: contextual-embedding readers, multi-hop and retrieval-augmented QA, and instruction-tuned generative readers.

would raise confidence

Rerunning ADDSENT/ADDANY-style attacks against modern pretrained and instruction-tuned readers to see whether the lexical-overlap shortcut survived pretraining (the field's answer has been "partially" — see SQuAD 2.0's unanswerable questions and HotpotQA's supporting-fact distractors, both direct descendants of this diagnosis); and a defense evaluated against an adaptive attacker rather than only the fixed ADDSENT generator, given Table 6's own negative result on that exact question.

// bottom line

Adversarial SQuAD is a landmark not because it broke one benchmark, but because it demonstrated — with a rigorous, human-verified control — that a leaderboard-topping F1 score can certify lexical pattern-matching as language understanding. That diagnosis reshaped how the field talks about benchmark saturation: SQuAD 2.0's unanswerable questions, HotpotQA's supporting-fact distractors, and the broader adversarial-NLI and dynamic-benchmark-construction movements all inherit its central move — build inputs a human handles trivially but a leaderboard-topping model doesn't. But the paper's own Table 6 carries a caution the field was slower to absorb: training against a fixed adversarial generator buys a narrow, gameable fix, not general robustness — a warning that reads uncannily like the 2020s contamination and benchmark-gaming debates around LLM evaluation, arriving a full architecture generation early.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. Concatenative adversaries, not perturbations. Instead of imperceptible noise (the vision playbook), the paper appends one full sentence to the paragraph — a semantics-altering but answer-preserving edit that targets model overstability (can't tell a real answer sentence from one that merely shares vocabulary), not oversensitivity. ADDSENT generates grammatical, human-vetted distractors via a four-step pipeline; ADDANY drops grammaticality for a raw, model-in-the-loop word search.
  2. The collapse is large and human-verified. Across sixteen published models, ADDSENT cuts average F1 from 75.4% to 36.4%; the unconstrained ADDANY drives the four main models down to 6.7% on average. A matched human control on the identical examples drops only 3.4 F1 points under the model-independent ADDONESENT variant (92.6% → 89.2%) — strong evidence the models, not the examples, are the problem.
  3. Retraining is not a fix, and the paper says so itself. BiDAF trained on raw ADDSENT examples recovers to 70.4% F1 against ADDSENT — but a nearly cosmetic variant (ADDSENTMOD) crashes it right back to 39.2%. The lesson that outlasted the specific attack: robustness to one fixed adversarial generator is not robustness in general, a caution the field would need to relearn repeatedly in the LLM-benchmark era.
