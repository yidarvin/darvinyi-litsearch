Pile of Law: Learning Responsible Data Filtering from the Law and a 256GB Open-Source Legal Dataset

A 256GB open-license corpus of legal and administrative text, paired with a provocative thesis: the centuries of rules courts and legislatures already built for redacting private and offensive content are a better, less ad-hoc guide for filtering LLM pretraining data than the word-lists researchers improvise.

Peter Henderson, Mark S. Krass, Lucia Zheng, Neel Guha, Christopher D. Manning, Dan Jurafsky, Daniel E. Ho · Stanford University

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

Every large language model is trained on a corpus that quietly contains private and toxic content, and what a team does about it is, in the authors' words, "largely a matter of researcher discretion." Some teams run elaborate filtering; others train on data "in virtually unmodified form." The paper's Table 1 lays the inconsistency bare: across CCNet, C4, GPT-3, Gopher, and The Pile, none explicitly filter potentially sensitive information (PSI), and toxicity is handled — if at all — by a crude word-list (C4) or "ad-hoc source deletion" (The Pile).

The reason filtering stays ad hoc is that undesirable content is deeply contextual. Whether a racial epithet is "toxic" depends on who is speaking and to whom; whether a fact is a privacy violation depends on what the speaker expected to be shared at the time. A word-list filter is blind to all of this — so it nukes a civil-rights opinion that quotes a segregationist statute in order to strike it down, while leaving a genteelly-worded but viciously racist holding intact. Filtering too aggressively for privacy, meanwhile, harms downstream applications, just as the Census Bureau's differential-privacy adoption introduced errors into Congressional redistricting.

The gap, then, is that there is no principled, context-aware standard for what to filter. The paper's central move is to argue that one already exists — and has for centuries. Courts and legislatures have spent enormous deliberative effort balancing transparency against privacy and offense, and they have written those trade-offs down. "There is no need to reinvent law."

// how it works

The method

The paper makes three moves. First, it curates the dataset. Pile of Law is ~256GB of open-license English-language legal and administrative text compiled from 35 data sources: court opinions and filings (Court Listener), contracts and SEC EDGAR filings, administrative rulings from bodies like the Board of Immigration Appeals and the Board of Veterans Appeals, statutes, regulations, casebooks, legislative records, and even r/legaladvice. The crucial property is that each subset arrives already filtered by the privacy and toxicity norms of its jurisdiction — so the corpus implicitly encodes those rules, and the redaction decisions made by real courts are sitting right there in the text as labels.

Second, it catalogs the legal norms. The authors survey how U.S., German, Chinese, and Canadian courts and U.S. agencies handle the transparency-versus-privacy trade-off (the paper's Table 2). From this they distill actionable lessons: some rules are bright lines everywhere — virtually every jurisdiction redacts juvenile names, dates of birth, and account/ID numbers — while others are contextual and value-laden (German courts strip all litigant names; U.S. courts lean hardest toward openness). Their prescription: treat the U.S. court standard as a floor, then make contextual decisions above it.

Third — the research contribution that makes the dataset more than a corpus — it shows the contextual filters can be learned from the data rather than hand-coded, via three case studies:

Learning contextual privacy (EOIR pseudonymity)

The Board of Immigration Appeals publishes some decisions with applicants' full names and others with pseudonymous initials. The authors split cases into paragraphs, mask the terms referring to the applicant, and train a distill-BERT model to predict whether the paragraph should use a pseudonym. They then run a perturbation analysis on the Bias-in-Bios dataset: prepend one sentence marking the person as an asylum-seeker/refugee, a torture survivor, a non-violent offender, or a violent offender, and measure how the pseudonymity likelihood shifts. A causal lexicon (fit with Pryzant et al.'s deep-residualization method, controlling for publication year) extracts the terms that causally trigger pseudonymity.

Does pretraining transmit the norm? (the Jane Doe score)

To test whether a model pretrained on Pile of Law internalizes pseudonymity norms, they collect ~500 paragraphs where courts used "Jane Doe"/"Jane Roe," and for each generate 100 variants that swap in a real name sampled from 1990 Census frequencies. Using a masked-LM (pseudo-log-likelihood) score, they ask whether the model finds the pseudonym more natural than a real name — comparing pol-bert (a BERT-large trained from scratch on Pile of Law) against a vanilla bert-large-uncased.

JaneDoe = PLLθ(s | "Jane Doe") − Ename[ PLLθ(s | name) ] // PLL = masked-LM pseudo-log-likelihood of the sentence; positive ⇒ the model prefers the pseudonym over a sampled real name

Do off-the-shelf toxicity filters agree? (Supreme Court text)

They run four standard toxicity classifiers — profanity-check, Perspective, Detoxify, and Toxigen — over Supreme Court opinions, measuring inter-model agreement (Cohen's κ) over time and the share of sentences flagged toxic by issue area. A separate test isolates the effect of the scoring window: take the 5,000 sentences Toxigen rates most toxic, add two sentences of context on each side, and measure how much the toxicity score moves.

// what they found

The results

Toxicity filters barely agree — and disagree about meaning, not just style. On Supreme Court sentences, Cohen's κ between profanity-check and Perspective is low and rises over time as older text moves further from the filters' training distribution. But the disagreement is conceptual, not merely temporal: civil-rights cases from 1950–1970 (the end of de jure segregation) show a large spike in sentences flagged toxic, because those opinions quote the racist laws they are striking down.

The single most damning example is Table 3. Dred Scott v. Sandford (1857) — "the most notoriously racist decision in U.S. history" — scores just 0.29 / 0.50 / 0.26 / 0.54 across the four filters; none is confident it is toxic. Meanwhile Loving v. Virginia (1967), quoting an anti-miscegenation statute precisely to invalidate it, scores 0.52 / 0.54 / 0.60 / 0.94. As the authors put it, document-level filtering on these scores "could thus easily delete core civil rights cases like Hunter and Loving — while leaving in Dred Scott." And the scoring window is a free dial: adding two sentences of context on each side of a toxic sentence drops its score by 55–57% (absolute, 95% CI) — you can launder an obviously toxic span just by widening the frame.

Privacy norms, by contrast, can be learned contextually. The EOIR pseudonymity classifier reaches ~80% F1. The perturbation analysis recovers real law: asylum and torture markers raise the pseudonymity likelihood while criminal-offense markers lower it — matching federal regulation (8 CFR § 208.6 shields asylum and Convention-Against-Torture information but permits disclosure in criminal proceedings). The learned causal lexicon clusters cleanly into asylum, competency, status, and crime. And pretraining transmits the norm: pol-bert ranks "Jane Doe" ~3 points higher than vanilla bert-large-uncased on true pseudonym cases, a gain the authors say persists after adjusting for pol-bert's slightly higher overall Jane-Doe bias.

Out-of-domain sanitization tools fail loudly. Running a HIPAA scrubber on Veterans (BVA) and Labor (DOL) appeals flags nearly everything — 99% of decisions contain "dates," 96% of BVA and 100% of DOL contain medical-facility names — yet the two agencies genuinely differ in a way the blunt tool buries: 26% of DOL cases name a physician versus just 0.36% of BVA cases (physician fraud is more common in workers' compensation).

Finally, the corpus is usable for pretraining. PoL-BERT-Large — trained from scratch on a ~30GB sample — reaches 75.0 F1 on CaseHOLD, beating a vanilla Bert-Large-Uncased (71.3) and approaching the domain-specialized CaseLaw-BERT (75.4, or 78.5 when tuned). The authors note, though, that training on such varied legal data was "surprisingly challenging," requiring a tiny learning rate (5e-6), batch size 128, and even distribution of data sources across shards just to converge.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

This is a dataset-plus-thesis paper, not a benchmark with a leaderboard, so the read is whether the demonstrations actually support the strong normative claim — "look to the law to filter your data" — and whether the corpus is as clean and usable as advertised. The descriptive half is excellent; the prescriptive half is suggestive but under-evaluated.

The filter-disagreement result is genuinely sharp, and the Dred-Scott-passes / Loving-fails inversion is the kind of single example that reframes a field — it is now the canonical demonstration that toxicity classifiers encode contestable value judgments rather than measure a property of text. But the authors are explicit that the learned-filter experiments are "not comprehensive" — proofs of concept, not validated tools. The EOIR classifier's ~80% F1 has no comparison to the word-list/PII-regex filters it is meant to replace, and no human-redaction-quality baseline, so we cannot tell whether 80% is deployable or merely suggestive. The Jane Doe "~3 points" gain rides on an intrinsic MLM-score proxy, not a downstream redaction task, and the authors concede pol-bert is "slightly more biased for Jane Doe use overall"; they assert the gain survives adjustment, but the adjustment is not quantified in the main text.

Contamination runs the opposite direction from a usual benchmark: Pile of Law is a pretraining source, and CaseHOLD is derived from the same Court Listener / Harvard CaseLaw universe that feeds the corpus — so PoL-BERT's 75.0-vs-71.3 edge could partly reflect in-domain familiarity rather than better legal reasoning, and the paper does not audit train/test overlap. The normative leap, finally, rests almost entirely on U.S. law (a stated limitation), which is the most transparency-favoring, least-redacting regime they survey — a thin "floor." And "the data already encodes the norm" cuts both ways: the corpus also inherits the eras when the law itself encoded the bias, exactly as their own civil-rights finding shows.

baselines

The learned filters (EOIR ~80% F1; Jane Doe ~3 pts) are never benchmarked against the ad-hoc word-list / Perspective pipeline they critique, nor against human redaction — so "better than current practice" is argued, not measured. PoL-BERT is compared fairly to BERT-large and CaseLaw-BERT on CaseHOLD.

contamination

No train/test overlap audit between the ~30GB pretraining sample and CaseHOLD, which is drawn from the same case-law universe; the in-domain pretraining edge may be partly familiarity. As a pretraining corpus, Pile of Law is itself a downstream contamination source.

what's missed

The thesis is U.S.-centric (the authors flag it), and U.S. law is the most transparency-favoring regime surveyed. "Implicit filtering" inherits the law's historical failures, and no experiment shows that law-grounded filtering actually changes a trained model's downstream privacy/toxicity behavior — only the filters' own scores.

would raise confidence

A head-to-head of a law-grounded learned filter vs. the standard word-list/Perspective pipeline on a held-out redaction task with human labels; a CaseHOLD contamination audit; multi-jurisdiction replication (German/Canadian norms differ sharply from the U.S. floor); and a downstream-behavior measurement of filtered vs. unfiltered models.

// bottom line

The descriptive contribution is durable: a large open-license legal corpus and the most-cited demonstration that toxicity filters encode value judgments — the Dred-Scott-passes / Loving-fails inversion. The prescriptive thesis ("filter by legal norm") is shown to be learnable, not yet shown to be better: the learned filters are early-stage, U.S.-centric, and unbenchmarked against the ad-hoc methods they aim to replace.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. Toxicity filters aren't neutral measurements — they encode a definition. On Supreme Court text the four standard filters can't agree (low Cohen's κ) and they invert on the cases that matter: Dred Scott scores as non-toxic (0.29/0.50/0.26/0.54) while Loving, quoting the law it overturns, scores high (0.52/0.54/0.60/0.94). Document-level filtering on those scores would delete Loving and keep Dred Scott.
  2. Context length is a free toxicity dial. Widening the scoring window by two sentences on each side drops a span's toxicity score by 55–57% (absolute) — so where you draw sentence boundaries silently changes what gets filtered, independent of the actual content.
  3. Contextual legal norms can be learned from data, not just hand-coded. An EOIR classifier (~80% F1) recovers the real asylum/torture-vs-crime distinction in federal regulation, and pretraining on Pile of Law transmits the Jane-Doe pseudonymity norm (~3 points) — a proof of concept that "filter by legal norm" is at least learnable, even if not yet validated against the ad-hoc filters it aims to replace.
