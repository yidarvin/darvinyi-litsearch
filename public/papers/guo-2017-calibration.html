On Calibration of Modern Neural Networks

A 5-layer LeNet from 1998 is worse at CIFAR-100 than a 110-layer ResNet — 44.9% error versus 30.6%. But ask each network how confident it is, and the LeNet is telling the truth while the ResNet is not. This paper is the first to show that the deep-learning era's accuracy gains came with a calibration cost, names the architectural culprits, and finds that a single learned number — one scalar temperature dividing the logits — fixes most of it.

Chuan Guo, Geoff Pleiss, Yu Sun, Kilian Q. Weinberger

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

A classification network's softmax output is routinely read as a probability — "90% confident" is supposed to mean that, among all predictions made at that confidence, about 90% are correct. The paper calls this property calibration, and it matters wherever a downstream system has to decide how much to trust a prediction: a self-driving car deciding whether to lean on its object detector or fall back to other sensors, a diagnosis network deciding when to defer to a human doctor, or a language model whose output is fused probabilistically with another model. In 2005, Niculescu-Mizil & Caruana had shown that neural networks of that era were, in fact, well calibrated on binary classification tasks — and that result was largely taken for granted ever since, even as architectures changed completely.

The authors show it no longer holds. Between LeNet (1998) and the ResNets, Wide ResNets, and DenseNets of 2016, classification accuracy improved dramatically — but nobody had checked whether the confidence scores that come along for the ride kept pace. They hadn't: modern networks are, by the paper's own headline comparison, substantially more overconfident than the shallow, narrow networks from a decade earlier, despite being far more accurate. Because confidence and accuracy had always moved together before, no widely used training recipe controlled for calibration, and no one had connected which specific ingredients of the modern deep-learning stack — depth, width, Batch Normalization, weight decay — were responsible. That diagnosis, plus a systematic comparison of fixes, is the paper's contribution.

// how it works

The method

Formally, perfect calibration means P(Ŷ = Y | P̂ = p) = p for every confidence level p ∈ [0, 1] — among all predictions the model made at confidence p, exactly a p-fraction should be correct. That's unmeasurable directly from finitely many samples, so the paper's diagnostic tool is the reliability diagram: bucket predictions into M equal-width confidence bins, and for each bin plot its empirical accuracy against its average confidence. A perfectly calibrated model traces the diagonal; any bar that falls short of the diagonal (the "gap") is miscalibration in that confidence range. Figure 1 is the paper's headline illustration of the problem: a 5-layer LeNet (1998) and a 110-layer ResNet (2016), both trained on CIFAR-100. LeNet's confidence histogram is spread out and its reliability diagram tracks the diagonal closely. The ResNet's confidence histogram piles up almost entirely near 1.0 — it is almost always "sure" — while its reliability diagram shows a large, systematic gap: its actual accuracy trails its stated confidence at every bin.

To turn that visual gap into a single number, the paper adopts Expected Calibration Error (ECE), introduced by Naeini et al. (2015): partition predictions into M equally-spaced confidence bins B₁,…,B_M, and take the sample-size-weighted average of the accuracy/confidence gap in each bin.

ECE = Σm=1M |Bm|n |acc(Bm) − conf(Bm)| // acc(Bₘ), conf(Bₘ): empirical accuracy and mean confidence of bin m; n = total samples; the paper uses M = 15

Section 3 then works out why the gap opened up. Holding everything else fixed and varying one factor at a time on ResNet/CIFAR-100 architectures, the authors find that model capacity (depth and width), Batch Normalization, and vanishing weight decay each independently push ECE up even when they hold accuracy flat or improve it. The mechanism they identify: once a network can classify nearly all training examples correctly, it can keep lowering its training negative log-likelihood (NLL) simply by pushing predicted probabilities closer to 0 or 1 — i.e., by getting more confident — without any further gain in 0/1 classification accuracy. They show this directly: training a 110-layer stochastic-depth ResNet on CIFAR-100, test NLL begins overfitting (rising) well before test classification error does, and in the region where NLL is overfitting, test error actually keeps falling, from 29% to 27%. The network is trading well-calibrated probabilities for a small amount of additional accuracy — a distinction invisible to anyone watching only the error curve.

The fix operates entirely post-hoc, on a held-out validation split, and never touches the trained weights or the predicted class. The classic baselines are histogram binning and isotonic regression (both non-parametric, mapping raw confidence into per-bin corrected scores), Bayesian Binning into Quantiles (BBQ, a Bayesian average over binning schemes), and Platt scaling (logistic regression on the logit). The paper's proposed fix is temperature scaling, the simplest possible multiclass generalization of Platt scaling: divide every logit by a single learned scalar T > 0 before the softmax.

q̂i = maxk σSM(zi / T)(k) // z_i: logit vector; T fit by minimizing NLL on the validation set; T > 1 "softens" the distribution toward uniform, T → 0 sharpens it to a point mass, T = 1 recovers the original softmax

Because T is a positive scalar applied identically across classes, it cannot change which class has the largest logit — so temperature scaling leaves the model's predictions, and therefore its accuracy, exactly unchanged. It only reshapes how confident the model is allowed to say it is. The paper also tests two richer multiclass extensions of Platt scaling — vector scaling (a per-class scale and bias) and matrix scaling (a full linear transform of the logits) — as a check on whether more parameters buy better calibration.

// what they found

The results

Table 1 reports ECE (M = 15 bins) across 6 image-classification and 4 document-classification tasks, spanning ResNets, ResNets with stochastic depth, Wide ResNets, DenseNets, LeNet-5, Deep Averaging Networks, and TreeLSTMs — before and after each calibration method. Miscalibration is pervasive and architecture-agnostic: most dataset/model pairs show uncalibrated ECE between roughly 4% and 17% (e.g. 16.53% for ResNet-110 on CIFAR-100, 9.19% for ResNet-50 on Birds), with only SVHN and Reuters — both already low-error datasets — under 1%.

Temperature scaling wins or ties on almost every vision benchmark despite fitting only one parameter. On CIFAR-100, it takes ResNet-110 from 16.53% ECE to 1.26%, and the stochastic-depth variant from 12.67% to 0.96%. On ImageNet, ResNet-152 goes from 5.48% to 1.86% and DenseNet-161 from 6.28% to 1.99%. It also beats the strictly more expressive vector-scaling and matrix-scaling variants of Platt scaling on vision tasks — the fitted vector-scaling solution turns out to have nearly constant entries, i.e. it collapses back into a scalar, which the authors read as evidence that network miscalibration is intrinsically low-dimensional. Matrix scaling, by contrast, overfits badly wherever the class count is large: 21.13% ECE on the 200-class Birds dataset and 25.49% on 100-class CIFAR-100 — both worse than doing nothing — and it fails to converge at all on 1000-class ImageNet, since its parameter count scales quadratically with K. Binning methods (histogram binning, isotonic regression, BBQ) reliably improve calibration but never beat temperature scaling on vision tasks, and the simplest of the three (histogram binning) usually outperforms the two more general ones. On the smaller NLP datasets the ranking is closer — Reuters is the one dataset temperature scaling fails to help, but it was already calibrated below 1% ECE with little room to improve.

Cost and simplicity track the ranking. Fitting T is a one-dimensional convex optimization solved in about 10 conjugate-gradient iterations — a fraction of a second — while vector scaling on CIFAR-100 (K = 100) needs at least two orders of magnitude more compute, histogram binning and isotonic regression an order of magnitude more, and BBQ roughly three orders of magnitude more.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

The core empirical claim — capacity and reduced regularization trade calibration for accuracy, and a single scalar mostly buys it back — is careful and has held up: temperature scaling is still the default calibration baseline in the field nearly a decade later, and it's the exact transform that later work like Calibrate Before Use (zhao-2021-calibrate) builds its own affine calibration on. But ECE, the paper's sole quantitative yardstick, has known blind spots that the paper doesn't stress-test, and the paper's own framing motivating this work — high-stakes deployment under real-world conditions — is precisely the setting its evaluation doesn't cover.

ECE's binning is a hidden knob. ECE bins predictions into M equal-width intervals and reports a weighted average gap; the paper fixes M = 15 for every number in Table 1 without showing the ranking is stable across other choices of M or bin scheme. Subsequent calibration literature (e.g. Nixon et al.'s "Measuring Calibration in Deep Learning," and Kumar et al.'s "Verified Uncertainty Calibration") showed the standard plug-in ECE estimator is biased and can be sensitive to exactly this choice — a model can look better or worse calibrated purely from how the bins are drawn, independent of any real change in behavior. The paper's own Section 2 flags a related, milder version of this — reliability diagrams "do not display the proportion of samples in a given bin" — but doesn't extend the caveat to ECE's headline numbers.

Only top-label calibration is measured. ECE and the reliability diagrams here only check whether the predicted class's confidence (the max softmax entry) is calibrated. A model can pass this test while the rest of its output distribution — the relative probabilities among the non-predicted classes — is badly miscalibrated, which matters for anything downstream that consumes the full distribution (ensembling, calibrated ranking, uncertainty-aware decision rules). Later work (e.g. Kull et al.'s Dirichlet calibration) built class-wise calibration metrics precisely to close this gap.

Everything is measured i.i.d. The paper states its assumption outright: "we assume that the training, validation, and test sets are drawn from the same distribution." Temperature scaling is fit on an in-distribution validation split and evaluated on an in-distribution test split throughout Table 1 — yet the paper's own motivating scenarios in the introduction (a self-driving car's obstacle detector, a disease-diagnosis network deciding when to defer to a doctor) are exactly the settings where the input distribution at deployment time drifts from the one the model was validated on. Whether a single fitted temperature survives that drift is left untested here; it was answered two years later (Ovadia et al., "Can You Trust Your Model's Uncertainty?"), which found calibration quality — including from temperature scaling — degrades substantially under dataset shift.

baselines

Fair and reasonably strong for 2017: histogram binning, isotonic regression, and BBQ were the standard non-parametric recalibration methods, and Platt scaling is the direct binary precursor to temperature scaling. The paper is also honest that its own more expressive extensions (vector and especially matrix scaling) frequently lose to the simpler temperature scaling — a good sign against cherry-picking the flashiest method as the "winner."

contamination

Not the LLM-era leakage concern — every dataset (Birds, Cars, ImageNet, CIFAR-10/100, SVHN, 20 News, Reuters, SST) reports explicit disjoint train/validation/test counts. The one soft spot: the validation split used to fit the calibration parameters is stated to be reusable as "the same set used for hyperparameter tuning," which mildly double-dips that split rather than holding out a third partition purely for calibration.

what's missed

No distribution-shift or out-of-distribution stress test of the calibrated models (despite citing Hendrycks & Gimpel's OOD-detection baseline as related work); no bin-count sensitivity ablation for ECE in the main text; no class-wise or full-distribution calibration metric alongside top-label ECE; classification only, no regression or structured prediction.

would raise confidence

An ECE-vs-M robustness check showing the method ranking doesn't flip with bin count or scheme; a shift/OOD evaluation of the same calibrated models (the gap Ovadia et al. later filled); and a class-wise calibration metric reported alongside the top-label ECE numbers in Table 1.

// bottom line

The diagnosis (capacity and regularization choices erode calibration even as accuracy improves) and the fix (temperature scaling) are both genuinely durable contributions — the field still reaches for this exact one-parameter trick as the default baseline. The gaps are less about what the paper got wrong than about what it never tried to measure: ECE's own binning sensitivity, calibration beyond the top label, and calibration under anything but an i.i.d. test split.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. Modern deep classifiers are systematically overconfident relative to their accuracy, and this got worse, not better, as architectures scaled up: a 5-layer LeNet from 1998 tracks its own accuracy closely, while a 110-layer ResNet with far lower classification error (30.6% vs. 44.9% on CIFAR-100) shows a large, systematic confidence-accuracy gap.
  2. The drivers are identifiable and separable: increased model capacity (depth, width), Batch Normalization, and reduced weight decay each independently worsen calibration even when they hold or improve accuracy — traced to networks continuing to minimize training NLL (i.e., inflating confidence) well past the point where classification error stops improving.
  3. Temperature scaling — dividing every logit by one learned scalar T before the softmax — is the simplest fix tested (a single parameter, no change to any prediction, fit in a fraction of a second), yet it beats histogram binning, isotonic regression, BBQ, and even the more expressive vector/matrix Platt-scaling variants on nearly every vision benchmark, typically cutting double-digit ECE down to roughly 1–2%.
