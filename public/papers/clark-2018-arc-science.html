Think you have Solved Question Answering? Try ARC, the AI2 Reasoning Challenge

7,787 natural grade-school science questions, split into an Easy Set and a Challenge Set defined operationally as "wrong answers from both a retrieval solver and a co-occurrence solver" — a partition that turns out to be much harder than it sounds.

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

Reading-comprehension datasets like SQuAD and SNLI had, by early 2018, produced systems that "matched human performance" on their leaderboards — but the paper opens by pointing out this is easy to mistake for general progress. Those tasks are largely retrieval-style: the answer, or the entailment signal, sits close to the surface of the given text, so systems can do well without much reasoning or outside knowledge. The paper also singles out standardized-test datasets as a promising but historically under-delivered idea (proposed as a Grand Challenge for AI as early as 2005): real human exams are designed to test people, not machines, so a large fraction of naively-collected exam questions turn out to be solvable by simple information-retrieval or word-correlation tricks — meaning aggregate scores on such datasets end up dominated by how good a system is at those tricks, not by how well it reasons.

ARC's specific gap-filling move is to make that failure mode structural rather than incidental: instead of releasing one undifferentiated pool of science questions, split the pool into questions two simple non-neural baselines can already answer (Easy) and questions neither baseline gets right (Challenge) — so that a system's score on the Challenge partition can't be inflated by exactly the shortcut the authors are trying to route around.

// how it works

The method

ARC consists of 7,787 natural, non-diagram, multiple-choice science questions (mostly 4-way, though the true range is 3–5 options) drawn from real standardized tests spanning grades 3–9 (ages roughly 8–13), collected over "several months of extensive search" across 21 named sources (Table 7) — state assessment programs (MCAS, NY Regents, TIMSS, and others) plus a large anonymous contributor the paper calls "Mercury." The questions are partitioned into an Easy Set (5,197) and a Challenge Set (2,590), with a fixed train/dev/test split for each (Challenge: 1,119/299/1,172; Easy: 2,251/570/2,376).

The Challenge/Easy split is defined operationally by two baseline "filter" solvers run against the Waterloo corpus (5×1010 tokens, 280GB of web text):

IR solver. For each candidate answer, query the corpus (via Elasticsearch) with question + answer text and take the top-scoring retrieved sentence that has non-stopword overlap with both the question and the answer; the option with the best-supported sentence wins.

PMI solver. Extract n-grams (unigrams through skip-bigrams) from the question and each answer option, and score each option by its average pointwise mutual information with the question n-grams over the same corpus — PMI(x,y) = log[p(x,y) / (p(x)p(y))].

PMI(x, y) = log p(x, y)p(x) · p(y) // strength of association between question n-gram x and answer n-gram y, over the Waterloo corpus

A question is Challenge if both solvers answer it incorrectly, otherwise it's Easy. For example, "Which property of air does a barometer measure?" is Easy because the corpus contains near-paraphrase sentences ("Air pressure is measured with a barometer") that both solvers can exploit; "Which property of a mineral can be determined just by looking at it?" (answer: luster) is Challenge because no corpus sentence directly supports "luster," and "mineral" spuriously co-occurs with wrong options like "hardness."

Alongside the questions, the authors release the ARC Corpus: 14M science-relevant sentences (1.4GB) mined by issuing ~100 query templates across 80 elementary/middle-school science topics to a search engine and de-duplicating the results, augmented with an earlier corpus (dictionary definitions, Simple Wikipedia science articles). A vocabulary check finds 99.8% of ARC's question vocabulary appears somewhere in the Corpus, and an informal sample estimates the Corpus mentions knowledge relevant to ~95% of Challenge questions — even when no single sentence directly answers the question. Use of the Corpus is optional and systems aren't restricted to it.

Eight baseline systems are evaluated: the IR and PMI solvers themselves (by definition near-zero on Challenge); a naive "guess-all" baseline that scores exactly 1/k on every k-way question; IR re-run against the ARC Corpus instead of Waterloo; TableILP (structured reasoning over semi-structured knowledge tables); TupleInference (matching against Open-IE tuples); and three neural entailment/comprehension models adapted to multiple-choice — DecompAttn and DGEM/DGEM-OpenIE (trained on an extended SciTail dataset), and BiDAF (SQuAD-trained, adapted by picking the option with the highest lexical overlap with BiDAF's predicted answer span).

// what they found

The results

On the Easy Set, most systems land between roughly 50% and 65% — DGEM reaches 58.97%, BiDAF 50.11%, IR-with-ARC-Corpus 62.55%. On the Challenge Set, the picture is starkly different: the guess-all random baseline scores 25.02%, and the best trained system, DGEM, reaches only 27.11% — inside the paper's own reported 95% confidence interval of ±2.5% around chance. TableILP does relatively better on Challenge (26.97%) than on Easy (36.15%, its worst Easy score of any system), an asymmetry the paper notes but doesn't dig into further.

The IR and PMI dataset-defining solvers score near-zero on Challenge by construction (1.02% and 2.03% respectively, marked with a dagger in the paper since these solvers were used to define the partition), but 74.48% and 77.82% on Easy. The authors' headline claim: none of the eight non-trivial baselines significantly outperforms random guessing on the Challenge Set, including two neural models (DecompAttn, BiDAF) that were near state-of-the-art on SNLI and SQuAD respectively at the time.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

The headline finding — nothing beats random on Challenge — is real, but the margin the paper reports (DGEM at 27.11% against a 25.02% floor with a stated ±2.5% 95% CI) means the Challenge Set as released has essentially no power to rank these eight baselines against each other. The authors report this honestly rather than overselling a "top" system, which is a real strength of the paper, but it also means later citations that treat "DGEM beats BiDAF on ARC-Challenge" as a finding are over-reading noise the original authors explicitly flagged.

The Challenge/Easy label itself is solver-relative rather than an intrinsic property of a question, and the paper's own data shows this: swapping the IR solver's corpus from Waterloo to the released ARC Corpus changes its Challenge-set score from ~0% (true by definition, since Waterloo-IR failure is what put those questions in Challenge) to 20.26% — a large jump that shows "hardness" here is defined against one specific retrieval setup at one point in time, not against retrieval in general. A stronger IR/PMI system than the two used for partitioning could in principle solve a chunk of "Challenge" questions through the same shortcut the label was designed to exclude, without doing any more reasoning than the excluded Easy questions require.

There's also a provenance question the paper is transparent about but doesn't dwell on: the single largest question source is "Mercury" — the paper's own label for "an anonymous content partner of AI2 who has generously provided some of their science exam question data for non-commercial research purposes" — contributing 1,762 of 2,590 Challenge questions and 3,366 of 5,197 Easy questions, roughly two-thirds of the entire dataset. Because the source is anonymous and non-commercially licensed, no third party can independently audit where those questions originally came from, how they were vetted, or whether they overlap with material that later entered web-scraped pretraining corpora.

baselines

Reasonable for March 2018: DecompAttn (SNLI SOTA) and BiDAF (SQuAD SOTA) were genuinely strong transplants, plus two purpose-built structured-reasoning systems (TableILP, TupleInference). No BERT-class pretrained-LM baseline is included — BERT's preprint wasn't public until October 2018, seven months after ARC, so this paper predates the fine-tuned-LM baseline family that became the strongest reported result on the related CommonsenseQA benchmark that November (still only 55.9% there, well short of "solved").

contamination

Not discussed (too early for the concern as usually framed today), but ~66% of the question pool traces to a single anonymous, non-commercially-licensed partner, and the 14M-sentence ARC Corpus is search-engine-scraped web science text of exactly the kind that later became common LLM pretraining material — anyone benchmarking a modern model on ARC needs an explicit decontamination check this paper had no way to anticipate.

what's missed

No per-category (knowledge-type / reasoning-type) accuracy breakdown for any baseline — Tables 4/5 and Figures 1/2 characterize the Challenge Set qualitatively, but the paper never reports whether, say, "multihop reasoning" questions (15.6% of the sample) are disproportionately responsible for the near-random scores.

would raise confidence

Confidence intervals per system rather than one aggregate ±2.5%, so "DGEM > BiDAF" claims could actually be checked; disclosure of Mercury's real provenance for independent audit; a per-category accuracy table analogous to what CommonsenseQA later published for its own top baseline.

// bottom line

ARC's real contribution is the filter-based Challenge/Easy split, which is a clean, reusable idea — construct a hard subset by definition, not by hope. But "hard" here is defined relative to two specific 2018 solvers over one specific corpus, and the paper's own IR-corpus-swap experiment shows that definition moves when the reference solver changes. Treat the Challenge/Easy boundary as a snapshot of what 2018 retrieval methods couldn't do, not a permanent taxonomy of question difficulty.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. 7,787 real grade-school science questions (grades 3–9), split into a 5,197-question Easy Set and a 2,590-question Challenge Set defined operationally as "questions both an IR solver and a PMI solver, run against a 280GB web corpus, answer incorrectly."
  2. On the Challenge Set, every 2018 baseline — including SNLI-SOTA and SQuAD-SOTA neural models adapted to the task — stayed within the paper's own ±2.5% 95% confidence interval around the 25.02% random-guess floor; the best score was DGEM at 27.11%.
  3. Ships with a companion 14M-sentence ARC Corpus (99.8% question-vocabulary coverage) for optional retrieval use, but roughly two-thirds of the question set traces to a single anonymous, non-commercially-licensed contributor ("Mercury"), which limits independent provenance auditing.
