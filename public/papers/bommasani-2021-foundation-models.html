On the Opportunities and Risks of Foundation Models

The 200-plus-page Stanford report that gave the post-GPT-3 paradigm a name. Its thesis: one class of models — trained on broad data, adapted to everything — is reshaping AI through two forces, emergence and homogenization, and getting that bet right is a sociotechnical problem, not just an engineering one.

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

By mid-2021 a strange convergence had taken hold. BERT, GPT-3, CLIP, DALL·E — models from different subfields — were all variations on a single recipe: pretrain one enormous model on broad data with self-supervision, then adapt it to many downstream tasks. The recipe was everywhere, but the field had no shared name for it and no unified account of what it meant. NLP, vision, and robotics were each rediscovering the same paradigm in isolation, and the conversation around it was badly fractured: capability researchers talked past safety researchers, who talked past the fairness, legal, and economics communities.

The available vocabulary all missed something. "Pretrained model" and "self-supervised model" describe how the thing is trained but say nothing about the downstream stakes. "Large language model" is too narrow — the paradigm had already escaped language into vision and code. "General-purpose model" overclaims a finished product, when these models are inherently unfinished and need adaptation. The real gap was conceptual and institutional: nobody had laid out in one place both the opportunities and the risks of betting the future of AI on a handful of these models — and argued that doing so responsibly is fundamentally an interdisciplinary, sociotechnical undertaking.

// how it works

A definition and two forces

This is not an experiment. It is a position paper from more than 100 Stanford-affiliated researchers — the report that launched Stanford's Center for Research on Foundation Models (CRFM). Its "method" is an act of definition plus a pair of organizing concepts that the rest of the report hangs on.

The definition is deliberately broad:

// the definition

"A foundation model is any model that is trained on broad data (generally using self-supervision at scale) that can be adapted (e.g., fine-tuned) to a wide range of downstream tasks." Current examples named in the report: BERT, GPT-3, and CLIP.

Figure 2 is the whole thesis in one picture. Heterogeneous data — text, images, speech, structured data, 3D signals — funnels into a single training run that produces one foundation model, which then fans out, via adaptation, to a wide range of tasks (question answering, sentiment analysis, information extraction, image captioning, object recognition, instruction following).

Force #1 — emergence

The model's behavior is implicitly induced rather than explicitly constructed. The canonical example is GPT-3's in-context learning: at 175 billion parameters (versus GPT-2's 1.5 billion), the model "can be adapted to a downstream task simply by providing it with a prompt" — a capability nobody trained in directly. Emergence is the report's word for capabilities that appear with scale but were never designed.

Force #2 — homogenization

The consolidation of methods. The report frames the entire history of AI as escalating emergence and homogenization (Figure 1): machine learning homogenized learning algorithms, deep learning homogenized model architectures, and foundation models homogenize the model itself — the same handful of models now sits beneath applications across every domain.

// why the two forces matter together

They interact in an "unsettling" way: homogenization concentrates risk. Because every downstream system is adapted from the same base, "the defects of the foundation model are inherited by all the adapted models downstream" — a single point of failure for bias, security holes, and blind spots. And because capability comes from emergence rather than explicit construction, the models are hard to understand, evaluate, and secure.

From there the report maps the territory in four parts: capabilities (language, vision, robotics, reasoning, human interaction, philosophy of understanding), the underlying technology (architecture, training, data, systems, security, robustness, AI safety, theory, interpretability, evaluation), applications (healthcare, law, education), and society (inequity, misuse, environmental and economic impact, legality, ethics).

// what they found

The framings that stuck

As a survey there is no headline metric to report; its "results" are the framings that became canonical and the agenda it set. Several have aged into common vocabulary:

  • The term itself. The report explicitly coined "foundation models" to fill a naming void, arguing that language model was too narrow and general-purpose model overclaimed. The term is now standard.
  • Emergence + homogenization became the default lens for discussing the paradigm — a compact way to say "capabilities you didn't design, atop a base everyone shares."
  • The single-point-of-failure argument. Homogenization means a flaw in one base model propagates to every application built on it. This reframing anticipated a central theme of later safety and evaluation work.
  • The ecosystem view (Figure 3). A foundation model is one stage in a pipeline running from data creation → curation → training → adaptation → deployment, with people at both ends — the data source and the recipients of benefits and harms.

The report's other enduring half is Part 5 (Society) — inequity, misuse, environmental and economic impact, legality, and ethics — one of the earliest unified maps of foundation-model harms, and the section most cited by the safety and policy communities that followed. Notably, the report makes essentially no new measurements: its evidence is the existing literature, synthesized into a single narrative and backed by a roughly 50-plus-page bibliography. Its contribution is framing, not findings.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

Judging this report by the usual evidentiary bar is a category error — it is a manifesto, not an evaluation — so the right question is whether its core constructs are sound and its claims falsifiable. On that test it is uneven.

The two load-bearing concepts, emergence and homogenization, are asserted and illustrated but never operationalized. There is no metric for "how emergent" a capability is. And the report's marquee emergence example — abilities appearing with scale — was later argued by Schaeffer et al. (2023, "Are Emergent Abilities of Large Language Models a Mirage?") to be partly an artifact of discontinuous, all-or-nothing metrics rather than a genuine phase change; on smooth metrics many "emergent" jumps disappear. The report had no way to distinguish the two, because it never measured.

The definition is also fuzzy by design. "Trained on broad data … adaptable to a wide range of tasks" has no sharp boundary, and does not cleanly separate a "foundation model" from any large pretrained model — part of why the field never fully settled whether "foundation models" was a real category or a rebranding of "large language models."

Then there is the irony of authorship. A report warning that homogenization narrows perspective and concentrates power was itself produced by ~100 authors from essentially one well-resourced institution (Stanford), and released alongside the launch of a center built on the term. Critics at the time read the naming as partly agenda-setting for CRFM; genuinely external and adversarial voices are thin. Most of the risk claims — misuse, inequity, market centralization, environmental cost — are forward-looking arguments rather than validated findings. That is fair for a survey, but they should be read as hypotheses: several (centralization, homogenized failure modes) have aged well; others were never tracked.

N/A — a survey, not an experiment. Its "baseline" is the prior literature, synthesized into one narrative rather than compared against alternatives.

contamination

N/A — no benchmark. The relevant risk is narrative selection bias: a single-institution author pool framing (and helping to build) the very field it surveys.

what's missed

No operational measure of "emergence" or "homogenization"; a definition too loose to separate foundation models from large pretrained models generally; little adversarial scrutiny of the term itself.

would raise confidence

Measurable definitions of the two core forces; institutionally diverse and critical co-authorship; an explicit, tracked scorecard of which 2021 predictions actually held.

// bottom line

A field-defining act of naming and synthesis. The framework it installed — emergence + homogenization, with the base model as a single point of failure — genuinely shaped how everyone now talks about the paradigm. But it is a manifesto, not a measurement: its central concepts remain more evocative than operational, and its most-quoted emergence claim was later shown to be metric-dependent.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. It coined "foundation models" and gave the post-GPT-3 paradigm a shared name and a 200-plus-page, interdisciplinary frame — capabilities through to societal impact.
  2. Two forces define the paradigm: emergence (capabilities like in-context learning appear with scale, unprogrammed) and homogenization (one model underlies everything).
  3. Homogenization is double-edged: enormous leverage, but it makes the base model a single point of failure — its biases and flaws are inherited by every downstream system.
  4. Its lasting other half is the Society analysis — an early canonical map of foundation-model harms — and its lasting weakness is that emergence and homogenization were never made measurable.
