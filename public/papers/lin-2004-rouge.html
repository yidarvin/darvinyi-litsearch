ROUGE: A Package for Automatic Evaluation of Summaries

The recall-oriented overlap metric that let summarization researchers stop renting NIST's 3,000-hour human pipeline — and that, twenty years on, is still the baseline every newer evaluator is built to beat.

Chin-Yew Lin · USC Information Sciences Institute
venue ACL Workshop (Text Summ. Branches Out) citations 20,737 topic Benchmarks & Evals source ↗

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

By 2004 summarization had a measurement problem. The field's flagship evaluation, NIST's Document Understanding Conference (DUC), scored systems with human judges rating coverage, coherence, conciseness, grammaticality and readability — and Lin estimates that even a modest manual pass over the DUC pool would demand over 3,000 hours of human effort. That is fine for an annual bake-off; it is fatal for iteration. You cannot tune a system, run an ablation, or compare ten ideas before lunch if every score requires a NIST-scale annotation campaign.

The obvious fix — an automatic metric — had two unsatisfying precedents. Saggion et al. (2002) proposed content-overlap measures (cosine similarity, unigram/bigram overlap, longest common subsequence) but never showed they correlated with human judgment, so there was no reason to trust a number they produced. And Lin & Hovy (2003) had just shown that BLEU-style n-gram co-occurrence — the trick that had made machine-translation evaluation cheap — could be ported to summaries. What was missing was a packaged, validated tool: a single piece of software implementing several overlap measures, plus a rigorous study of which measures actually track human content judgments, on which kinds of summarization tasks. ROUGE is that package and that study.

// the one-line gap

BLEU made MT evaluation cheap by counting n-gram overlap. Summarization needed the same — but recall-oriented (did you cover the reference's content?) rather than precision-oriented, and with evidence that the resulting scores agree with humans before anyone builds on them.

// how it works

The method

ROUGE — Recall-Oriented Understudy for Gisting Evaluation — is a family of overlap measures between a candidate summary and one or more human reference summaries. The defining design choice is in the name: where BLEU is a precision measure (what fraction of the candidate's n-grams appear in a reference), ROUGE anchors on recall (what fraction of the reference's units the candidate recovered). The paper introduces four variants.

ROUGE-N — n-gram recall

The base measure: the count of n-grams the candidate shares with the references, divided by the total number of n-grams on the reference side. Because the denominator is reference n-grams, adding more reference summaries enlarges the target content space, and the numerator's sum over references gives extra weight to n-grams that several humans agreed on — so a candidate that matches the human consensus is favored.

ROUGE-N =  ΣS∈Refs Σgramn∈S Countmatch(gramn) ΣS∈Refs Σgramn∈S Count(gramn) // recall: matched reference n-grams over total reference n-grams. ROUGE-1 = unigrams, ROUGE-2 = bigrams, …

With multiple references, ROUGE takes the maximum pairwise score against each reference, and uses a jackknifing procedure (average the best score over all M leave-one-out sets of M−1 references) so that machine scores can be compared against an estimated human ceiling computed the same way — one human reference scored against the rest. The jackknife is applied to all four ROUGE measures.

ROUGE-L — longest common subsequence

Instead of fixing an n-gram length, ROUGE-L rewards the longest in-sequence (not necessarily contiguous) word match. Treating a reference sentence X (length m) and a candidate Y (length n), it forms an LCS-based F-measure:

Rlcs = LCS(X,Y)m   Plcs = LCS(X,Y)n   Flcs = (1+β²) Rlcs PlcsRlcs + β² Plcs // β is set very large, so Flcs is effectively recall (Rlcs). No n-gram length to pick.

The intuition: the longer the LCS of two summaries, the more similar they are, and unlike a fixed bigram window LCS automatically credits longer co-occurring word sequences wherever they fall. At the summary level ROUGE-L unions the LCS hits over all reference sentences.

ROUGE-W — weighted LCS

Plain LCS cannot tell a consecutive match from a scattered one — "A B C D" shares the same LCS length with both "A B C D" and "A x B x C x D". ROUGE-W fixes this with a weighting function f (e.g. f(k)=kα) that pays more for consecutive matches, computed with a dynamic-programming recurrence that remembers the run length of the current match. The paper uses weighting factor α = 1.2.

ROUGE-S / ROUGE-SU — skip-bigram co-occurrence

ROUGE-S counts skip-bigrams: pairs of words in sentence order with arbitrary gaps between them, as an F-measure. A maximum skip distance dskip (the paper tests 1, 4, 9) caps how far apart a pair may be. Pure skip-bigram scoring can assign zero to a candidate that shares no ordered pair with the reference, so ROUGE-SU adds unigram counting as a backstop. These capture word order more flexibly than a contiguous bigram while staying cheaper and looser than full LCS.

// what they found

The results

The validation is a correlation study, not a leaderboard. Lin runs 17 ROUGE configurations (ROUGE-N for N=1–9, ROUGE-L, ROUGE-W with α=1.2, and ROUGE-S/SU with dskip = 1, 4, 9) over three years of DUC data, and measures how well each metric's system ranking agrees with humans' average content-coverage scores. Agreement is reported as Pearson correlation (Spearman and Kendall "track Pearson's very closely"), with bootstrap resampling for 95% confidence intervals. Each setting is run across CASE / STEM (Porter-stemmed) / STOP (stopwords removed) variants and single vs. multiple references. The critical value for significance is 0.632 at 95% confidence with 8 degrees of freedom.

Single-document summaries: ROUGE works, especially with bigrams

On the 100-word single-document task, the strong performers are ROUGE-2, ROUGE-L, ROUGE-W and ROUGE-S. ROUGE-2 reaches 0.84–0.87 on DUC 2001, and on DUC 2002 nearly everything saturates at 0.98–0.99 — which Lin attributes to the doubled sample size (295 vs. 149 judged summaries per system). Stemming and stopword removal barely move the numbers here, and using multiple references helps only slightly.

Very short / headline summaries: unigrams win, higher n-grams collapse

On the DUC 2003 ~10-word "very short" task, the picture flips: ROUGE-1, ROUGE-L, ROUGE-SU4/SU9 and ROUGE-W are excellent (0.96–0.97), while ROUGE-N with N>1 falls apart — ROUGE-2 sits around 0.75 and the higher-order measures collapse, with ROUGE-8/9 hitting 0.00 in the STOP setting (there simply aren't enough matching long n-grams in a ten-word summary). Excluding stopwords generally helps here, except for ROUGE-1. With 624 judged summaries per system, multiple references add nothing.

Multi-document summaries: the weak spot

Multi-document summarization is where ROUGE struggles. Across DUC 2001–2003 100-word multi-doc data, correlations "rarely reached high 90%" except on the longest (200- and 400-word) summaries; ROUGE-L and ROUGE-W "did not work well" here, while ROUGE-1/2 and the skip-bigram variants (S4, S9, SU4, SU9) only clear 0.70 once stopwords are excluded. Lin pins the instability on sample size — single-document tasks had >100 samples per system, multi-document tasks only ~30 — which makes the human gold ranking itself noisy and the correlations fragile.

The conclusion is deliberately conditional. ROUGE-2/L/W/S are good for single documents; ROUGE-1/L/W/SU4/SU9 are best for very short summaries; multi-document is hard and only works "reasonably well" with stopword removal; excluding stopwords usually helps; and more references usually raise correlation. There is no single "use ROUGE" recommendation — there is a lookup table indexed by task.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

ROUGE is the rare metric paper that is honest about its own scope — and reading it twenty years later, the honesty is also the indictment. What ROUGE validates is narrow: that the system-level ranking produced by lexical overlap correlates with the system-level ranking produced by humans on the single DUC "content coverage" axis. It says nothing about whether any individual summary is good, faithful, or readable — coherence, conciseness, grammaticality and readability are listed in the intro and then dropped from the evaluation entirely.

That has three consequences worth being precise about. First, it certifies overlap, not meaning. A faithful paraphrase that reuses none of the reference's words scores low; a fluent-but-wrong summary that recycles reference vocabulary scores high. ROUGE cannot distinguish a correct summary from a hallucinated one that happens to share n-grams — which is exactly the failure that later factuality work and LLM-judge metrics were built to catch. Second, the validation rests on very thin system pools. Each correlation is computed over only 12–18 systems (as few as ~30 samples per system in the multi-doc tasks), so a Pearson coefficient over a dozen points is fragile — and the paper's own tables show multi-document cells falling below the 0.632 significance line, with several large-N cells reading 0.00. Lin says as much, attributing the multi-doc instability to sample size. Third, the headline strength is the regime real users rarely occupy. Correlations are highest with multiple references and jackknifing on large single-document sets; everyday evaluation uses a single reference — and Lin notes the DUC judges themselves used only one manual summary — which is precisely the lower-correlation case.

None of this was wrong for 2004 — there was no semantic alternative to compare against, and a cheap, deterministic, roughly language-agnostic metric that tracks human rankings on newswire was an enormous practical win. The problem is what the field did next: it treated a system-ranking proxy as a summary-quality score, optimized directly against it, and rewarded extractive systems that lift reference-like spans. In the LLM era that proxy is effectively saturated — strong models match surface form trivially — which is why nearly every modern evaluator (BERTScore, BARTScore, factuality metrics, and rubric / LLM-judge approaches such as REBEL) defines itself in opposition to ROUGE.

baselines

The reference "baseline" is human coverage judgment, and on single-document newswire ROUGE-2/L/W track it well (0.84–0.99). But the only competitors are other lexical measures — no semantic/embedding baseline existed in 2004 — so "good correlation" is established relative to overlap metrics, not to meaning-aware ones.

contamination

Not a 2004 concern, but the modern analogue bites hard: ROUGE rewards surface mimicry, so an LLM (or a contaminated model that has seen the references) can score well without understanding. Overlapping ≠ faithful.

what's missed

Meaning, factuality, fluency and coherence — excluded by design. It scores system rankings, not individual summaries, and its best numbers come from the multi-reference, large-sample, single-document regime rather than the single-reference case most people actually run.

would raise confidence

More systems and samples per task (multi-doc's ~30 is thin), correlation against per-summary human scores rather than only system means, and validation beyond English newswire — dialogue, long-form, code, non-English — none of which the DUC study covers.

// bottom line

ROUGE solved the real 2004 problem — a cheap, packaged, recall-oriented metric whose rankings demonstrably agree with human content judgments on newswire summarization — and that pragmatic win made it the field's default for two decades. But it certifies lexical overlap, not understanding; its strongest correlations come from a multi-reference, large-sample, single-document regime real evaluations rarely enjoy; and its inability to tell a faithful summary from a merely-overlapping one is the exact gap the modern eval stack exists to close.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. Recall is the whole idea. ROUGE = n-gram / LCS / skip-bigram overlap against human references, anchored on recall (did you cover the reference's content?) where BLEU anchors on precision. That single framing choice is why it became the summarization metric rather than just "BLEU for summaries."
  2. There is no single ROUGE. The DUC 2001–2003 study shows the best variant depends on the task: ROUGE-2/L/W/S for single documents (up to 0.99), ROUGE-1/L/SU for ~10-word headlines (0.96–0.97, while ROUGE-N>1 collapses to near 0), and a weak, sample-starved story for multi-document. "ROUGE correlates with humans" hides all of that nuance.
  3. It validates rankings, not summaries. ROUGE's authority is a system-level correlation on one human axis (coverage), computed over a dozen-odd systems. It is cheap and deterministic — which is why it scaled — and blind to meaning and factuality — which is why BERTScore, factuality metrics, LLM-judges and rubric scoring are largely a reaction to it.
