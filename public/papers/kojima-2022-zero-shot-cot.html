Large Language Models are Zero-Shot Reasoners

Add one task-agnostic phrase — "Let's think step by step." — before the answer, and a frontier LLM stops blurting a wrong number and starts reasoning. With no exemplars, no finetuning, and a single shared template, Zero-shot-CoT lifts MultiArith from 17.7% to 78.7% and GSM8K from 10.4% to 40.7% on InstructGPT-175B.

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

Months earlier, chain-of-thought (CoT) prompting had cracked open multi-step reasoning by feeding the model a handful of worked examples — question, then a written-out rationale, then the answer — so it would imitate the rationale on a new problem. It worked, but every benchmark needed its own hand-authored exemplars, and the framing was that reasoning is a few-shot trick: a capability you have to demonstrate before the model will use it.

That left a sharp scientific question. Is step-by-step reasoning genuinely contingent on task-specific demonstrations, or is it already latent in a large pretrained model and merely waiting to be triggered? Plain zero-shot prompting was no help here: on system-2 arithmetic and symbolic tasks it scored near the floor (MultiArith 17.7%, GSM8K 10.4%, Coin Flip 12.8%), so the field assumed those tasks simply required exemplars.

The paper targets exactly that gap — a single, task-agnostic prompt that elicits reasoning with zero examples. If it works, it does two things at once: it makes CoT cheap and universal (one prompt for every task), and it reframes reasoning as a broad zero-shot property of scale rather than a narrow few-shot behavior.

// how it works

The method

Zero-shot-CoT is a two-stage prompt, and that second stage is the quiet engineering trick that makes it work. A single LLM, queried twice, with no gradient updates and no per-task examples.

Stage 1 — reasoning extraction. Take the question x, drop it into a trivial template "Q: [x]\nA: Let's think step by step." and let the model generate. Because the trigger invites a process rather than an answer, the continuation is a reasoning chain instead of a bare guess.

Stage 2 — answer extraction. A free-form chain isn't a gradeable answer, so the chain is fed back: concatenate the question, the trigger, the generated reasoning, and an answer-cue like "Therefore, the answer (arabic numerals) is", then sample again. The model now emits a cleanly formatted final answer that a simple parser can read off.

stage 1 → [ Q: x  |  A: Let's think step by step. ] → reasoning z
stage 2 → [ Q: x  |  A: …z…  |  Therefore, the answer is ] → answer // one prompt template, two forward passes, every task — no exemplars, no training

The contrast with Few-shot-CoT is the whole point: that method needs eight hand-written rationales per dataset; this one ships the same 12 words to arithmetic, symbolic, and commonsense benchmarks alike. The trigger itself was not magic out of thin air — it was the best of 16 candidate templates the authors tried (instructive phrasings like "Let's think step by step" worked; misleading or irrelevant ones did not).

// what they found

The results

On the large InstructGPT model (text-davinci-002, 175B), Zero-shot-CoT crushes plain zero-shot on the multi-step tasks: MultiArith 17.7% → 78.7% and GSM8K 10.4% → 40.7%. The gains are even more dramatic on symbolic reasoning, where greedy zero-shot is essentially helpless — Last Letter Concatenation 0.2% → 57.6% and Coin Flip 12.8% → 91.4%.

It is not a universal win, and the paper is candid about that. Single-step arithmetic barely moves (SingleEq 74.6% → 78.0%) or even slips (AddSub 72.2% → 69.6%), because those problems don't need a chain. And on CommonSenseQA the trigger actually hurts (68.8% → 64.6%): the model reasons fluently toward an answer the multiple-choice format then penalizes.

Two comparisons frame how big a deal this is. Against demonstrations, Zero-shot-CoT (78.7 on MultiArith) obliterates 8-shot plain Few-shot (33.8) — exemplars without rationales barely help — yet still trails 8-shot Few-shot-CoT (93.0), so it is a strong label-free baseline, not a new state of the art. Against training, on GSM8K its 40.7 beats a finetuned GPT-3 175B (33) and approaches finetuned-plus-verifier (55). Pair it with PaLM 540B and self-consistency and Zero-shot-CoT reaches 70.1 on GSM8K.

Crucially, the effect is emergent with scale. On vanilla GPT-3 the MultiArith lift only appears at 175B (3.3% → 19.0%) and is absent or negative on smaller models; on PaLM, GSM8K climbs 2.4% → 10.5% → 43.0% across 8B / 62B / 540B. Below ~100B parameters, "Let's think step by step" does nothing useful.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

The headline numbers are real, but two of the paper's own tables quietly explain how much of the gap is reasoning versus plumbing — and the answer is "less than it looks."

First, the baseline is sensitive to answer extraction, not just reasoning. Table 1 reports every task twice: once with a format-specific answer cue, once with a generic "The answer is." Switching cues moves the zero-shot baseline enormously — StrategyQA leaps 12.7% → 54.3% and Coin Flip 12.8% → 53.8% with no change to the model's reasoning at all. In other words, a chunk of the celebrated gap is the harness learning to parse answers the model could already produce. The fairest read takes the parsing-robust baseline as the reference, which shrinks several of the eye-popping deltas.

Second, the demo number rides on instruction tuning. The 17.7% → 78.7% MultiArith result is on text-davinci-002 — an InstructGPT model already aligned with human feedback. On raw GPT-3 175B (davinci) the same trigger lifts only 3.3% → 19.0%. So "LLMs are zero-shot reasoners" is, more precisely, "instruction-tuned LLMs at ≥100B scale are zero-shot reasoners." The paper shows this in its scale tables; the title doesn't.

Everything runs on closed, frozen APIs (GPT-3 series queried April–May 2022, plus PaLM) whose pretraining corpora are unknown, with no contamination analysis and a snapshot that is no longer reproducible. And the chains themselves are not audited for faithfulness beyond a small manual pass, which already found correct answers resting on incorrect reasoning — a thread later work on CoT faithfulness and monitorability would pull hard.

baselines

The zero-shot baseline is parsing-sensitive: changing only the answer cue lifts the StrategyQA / Coin-Flip baselines from ~12% to ~54%, so part of the headline gap is extraction, not reasoning. And Few-shot-CoT (93.0 on MultiArith) still beats Zero-shot-CoT (78.7) — this is a strong floor, not SOTA.

contamination

None checked. Closed GPT-3 / PaLM with unknown pretraining data and a single April–May 2022 text-davinci-002 snapshot; GSM8K-style benchmarks could be in pretraining and the run isn't reproducible.

what's missed

The lift is largely an instruction-tuning effect (raw GPT-3 175B only 3.3% → 19.0% vs InstructGPT 17.7% → 78.7%) and is emergent only at ~100B+; single-step tasks barely move and CommonSenseQA drops 4.2 points. The trigger is also the best of 16 templates hand-picked on these tasks.

would raise confidence

Open-weight models with known corpora and contamination controls; reporting the parsing-robust "The answer is" baseline as primary; and human-graded chain faithfulness rather than final-answer accuracy alone.

// bottom line

A one-line prompt that reframed reasoning as something to elicit, not demonstrate — a genuinely important, dirt-cheap baseline and a clean scaling probe. But the spectacular deltas lean on an instruction-tuned closed model and a parsing-weak baseline. The honest claim is "a strong zero-shot reasoning floor that emerges with scale and instruction tuning," not "LLMs reason for free."

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. One task-agnostic trigger — "Let's think step by step." — plus a two-stage reason-then-extract prompt turns greedy zero-shot decoding into multi-step reasoning with no exemplars and no finetuning: MultiArith 17.7% → 78.7%, GSM8K 10.4% → 40.7% on InstructGPT-175B.
  2. The effect is emergent and conditional: it appears only at ~100B+ scale (raw GPT-3 175B 3.3% → 19.0%, PaLM 540B GSM8K to 43.0%), and it can hurt on single-step or commonsense tasks (CommonSenseQA −4.2). Much of the magnitude also reflects instruction tuning and answer-extraction formatting, not pure reasoning.
  3. It is a baseline and a probe, not a SOTA method — Few-shot-CoT still wins (93.0 MultiArith) — but by showing reasoning is latent and cheaply elicitable, it seeded the test-time-reasoning line that runs through self-consistency, STaR, and today's RLVR reasoning models.
