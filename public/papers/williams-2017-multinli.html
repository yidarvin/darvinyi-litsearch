A Broad-Coverage Challenge Corpus for Sentence Understanding through Inference

MultiNLI: 433k premise–hypothesis pairs across ten genres of written and spoken English — built to be the harder, broader successor to SNLI, with a matched-vs-mismatched split that doubles as a cross-genre generalization test.

Adina Williams, Nikita Nangia, Samuel R. Bowman · New York University

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

Natural language inference (NLI), also called recognizing textual entailment, is one of the cleanest probes of sentence understanding we have. Show a model a premise and a hypothesis and ask it to pick one label — entailment, neutral, or contradiction. Doing well requires extracting the meaning of both sentences — lexical and compositional semantics, plus phenomena like quantification, coreference, tense, belief and modality — but, unlike translation or question answering, it doesn't force the model to also solve a separate hard problem like structured generation or document retrieval. The label is the whole task.

The Stanford NLI corpus (SNLI; Bowman et al., 2015) turned that probe into a benchmark. At 570k human-labeled pairs it was the first NLI dataset large enough to train neural sentence encoders from scratch, and it spurred a wave of work on attention, memory, and parse-aware models. But by 2017 it had two structural limits:

  • One genre. Every SNLI premise is an image caption (drawn from Flickr30k). Captions describe concrete visual scenes, so the hypotheses workers write are short and simple, and whole classes of phenomena — temporal reasoning (yesterday), belief (know), modality (should) — are rare enough to be irrelevant to the score. A model can top SNLI without ever handling them.
  • No headroom. The best models were already within a few points of human accuracy, leaving little room to distinguish strong systems from each other.

There was a second, quieter gap. In vision, pretrained feature extractors transferred across datasets with little retraining; in NLU almost every success still required training data that closely matched the evaluation data in both task and style. No corpus let you measure, head-on, whether an NLI model's sentence representations survive a shift to text it was never trained on. MultiNLI was designed to close both gaps at once: a corpus spanning the stylistic range of English, hard enough to leave headroom, and carrying an explicit domain-shift evaluation inside it.

// how it works

The method

MultiNLI — the Multi-Genre NLI corpus — keeps SNLI's collection recipe but widens its source text dramatically. As in SNLI, a crowdworker is shown a premise and writes three hypotheses for it: one that is definitely true (entailment), one that might be true (neutral), and one that is definitely false (contradiction). The difference is where the premises come from. Instead of image captions, MultiNLI draws on ten genres of the Open American National Corpus and related sources: five written non-fiction genres (SLATE, OUP, GOVERNMENT, VERBATIM, TRAVEL), two spoken genres (TELEPHONE from Switchboard, FACE-TO-FACE), two less-formal written genres (FICTION, LETTERS), and a specialized genre built from the 9/11 Commission Report. The result is 433k pairs — 392,702 train, 20,000 dev, 20,000 test.

Matched vs. mismatched: a domain shift built into the splits

The signature design choice is what gets trained on. Only five of the ten genres appear in the training set (FICTION, GOVERNMENT, SLATE, TELEPHONE, TRAVEL), each with ~77k–83k examples. All ten genres appear in dev and test, 2,000 examples apiece. So every model is evaluated twice: on the matched dev/test sets (the five genres it trained on) and on the mismatched sets (the five it never saw). One corpus, an in-domain score and a built-in cross-genre generalization score. No premise sentence appears in more than one split.

Five genre-tailored annotation prompts were piloted before collection, each with its own FAQ modeled on SNLI's. Data was gathered on the Hybrid platform (a Mechanical-Turk-like service) from an organized pool of 387 annotators. Every dev and test pair was then relabeled by four additional workers, giving five labels per example; the gold label is the majority vote, and a pair that fails to reach a three-way consensus is marked '-' and excluded from standard evaluation.

// harder text, same annotation quality

Despite far more abstract source material, inter-annotator agreement essentially matched SNLI: an individual label agreed with the gold label 88.7% of the time (SNLI: 89.0%), and the gold label matched the original author's intended label 92.6% of the time (SNLI: 91.2%). MultiNLI premises are also genuinely longer and more complex — mean premise length 22.3 words vs. SNLI's 14.1 (max 401), and 91% of sentences parse as a complete clause vs. 74% in SNLI. Hypotheses can't be reached by trivial edits: only 0.9% differ from their premise by deletion (SNLI 2.5%).

// what they found

The results

To gauge difficulty, the authors trained three sentence-encoding baselines — a continuous-bag-of-words (CBOW) model, a BiLSTM, and ESIM (the Enhanced Sequential Inference Model of Chen et al., 2017, then roughly state-of-the-art on SNLI) — each initialized with 300-D GloVe vectors and trained on SNLI, on MultiNLI, and on a mixture.

The headline is that MultiNLI is about 15 points harder than SNLI for the same architectures. Trained and tested on SNLI, all three models clear 80% — CBOW 80.6%, BiLSTM 81.5%, ESIM 86.7%. Trained and tested on MultiNLI, only ESIM breaks 70%: ESIM 72.3% matched / 72.1% mismatched, BiLSTM 66.9% / 66.9%, CBOW 64.8% / 64.5%. The same ESIM that scored 86.7% on SNLI falls to 72.3% on MultiNLI — exactly the headroom the authors were after, and against a most-frequent-class floor of just 36.5%.

Two secondary findings matter as much as the headline. First, the cross-genre gap is almost zero: matched and mismatched accuracy are nearly identical for every model (ESIM 72.3 vs. 72.1; BiLSTM exactly 66.9 vs. 66.9). Second, mixing SNLI into training doesn't help MultiNLI: going from MultiNLI to MultiNLI+SNLI lifts ESIM's SNLI score from 60.7% to 79.7% but leaves its MultiNLI score essentially flat (72.3% → 72.4%). The two corpora aren't interchangeable training signal.

A diagnostic by linguistic phenomenon

To show what MultiNLI exercises, the authors tagged the corpus for thirteen phenomena (pronouns, quantifiers, modals, negation, belief verbs, conditionals, comparatives, and so on) using parser output and keyword lists, and compared frequencies against SNLI. The frequency jumps are large: pronouns appear in 68% of MultiNLI dev examples vs. 34% in SNLI, quantifiers 63% vs. 33%, negation 31% vs. 5%, modals 28% vs. under 1%, belief verbs 19% vs. under 1%. MultiNLI clearly contains the phenomena SNLI lacked.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

MultiNLI delivered on its narrow promise — a bigger, harder, genre-diverse NLI corpus with SNLI-level annotation quality — and it became load-bearing infrastructure: a core task in GLUE and a standard pretraining/transfer source for sentence encoders (SuperGLUE later retired it as near-solved, keeping it only as auxiliary training data). But the two ways the paper frames its contribution both came with asterisks that later work made explicit.

The sharpest one is annotation artifacts. MultiNLI reports a "most frequent class" floor (~36%) but never runs a hypothesis-only baseline — a model that sees the hypothesis and not the premise. Within a year, Gururangan et al. (2018) and Poliak et al. (2018) showed that such a model is shockingly strong on crowdsourced NLI: reading the hypothesis alone reaches about 67% on SNLI and ~53% on MultiNLI, far above the ~36% majority floor. Because workers were paid to invent hypotheses, they fell into tells — generic hypotheses for entailment, negation words for contradiction, long hedged sentences for neutral. The paper's own phenomenon diagnostic quietly foreshadows this: negation is the clearest tag where its models score above their average, and the authors note they "may be tracking it as a predictor of contradiction." So a meaningful slice of any 72% is cue-matching, not inference.

The second asterisk is the cross-genre story, which is the paper's marquee design but is undemonstrated at publication. Matched and mismatched accuracy are essentially equal (ESIM 72.3 vs. 72.1), so the held-out genres were not yet acting as a generalization stressor. The authors are candid about it — "we expect genre mismatch issues to become more conspicuous as models are developed that can better fit MultiNLI's training genres" — but as shipped, the mismatched split is a promissory benchmark, and the matched/mismatched cut tracks source genre rather than any characterized distribution shift.

Third, the 13-phenomenon diagnostic is largely null. The frequencies change enormously between SNLI and MultiNLI, but model accuracy lands within ~3 points of the overall average on nearly every tag (comparatives/superlatives, the hardest, cost only 3–4 points). The analysis establishes that MultiNLI contains harder phenomena, not that those phenomena are where models fail — difficulty is diffuse (longer, more varied sentences), or the coarse keyword/POS tags don't isolate the reasoning each phenomenon demands.

baselines

CBOW / BiLSTM / ESIM only — reasonable for 2017, but pre-transformer, and crucially with no hypothesis-only or premise-only baseline. The reported floor is the 36% majority class, which badly understates how much label signal lives in the hypothesis alone (~53% on MultiNLI).

contamination

Premises are public corpora (the OANC, the 9/11 Commission Report) and the full corpus is openly released, so later pretrained models almost certainly ingested the source text. Test labels are withheld on Kaggle, which limits direct label leakage but not exposure to the premises.

what's missed

matched ≈ mismatched, so the headline doesn't yet measure the generalization the corpus was built for; the phenomenon tags don't localize failures; and crowd-written hypotheses encode artifacts the accuracy number silently rewards.

would raise confidence

Report hypothesis-only and premise-only baselines beside full-input; quantify the matched→mismatched shift; add adversarial / artifact-controlled subsets and per-genre human ceilings; and re-benchmark with stronger contemporary models.

// bottom line

A clean, well-executed dataset that did its core job and earned its place as a core GLUE task — but its two big framings, "a cross-genre domain-adaptation testbed" and "a hard probe of sentence understanding," both held only with caveats. The genre-shift split barely moved 2017 models, and crowd-sourced hypothesis artifacts mean part of any score is shortcut-learning. The headroom proved short-lived too: BERT pushed MultiNLI to 86.7% within a year, and stronger models since pushed it into the low 90s — leaving the artifact critique, not the difficulty bar, as MultiNLI's most durable lesson for how benchmarks should be built and audited.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. MultiNLI generalized SNLI from a single genre (image captions) to ten genres of written and spoken English — 433k pairs — and added a matched-vs-mismatched split so one corpus measures both in-domain accuracy and cross-genre generalization, all while holding SNLI-level annotator agreement (88.7% individual-vs-gold) on far more abstract text.
  2. It is genuinely harder by design: the same ESIM model drops from 86.7% on SNLI to 72.3% on MultiNLI, and only ESIM cleared 70% — restoring the headroom SNLI had lost. But the cross-genre split it was built around barely separated 2017 models (matched 72.3 ≈ mismatched 72.1).
  3. Its most durable legacy is cautionary: later hypothesis-only baselines (~53% on MultiNLI vs. a ~36% floor) showed crowd-written NLI hypotheses leak the label, so a slice of any score is cue-matching rather than inference — a finding that reshaped how entailment and benchmark data are built and stress-tested.
