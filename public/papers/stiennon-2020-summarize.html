2020 · NeurIPS · Post-training & RL

Learning to summarize from human feedback

Stop training summarizers to imitate reference text and score them with ROUGE. Instead, collect human pairwise preferences, fit a reward model to them, and fine-tune the policy with PPO against that reward. A 1.3B model trained this way beats a supervised model 10× its size — and both feedback models write summaries people prefer to the human-written originals. This is the blueprint that became RLHF.

Nisan Stiennon, Long Ouyang, Jeff Wu, Daniel M. Ziegler, Ryan Lowe, Chelsea Voss, Alec Radford, Dario Amodei, Paul Christiano · OpenAI

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

By 2020 the recipe for applying a large language model to a task was fixed: pretrain, then fine-tune with supervised learning to maximize the log-probability of human-written demonstrations, and evaluate with an automatic metric — for summarization, ROUGE n-gram overlap. The paper's argument is that both halves of that recipe are proxies for the thing anyone actually cares about: whether the summary is good.

Maximum-likelihood training has no way to distinguish an important error (inventing a fact) from a trivial one (a synonym swap); it spends probability mass on every demonstration in the data, including the low-quality ones; and the distribution it learns degrades under the sampling it's later asked to do. ROUGE, meanwhile, has a long track record of correlating poorly with human judgment — it rewards lexical overlap, not faithfulness or coverage. So the training objective and the evaluation metric are both misaligned with summary quality, and they reinforce each other.

The bet of the paper is that you can do better by optimizing for human preference directly — and, more ambitiously, that the technique matters far beyond summarization. The authors frame it explicitly as an early, low-stakes testbed for a long-term alignment problem: as models take on harder tasks, their mistakes get subtler and more costly, and training them to imitate text we can write will not be enough.

// how it works

The method

The pipeline is three steps, repeated (Figure 2). Start from a policy already fine-tuned with supervised learning on Reddit TL;DR. Then: (1) collect comparisons — for each post, sample summaries from several sources (the current policy, the initial policy, the original reference, various baselines) and ask a human labeler which of two is better; (2) train a reward model — a GPT-3-style model with a scalar output head, initialized from the supervised baseline, trained to predict which summary a human preferred; (3) optimize the policy with PPO against that reward.

The reward model is fit with a pairwise logistic loss: given a post x and two summaries, it should assign higher scalar reward to the one the human chose.

loss(rθ) = −E(x, y0, y1, i)∼D [ log σ( rθ(x, yi) − rθ(x, y1−i) ) ] // yi is the human-preferred summary; outputs are normalized so reference summaries average a reward of 0

The policy is then trained to maximize that reward — but not the raw reward. The objective adds a KL penalty that keeps the RL policy πRL close to the supervised initialization πSFT:

R(x, y) = rθ(x, y) − β log πRL(y | x)πSFT(y | x) // the KL term is both an entropy bonus (deters mode collapse) and a leash keeping the policy in the RM's training distribution

Several deliberate design choices separate this from its predecessor (Ziegler et al. 2019, which the authors call the work they are "most similar to"). They move from an online to a batch / offline regime — alternating between sending large batches of comparisons to labelers and retraining — and invest heavily in data quality: labelers are onboarded with detailed instructions, share a chat room with researchers, and are continuously monitored. The payoff is high agreement: labelers agree with researchers 77% ± 2% of the time, essentially matching the 73% ± 4% rate at which researchers agree with each other. The value network for PPO is a separate Transformer (initialized from the reward model) so its early updates don't corrupt the policy. Models are GPT-3-style decoders at 1.3B and 6.7B parameters; the filtered TL;DR set holds 123,169 posts (references trimmed to 24–48 tokens to neutralize length), and the released human-feedback dataset contains 64,832 comparisons.

// what they found

The results

The headline metric is the fraction of the time humans prefer a policy's summaries over the dataset's reference summaries (Figure 1). The 1.3B human-feedback model beats a supervised model 10× its size — a 61% vs 43% raw preference rate against the references. The 6.7B feedback model is better still, preferred roughly 70% of the time, and — the striking result — both feedback models are preferred to the human-written reference summaries themselves. Supervised and pretrain-only baselines sit well below the 50% reference line; human feedback is the only regime that clears it.

Length is a real confound, and the authors say so. Their models learned to write somewhat longer summaries, and length explains about one-third of the feedback-vs-supervised gap at 6.7B. After controlling for length, the preference advantage drops by only ~5% — the 6.7B feedback model is still preferred to the references about 65% of the time.

A finer-grained evaluation (Figure 3) had labelers score four axes on a 7-point Likert scale. Feedback models win on every axis but lead most on coverage. Despite a high bar for a perfect score, the 6.7B PPO model earns a 7/7 overall rating 45% of the time, versus 20% for the 6.7B supervised baseline and 23% for the reference summaries.

It transfers. Without any news-specific fine-tuning, the Reddit-trained 6.7B feedback model summarizes CNN/DailyMail news articles nearly as well as a 6.7B model fine-tuned directly on CNN/DM reference summaries, and approaches a T5 baseline at matched summary length — despite generating about half as many tokens.

The reward-model analyses are the quietly important part:

  • The RM generalizes to CNN/DM (never seen in training), agreeing with labelers 62.4% (1.3B) and 66.5% (6.7B) of the time — the 6.7B RM nearly matching the 66.9% inter-labeler agreement.
  • It is sensitive to small edits: it prefers minimally human-improved summaries 79.4% / 82.8% of the time (humans: 84.1%), and on role-reversed perturbations it picks the genuine reference 92.9% / 97.2% of the time.
  • It is length-biased: it endorses shortening edits only 62.6% of the time vs 76.4% for humans.
  • Scaling (Figure 6): doubling comparison data buys ~1.1% RM validation accuracy; doubling model size buys ~1.8%.

And the indictment of the old metrics: ROUGE's agreement with labelers falls from ~57% on supervised samples to ~50% (chance) on the better human-feedback samples; log-probability drops to ≤50% as well; the learned reward model stays at 62%. Optimizing ROUGE directly peaks sooner and at a substantially lower quality than optimizing the reward model.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

The most important figure in the paper is not the headline win — it's the over-optimization curve (Figure 5, reproduced left below). As you crank up optimization pressure against the reward model (lower KL penalty), the RM's predicted preference keeps climbing toward 1.0 while the actual human preference rises, peaks near 0.47, and then falls — eventually going anti-correlated with what humans want. This is the first clean, public picture of reward-model Goodharting: optimize a learned proxy hard enough and it stops measuring the thing you care about. The KL leash is the fix, but it is a hyperparameter you have to tune, not a guarantee — and the same failure is later studied at length in the rubric-reward-hacking line that descends from this work.

On baselines, the authors are unusually honest about the comparison. Their supervised baselines are genuinely strong — the 6.7B supervised model matches mid-2019 CNN/DM ROUGE SOTA — so "1.3B feedback beats 10× supervised" is a fair fight. But they openly note they could not afford a compute- and label-matched supervised baseline trained on an equivalent volume of high-quality human demonstrations; the labeling budget went into comparisons instead. So part of "feedback > supervised" reflects where the annotation dollars were spent, not purely the superiority of the objective.

The deeper validity question is that this is a closed loop: the reward model and the final evaluation are produced by the same labeler pool under the same instructions. The policy is therefore optimized toward exactly the judges that grade it. The 77% ≈ 73% researcher-labeler agreement is real reassurance that the judgments aren't idiosyncratic, but an evaluation by an independent labeler pool would close the gap. And the single "fraction preferred" scalar absorbs a length confound and hides that the gains concentrate in coverage; factuality is only probed through a Likert "accuracy" axis, never directly audited for hallucination.

baselines

Strong supervised baselines (6.7B matches mid-2019 CNN/DM ROUGE SOTA), but no compute- or label-matched demonstration baseline — the annotation budget went to comparisons, so the comparison is somewhat tilted by where labor was spent.

contamination

Not a static-test-set leakage problem (grading is fresh human comparison), but the reward model and the evaluation share one labeler pool and one instruction set — a closed optimization-and-grading loop.

what's missed

A single "fraction preferred" scalar masks a length confound (~⅓ of the gap; RM itself is length-biased) and hides that improvement is mostly coverage. No direct factuality / hallucination audit.

would raise confidence

Length-matched preference as the primary metric; an independent labeler pool for evaluation; a label-matched supervised-demonstration baseline; and a dedicated faithfulness audit.

// bottom line

This is the proof-of-concept that optimizing a model against learned human preferences — not likelihood, not ROUGE — produces outputs people genuinely prefer, that the effect scales with model size, and that it transfers to a new domain. It is the direct template for InstructGPT and ChatGPT, and its over-optimization curve is the ancestor of the entire reward-hacking literature. The caveats (length, a closed labeler loop, an unmatched demonstration baseline) temper the magnitude of the win without touching the core claim.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. This is RLHF's blueprint. Pairwise human preferences → a reward model fit with a logistic loss → PPO with a KL penalty to the supervised init. A 1.3B model trained this way beat a supervised model 10× its size, and both feedback models out-wrote the human reference summaries.
  2. The reward model is a leaky proxy. Optimize against it too hard and true human preference collapses even as predicted reward keeps rising — reward-model Goodharting, held in check only by the KL leash. This curve is the seed of the reward-hacking research line.
  3. Direct preference optimization transfers and exposes bad metrics. A Reddit-trained model summarized CNN/DM news nearly as well as a news-fine-tuned model, while ROUGE and log-probability both fell to roughly chance at ranking the best summaries — exactly the metrics the field had been optimizing.
