Chain-of-Thought Prompting Elicits Reasoning in Large Language Models

Ask a large language model to write out its intermediate reasoning steps — a chain of thought — before committing to an answer, and multi-step reasoning that scaling alone never unlocked suddenly appears. The trick is just a handful of hand-written exemplars in the prompt; no model is finetuned. Eight exemplars take PaLM 540B from 17.9% to 56.9% on GSM8K, beating a finetuned-GPT-3-with-verifier prior best. This is the paper that turned "thinking out loud" into a first-class capability — the substrate every reasoning model and trace-grading eval since is built on.

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

By early 2022, scaling language models had paid off almost everywhere — except on the tasks that look most like reasoning. Arithmetic word problems, commonsense inference, and symbolic manipulation showed flat scaling curves: a 137B model was barely better than a 7B one, and simply training a bigger model "has not proved sufficient" (the paper cites Rae et al., 2021). Something other than raw scale was missing.

Two existing ideas each pointed at the missing piece, and each had a disqualifying limitation:

  • Rationale-augmented training. Earlier work taught models to emit natural-language intermediate steps — by training from scratch (Ling et al., 2017) or finetuning (Cobbe et al., 2021). It works, but it is costly: you need a large corpus of high-quality, hand-written rationales, far more expensive to produce than plain input–output pairs.
  • Few-shot prompting. GPT-3 (Brown et al., 2020) showed you can skip finetuning entirely and just show a model a few input–output exemplars. It is cheap and general, but it works poorly on reasoning tasks and "often does not improve substantially with increasing language model scale."

The gap, then, is a method that has the cheapness of prompting and the step-by-step structure of rationales — without paying the annotation cost of finetuning or the flat scaling of vanilla prompting. Chain-of-thought prompting is exactly that combination.

// how it works

The method

A chain of thought is a series of intermediate natural-language reasoning steps that lead to a final answer — the model's "show your work." Chain-of-thought prompting elicits it with the simplest possible intervention: in the few-shot prompt, replace each ⟨question, answer⟩ exemplar with a ⟨question, chain of thought, answer⟩ triple. Shown a few such demonstrations, a sufficiently large model conditions on the pattern and produces its own chain of thought before answering a new question. That is the entire technique.

Two things make it remarkable. First, it is off-the-shelf: "No language models were finetuned in the process of writing this paper." There are no gradient updates, no rationale-labeled training set — only a handful of exemplars (eight, for the arithmetic tasks) written once by hand. Second, it is general: because a chain of thought is just language, the same recipe applies to arithmetic, commonsense, and symbolic tasks alike.

standard  [ (q₁, a₁), … , (qk, ak),  qtest ]  →  a
chain-of-thought  [ (q₁, c₁, a₁), … , (qk, ck, ak),  qtest ]  →  c then a // the only change is inserting a reasoning trace cᵢ into each exemplar; the frozen model then generates its own chain c before the answer a. Greedy decoding, k = 8 exemplars for arithmetic.

The authors argue the trick has four attractive properties: a chain of thought lets a model decompose a hard problem into steps (allocating more computation to harder ones); it offers an interpretable window into how an answer was reached (and where it went wrong); it applies in principle to any task a human can solve via language; and it can be elicited for free in large off-the-shelf models just by adding exemplars.

Experiments span three model families across scale — GPT-3 (350M–175B), LaMDA (422M–137B), and PaLM (8B–540B), plus UL2 20B and Codex — over three task categories: arithmetic (GSM8K, SVAMP, ASDiv, AQuA, MAWPS), commonsense (CSQA, StrategyQA, BIG-bench Date and Sports Understanding, SayCan robot planning), and symbolic reasoning (last-letter concatenation, coin flip). For every task the exemplar chains of thought were composed by hand by the authors.

// what they found

The results

On arithmetic, the gains are striking. With PaLM 540B and eight chain-of-thought exemplars, GSM8K jumps from 17.9% to 56.9% (+39.0) — a new state of the art that surpasses even a finetuned GPT-3 175B equipped with a verifier (the prior best, ≈55%, from Cobbe et al., 2021). The other arithmetic benchmarks move too: SVAMP 69.4→79.0, ASDiv 72.1→73.9, AQuA 25.2→35.8, MAWPS 79.2→93.3. The pattern is consistent — the harder the benchmark, the larger the lift; on SingleOp, the easiest single-step subset of MAWPS, gains were negative or negligible.

The most consequential finding is when it works. Chain of thought is an emergent ability of model scale: for models below roughly 100B parameters it gives little benefit — and can even hurt, producing fluent but illogical chains — while above that threshold the flat scaling curve turns sharply upward. Standard prompting, the paper concludes, "only provides a lower bound on the capabilities of large language models."

The effect generalizes well beyond math. On commonsense, PaLM 540B with chain of thought beats the prior state of the art on StrategyQA (75.6% vs 69.4%) and outperforms an unaided sports enthusiast on Sports Understanding (95.4% vs 84%). On symbolic tasks, chain of thought not only solves last-letter concatenation and coin-flip state tracking that standard prompting fails, but enables length generalization: trained on two-item examples, the model extends to longer out-of-distribution inputs (3- and 4-item), where standard prompting collapses to near zero.

Two control experiments tell you why it works. In an ablation (Figure 5), three alternatives that share surface features of chain of thought all land back at the standard-prompting baseline: emitting only the bare equation, emitting a string of dots equal to the equation's length ("variable compute only"), and emitting the reasoning after the answer. So the benefit is not extra tokens of computation, and not merely activating relevant pretraining knowledge — it is the sequential, natural-language reasoning that precedes and produces the answer. And in an error analysis of LaMDA 137B on GSM8K, of 50 correct answers 48 had logically valid chains (two were right by coincidence), while of 50 wrong answers 46% were "almost correct" (a calculator slip, a symbol-mapping error, one missing step) and 54% had major semantic errors — a profile that scaling from PaLM 62B to 540B substantially repairs.

It is also reasonably robust: different annotators, independently written exemplars, different exemplar orderings, and varying exemplar counts all show variance (as expected of prompting) but consistently beat standard prompting (Figure 6).

// does it hold up?

The eval read

This is a prompting paper, not a benchmark, so the right way to read it is: is the comparison clean, and does the headline claim survive scrutiny? The comparison is clean — the model is frozen and only the prompt changes, so the +39 points on GSM8K cannot be a training artifact. The ablations are genuinely strong: by isolating "equation only," "dots-only compute," and "reasoning after answer," the authors rule out the most obvious alternative explanations and pin the effect on natural-language sequential reasoning. That is careful work.

The load-bearing — and most contested — claim is emergence: that chain of thought helps only above ~100B parameters. Later work argued this kind of threshold can be partly an artifact of discontinuous metrics (exact-match solve rate) rather than a true phase change in the underlying model, and that smaller models can reason when distilled or prompted differently. The contemporaneous zero-shot result — simply appending "Let's think step by step" (Kojima et al., 2022) — also shows hand-written exemplars are not essential, which both broadens the phenomenon and undercuts the idea that the specific demonstrations matter much.

Two softer concerns. Prompt craft and reproducibility: the exemplar chains were authored by hand, and Figure 6 shows real variance across annotators — the paper even cites GPT-3's notorious sensitivity (SST-2 accuracy ranging from 54.3% to 93.4% just by permuting exemplars). Results depend on undisclosed prompt-writing skill. Faithfulness: the authors are admirably explicit that a chain "does not answer whether the network is actually reasoning," and that two of fifty correct chains reached the right answer for the wrong reasons. That crack — a plausible-looking trace that does not reflect the model's actual computation — is exactly what later work formalized as unfaithful chain of thought, and it is the reason grading reasoning traces (as MoReBench does for moral reasoning) is harder than it looks.

Finally, leakage: GSM8K, SVAMP, and CSQA are public, and the "new state of the art" is a 2022 snapshot. These sets are now standard pretraining fodder, so the result should be read as a demonstration that prompting elicits latent capability, not as a held-out generalization guarantee. The gains also concentrate where baselines were low — the dramatic +39 on GSM8K is the exception, not the rule (ASDiv moved +1.8).

baselines

Clean within-model comparison — same frozen LLM, only the prompt changes — plus prior finetuned SOTA (GPT-3 + verifier at ~55% on GSM8K). The "prior best" reference numbers are 2021-era.

contamination

GSM8K / SVAMP / CSQA are public; SOTA claims are a 2022 snapshot and these sets are now common training data. Read as capability elicitation, not held-out generalization.

what's missed

Gains concentrate on low-baseline multi-step tasks; the emergence claim is metric-dependent and later contested; whether the chain is faithful to the model's computation is left open by the authors themselves.

would raise confidence

Zero-shot / automatic CoT to remove hand-authored-exemplar variance (later shown to work); explicit faithfulness probes; re-examining emergence under continuous metrics.

// bottom line

Chain of thought did not add a capability — it revealed that standard prompting was underselling large models, and that a few natural-language reasoning exemplars unlock latent multi-step reasoning for free. Its enduring contribution is conceptual: it made the reasoning trace a first-class, inspectable object — the substrate that test-time-compute reasoning models (R1, the o-series) and trace-grading evaluations are built on.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. Inserting a worked reasoning trace into a few prompt exemplars — no finetuning — lifts PaLM 540B on GSM8K from 17.9% to 56.9%, beating a finetuned-GPT-3-plus-verifier prior best.
  2. Chain of thought is an emergent ability of scale: flat or harmful below ~100B parameters, steep above — the most influential demonstration that standard prompting is only a lower bound on what an LLM can do.
  3. Ablations show the effect is the natural-language sequential reasoning itself, not extra compute or knowledge-activation: dots-only and reasoning-after-answer both flatline at baseline.
  4. The authors leave open whether the model is "really reasoning," and chains can be right for the wrong reasons — the faithfulness question that downstream oversight and trace-grading evals inherit.
