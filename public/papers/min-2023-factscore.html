FActScore: Fine-grained Atomic Evaluation of Factual Precision in Long Form Text Generation

A model's bio of a person is rarely all-true or all-false — it's a mix. FActScore stops scoring that mix as a single yes/no verdict and instead measures the share of atomic facts a trusted source supports, then builds an automatic estimator that lands within ~2% of human annotators.

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

By 2023, language models were fluent enough to write paragraph-length answers, but there was no good way to ask how factual those paragraphs were. A long generation isn't true or false — it's a blend. The authors note that a single ChatGPT sentence carries about 4.4 pieces of information, and in roughly 40% of sentences those pieces are a mix of supported and unsupported claims. A binary "is this bio correct?" judgment throws that structure away.

Prior work had two unsatisfying escapes. The first added a partial-support label — but its boundary is subjective and annotators disagree. The second took the strict line that every piece must be supported, which collapses a mostly-correct generation and a near-fabrication to the same score. In Figure 1, the strict rule assigns 0.0 to both bios even though the first gets two-thirds of its facts right and the second almost none.

Two more problems compounded it. Truth was usually treated as a single global fact, ignoring that real knowledge sources disagree; and validating every claim by hand is slow and expensive — about $4 per generation when each bio contains 26–41 atomic facts. So researchers fell back on short-answer QA benchmarks, which can't see errors that accumulate over a long generation. FActScore targets exactly that blind spot: a graded, source-relative, and ultimately automatable measure of factual precision in long-form text.

// how it works

The method

FActScore rests on two ideas. First, the atomic fact — a short sentence carrying exactly one piece of information — is the unit of evaluation, not the sentence. Second, factual precision is defined relative to a knowledge source C the user trusts, rather than against some global notion of truth. A generation is decomposed into its atomic facts, each fact gets a binary supported / not-supported label against C, and the score is the fraction supported.

f(y) = 1|Ay|  Σa∈Ay 𝕀[ a supported by C ]     FActScore(M) = 𝔼x∈X[ f(Mx) | Mx responds ] // Ay = atomic facts in response y; the expectation is taken only over prompts the model does not abstain on

The conditioning on "M responds" matters: FActScore is a precision metric, and it deliberately ignores prompts where the model abstains. The setup makes three assumptions — that support is undebatable, that every atomic fact carries equal weight, and that the source doesn't contradict itself. To satisfy them in practice the authors evaluate on people biographies (objective, specific, broad across nationalities and professions) using English Wikipedia as C, sampling 183 entities uniformly across rarity buckets from Wikidata.

From human annotation to an automatic estimator

Hand-labeling at $4 a generation doesn't scale, so the second half of the paper builds a model that estimates FActScore. It reuses InstructGPT to break a generation into atomic facts, then validates each one with a separate evaluator LM (LMEVAL) via zero-shot True / False prompting. Four validators are compared: No-context LM (ask the model directly), Retrieve→LM (prepend GTR-retrieved Wikipedia passages), Nonparametric Probability (NP) (score the fact with a masked-LM likelihood), and the Retrieve→LM + NP ensemble, which labels a fact supported only if both agree. The evaluators are a LLaMA 7B tuned on Super-NaturalInstructions ("Inst-LLAMA") and ChatGPT.

Quality is measured by Error Rate (ER) — the absolute gap between the human FActScore and the estimated one — plus whether the estimator preserves the ranking of subject models. ER is an aggregate measure, which is subtle: an evaluator can mislabel individual facts yet still land a low ER if its errors cancel out.

// what they found

The results

Under human annotation, the commercial models of the day were riddled with errors. InstructGPT scored a FActScore of 42.5%, ChatGPT 58.3%, and search-augmented PerplexityAI 71.5%. That last number is the surprise: a system wired to a live search engine — which should approach 100% if it simply copied the right Wikipedia page — still got nearly three in ten atomic facts wrong. Agreement between annotators on a re-labeled 10% slice was high (96% / 90% / 88%).

Two failure patterns generalize across models. Precision collapses as entities get rarer: ChatGPT falls from about 80% to 16% moving from the most- to least-frequent people. Even PerplexityAI, with retrieval, drops by a relative 50% (atomic-level) and 64% (sentence-level) — contradicting prior claims that retrieval makes models robust to entity rarity. Precision also decays with position: the later part of a generation is reliably less factual, as early, common facts (nationality, profession) give way to error propagation. A short-answer benchmark would never catch that tail.

The automatic estimator closely tracks the humans. Retrieval is essential (the No-context validator either has high ER or scrambles the ranking), and while Retrieve→LM alone can over-estimate by up to 17%, ensembling it with NP brings the best variants to an ER below 2% — e.g. Inst-LLAMA's ensemble hits ER 1.4 on InstructGPT and 0.4 on ChatGPT. The two best estimators correlate at 0.99 Pearson across 10+ models. With a cheap, faithful estimator in hand, the authors ran a case study no human budget would allow: 6,500 generations from 13 subjects (12 LMs plus human-written bios, 500 each), which would have cost roughly $26K to annotate by hand.

The scaled findings sharpen the picture: every LM is substantially less factual than human-written bios, even though writing a biography is an easy task. GPT-4 and ChatGPT are comparable in precision, but GPT-4 abstains less (12% vs. 16%) and generates far more facts (60.8 vs. 37.0 per response). Within a family, scale helps — Alpaca 65B > 13B > 7B, Vicuna 13B > 7B — yet among 7B models the gap is wide (Alpaca/Vicuna ~40% vs. StableLM ~17%), pointing at base model, data, and recipe rather than size alone.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

FActScore's framing — graded, atomic, source-relative — has aged into a standard, and the paper is unusually honest about its own seams. But three of those seams are load-bearing, and anyone quoting a FActScore should know them.

First, the metric is precision-only, and precision is gameable. Because the score conditions on the model responding and never penalizes abstention or terseness, a model can lift its FActScore by saying less. PerplexityAI's 71.5% sits on top of 9.3% abstention; ChatGPT's 58.3% on top of 14.2%. The paper's own Table 6 shows a Mary I bio that is high in precision yet low in recall — every stated fact checks out, but it never mentions how she regained the line of succession and became queen. The authors know this and tell you to report FActScore alongside abstention rate and fact count — sound advice that a single headline number invites everyone to ignore.

Second, truth is whatever Wikipedia says. A correct fact absent from Wikipedia reads as "not-supported" (the paper's own example is a memoir found only in Google Books, not Wikipedia), and the ground truth itself is noisy: in a 30-case audit of PerplexityAI's "not-supported" labels, 10% were actually annotation errors and 3.3% traced to Wikipedia being internally inconsistent or wrong. The "support is undebatable" and "source is self-consistent" assumptions hold reasonably for biographies and break elsewhere — which is why the move beyond bios is only a proof of concept (Appendix B.5).

Third, the celebrated "<2% error" is an aggregate ER, not per-fact accuracy. Figure 2 makes the point internally: a validator with worse per-item F1 can post a better ER through cancellation. And there is no single best estimator — the winning variant depends on the subject model (LLAMA+NP for InstructGPT/ChatGPT, ChatGPT for PerplexityAI), so the authors recommend running both and reporting their correlation. There's also a dependency worth flagging: atomic-fact decomposition leans on InstructGPT, a reproducibility and granularity risk since the fact count sets the denominator. And because the subject LMs were themselves trained on web text including Wikipedia, the rarity curve is partly a memorization curve — frequent entities are flattered, so absolute scores reflect what a model memorized as much as what it can verify.

baselines

Fair for 2023: validator baselines (No-context self-eval à la Kadavath; SelfCheckGPT) and the 13 subject models (GPT-4, ChatGPT, Alpaca, Vicuna, …) were the frontier. The absolute scores are now dated, but the methodology is model-agnostic.

contamination

The subject LMs were trained on web text including Wikipedia — the same source used as ground truth — so frequent entities are flattered and the rarity decline conflates entity difficulty with how much the model memorized.

what's missed

No factual recall: a model that abstains or emits fewer facts can score higher. Domain is narrow (people bios + Wikipedia). Ground truth is imperfect: inter-annotator agreement ran 88–96%, and a slice of "not-supported" errors trace to annotation mistakes or Wikipedia itself.

would raise confidence

A recall companion metric, multi-source grounding, a human re-audit quantifying the false-negative rate, per-judgment accuracy reported next to aggregate ER, and natural (not rarity-uniform) prompt distributions.

// bottom line

FActScore is the right reframing of long-form factuality and a genuinely useful, ~$4-to-near-free measurement tool — but it scores precision against Wikipedia, not truth. Treat the number as one coordinate (always beside abstention and fact count), not a verdict, and remember its rankings were validated on biographies.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. FActScore turns long-form factuality from an all-or-nothing verdict into the fraction of atomic facts supported by a chosen knowledge source — letting a 66.7%-accurate bio rank above a 10% one where strict metrics scored both 0.0.
  2. Even strong, search-augmented models are unreliable: ChatGPT 58.3%, PerplexityAI 71.5%, with precision collapsing for rare entities (ChatGPT 80%→16%) and later in the generation — failures short-answer benchmarks can't see.
  3. An automatic estimator (retrieval + LM, ensembled with a nonparametric likelihood) matches human FActScore within ~2% aggregate error and scaled the eval to 13 models / 6,500 bios for ~$26K saved — but it's precision-only and bios/Wikipedia-bound, so report it alongside abstention and fact count.
