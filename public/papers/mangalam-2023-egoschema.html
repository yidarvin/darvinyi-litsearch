EgoSchema: A Diagnostic Benchmark for Very Long-form Video Language Understanding

5,063 three-minute egocentric clips, each with a five-way question that cannot be answered from any single moment — plus a new metric, the temporal certificate, that measures how much of a video you actually have to watch to verify an answer, and shows most "long-video" benchmarks aren't long at all.

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

By 2023, "long-video" benchmarks already existed — clips running minutes, sometimes hours. The paper's starting observation is that clip duration is a bad proxy for how much genuine long-term reasoning a task requires. Two failure modes recur in prior long-form video datasets. The first is forcing the "explosive diversity of human behavior" into narrow, pre-defined label spaces, which the authors say leads to "unduly narrow and oddly specific tasks, such as like ratio or relationship prediction" in one prior long-form benchmark. The second, more insidious failure mode is a long clip whose actual verifiable content is short: a movie-grounding dataset can have an average clip length of 110 minutes while the actually-relevant retrieved sub-clip averages only 4.1 seconds — a long-duration task "disguised" as one requiring long-term understanding, when in fact a system only ever needs to look at a few seconds to answer.

The paper's core methodological move is to stop measuring video tasks by clip length and instead measure them by temporal certificate length: the length of video a human verifier would actually need to watch to be convinced a given answer is correct. This reframes "how long-form is this task" as an empirical, checkable quantity rather than an assumption baked into the clip length. Built on top of that metric is EgoSchema, a diagnostic benchmark of egocentric ("from the camera wearer's point of view") three-minute clips, each paired with a five-way multiple-choice question, designed specifically to have a very long certificate — so that a system genuinely has to integrate information across the full clip, not just a keyframe or two, to answer correctly.

// how it works

The method

EgoSchema is built on top of Ego4D, a large egocentric-video corpus with dense, timestamped human narrations of a camera wearer's ("C") actions. From Ego4D's raw footage the authors extract non-overlapping three-minute clips, each requiring at least 30 human-annotated narrations in that window, and run every clip through a four-stage pipeline that turns narrations into curated, hard multiple-choice questions.

Stage II is itself an ablation: the authors compare four LLM prompting strategies for producing question–answer–wrongs (QAW) triplets — one-shot (all N triplets in one call, cheapest but low quality), N-shot (one call per triplet), QAW-shot (three chained calls: questions, then correct answers, then wrong answers), and the one they settle on, Q(AW)-shot (questions in one call, then all correct-and-incorrect answers together in a second call) — which they find 30% cheaper than QAW-shot and produces higher-quality answers, "perhaps since [the] LLM can jointly model [wrong answers] while generating [the correct answer]." They iterated on the seed prompt itself across roughly 85 versions, and found that only GPT-4, Bard, and Claude — not GPT-3 or ChatGPT — generated usable QAW at a sufficient rate.

Stage III's key idea is blind filtering: feed an LLM the generated question and its five options with no access to the narrations, and discard any QAW it answers correctly anyway, since that means the question can be solved from surface language alone. The authors note this is deliberately precision-over-recall — "it is possible that a question is guessed correctly through chance and is not necessarily ungrounded" — but they accept discarding some good questions to keep the surviving set clean. A companion "No-Q" baseline (guess the answer from narrations but without seeing the question) was found to score near-random (~20%) and was dropped from the final pipeline.

Stage IV is two rounds of human annotation. Round one checks three conditions per QAW — the question is well-formed and the marked answer correct, the four distractors are genuinely wrong, and the temporal certificate for answering is at least 30 seconds — discarding anything that fails, which cuts the admissible set by roughly 4–5×. Round two re-verifies; the authors report that more than 97% of questions passing round one also pass round two. The datasheet in the supplementary material puts the final count at 5,063 instances, each one video clip, one question, and five options, released in full — video, text, and answer key — under the Ego4D license.

certificate length = Σ (lengths of the minimal necessary-and-sufficient sub-clips) // the minimum total sub-clip time a human verifier must watch to confirm the marked answer is correct — not the clip's raw duration

To make certificate length comparable across very different tasks, the authors also introduce meta-rules — implicit dataset-wide conventions the verifier is allowed to assume (e.g., action classes in Kinetics-400 are mutually exclusive), which keep the certificate from ballooning to the whole clip just to rule out irrelevant alternatives — plus two conventions: a 0.1-second minimum per certificate, and merging two sub-clips into one if their nearest ends are under 5 seconds apart.

// what they found

The results

Applying the certificate metric to 15 video datasets spanning action classification, detection, relationship classification, concept classification, video classification, and question answering, the authors find EgoSchema's median certificate to be about 100 seconds — the abstract reports this as 5.7× longer than the second-longest-certificate dataset and 10× to 100× longer than any other video-understanding dataset, while the introduction and conclusion instead put the second figure at 25× to 100× longer than all other datasets (the paper is internally inconsistent on this exact multiplier — see the eval read below).

On the actual QA task, the picture is stark. Random guessing among five options scores 20.0%. The strongest models tested top out well short of even a third correct:

Human performance is the other half of the picture, benchmarked under several conditions:

Two details stand out. Extending the time budget from one minute to three barely moves accuracy (+1.0 point), suggesting most of the achievable human signal is available almost immediately. And the most constrained setting — watch the video once, then answer without the text visible and without re-watching — actually beats the unconstrained setting (76.2% vs. 75.0%), which the authors hypothesize is because splitting attention between reading and watching hurts more than it helps. The headline gap the paper leads with — humans ~76% vs. models <33% — is the clearest statement of how far zero-shot long-form video-language understanding lagged behind human performance in mid-2023.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

The temporal-certificate idea is a genuinely useful conceptual contribution — it gives the field a way to argue about "how long-form is this task" with a measurement instead of an assumption about clip duration. But the benchmark built on top of it has several rigor gaps worth naming plainly.

No contamination safeguard at all. The paper's own datasheet states the "entirety of the dataset will be made publicly available" — video, question, five options, and the correct-answer label — under the Ego4D license, with no held-out private split and no canary string anywhere in the paper. For a benchmark that has since become a standard line item in multimodal model cards, a fully public answer key with no contamination mitigation is a real long-run liability: any post-2023 web crawl that touches egoschema.github.io or its GitHub mirror can, in principle, absorb the answer key directly.

The headline multiplier is internally inconsistent. The abstract states EgoSchema's certificate length is "10× to 100× longer than any other video understanding dataset," while the introduction and the conclusion both instead state "25× to 100× longer than all other existing video understanding datasets." These aren't the same claim, and the paper never reconciles them — a small thing, but it undercuts trust in a number the whole benchmark is named for.

The generation pipeline is LLM-shaped by construction. Every question and every distractor in EgoSchema was produced by GPT-4, Bard, or Claude conditioned on Ego4D narrations, then filtered by another LLM's blind-guessing behavior. That means the benchmark's notion of "a good hard distractor" is bounded by what 2023-era LLMs found plausible to write and what they failed to blindly detect — a very different sampling process from a benchmark whose distractors come from real human confusions (e.g., an exam-writer's exam), and one that could bake in generator-specific stylistic artifacts the human curation step wasn't designed to catch.

The human baseline is thin and not obviously independent. The reported human numbers are drawn from 5 hours of video for most settings and 9 hours for the unconstrained condition — on the order of 100–180 questions each — a modest sample for a number ("humans ≈ 76%") that anchors the entire headline comparison, and the paper does not report who the annotators were relative to the pool that curated the dataset, so independence from the curation process isn't established.

baselines

All four evaluated models (VIOLET, FrozenBiLM, mPLUG-Owl, InternVideo) predate the paper by well under a year and are all pre-GPT-4V/Gemini-era; there is no frontier long-context video-language model in the comparison because none suitable existed yet at submission time.

contamination

Not addressed anywhere in the paper. The full dataset — video, question, options, and answer key — ships publicly with no private held-out split and no canary string.

what's missed

An oracle/frame-budget ceiling analysis (how much of the 100%-accuracy gap to humans is attention vs. representation vs. temporal reasoning); independence of the human-eval annotator pool from the curation team; a reconciliation of the 10–100× vs. 25–100× certificate claims.

would raise confidence

A canary string plus a private held-out split as the benchmark ages; a larger, clearly-independent human-eval sample; and a re-run against post-2023 long-context video LLMs to see whether the <33% ceiling from 2023-era models has actually moved.

// bottom line

The temporal-certificate framework is the paper's most durable idea — a clean, general way to measure whether a "long video" task is actually long. EgoSchema itself is a legitimately hard, well-motivated diagnostic at the time of release (humans ~76%, best model <33%), but its LLM-generated-and-LLM-filtered construction, fully public answer key with no contamination guard, and thin, not-clearly-independent human baseline are the kind of gaps that matter more the longer a benchmark stays in circulation.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. Clip length isn't task length. The paper's temporal certificate — how much video a verifier must actually watch to confirm an answer — reframes "long-form video understanding" as a measurable property instead of an assumption, and shows prior "long" benchmarks often have certificates only seconds long.
  2. A wide gap, at least in 2023. On EgoSchema's 5,063 three-minute clips, the best zero-shot video-language models scored under 33% (random is 20%) while humans reached about 76% — the headline number that made EgoSchema a fixture in long-video model cards.
  3. Built by chaining LLMs to generate and filter its own questions (GPT-4, Bard, Claude for generation; blind LLM filtering for grounding), then human-curated in two rounds — and shipped with a fully public answer key and no contamination safeguard, a real risk for a benchmark still cited in 2026-era model cards.
