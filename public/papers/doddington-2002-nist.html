2002 · HLT · Benchmarks & Evals

Automatic Evaluation of Machine Translation Quality Using N-gram Co-occurrence Statistics

BLEU multiplies a geometric mean of n-gram precisions by a brevity penalty and stops there — every matched n-gram, common or rare, counts the same. The NIST metric keeps BLEU's n-gram-overlap skeleton but sums co-occurrence counts arithmetically instead of geometrically, weights each n-gram by how informative it is (rare, unpredictable continuations count for more than common ones), and softens the brevity penalty. On the same DARPA MT corpora BLEU itself was validated on, the result correlates better with human Adequacy judgments and is measurably more stable across documents and reference sets.

George Doddington · National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST)

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

At the July 2001 TIDES PI meeting, IBM presented an "evaluation understudy" — a way to score machine translation automatically by comparing MT output against reference translations in terms of shared word n-grams, later named BLEU. IBM showed it correlated strongly with human judgments of translation quality. DARPA, which needed a cheap forcing function for its TIDES MT research program, commissioned NIST to turn that idea into a production evaluation facility. This paper is NIST's technical writeup of that facility — and, in the process of reproducing IBM's result, Doddington identifies two specific mechanical weaknesses in BLEU's formulation worth fixing before shipping it as the government's standard metric:

  • The geometric mean is brittle. BLEU combines n-gram precisions for N=1–4 with a geometric mean, which makes the score equally sensitive to proportional differences in co-occurrence at every N. A single low count at a higher N — where matches are naturally sparser — can swing the whole score in a way that has nothing to do with translation quality.
  • Every n-gram counts the same. BLEU credits a match on "the" identically to a match on a rare technical phrase, even though the rare phrase is far stronger evidence that the translation actually got the content right. Uniform weighting also makes the score easier to game: filler n-grams that are likely to co-occur by chance add just as much score as n-grams that could only match by genuinely translating the source.

Both gaps point the same direction: score co-occurrence in a way that (a) doesn't let one N dominate through geometric averaging, and (b) rewards n-grams in proportion to how much information they actually carry. That's the NIST scoring formula.

// how it works

The method

NIST keeps BLEU's basic shape — score a candidate translation against one or more reference translations by counting shared word n-grams, then apply a brevity penalty — but changes three things: the average, the weight per n-gram, and the brevity curve.

1 · Arithmetic sum, not geometric mean

Instead of a geometric mean of precisions for N=1–4 (BLEU), NIST sums co-occurrence scores arithmetically across N=1–5. A weak or zero count at one N no longer has the outsized, multiplicative effect a geometric mean gives it.

2 · Weight n-grams by how informative they are

The core idea: an n-gram that's hard to predict from its own prefix is more informative — and more convincing evidence of a correct translation — than one that's nearly guaranteed once the first n−1 words are seen. NIST estimates that information value directly from n-gram counts over the reference translations:

Info(w1…wn) = log2count(w1…wn−1)count(w1…wn) // Eqn 2 — how much less predictable wₙ made the n-gram, given its prefix; rare continuations score high, near-certain ones score near 0

A word that almost always follows its prefix contributes almost nothing; a word that rarely does contributes a lot. This directly rewards genuinely-translated content over gameable, high-frequency filler.

3 · The NIST score

Score = Σn=1N Σ Info(w1…wn) over co-occurring n-gramsΣ 1 over all n-grams in system output  · exp{β·log2[min(Lsys/L̄ref, 1)]} // Eqn 3 — information-weighted co-occurrence, summed over N=1–5, times a brevity penalty; β is set so the penalty = 0.5 exactly when the system output is 2/3 the average reference length

Lsys is the number of words in the translation being scored and L̄ref the average reference length. That β-calibration is the third change: NIST's brevity penalty is deliberately gentler than BLEU's near the target length, to avoid over-punishing small, harmless length variation while still discouraging degenerate short outputs.

// what they found

The results

Doddington evaluates on four DARPA MT corpora: 1994 French→English, Japanese→English, and Spanish→English (100 documents each, 4–5 commercial systems, 2 human references), plus the 2001 Chinese→English "dry run" (80 documents, 6 commercial systems and 7 professional translators, up to 8 references). Two measures do the work throughout: correlation with human Adequacy/Fluency/Informativeness judgments, and the F-ratio — between-system score variance divided by within-system (document-to-document) variance — as a measure of how reliably a score separates systems from noise.

Information weighting clearly helps at N=1 (F-ratio 98.6 → 149.2), is roughly a wash at N=2, and is worse on both F-ratio and Adequacy correlation for N≥3 — which the paper attributes to unreliable likelihood estimates for higher-order n-grams from a reference corpus that's simply too small (a follow-up experiment using a much larger auxiliary corpus, TDT2/TDT3, produced only "equivocal" improvement).

The combined NIST score vs. BLEU

Put together (arithmetic sum over N=1–5, information weighting, and the recalibrated brevity penalty), the NIST score beats BLEU's F-ratio on every one of the four corpora studied, and — for Adequacy — correlates better with human judgment on every corpus too. The Fluency picture is weaker: NIST only out-correlates BLEU on Fluency for the Chinese corpus; on the 1994 French, Japanese, and Spanish corpora BLEU's Fluency correlation is as good or better.

Secondary ablations reinforce that the score is robust rather than fragile: correlation degrades "only slightly" even scoring one segment per whole document (271 words on average) instead of per-sentence; going beyond a single reference translation yields only modest further gains in correlation; and stripping case information barely moves either F-ratio or correlation.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

Doddington's two diagnoses of BLEU — a geometric mean is brittle across N, and uniform n-gram weighting is gameable and uninformative — are sound engineering observations, and NIST's fixes are simple, well-motivated, and (mostly) validated on exactly the corpora BLEU itself was built on. But the paper is also candid enough, in its own tables, to undercut its own headline story if you read past the prose.

The "information weighting works" claim is the paper's centerpiece, yet Table 4 shows it clearly helping only at N=1 (F-ratio 98.6 → 149.2): at N=2 it's roughly a wash, and at N=3–5 it's worse on both F-ratio and Adequacy correlation than uniform weighting. The paper attributes this to poor likelihood estimation from a reference corpus too small to estimate higher-order n-gram statistics reliably — a reasonable explanation, but one that means the combined NIST score's improvement over BLEU is driven substantially by the arithmetic-sum/brevity-penalty changes and the unigram weighting, not by "information weighting" as a general principle across all N as the framing implies.

The correlation win is also narrower than the framing suggests. NIST beats BLEU on Adequacy correlation across all four corpora, but on Fluency it only wins on Chinese — the paper states this plainly ("For Fluency judgments, however, the NIST score correlates better than the BLEU score only on the Chinese corpus") but the surrounding narrative still describes NIST as offering "significant improvement in score stability and reliability" without foregrounding that split result. And every comparison here runs on 4–7 systems per corpus with no significance testing on the reported deltas — a Pearson-style correlation gap on that few points, reported to a tenth of a percent, deserves more scrutiny than a bare table gives it.

baselines

The one comparison offered — BLEU itself, on the same four DARPA corpora BLEU was validated on — is the right comparison to make, but it's the only one; there's no comparison to any non-n-gram alternative, and no significance test on any of the reported F-ratio or correlation deltas across just 4–7 systems.

contamination

No learned model, so no train/test leakage in the modern sense. The closer analogue: n-gram information weights are estimated from the same reference-translation corpus used for scoring, a self-referential estimate the paper concedes needs "a much larger corpus" to do well — an auxiliary TDT2/TDT3 corpus experiment gave only "equivocal" results.

what's missed

Still pure surface n-gram co-occurrence — no alignment, no stemming, no synonymy, no semantics. Reweighting which n-grams count more doesn't change what counts: a fluent paraphrase that shares no reference n-grams still scores near zero. Table 4's own numbers show the "informative n-grams" idea working reliably only at the unigram level.

would raise confidence

More MT systems per language pair (n ≫ 6–7) with proper significance tests on the F-ratio/correlation deltas; information weights estimated on a held-out corpus large enough to make higher-order estimates reliable; and evaluation beyond Chinese/French/Japanese/Spanish→English into more language pairs and text genres.

// bottom line

The NIST score correctly targets two real mechanical weaknesses in BLEU and fixes them with a straightforward reweighting that measurably improves score stability and Adequacy correlation on BLEU's own validation corpora — enough that NIST adopted it as the U.S. government's standard automatic MT metric for the TIDES program for years. But it's a tuning of BLEU's arithmetic, not a rethink of its premise: it never leaves surface n-gram matching, its own ablation shows the "informative n-grams" idea working reliably only at N=1, and it inherits BLEU's blindness to paraphrase and meaning. Three years later, METEOR attacked that premise directly — explicit word alignment, stemming, synonymy, and recall — and named NIST (alongside BLEU) as one of the baselines it had to beat; two decades on, learned and LLM-judge metrics (BERTScore, BLEURT, G-Eval) replaced the n-gram-counting lineage almost entirely. NIST's real legacy is narrower and more durable than the score itself: it's the clearest early demonstration that an automatic metric's exact arithmetic — mean vs. sum, uniform vs. weighted — is not a footnote but a first-order design choice.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. Not every n-gram is equally good evidence. NIST's information weight — log₂ of how much less predictable a word made its n-gram, estimated from reference-corpus counts — directly attacks BLEU's blind spot of crediting a match on "the" the same as a match on a rare, meaning-bearing phrase.
  2. The averaging rule is a first-order design choice, not a footnote. Swapping BLEU's geometric mean over N=1–4 for an arithmetic sum over N=1–5, plus a brevity penalty recalibrated to be gentler near the target length, measurably raised F-ratio and Adequacy correlation on the same DARPA corpora BLEU was validated on.
  3. Read the ablation table, not just the headline. The paper's own Table 4 shows information weighting clearly helping only at N=1 and hurting at N≥3 — a limitation the authors attribute to noisy higher-order estimates, and one that later metrics (METEOR's explicit alignment, then learned and LLM-judge scorers) sidestepped by leaving n-gram counting behind rather than reweighting it.
