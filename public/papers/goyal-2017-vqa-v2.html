Making the V in VQA Matter: Elevating the Role of Image Understanding in Visual Question Answering

VQA v1 let a question-only model score 48.21% by pattern-matching the question and ignoring the picture. VQA v2 pairs every question with two visually similar images that get two different answers — nearly doubling the dataset to ~1.1M (image, question) pairs and forcing every model that wants a high score to actually look.

Yash Goyal, Tejas Khot, Douglas Summers-Stay, Dhruv Batra, Devi Parikh

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

The original VQA dataset (Antol et al., 2015) gave every question exactly one image. That single-image design leaves an escape hatch: language alone is a much easier signal to fit than vision, so a model can score well by memorizing which answers go with which question n-grams and never looking at the picture. The authors quantify this precisely. In VQA v1, "tennis" is the correct answer for 41% of questions starting "What sport is," "2" is correct for 39% of "How many" questions, "yes" is correct 98% of the time for questions starting "Is there a clock," and "no" is correct 69% of the time for "Is the man standing" questions. Most strikingly, for the n-gram "Do you see a …", blindly answering "yes" without reading the rest of the question or looking at the image at all reaches 87% accuracy. Zhang et al. had separately identified a "visual priming bias": because annotators wrote questions while looking at the image, the questions themselves leak information about what's in the scene (nobody asks "is there a clock tower?" of a beach photo).

These aren't cosmetic quirks — they're a validity problem for the whole benchmark. A leaderboard that state-of-the-art VQA models climb by exploiting language priors gives a false signal of progress in image understanding, the thing VQA was built to measure. Reported accuracies improve, but it's not clear the underlying vision is getting any better. The paper's stated goal is blunt: force the "V" (visual) back into VQA, by constructing a dataset where language priors alone are provably insufficient to answer correctly.

// how it works

The method

The construction is deliberately simple and per-question, not just per-dataset. For every (image, question, answer) triplet (I, Q, A) already in VQA v1, the goal is to find a second image I′ that is visually similar to I but for which the answer to the same question Q becomes a different A′. Similarity is computed in VGGNet fc7 embedding space with ℓ2 distance, and each original image's 24 nearest neighbors become the candidate pool. A custom Amazon Mechanical Turk interface (Fig. 3 below) shows a worker the question, the original answer, and the 24 candidates, and asks them to pick a neighbor for which the question still "makes sense" (any premise the question assumes — e.g. that a woman is visible for "what is the woman doing?" — must hold) and whose true answer is not A. If none of the 24 qualifies, the worker can mark the item "not possible" rather than force a bad match.

Picking the complementary image is only stage one. A second round of 10 fresh AMT workers is then shown the picked image I′ with the same question Q and asked to answer it from scratch — exactly the VQA v1 annotation protocol — and the majority vote becomes A′. This two-stage design matters for the eval-rigor question later in this page: the "different answer" property isn't asserted by the worker who picked the image, it's independently re-verified by a disjoint set of annotators who never saw the original answer.

Crucially, the authors are explicit that this is not the same as flattening the marginal answer distribution P(A). Language models don't exploit P(A) directly — they exploit the conditional P(A | Q), the correlation between question wording and answer. Balancing per-question, so that the same Q literally has two different correct answers across the dataset, directly attacks P(A|Q): a language-only model has no basis whatsoever for telling (Q, I) apart from (Q, I′), and by construction must get at least one of the pair wrong.

The candidate pool matters: because it's capped at 24 VGG-nearest-neighbors, some questions simply have no valid complementary image among them (rare objects, or objects too small in the frame to register in a global fc7 embedding). Workers picked "not possible" for about 135K questions — 22% of the VQA v1 question set. The paper's own accounting of these misses ("what color is the banana?" needing a non-yellow banana that plausibly doesn't exist nearby in COCO) is one of the load-bearing details for judging how complete the balancing really is — see "does it hold up?" below.

// what they found

The results

Scale first: the complete balanced dataset (VQA v2.0) contains ~1.1 million (image, question) pairs — almost double VQA v1's 614K questions — with ~13 million answers collected over the same ~200K COCO images. Concretely: 443K train, 214K val and 453K test (image, question) pairs, built from ~195K/93K/191K freshly-collected complementary images per split plus ~18K extra test pairs added purely to help detect anomalous submission behavior. Balancing worked as intended on the answer distribution: averaged across question types (weighted by frequency), the entropy of the answer distribution rises 56% after balancing — binary ("is the", "is this", "is there", "are", "does") questions move toward an even yes/no split, and "tennis"/"baseball" and "2" dominate far less among sport and count answers respectively (Figure 4).

The headline benchmarking result: every 2016-era state-of-the-art VQA model gets noticeably worse when the exact same model, trained on the original unbalanced training set, is evaluated on the new balanced validation set (UU→UB in the table below) instead of the original unbalanced validation set it was tuned against. The Prior baseline (always answer the training set's most common answer, "yes") drops only modestly, 27.38→24.04, but the language-only ablation — same architecture as the base VQA model but with the image blacked out — falls from 48.21% to 41.40%. Every real model shows the same pattern: d-LSTM+n-I 54.40→47.56, HieCoAtt 57.09→50.31, and the 2016 VQA Challenge winner MCB 60.36→54.22. Breaking accuracy out by answer type, the "yes/no" category — the type most exploitable by priors — drops hardest: ~10.8 points for MCB and ~12.4 points for HieCoAtt going from unbalanced to balanced evaluation.

Retraining on the balanced set recovers some of that loss and adds a data-scale finding: models trained on a balanced set the same size as the original (BhalfB) already improve over UB, and training on the full ~2×-sized balanced set (BB) improves accuracy a further 2–3 points over BhalfB — evidence, the authors argue, that these models were data-starved rather than architecturally maxed out. A pair-level analysis of HieCoAtt is the sharpest single number in the paper: trained on the unbalanced set, the model answers both images in a complementary pair correctly only 13.5% of the time (and gives the identical prediction to both images — right or wrong — 59.9% of the time, i.e. it mostly can't tell the pair apart at all). Trained on the balanced set, both-correct rises to 17.7% (a 4.2-point increase) and identical-prediction rate falls 10.5 points to 49.4% — direct evidence the model has started using the image to distinguish two otherwise-similar scenes, even though the large majority of pairs still aren't both answered correctly.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

The central question for a "de-biasing" benchmark is whether it removes the shortcut or just makes it more expensive to exploit — and the paper's own numbers say it's the latter. The Prior baseline (always answer the training set's most frequent label) still scores 24.04% on the balanced val set, not near-chance; "yes" remains the single most common answer in both datasets. The authors are explicit about this themselves: the balanced set is "not perfectly balanced," only "significantly more balanced" than the original. That's an honest framing, but it means VQA v2 reduces the exploitability of language priors rather than eliminating them — a strong enough model can still bank real points from answer-frequency alone before touching the image.

The construction also introduces a source of residual bias the paper doesn't stress-test: complementary images are drawn only from each image's 24 nearest neighbors in VGG-fc7 space. That candidate pool is itself a learned representation with its own biases (VGG's training distribution, COCO's photographic conventions), so "similar according to VGG" is not the same as "similar in every way a model might exploit" — a sufficiently capable model could in principle still find scene-level or stylistic correlates within that 24-neighbor pool that survive the per-question answer swap. The paper doesn't test for this. Two more numbers temper the "balanced" claim further: workers marked 22% of questions "not possible" — concentrated, by the authors' own qualitative account, on rare or small objects, meaning the balancing is weakest exactly where fine-grained visual understanding matters most — and after independent re-annotation, about 9% of "complementary" pairs turned out to share the same answer anyway (A = A′), a rate the paper reports but does not filter out of the released dataset.

On the modeling side, the five-model comparison (Prior, language-only, d-LSTM+n-I, HieCoAtt, MCB) was state-of-the-art in 2016 and reuses each model's own public code plus the actual VQA Challenge 2016 winner — a fair, unmanipulated comparison for its era. But it's now a decade-old model lineup, and the paper can't and doesn't test whether the drop it measures generalizes to the attention/transformer-era architectures (several of which, per the citation graph, cite this very paper — e.g. Bottom-Up-Top-Down attention the following year) that substantially closed the accuracy gap it documents. That leaves an open question the paper can't answer from its own data: how much of the "language-prior exploitation" story is a permanent property of VQA as a task, versus a property of 2016-generation shallow fusion architectures specifically.

baselines

Fair for 2016 — reuses each model's original public code, including the actual VQA Real Open-Ended Challenge 2016 winner (MCB) — but the lineup is now a decade old; no modern attention/transformer VLM is tested against the balanced split in this paper.

contamination

Not train/test leakage in the LLM sense (pre-dates that concern), but a structural one: complementary images are mined from the same VGG-fc7 embedding space used across train/val/test, so any bias baked into that representation is shared across all three splits rather than independently sourced.

what's missed

The paper doesn't separate "model learned a genuine shortcut" from "model was never required to resolve fine-grained visual differences" — the reported 2–3 point gain from simply doubling the balanced training data (BhalfB → BB) suggests scale, not just debiasing, is doing real work, which softens the pure "language-bias" narrative.

would raise confidence

A systematic (not anecdotal) breakdown of the 22% "not possible" and 9% same-answer cases by question/answer type; a test of whether the 24-NN candidate pool itself carries an exploitable residual prior; and a re-run of the UU→UB drop with a modern VLM to see whether the gap has closed or simply moved.

// bottom line

A genuine methodological advance — moving from "balance the answer marginal" to "balance per question" is the right level to attack language priors, and the pair-level HieCoAtt analysis (13.5%→17.7% both-correct) is convincing direct evidence models started using the image. But "balanced" here means measurably less exploitable, not unbiased: a quarter of questions had no valid complementary image at all, a non-trivial fraction of the pairs that were collected didn't actually differ on reannotation, and the underlying VQA accuracy metric — with its answer-vocabulary cap and exact-string matching — carried over unchanged. Read VQA v2's accuracy drop as a real signal that 2016-era models were gaming priors, not as proof the dataset itself is prior-free.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. VQA v2 pairs every question with two visually similar images that get two different answers, growing VQA v1's ~614K questions to ~1.1M (image, question) pairs and ~13M answers over the same ~200K COCO images — and, by construction, making it impossible for a language-only model to be right on both members of a pair.
  2. Every 2016-era state-of-the-art model dropped when evaluated on the balanced set instead of the original: language-only fell 48.21%→41.40%, and the strongest model (MCB) fell 60.36%→54.22%, with the "yes/no" answer type losing the most (~11–12 points) — direct empirical confirmation that those models had been exploiting language priors, not just understanding images better.
  3. "Balanced" is a matter of degree, not a solved problem: the Prior baseline still scores 24.04% on the new set, 22% of questions had no valid complementary image among the 24-nearest-neighbor candidates, and ~9% of collected pairs turned out to share the same answer on reannotation — real residual bias worth tracking in anything built on top of VQA v2.
