BetterBench: Assessing AI Benchmarks, Uncovering Issues, and Establishing Best Practices

A 46-criteria audit of the benchmark lifecycle — design, implementation, documentation, maintenance — turns up large, measurable quality gaps between benchmarks the field treats as interchangeable, like MMLU and GPQA.

Anka Reuel, Amelia Hardy, Chandler Smith, Max Lamparth, Malcolm Hardy, Mykel J. Kochenderfer

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

Every model card leans on the same move: report a number on MMLU, another on GSM8K, another on HellaSwag, and let the reader treat all three as equally trustworthy signals of capability. BetterBench's authors start from a simple observation: nothing forces that trust to be earned. Following Raji et al.'s definition, they treat a benchmark as "a particular combination of a dataset or sets of datasets [...], and a metric, conceptualized as representing one or more specific tasks or sets of abilities, picked up by a community of researchers as a shared framework for the comparison of methods." Under that definition, a benchmark is infrastructure — and infrastructure can be built well or badly, documented or undocumented, reproducible or not. The paper's claim is that, as of late 2024, no structured assessment framework existed to tell the two apart, for either foundation-model (FM) benchmarks or non-FM benchmarks, and no comparative study had been run across the two categories.

That absence has consequences beyond academic tidiness. Model developers cite whichever benchmarks flatter their release, without disclosing that some of those benchmarks are far less rigorously constructed than others. The paper's own discussion section documents this directly: recent model reports from GPT-4, Claude-3, and Gemini all report both MMLU and GPQA scores side by side, with no acknowledgment that (as the authors' own assessment later shows) the two differ enormously in documented quality. Meanwhile, regulators are starting to lean on benchmark scores as compliance evidence — the UK AI Safety Institute's Inspect framework bundles MMLU and GPQA together as evaluation tools, and the EU AI Act's Article 51 treats benchmark-based evaluation as a route to demonstrating systemic-risk compliance for general-purpose AI models. If benchmark quality is invisible to the people using benchmark scores to make decisions, the whole evaluation ecosystem is one of unlabeled instruments.

Prior work had chipped at pieces of this problem without building the actual measuring stick. Some papers (McIntosh et al.; Liao and Xiao) diagnose limitations of specific benchmarks or narrow scoring paradigms, but stop at description rather than offering developers a practical tool to act on. Others (Narayanan and Kapoor's "Evaluating LLMs is a minefield," Ott et al. on benchmark saturation) raise structural concerns about evaluation validity without proposing a scorable framework. Adjacent fields have their own best-practice literatures — data cards and FAIR principles for dataset documentation, benchmarking guidelines in bioinformatics, transistor hardware, and environmental science — but none of these extend to the AI-specific infrastructure concerns (API access, contamination protections, statistical significance reporting) that make an AI benchmark trustworthy or not. BetterBench's contribution is to synthesize all of this into one thing benchmark developers, model developers, model users, AI researchers, and regulators can actually apply: a scored checklist, run against 24 real benchmarks, that turns "this benchmark feels sloppier than that one" into a number.

// how it works

The method

The authors model a benchmark's life as five stages — design, implementation, documentation, maintenance, and retirement — and derive their assessment criteria stage by stage (Fig. 1). Design covers defining the tested capability, its tasks, datasets, and metrics; implementation covers actually building the benchmark and protecting it against contamination and gaming; documentation covers describing all of the above so others can use and scrutinize it; maintenance covers keeping it usable and current after release. Retirement — communicating a sunset plan and archiving data, code, and docs once a benchmark saturates or loses relevance — is acknowledged as part of the lifecycle but was left out of scoring, since the authors "cannot assess the retirement of active benchmarks" that haven't retired yet.

The 46 criteria themselves came from a two-part process: a literature review of benchmarking practices both within AI and in other empirical fields, plus unstructured interviews with more than 20 people spanning five stakeholder groups — benchmark developers, model developers, model users, AI researchers, and regulators/standard-setting organizations (detailed in App. A). Each interview surfaced what that stakeholder actually needs from a benchmark assessment; those needs were translated into concrete, checkable criteria. Critically, the authors triage the resulting list into three buckets: (a) criteria fully controlled by the benchmark developer, where the authors and interviewees reached a "normative consensus" — these are the ones actually scored; (b) developer-controlled but context-dependent criteria that are hard for an outside party to judge, discussed qualitatively as design considerations (Sec. 5: general vs. specific scope, detecting small improvements, static vs. dynamic design, positionality statements); and (c) considerations outside the developer's control or requiring further research (contamination enforcement, construct validity, standardized reporting — Sec. 8's "open challenges"). Only bucket (a) — the criteria a developer can unilaterally fix — makes it into the scored framework, which the authors frame as a deliberate choice to keep the assessment actionable rather than aspirational.

Design contributes 14 of the scored criteria (Fig. 2 shows all 14 — is the tested capability defined? are domain experts and literature involved? are floors, ceilings, and human/random performance levels reported?). Implementation contributes 11 (is evaluation code available, is there a canary-string-style training_on_test_set task, is statistical significance reported?). Documentation contributes 19 (requirements files, quick-start guides, documented assumptions and limitations, standardized dataset metadata). Maintenance contributes 3 (was the code checked in the last year, is there a feedback channel, is a contact person listed?). Each criterion is scored on a discrete 0/5/10/15 scale — 0 for "not acknowledged and not addressed," 5 for "acknowledged but not addressed," 10 for "partially addressed," 15 for "fully addressed," or n/a where a criterion doesn't apply — chosen over a continuous scale because, in the authors' words, "the difference between a score of 5 ... and 10 ... is easier to see than between a 2 and 3 on a narrower scale."

SU = nI·sI + nDo·sDo + nM·sMnI + nDo + nM // usability score: a weighted average of the implementation (s_I), documentation (s_Do), and maintenance (s_M) subscores, weighted by how many non-n/a criteria (n_I, n_Do, n_M) each stage contributed for that benchmark. Design score S_D is just the design subscore s_D on its own.

Two authors independently scored every one of the 24 benchmarks against all 46 criteria — reconciling disagreements through discussion, with a third reviewer available to break ties (a case that, per the paper, never actually arose). Before that full run, three authors independently scored the same benchmark up front purely to calibrate the scoring guidelines and catch ambiguous wording in the criteria. The 24 benchmarks themselves — 16 targeting foundation models (MMLU, GPQA, BIG-bench, HellaSwag, AgentBench, DecodingTrust, GSM8k, ARC Challenge, BBQ, BOLD, Codex HumanEval, MLCommons AI Safety v0.5, MMMU, TruthfulQA, WinoGrande, and Machiavelli) and 8 targeting non-foundation models (ALE, FinRL-Meta, MedMNIST v2, PDEBench, Procgen, RL Unplugged, SafeBench, and Wordcraft) — were chosen for being commonly used or recently reported by major model developers, evaluated from official websites, papers, and GitHub repositories (the authors explicitly exclude any "third-party information that was not released by the benchmark developers themselves"). The whole assessment is published as a living, continuously updated repository at betterbench.stanford.edu under a CC BY 4.0 license, with a community feedback channel for submitting new benchmarks or correcting scores — and, separately, the same 46 criteria are repackaged as a pre-deployment checklist (App. I) for developers building a new benchmark from scratch.

// what they found

The results

Averaged across all 24 benchmarks, the overall design score comes out to 10.7 (on the 0–15 scale) and the weighted usability score to 8.7 — meaning benchmarks are, on average, better at stating what they test than at being easy to actually run, replicate, and maintain. Breaking usability into its three component stages makes the gap stark (Table 1, Fig. 6): implementation is by far the weakest lifecycle stage for both FM and non-FM benchmarks, averaging just 6.2 across all 24, versus 10.8 for design, 10.1 for documentation, and 9.6 for maintenance. The implementation box plot in Fig. 6 shows this isn't just a low mean — it's a genuinely wide, right-skewed spread, from benchmarks scoring near 0 up to a ceiling near 10, while design and documentation cluster much more tightly in the 7–14 range. Maintenance is the most bimodal category of all, with scores piling up at both 0 and 15 — benchmarks tend to either actively maintain their repository or almost entirely abandon it, with little in between.

Naming names makes the spread concrete (Fig. 7, plotting design score against usability score for all 24 benchmarks). MMLU — arguably the single most-cited FM benchmark in the field — scored the lowest weighted average of all 24 at 5.5, while GPQA scored 11.0, roughly double. HellaSwag, BOLD, and MMMU cluster in the same low-usability region as MMLU; DecodingTrust, PDEBench, GPQA, and AgentBench sit in the high-design, high-usability quadrant. The authors verify with a Pearson correlation test (Table 2) that design and usability scores are meaningfully related — benchmarks with strong design tend to also be well-implemented, documented, and maintained, and vice versa — significant for FM benchmarks alone (ρ=0.730, p=0.001) and for all 24 combined (ρ=0.693, p<0.001), though not reaching significance for the 8 non-FM benchmarks scored alone (ρ=0.477, p=0.279). A separate bootstrap-resampling test (Fig. 8, App. D) finds no statistically significant difference between FM and non-FM benchmarks' mean design or usability scores at the 95% confidence level — despite the visibly different implementation and maintenance means in Table 1, the confidence region for that difference straddles zero.

Two specific findings stand out as the paper's sharpest indictments of current practice. First, on statistical rigor: 14 of the 24 assessed benchmarks do not report statistical significance or run their evaluation multiple times (different seeds or sampling temperatures) to distinguish genuine model differences from noise — the average score across all benchmarks on the "reporting statistical significance" criterion is just 5.62 out of 15. Second, on reproducibility: 17 of 24 benchmarks provide no easy-to-run script to replicate the results in their original paper, and a further 4 only support replicating part of the results — leaving just 3 of 24 with full, easy replication support, for an average score of 3.75 out of 15 on that criterion. A related engineering signal, whether a benchmark's GitHub repository has a passing build/CI status, is met by only 3 of the 24 benchmarks. None of this is exotic infrastructure — the authors note explicitly that "small changes can lead to significant improvements": adding code documentation or a point of contact is neither difficult nor time-consuming, yet most benchmarks in the sample skip it.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

BetterBench is itself an evaluation-methodology paper — a benchmark for benchmarks — which makes it fair (and, per the authors' own checklist criteria on documenting limitations, expected) to point the paper's own lens back at itself. The authors do a genuinely unusual and commendable thing here: Appendix I.2 runs their own 46-criteria checklist against BetterBench, as a self-administered proof-of-concept. That self-audit surfaces exactly the kind of edge case that should make a reader want independent validation. Three design criteria come back "N/A" with justifications that are themselves debatable calls: "Human performance level is included" is marked N/A because "a human could not be used as an evaluation target in our context"; "Random performance level is included" is N/A because "random generation cannot constitute an AI benchmark"; and — most tellingly — "Automatic evaluation is possible and validated" is marked N/A with the justification "we manually evaluate all benchmarks," since the source material (websites, papers, GitHub repos) "does not follow a standard structure." In other words, the one criterion that would ask whether BetterBench's own scoring process is validated against an automatic, checkable ground truth is waved off as inapplicable to itself — a comfortable place to land for a framework whose entire scoring pipeline runs on two humans' judgment calls.

That judgment-call dependency is the paper's central unresolved tension. The scoring rubric is coarse by design (0/5/10/15/n/a), and the authors say so plainly in their own limitations section: "a benchmark barely fulfilling a criterion and one almost entirely fulfilling it would receive the same 10-point score." Reliability rests on "two authors independently reviewed each benchmark" reaching consensus, with a third reviewer as tiebreaker — a mechanism the paper reports was never invoked across 24 benchmarks × 46 criteria (over a thousand individual scoring decisions). No inter-rater agreement statistic — a Cohen's kappa, a raw disagreement rate before reconciliation — is reported, so a reader has no way to independently gauge how tight or how negotiated that "always agreed" outcome actually was. A small, easily-overlooked detail reinforces the point: the four lifecycle figures (Figs. 2–5) enumerate 14 + 11 + 19 + 3 = 47 named criteria, one more than the "46 criteria" the abstract and Section 3 repeatedly cite — a tiny, harmless miscount, but also exactly the kind of documentation slip the framework itself would dock a benchmark 5–10 points for.

The deeper gap is one the authors name themselves rather than hide: this checklist measures process — is the benchmark documented, reproducible, maintained — not construct validity, whether the benchmark actually measures the capability it claims to. Section 9's limitations state this outright: "assessing the construct validity of a benchmark and determining whether its approach to assessing a concept is truly effective would presumably require in-depth analysis by domain experts in the respective fields, which is beyond the scope of this assessment." That's an honest scoping decision, but it means a benchmark can score full marks on all 46 criteria — beautifully documented, fully reproducible, actively maintained — while still measuring the wrong thing entirely, or nothing at all. Conversely a conceptually excellent but sparsely documented benchmark scores poorly. The 46-point score is therefore best read as a floor (a "minimum quality assurance," in the paper's own phrase for the checklist) rather than a ceiling on trust — a distinction easy to lose once a number like "MMLU: 5.5, GPQA: 11.0" starts circulating as shorthand for "which benchmark is better."

Sample composition is a related soft spot. The 24 benchmarks are not a random or stratified sample of the AI benchmarking landscape — they're the ones "commonly used" or "recently reported by model developers," which skews toward older, more established, more heavily cited benchmarks (MMLU dates to 2020, GSM8k and ARC Challenge earlier still). Practices the framework rewards — canary strings, encrypted eval sets, CI build badges — became common wisdom only gradually; scoring a 2020-era benchmark against 2024 best practices without any discussion of when a given practice became standard risks penalizing benchmarks for not anticipating norms that didn't exist yet. The non-FM group is also just 8 benchmarks, which is almost certainly why its design-usability correlation (ρ=0.477) misses significance (p=0.279) where the FM group's (n=16) doesn't — a sample-size story more than a genuine categorical difference, and one the paper's own bootstrap analysis (Fig. 8) implicitly concedes by finding no significant FM/non-FM gap at all.

Credit where due: the authors' own impact statement raises an equity concern most benchmark papers never touch. They note that criteria like "domain experts are involved" or "domain literature is integrated" — both scored as part of the 14-item design rubric — "may unequally impact researchers based on their access to resources and connections, potentially hindering the development of benchmarks from a broader range of research institutions and underrepresented communities." That's a real structural bias built into the scoring: a well-funded lab with an existing network of domain-expert collaborators can rack up design points that a smaller or less-connected team structurally cannot, regardless of how sound the underlying benchmark concept is. The paper flags this rather than pretending its 0/5/10/15 scale is institution-neutral, but flagging isn't correcting — nothing in the scoring itself down-weights or contextualizes a criterion for the teams least able to satisfy it, so a "low design score" and a "poorly conceived benchmark" remain conflated for exactly the benchmarks built with the fewest resources.

baselines

There's no empirical baseline to compare against — BetterBench is explicitly the first scored, cross-cutting assessment framework of its kind, and prior work (McIntosh et al.; Narayanan & Kapoor's "minefield" essay) only identifies limitations qualitatively rather than proposing a comparable score.

contamination

Scored as developer intent — does the benchmark include a canary string, a globally unique identifier, or a training_on_test_set task — not as measured outcome. A benchmark can score full marks here while still being contaminated in practice; the paper is explicit in Sec. 8 that enforcing this is outside any developer's unilateral control.

what's missed

Construct validity — whether a benchmark actually measures the capability it claims to — is explicitly out of scope (Sec. 9). Also unscored: inter-rater reliability numbers for the two-author scoring process, and any external check that a high BetterBench score predicts a trustworthy, replicable model ranking in practice.

would raise confidence

Reported Cohen's-kappa-style agreement across the 2-author scoring; a larger, less popularity-biased benchmark sample (the current 24 lean toward well-established, older benchmarks); and a follow-up study tying checklist score to a real downstream outcome — contamination incidents, retracted results, or leaderboard rank stability over time.

// bottom line

BetterBench doesn't prove that GPQA is a better benchmark than MMLU in any deep, construct-validity sense — it proves that GPQA is more thoroughly documented, reproducible, and maintained by the paper's own 46-point rubric, and says so with admirable candor about what the rubric can't yet tell you. That's a real and useful contribution: a shared vocabulary and a public, continuously-updated scoreboard where none existed before. Whether a high score on that scoreboard actually correlates with anything the field cares about — trustworthy rankings, less contamination, fewer retracted claims — is the open question the authors themselves flag, not one this paper answers.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. Benchmark quality is not uniform, and the field mostly reports scores as if it were — MMLU (weighted average 5.5, the lowest of 24) and GPQA (11.0) appear side by side, uncaveated, in GPT-4, Claude-3, and Gemini's own reports, and in the UK AI Safety Institute's Inspect evaluation framework.
  2. Implementation is the field's biggest blind spot: 17 of 24 benchmarks ship no working replication script, 14 of 24 never report statistical significance or run multiple seeds, and only 3 of 24 have a passing CI build status — none of which requires new research, just basic software-engineering hygiene benchmark developers routinely skip.
  3. A checklist for rigor still needs its own rigor-check: BetterBench's 0/5/10/15 scores measure documented process (is it described, reproducible, maintained), explicitly not construct validity (does it measure the right thing), and the paper is candid that whether its score predicts anything about real-world trustworthiness is still an open question.
