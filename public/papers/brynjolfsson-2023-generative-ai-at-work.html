Generative AI at Work

The first study of a generative-AI tool deployed at scale in a real workplace. A GPT-based assistant lifts customer-support productivity 15% — and almost all of the gain goes to the least skilled workers, inverting decades of skill-biased technical change.

Erik Brynjolfsson · Danielle Li · Lindsey Raymond  |  Stanford & MIT & NBER

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

By early 2023, generative AI had dazzled in the lab — passing bar exams, writing code, acing benchmarks — but no one had measured what it actually does to work when you hand it to thousands of employees and let it run for months. The worry was that lab numbers wouldn't survive contact with reality: real jobs throw unfamiliar problems at you, models hallucinate in consequential settings, and a long history of decision-support tools shows that human-plus-AI often performs worse than either alone. A meta-analysis of 100+ studies the authors cite found exactly that.

There was a second, deeper question. Every earlier wave of computing — PCs, the internet, industrial robots — was skill-biased: it complemented the already-educated and widened wage gaps, because someone first had to codify the task into explicit instructions. Generative AI works the opposite way. It learns tacit know-how from examples of people doing a job well and badly, which means it might bottle up what the best workers do and hand it to everyone else. If so, AI could compress the skill distribution rather than stretch it — but that was a theory, not a measurement.

This paper is the first field evidence on both. It studies the staggered rollout of a generative-AI assistant to 5,172 customer-support agents at a Fortune 500 software firm — 3 million real chats, a real product, real customers — and asks: does AI raise productivity outside the lab, and who actually captures the gains?

// how it works

The setting and the design

The data firm is a Fortune 500 enterprise-software company selling business-process software to US small businesses; its chat agents answer technical-support questions, and 89% of them work outside the US, mostly in the Philippines. The AI tool, supplied by a third-party vendor, is built on "a recent version of the GPT family" and fine-tuned on a large corpus of past customer–agent conversations labeled with outcomes (resolution success, handle time) and with whether the agent was a firm-designated "top performer." The vendor further tunes it with a process "similar in spirit to Ouyang et al. (2022)" — i.e. RLHF — to favor empathetic, professional language. It watches a live chat and, in real time, suggests a reply and links to internal documentation. Crucially, it augments rather than replaces: the agent sees the suggestion and is free to use, edit, or ignore it.

The rollout is what makes the study work. Because of training-session bottlenecks and a fixed license budget, agents were onboarded at different times over Fall 2020–Winter 2021 — within a single team, some had AI access months before their deskmates (team-level access rose from ~5% in October 2020 to ~70% by January 2021). That staggered timing gives a difference-in-differences design. For agent i in month t:

yit = δt + αi + β·AIit + γXit + εit // δt = year-month fixed effects · αi = agent fixed effects · Xit = tenure-in-months controls · β = the AI effect

The headline outcome is resolutions per hour (RPH), decomposed into average handle time (AHT), chats handled per hour (CPH, which captures multitasking), and resolution rate (RR); customer satisfaction is the net promoter score (NPS). Standard errors cluster at the agent level. Because naïve two-way fixed effects can be biased when treatment effects evolve over time, the event studies use the Sun & Abraham (2021) interaction-weighted estimator, and the results replicate across the other modern robust DiD estimators, a small August-2020 RCT (~50 workers, 22 identifiable as treated), and an IV that instruments an agent's adoption date with their team's rollout timing.

// what they found

The results

Productivity rises ~15%. With time and location controls, access to AI raises RPH by 0.47 chats (+23.9%) off a pre-treatment mean of 1.97; adding agent and tenure fixed effects — the preferred spec — brings it to 0.30 chats, +15.2%. The IV pushes it to +0.55, and the tiny RCT lands in the same range. The gain comes from agents handling chats faster (AHT −3.7 minutes, −8.5% off a 43-minute baseline) and juggling more at once (CPH +0.37, ~+15%), with a small, insignificant bump in resolution rate (+1.3pp on an 82% base) and no change in customer satisfaction (−0.12pp on an 80% base). Quality holds while speed climbs.

Who gets the gains — the signature finding

The average hides everything interesting. Sort agents by pre-AI skill (a composite of speed, resolution rate, and satisfaction) and the effect is monotone and stark: the lowest-skill quintile gains ~0.5 RPH (+36%), while the highest-skill quintile gains essentially nothing. Sort by tenure and you see the same shape: agents with under a month on the job gain +0.7 RPH; agents with more than a year gain ~0. Worse for the stars — the most-skilled and most-experienced agents see small but significant declines in resolution rate and customer satisfaction, consistent with AI nudging them toward the faster, less effortful option than their own best work.

One way to read this: AI lets novices move down the experience curve faster. Everyone starts around 1.8 RPH. Never-treated agents drift up to ~2.5 RPH only after 8–10 months; agents with AI from day one hit 2.5 in about two months and pass 3.0 by month five. Agents who join without AI and get it in month five snap onto the always-treated trajectory the moment access turns on. In short, a treated agent with two months of tenure performs like an untreated agent with six-plus months.

Is it learning, or a crutch?

Agents follow only ~38% of suggestions (IQR 23–50%, comparable to the 27–46% GitHub-Copilot acceptance rate), and higher adherence tracks bigger gains (+10% in the lowest adherence quintile vs. ~25% in the highest). The sharpest evidence that something durable happens comes from software outages — moments when the AI silently stops suggesting. Treated agents stay faster than their own pre-AI baseline even mid-outage, and that residual gain grows with months of prior exposure and is concentrated among high-adherence agents (low-adherence agents show none). People who actually try the suggestions appear to internalize the behavior. The effect also lands where humans need it most: AI helps most on moderately rare problems (−14% handle time at the 75th–90th rarity percentile) rather than the routine ones agents already know cold — and most on topics an individual agent has personally seen least.

The job gets better, too

Beyond throughput, the workplace improves. Customer sentiment rises +0.18 points (~half a standard deviation) immediately and persistently; requests to "speak to a manager" fall ~25% off a 6pp base; and AI access is tied to a 40% drop in attrition among the newest agents (a ~10pp fall from a 25% baseline). AI also lifts measured English fluency, especially for Philippines-based agents, and low-skill agents' writing drifts toward how high-skill agents write (cross-group text similarity rises 0.55 → 0.61).

// does it hold up?

The eval read

This isn't a benchmark — it's an econometric field study, so the question is whether the causal claim survives scrutiny, not whether a leaderboard is contaminated. On its own terms it's careful work: flat pre-trends in Figure 2, a battery of modern DiD estimators that agree, an IV, and a (very small) RCT all point the same direction. But the load-bearing assumptions deserve a hard look.

The rollout was not random. Managers chose who to onboard and when, explicitly to "minimize customer-service disruptions." The authors defend identification with the team-level IV — plausibly exogenous to an individual's performance — but if managers front-loaded their weakest agents (the ones with the most to gain), the +15% would be partly a selection artifact, and the IV only partly closes that door. The RCT that would settle it covers just 22 treated workers with the control group unobserved. And the headline outcome, RPH, is only available for the subset of agents whose subcontractors bothered to record quality metrics — a non-random slice of the sample.

Generalizability is the bigger caveat, and the authors say so first: one proprietary tool, one firm, one occupation. Customer support is close to a best case for LLM augmentation — a stable product, highly repetitive issues, and a giant corpus of recorded, outcome-labeled conversations to fine-tune on. The model itself is a black box ("a recent version of GPT," tuned by an anonymous vendor); we never learn which model or how good it is standalone, so the result is impossible to reproduce and hard to port. "Quality" is also thin: resolution rate plus NPS, with sentiment, fluency, and topic labels generated by other LLMs (SiEBERT, Gemini) — validated against humans in the appendix, but still judge-graded. The durable-learning story rests on outages that are rare and admittedly non-random, with noisy estimates; it's the most contestable claim in the paper.

Finally, scope: these are partial-equilibrium, short-run effects (the panel runs out ~5 months post-treatment). The paper can't see wages, employment levels, or job redesign — and it flags two unsettling possibilities its own data can't resolve: a back-of-envelope note that the firm could field the same volume with 12% fewer worker-hours, and a feedback loop where star agents, now leaning on AI, stop producing the high-quality transcripts the model is trained on.

identification

Staggered DiD on a manager-assigned (non-random) rollout; shored up with Sun–Abraham IW, a team-level IV, and modern robust estimators. Flat pre-trends are reassuring; the tiny RCT (22 treated, control unobserved) is not decisive.

generalizability

One firm, one occupation, one undisclosed model. Customer support is unusually favorable to LLMs (stable, repetitive, richly logged). The authors explicitly warn against extrapolating.

what's missed

Wages, employment levels, equilibrium and long-run effects; standalone model quality; the data-feedback loop where top performers stop contributing training-grade transcripts.

would raise confidence

A full-scale RCT; replication across firms/occupations; a named model with a standalone eval; a longer panel to test persistence and the bonus "ratchet" the authors anticipate.

// bottom line

The most credible real-world evidence to date that generative AI raises knowledge-worker productivity — and the canonical citation for AI compressing the skill distribution rather than widening it. Just don't read the specific "15%" as a universal constant: it's one ideal-case occupation, one undisclosed model, and a short window. The direction (novices gain most, quality holds, the job improves) is the durable finding.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. +15% productivity, in the wild. The first at-scale field study of generative AI at work: a GPT assistant raised customer-support resolutions per hour by ~15% across 5,172 agents and 3M chats — lab promise survived the real workplace.
  2. It compresses skill, it doesn't stretch it. The lowest-skill quintile gained +36% and the newest agents +0.7 RPH, while top performers gained ~0 (and lost a little quality). AI bottles up best-practice and hands it to novices — the inverse of prior skill-biased technical change.
  3. Some of it is real learning, and the job gets better. Workers stay faster even during AI outages (more so with exposure and adherence), customer sentiment rises ~0.5 SD, escalations fall ~25%, and new-hire attrition drops 40% — but it's one firm, one occupation, short-run, and the equilibrium effects on jobs and wages remain unknown.
