TÜLU 3: Pushing Frontiers in Open Language Model Post-Training

The recipe, not just the weights. Ai2 published the entire post-training pipeline — data, code, eval, and the dead ends — for a Llama-3.1 family that matches Llama-3.1-Instruct, Qwen 2.5, GPT-4o-mini and Claude 3.5 Haiku. Along the way it named RLVR: reinforcement learning whose reward is simply "did the answer pass a verifier."

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

Post-training — instruction tuning, preference tuning, RLHF, and the rest of the finetuning stack — is what turns a raw base model into something you'd actually talk to. It is also the least transparent part of a frontier model. As the authors point out, as of November 20, 2024, not one of the top 50 models on Chatbot Arena had released its post-training data. The data and recipe are simultaneously the most important and the most hidden pieces of the puzzle.

Open efforts existed — Tülu 2, Zephyr-β — but they leaned on simpler, cheaper pipelines and had quietly fallen behind on the skills that matter: MATH, IFEval, GSM8K. The gap was never a single secret algorithm. It was the whole apparatus: which prompts to source and synthesize, how to mix them, how many training stages and in what order, how to decontaminate against your own benchmarks, and — hardest of all — how to evaluate yourself without quietly overfitting to the test.

TÜLU 3's goal is to close that gap by publishing the apparatus in full: a family of state-of-the-art open models plus the datasets, the training and decontamination code, the OLMES evaluation suite, and an honest accounting of the methods that didn't work. The artifact is not the weights; it's the reproducible recipe.

// how it works

The method

TÜLU 3 starts from Llama 3.1 base models (8B, 70B, and 405B) and runs them through a three-stage pipeline — supervised finetuning (SFT) → Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) → Reinforcement Learning with Verifiable Rewards (RLVR) — with a standardized evaluation suite watching at every step.

Data: curate, synthesize, and decontaminate

The team first names the core skills it wants — knowledge recall, reasoning, math, coding, instruction following, chat, and safety — then builds prompts to target each. Prompts come from public datasets and from persona-driven synthetic generation (a persona conditions the generator to produce diverse, skill-specific instructions). Responses are kept from humans or frontier models where good, or freshly generated with GPT-4o. Critically, every training source is run through 8-gram decontamination against the eval suite — n-gram matching beat both full-string and embedding matching, catching trivially-perturbed test items (e.g. a math problem where only the numbers changed). The releases include the cleaned datasets and the decontamination tool itself; for example they stripped 11.3% of NuminaMath-TIR, 5.4% of WildChat, and 3.5% of Evol-CodeAlpaca for overlap.

SFT then DPO

Data-mixture ablations produce the Tülu3-SFT checkpoint, which then undergoes DPO on a preference mix that blends off-policy preferences with on-policy generations from the model itself. To make DPO scale to 70B/405B, they cache the reference policy's log-probs and run separate forward passes on the chosen and rejected completions instead of concatenating them — halving the activation memory at near-identical loss.

RLVR: the reward model becomes a verifier

The headline contribution is RLVR. It keeps the standard KL-constrained RLHF objective but throws out the learned reward model, replacing it with a deterministic verifier. The policy is rewarded only when its completion is checkably correct:

maxπθ  𝔼y∼πθ(x)[ RRLVR(x,y) ]  =  v(x,y) − β KL[ πθ(y∣x) ‖ πref(y∣x) ] // reward = verifier output minus a KL leash to the reference policy
v(x,y) = α  if the answer is correct,   0  otherwise   (α = 10) // a binary correctness check, not a learned preference score

They optimize this with PPO, over a mixture of roughly 30,000 verifiable prompts: GSM8K (7,473) and MATH (7,500), graded by exact-match against the extracted answer, plus 14,973 verifiable instruction-following prompts whose per-constraint verifier templates (drawn from the IFEval taxonomy) check things like "answer in exactly three bullet points." A set of PPO implementation details earns its keep: initialize the value model from a general reward model, disable dropout (so KL is estimated cleanly), apply a −10 penalty when a response lacks an EOS token, and whiten advantages.

Evaluation built to resist self-deception

TÜLU 3's eval suite (run through the open OLMES toolkit) is split into a development set used to make decisions and an unseen held-out set that they did not look at during development — a deliberate guard so they can later measure how much they overfit their data and hyperparameter choices to the benchmarks they were optimizing.

// what they found

The results

On TÜLU 3's own multi-skill suite, the finished models beat the same-size open instruct models and reach into closed-model territory. At 8B, TÜLU 3 averages 65.1 versus Llama 3.1 8B Instruct's 62.9. At 70B it averages 76.2, ahead of Llama 3.1 70B Instruct (74.1), Qwen 2.5 72B Instruct (72.8), GPT-3.5 Turbo (64.7) and GPT-4o-mini (69.6), and edging past Claude 3.5 Haiku (75.3). At 405B it averages 80.7 (with safety), beating DeepSeek-V3 (75.9) and Llama 3.1 405B Instruct (79.0) and landing just behind GPT-4o 11-24 (81.6).

Read down the table and the story of where the lift comes from is plain. SFT+DPO does the heavy lifting — the 8B jumps from 62.2 to 64.4 and the 70B from 73.4 to 75.9. RLVR is the thin top layer: it moves the 8B average by +0.4 (64.4 → 64.8) and the 70B average by +0.1 (75.9 → 76.0). Its targeted wins are real and concentrated on exactly the skills it verifies: at 8B, GSM8K climbs 84.3 → 87.6, MATH 42.0 → 43.7, and IFEval 81.1 → 82.4. Some 8B runs even hit GSM8K 89.4 and IFEval 84.8, though those models regressed elsewhere and dragged the average down. At 70B, RLVR can't improve GSM8K at all — it's already saturated at 93.5.

At 405B the recipe scales: the team report that even 25 RLVR steps lifted MATH by over 5 points (they dropped GSM8K and IFEval data at this scale and trained RLVR on MATH alone), and MATH had not yet saturated when they stopped at 75 steps for compute reasons. The 405B model posts the best GSM8K in its class (95.5) and a 51.4 AlpacaEval 2 length-controlled win rate.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

As a piece of open science this is close to exemplary — and the paper is unusually honest about its own weak spots, which makes the critique mostly about how to read the headline rather than whether to trust the work.

Start with the "state of the art at its size" framing, which is an aggregate-average claim. The 8B average of 65.1 hides large per-skill losses: Qwen 2.5 7B Instruct beats TÜLU 3 8B on MATH by a wide margin (69.9 vs 43.7) and on HumanEval (93.1 vs 83.9). TÜLU's averages win because they're broad and safety-heavy, not because the model is uniformly stronger — a per-skill table tells a more honest story than the mean.

Next, the closed-model comparisons aren't all measured the same way. Several GPT/Claude entries in the main results table are flagged as taken from model cards or imputed with MICE (multiple imputation) because TÜLU's own harness hit "substantial formatting errors" on them. So the cleanest claims ("we beat GPT-4o-mini / Claude 3.5 Haiku") rest partly on numbers that were not produced under a single shared evaluation setup.

Then there's RLVR itself, the paper's named novelty. Its contribution to the overall average is a few tenths of a point, and its concrete gains land on the exact three benchmarks it is trained to verify — GSM8K, MATH, IFEval — which are also reported as headline results. The authors are refreshingly candid that an increase in average score "is not guaranteed," that overoptimization happens as KL grows (Figure 23's runaway response length), and that the IF gains may be overfitting to the constraint taxonomy: on the held-out IFEval-OOD constraints the improvement largely evaporates. That's the real question RLVR leaves open — is it teaching math, or climbing the verifier on the train distribution of the benchmarks being graded?

baselines

Fair and current for late-2024 (Llama 3.1, Qwen 2.5, Mistral, Hermes 3, GPT-4o-mini, Claude 3.5 Haiku, DeepSeek-V3, GPT-4o) — but closed-model numbers are partly model-card or MICE-imputed, not measured in one shared harness, and "SOTA" is an average that hides Qwen's large MATH/coding lead.

contamination

A genuine strength: 8-gram decontamination against the eval suite, released cleaned datasets and a decontamination toolkit, plus an "unseen" split inspected only at the very end to bound overfitting. The catch — that unseen split can't be trusted for the competitors, whose training data is closed ("we cannot verify they are not training on the unseen benchmarks").

what's missed

English-only; short, mostly single-/two-turn data (avg 2.4 turns, most under 2,048 tokens); no long-context, no tool-use or agentic evaluation. RLVR is confined to verifiable domains and shows weak transfer to new instruction-following constraints.

would raise confidence

Per-skill (not just average) head-to-heads vs Qwen 2.5; RLVR ablations that hold out the trained benchmarks and measure transfer to genuinely unseen verifiable tasks; closed-model scores re-run in one harness instead of imputed.

// bottom line

The lasting contribution isn't a leaderboard number — it's that the entire pipeline is open and reproducible, and that RLVR packaged the "reward = passed the verifier" idea that the DeepSeek-R1 / RLVR wave scaled months later. As a science artifact, top-tier. As a "we beat the closed models" headline, lean on the per-skill numbers and remember the measurement caveats.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. TÜLU 3 is the first fully-open post-training stack — data, code, recipe, and evaluation — to reach and surpass Llama-3.1-Instruct quality, via SFT → DPO → RLVR on Llama 3.1 base at 8B/70B/405B.
  2. RLVR swaps the learned reward model for a binary verifier (correct → reward α=10, else 0) optimized with PPO. It delivers sharp, targeted gains on math and instruction following but only tenths of a point on the overall average — and it overoptimizes if you let KL drift.
  3. The most copyable ideas are methodological: 8-gram decontamination against your own benchmarks, a held-out "unseen" eval split you inspect only at the end, and a rare published catalogue of what didn't work (online DPO, rejection sampling, using RM scores inside RLVR).
