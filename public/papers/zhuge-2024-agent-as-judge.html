Agent-as-a-Judge: Evaluate Agents with Agents

Judge agents not on their final answer but on the whole trajectory: an agentic evaluator that builds a graph of the workspace, reads files, and checks each requirement in a dependency DAG — scored on DevAI, 55 realistic automated-AI-development tasks. It matches a panel of human experts ~90% of the time at ~2% of the cost.

What problem is this solving?

Agentic systems solve problems step by step — they browse, write code, run it, read the error, and try again. But we evaluate them as if they were multiple-choice test-takers: with a single number at the end. SWE-Bench reports only a final resolve rate; HumanEval and MBPP report pass@1 on self-contained snippets. None of these tells you where inside a long, multi-stage run the agent went wrong, which means they give almost no usable feedback for improving the agent.

The paper argues this matters more for coding than anywhere else. SWE-Bench is "automated repair" of GitHub issues — and recent work showed that over 27% of its tasks can be solved with no real agent at all (Agentless), while teams have begun hard-coding mechanisms for individual SWE-Bench instances, the classic Goodhart failure: "when a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure." No public benchmark captures a complete AI-development cycle (load data → build a model → train → report results → visualize), and none scores the intermediate milestones.

The thorough alternative — having human experts watch the whole trajectory — is accurate but brutal: in this paper one full pass over the benchmark took three experts 86.5 hours (≈ $1,297.50 at a $15/hr floor). And LLM-as-a-Judge (MT-Bench, G-Eval) is cheap but blind: it scores a final text blob in one shot, with no ability to open the generated repo, run the code, or look at a produced image. The gap is an evaluator that acts like an agent — exploring the workspace and the trajectory — while staying as cheap as an LLM judge.

// how it works

The method

Two contributions ship together: a benchmark that makes intermediate evaluation possible, and the agentic judge that performs it.

DevAI — a benchmark with a requirements DAG

DevAI is 55 realistic "build me an AI app" tasks written by expert annotators. Each task is a plain-text user query plus a set of requirements (365 in total) and softer preferences (125). Crucially, the requirements are arranged as a directed acyclic graph of dependencies — "visualize the results" depends on "train the model" depends on "load the data." Instead of one binary solved?, an agent earns credit milestone by milestone, and the DAG makes memorization useless: you have to actually do the earlier steps to unlock the later ones. The tasks span supervised learning, RL, computer vision, NLP and generative models, and are kept small enough to run cheaply.

Agent-as-a-Judge — eight modules, then ablated down to five

The judge is itself an agentic system, imitating how a careful human reviewer works. The proof-of-concept has eight modules: a graph that maps the whole project (files, modules, dependencies, code chunks); locate to find the file a requirement refers to; read to understand content across 33 formats (code, images, video, docs); search for contextual code retrieval; retrieve to pull relevant spans out of long trajectories; ask to decide whether a requirement is met; plus memory and planning. After ablations, the winning combination drops the last two:

graph locate read retrieve ask memory planning search

Memory hurt — a wrong earlier judgment chained into wrong later ones — and planning was unstable. The judge is sensitive to noise and rewards clean factual grounding over cleverness. It runs in two access regimes: black-box (only the agent's generated workspace) and gray-box (workspace plus a hand-collected trajectory log, which the authors note is "nearly inaccessible in practical situations").

Quality is scored two ways against the human-consensus labels. Judge Shift is the absolute deviation of a judge's pass-rate from the human consensus (lower is better). Alignment Rate is the fraction of the 365 individual requirement verdicts that exactly match the human consensus:

The results

DevAI is genuinely hard for today's agents

Under careful human evaluation, the two strongest open systems — GPT-Pilot and OpenHands — satisfy only about 29% of requirements once dependencies are enforced (≈44% if you ignore them), and across all 55 tasks each fully completed exactly one task. MetaGPT trails badly. So the benchmark is hard but not hopeless — the right difficulty band for measuring progress.

The human gold standard is itself noisy

Before trusting the automatic judge, the paper turns the lens on the humans. The three expert evaluators disagreed with each other on 10–30% of requirements (Figure 4); one evaluator (cn9o) erred on 23.77% of GPT-Pilot's. Only after a debate round and a majority vote did the error against consensus fall to 6.01%. The lesson the authors lean on: a single human evaluator is an unreliable yardstick, so beating one is a meaningful bar.

Agent-as-a-Judge tracks the humans; LLM-as-a-Judge does not

On per-requirement agreement with the human consensus, the agentic judge sits in the high 80s–low 90s — within range of the best single human and, judging OpenHands, even edging past it (90.44% vs the best human's 89.89%). LLM-as-a-Judge lags by 20–30 points, and its Judge Shift on OpenHands blows out to ~31% against the agentic judge's best of 0.27%.

Each module earns its keep — and it's nearly free

Stacking modules while judging OpenHands (black-box) shows where the alignment comes from: bare ask gets 65.03%, the workspace graph lifts it to 75.95%, read to 82.24%, and locate jumps it to 90.44%. retrieve adds nothing here (these workspaces are only hundreds of lines) but helps when judging MetaGPT and GPT-Pilot.

The cost case is the punchline. A full human pass cost $1,297.50 and 86.5 hours; Agent-as-a-Judge did it for $30.58 in API calls and 118.43 minutes — about 2.29% of the cost and 2.36% of the time. (LLM-as-a-Judge was faster at 10.99 minutes but barely cheaper at $29.63 — and much less accurate.)

// does it hold up?

The eval read

The reframing is the real win: scoring a DAG of intermediate requirements instead of one final outcome is the right idea, and the cost result (~2% of human time and money) is hard to argue with. But the headline — "as reliable as our human evaluation baseline" — leans on a small, self-built reference, and the metric that carries it is base-rate-fragile.

Start with the ground truth. The "human consensus" is a negotiated agreement among three annotators after a debate round, on 55 tasks. The judge is then declared "more useful than an average lone human" — but the lone humans being beaten are the very people whose votes built the consensus the judge is scored against. The bar that actually matters, the human majority vote, still beats Agent-as-a-Judge across the board (95.08 / 93.98 / 94.26 vs 88.5–92). The paper is careful here, but readers quoting "matches humans" should note it matches a single human, not the panel.

Then the metric. Requirements are rarely satisfied (recall the ~6–29% met rates), so "not satisfied" is the overwhelming majority class — and a judge that simply leans negative scores high. The authors say so plainly: LLM-as-a-Judge hits 84.15% Alignment on MetaGPT in black-box because MetaGPT almost never meets a requirement, so guessing "no" is usually right. Alignment Rate (and Judge Shift) are inflated by this imbalance; the PR curves are the honest view, and they're relegated to a single figure rather than the headline. Notably the same imbalance makes LLM-as-a-Judge's MetaGPT number (84.15) drop to 68.86 in gray-box — more evidence the metric is reacting to base rates, not just judge quality.

Finally, backbone risk. The three developer agents all run on gpt-4o-2024-05-13; the paper never reports which model powers the LLM-as-a-Judge or the Agent-as-a-Judge. LLM-as-a-judge self-preference is documented, so a judge sharing the developers' model family could be scoring its own kind — and there's no cross-model judge (e.g. a Claude or Gemini judge over gpt-4o developers) to rule that out. The best numbers also come from the gray-box setting, which the authors admit needs hand-collected trajectory data you won't have in deployment; the realistic black-box setting is where this should be read, and it still wins — just with a different module mix.

baselines

Fair and current for late-2024: LLM-as-a-Judge (the right cheap baseline), three expert humans (the gold standard), and three popular open coding agents (MetaGPT, GPT-Pilot, OpenHands). Weakness: the developers all run on one gpt-4o snapshot and the judges' backbone is unspecified, so cross-model robustness is untested.

contamination

Tasks are new (Oct 2024) and execution-based, which blunts memorization. But several are adapted from public blog/product demos (e.g. the Devin "Hidden in Plain Sight" task), and there's no held-out or paraphrase check — minor here, since credit requires actually running code, not recalling text.

what's missed

Alignment Rate is inflated by heavy class imbalance (most requirements unmet → "negative" guessing scores high); the human-consensus "ground truth" is a 3-person construct on 55 tasks, and the judge is only shown to beat a single human, not the majority vote it still trails.

would raise confidence

More than 55 tasks; a judge backbone different from the developer backbone; variance across repeated developer/judge runs (everything is single-run with a hard 1800s cutoff); an independent expert panel not used to build the consensus; and leading with precision/recall instead of raw alignment.

// bottom line

A genuinely useful idea executed as an honest proof-of-concept: judge agents with agents, against a dependency DAG of milestones, at ~2% of the cost of human review. Take DevAI + Agent-as-a-Judge as a promising reward-signal prototype for agent self-improvement — not yet a settled measurement instrument, because the "matches humans" claim rests on a small, self-constructed consensus and a base-rate-inflated metric.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. Agent-as-a-Judge = LLM-as-a-Judge that can act. It builds a graph of the generated workspace, locates and reads files, retrieves from the trajectory, and checks each requirement — producing intermediate, per-requirement feedback instead of one pass/fail. Ablations keep graph + locate + read + retrieve + ask and drop memory and planning (they chained errors).
  2. DevAI's requirements DAG is the durable contribution. 55 tasks, 365 hierarchical requirements with dependencies turn "solved?" into a dense, milestone-level reward — and reveal that today's best open coding agents satisfy only ~29% of requirements and almost never finish a whole task.
  3. ~90% human alignment at ~2% of the cost — with caveats. The agentic judge matches the best single human and trounces LLM-as-a-Judge by 20–30 points, but the consensus it's scored against is a 3-person construct, the alignment metric is inflated by class imbalance, and the developers share one gpt-4o snapshot while the judges' backbone goes unreported. Promising signal, not a verdict.
