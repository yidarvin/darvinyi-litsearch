QLoRA: Efficient Finetuning of Quantized LLMs

4-bit NormalFloat quantization plus Low-Rank Adapters cut 65B-parameter LLM finetuning from over 780GB of GPU memory to under 48GB — with no measured loss against full 16-bit finetuning.

Tim Dettmers, Artidoro Pagnoni, Ari Holtzman, Luke Zettlemoyer
venue NeurIPS 2023 citations 5,074 topic Post-training & RL source ↗

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

Regular 16-bit finetuning of a 65B-parameter LLaMA model requires more than 780GB of GPU memory — far beyond even a well-stocked multi-GPU workstation, let alone a single card. Prior quantization work (LLM.int8(), GPTQ, SmoothQuant) had shown that 4-bit and 8-bit weights work fine for inference, but such techniques “only work for inference and break down during training”: backpropagating through frozen low-precision weights had not been shown to recover full-precision accuracy. Parameter-efficient finetuning methods like LoRA attack the problem from a different angle — freeze the full-precision base model and train only small low-rank adapters — but that barely touches the memory bill, because the frozen base model itself is what dominates. The paper's own accounting makes this concrete: for a 7B LLaMA model finetuned with a batch size of 1, the LoRA adapters cost just 26MB and their (non-checkpointed) input gradients 567MB, while the 4-bit base model alone still consumes 5,048MB. Quantizing that frozen base model to 4-bit was the obvious remaining lever — the open question was whether it could be done without destroying the adapter's ability to recover 16-bit-level performance.

// how it works

The method

QLoRA keeps one 4-bit storage datatype for the frozen base model and one 16-bit (BFloat16) compute datatype for everything else, bridged by three components. 4-bit NormalFloat (NF4): pretrained transformer weights are empirically zero-centered and normally distributed, so instead of a generic 4-bit integer or float grid, QLoRA builds a quantile-quantization data type whose 16 bins each hold an equal share of probability mass under a standard normal — information-theoretically optimal for that distribution — then rescales each 64-weight block into [−1, 1] and rounds to the nearest NF4 level. Double Quantization (DQ): storing one 32-bit constant per 64-weight block already costs 32/64 = 0.5 bits/parameter of overhead, so QLoRA quantizes those constants a second time with an 8-bit float and a blocksize of 256, cutting the overhead to 8/64 + 32/(64·256) ≈ 0.127 bits/parameter — a savings of about 0.373 bits/parameter, roughly 3GB on a 65B model. Paged Optimizers: gradient checkpointing on long sequences produces intermittent memory spikes that would otherwise crash training with an out-of-memory error, so QLoRA allocates optimizer state in NVIDIA unified memory, which the driver automatically pages out to CPU RAM and back exactly when the GPU needs the headroom. For a single linear layer, the three combine as:

YBF16 = XBF16·doubleDequant(c1FP32, c2k-bit, WNF4) + XBF16L1BF16L2BF16 // storage dtype = 4-bit NormalFloat, compute dtype = BFloat16 — gradients only ever flow to the adapters L1, L2, never to the 4-bit base weight W

One more finding matters as much as the quantization math: applying LoRA only to the attention query/value projections — the common default — is not enough to match full finetuning. Adapters have to sit on every linear layer, attention and feed-forward alike, before QLoRA closes the gap with a 16-bit baseline.

// what they found

The results

On memory: QLoRA finetunes a 65B model on a single 48GB GPU and a 33B model on a single 24GB consumer GPU. The paper's own appendix breakdown (Figure 6) shows the frozen 4-bit base model dominating the footprint at every scale — 5,046 / 8,476 / 19,302 / 37,074 MB for the 7B/13B/33B/65B LLaMA sizes — dwarfing the 288–1,440 MB spent on LoRA adapters. On accuracy, NF4 is the load-bearing choice: at matched bit-width, NF4+Double Quantization reaches 27.41 mean perplexity on Pile-CommonCrawl versus 29.48 for a 3-exponent-bit float, 31.07 for a 2-exponent-bit float, and 34.34 for plain 4-bit integers (Table 2). Across GLUE (RoBERTa-large), Super-NaturalInstructions (T5, 80M–11B), and 5-shot MMLU (LLaMA 7B–65B), 4-bit QLoRA with NF4+DQ statistically matches both full 16-bit finetuning and 16-bit LoRA: mean 5-shot MMLU across all four LLaMA sizes and both finetuning datasets comes out to 53.1 for NF4+DQ versus 53.0 for BFloat16 and only 52.2 for a plain 4-bit float (Tables 3–4).

The headline system, Guanaco — QLoRA-finetuned LLaMA on the 9k-example OASST1 dataset — reaches 99.3% of ChatGPT's score on the GPT-4-judged Vicuna benchmark at 65B (41GB, 24 hours on one professional GPU) and 97.8% at 33B (21GB, under 12 hours on one consumer GPU); the 7B model needs just 5GB and still beats a 26GB Alpaca-13B by roughly 20 percentage points (Table 6). A striking ablation: the 9k-example OASST1 outperforms a 450k-example FLAN v2 subsample on chatbot quality even though FLAN v2 wins on MMLU — dataset–benchmark similarity, not raw size, drives the result.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

The 99.3%-of-ChatGPT headline is exactly the kind of single-scalar eval claim that deserves scrutiny — and unusually, the paper's own §6.2 (“Considerations”) does most of that scrutinizing for you. The score comes from GPT-4 rating both ChatGPT's and Guanaco's response to each of the 80 Vicuna-benchmark prompts out of ten, expressed as a percentage of ChatGPT's total score and averaged over both response orderings; Table 6 also reports a 95% confidence interval of ±4.4 points for Guanaco-65B, and the authors note the intervals are wide enough that many models overlap — meaning the crisp “99.3%” conceals real ranking uncertainty the abstract doesn't convey. The GPT-4 judge is also shown, in this very paper, to be a biased instrument: it exhibits a strong order effect (favoring whichever response appears first in its prompt) and rates its own model's outputs more favorably than human raters do — GPT-4 gives itself an Elo of 1348 in its own tournament versus 1176 when human annotators run the identical tournament (Table 7), which the authors themselves translate to “an additional 20% probability of winning.” System-level agreement between GPT-4 and humans is only moderate (Kendall τ=0.43, Spearman r=0.55), and at the individual-example level it's markedly weaker (Fleiss κ=0.25) — even human-to-human agreement on the same task is just Fleiss κ=0.42. None of this is buried; the authors report all of it themselves, a genuine model of eval transparency that most contemporaneous chatbot papers didn't practice — but it does mean the “matches ChatGPT” framing that leads the abstract is more confident than the paper's own error bars support.

baselines

Real, contemporaneous, strong systems (GPT-4, ChatGPT/GPT-3.5-Turbo, Bard, Vicuna-13B, Open Assistant-33B) rather than weak strawmen — a genuine strength. But the comparison is cross-paradigm: Guanaco is trained with plain cross-entropy supervised finetuning only (the paper is explicit that it uses no RLHF), while ChatGPT, Bard, and Open Assistant were RLHF- or RLAIF-tuned, so the benchmark conflates finetuning-data quality and quantization fidelity with a fundamentally different training recipe.

contamination

The authors checked for overlap between OASST1 training data and the 80 Vicuna prompts via fuzzy string matching plus manual inspection of the closest matches, and found none — a narrow check (no semantic/embedding similarity, and no audit of LLaMA's own undisclosed pretraining corpus, which is where MMLU-style contamination would actually live).

what's missed

Table 6's 95% confidence intervals (±3.6 to ±5.4 points across systems) overlap heavily between neighboring models, so the precise ranking implied by a single percentage (99.3% vs 97.8%, say) is less resolved than it looks; the paper itself prefers the Elo ranking (Table 7) for exactly this reason, yet the percentage is what leads the abstract.

would raise confidence

A larger, more adversarial prompt set than 80 Vicuna questions; more raters per example to push the example-level Fleiss κ past 0.25; the 33B/65B QLoRA-vs-true-16-bit-full-finetuning comparison the paper explicitly could not afford to run (recovery was only demonstrated up to 3B parameters); and a bias/safety evaluation broader than the single CrowS-Pairs check in Table 8.

// bottom line

The quantization method itself is unusually well-supported — matched full 16-bit and LoRA performance across GLUE, Super-NaturalInstructions, and MMLU at every scale from 80M to 65B parameters, with NF4 beating FP4/Int4 head-to-head on perplexity. The weaker link is the headline chatbot claim, which rests on an 80-prompt, GPT-4-judged benchmark that this paper's own considerations section shows to carry order bias, self-preference bias, and only fair human agreement — treat “99.3% of ChatGPT” as a noisy, Elo-adjacent signal rather than a precise measurement, exactly as the authors themselves suggest.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. NF4 (4-bit NormalFloat) + Double Quantization + Paged Optimizers cut 65B-parameter finetuning memory from over 780GB to under 48GB, and NF4+DQ statistically matches 16-bit full finetuning / 16-bit LoRA on GLUE, Super-NaturalInstructions, and 5-shot MMLU at every scale tested (mean MMLU 53.1 vs 53.0 for BFloat16).
  2. Guanaco-65B (41GB) reaches 99.3% of ChatGPT's score on the GPT-4-judged Vicuna benchmark after 24 hours on one professional GPU; Guanaco-7B needs just 5GB and still beats a 26GB Alpaca-13B by roughly 20 percentage points.
  3. Finetuning-data quality beat quantity: the 9k-example OASST1 dataset outperformed a 450k-example FLAN v2 subsample on chatbot quality, even though FLAN v2 won on MMLU — the two benchmarks reward different things.
  4. The paper's own §6.2 documents its GPT-4 judge's order effect and self-preference bias (Elo 1348 self-judged vs 1176 human-judged) and only fair example-level agreement with humans (Fleiss κ=0.25) — rare, valuable transparency that should temper how literally readers take “matches ChatGPT.”
