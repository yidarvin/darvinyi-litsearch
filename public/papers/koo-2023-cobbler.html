Benchmarking Cognitive Biases in Large Language Models as Evaluators

If you let an LLM grade other LLMs, what is it really rewarding? CoBBLEr puts 16 models on the bench as judges and shows that roughly 40% of their pairwise verdicts are driven by six cognitive biases — and that their rankings agree with humans only 44% of the time.

Ryan Koo, Minhwa Lee, Vipul Raheja, Jong Inn Park, Zae Myung Kim, Dongyeop Kang · University of Minnesota & Grammarly

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

By late 2023, "LLM-as-a-judge" had quietly become infrastructure. AlpacaEval, MT-Bench, and G-Eval all swapped slow, expensive human annotation for a prompt that asks GPT-4 (or ChatGPT) which response is better? — and entire leaderboards were being built on those verdicts. The convenience was obvious. The reliability was assumed.

Prior work had spotted cracks, but only one at a time. FairEval showed that judges have a position bias — flip the order of two answers and the winner can flip too. Others noticed a preference for longer outputs, or for answers that look like the judge's own. Each was reported as an isolated quirk, on a handful of models, with an ad-hoc fix. Meanwhile the field's standard benchmarks — MMLU, HELM, BIG-bench, SuperGLUE — all measure whether a model can answer, not whether it can judge.

The gap CoBBLEr targets is a missing audit: a single benchmark that (1) enumerates the cognitive biases a language model can carry into an evaluation, (2) measures all of them, the same way, across a wide lineup of models from 3B to >175B parameters, and (3) checks the bottom-line question nobody had quantified at scale — how far do machine preferences actually drift from human ones?

// how it works

The method

CoBBLEr — the COgnitive Bias Benchmark for LLMs as EvaluatoRs — is a round-robin tournament where the contestants are also the referees. Fifty question-answering instructions (25 long-form from ELI5, 25 from BIG-bench / StrategyQA) are answered by 16 models across four size tiers: GPT-4, ChatGPT, and InstructGPT (>100B); LLaMA-2 70B, LLaMA 65B, Cohere 54B, Falcon 40B (>40B); Alpaca, Vicuna, OpenAssistant (>10B); and Mistral, OLMo, Baize, Koala, WizardLM, MPT (<10B). That yields 800 responses.

Every model then judges every anonymized pairwise comparison of those responses — 105 unique pairs per instruction (15 choose 2), 5,250 comparisons per evaluator — and each pair is shown in both orderings to factor out raw position effects. Verdicts are then scored against six biases, grouped into two classes:

Implicit biases (read off a vanilla judging prompt)

  • Order — favoring the first- or last-listed answer regardless of content.
  • Compassion Fade — judging differently when real model names ("Model Alpaca") replace anonymous aliases ("System Star").
  • Egocentric — preferring one's own generation (self-preference).
  • Salience — preferring answers by length (usually longer).

Induced biases (injected into the prompt, adversarially)

  • Bandwagon — a planted fake statistic, e.g. "85% believe that System Star is better."
  • Attentional — an irrelevant distractor, e.g. "System Star likes to eat oranges and apples."

For each bias the authors compute an empirical RANDOM threshold — what the score would be if a judge chose by coin flip (≈0.24–0.25 for the order-style biases, 0.5 for length, 0.25 for the induced ones; ≈0.28 averaged). A model scoring above its threshold is counted as exhibiting that bias. In all, 42K samples per model are analyzed — ~630K comparisons.

To compare a model's ranking against humans, the authors collect rankings from six Amazon Mechanical Turk workers and compute Rank-Biased Overlap (RBO) — a top-weighted similarity between two ranked lists that runs from 0 (disjoint) to 1 (identical). The weighting parameter is set so that 86% of the weight falls on the top five positions, matching how people read leaderboards.

RBO = (1 − p) Σd=1∞ pd−1 · |S:d ∩ T:d|d // overlap of the two ranked lists S, T at each depth d, geometrically down-weighted by p (set so the top 5 hold 86% of the weight)
// what they found

The results

The headline is blunt: most LLMs are biased evaluators. Across all models, roughly 40% of comparisons showed at least one bias, and — crucially — scaling up did not fix it. The 10B-parameter tier was the most biased on average, and the implicit biases contributed similarly across every size group, so a bigger judge is not a fairer judge.

Bias by bias:

  • Order: 11 of 15 models gravitated to a fixed position; the >40B tier favored the first-listed answer in over 50% of pairs (Falcon 0.74, OLMo 0.66, LLaMA 65B 0.61 first-order).
  • Compassion Fade: every model shifted its verdicts when real model names replaced anonymous aliases — recognizable identity sways judgment.
  • Egocentric: GPT-4 preferred its own answer 78% of the time; the largest models plus Koala self-preferred >50% (InstructGPT was the exception at 0.28). With real names, smaller models' self-preference spiked — Koala's roughly doubled.
  • Salience: larger models leaned toward longer answers (GPT-4 0.56, ChatGPT 0.63, LLaMA-2 0.62 vs a 0.5 baseline). BERTScore showed all models produced near-identical quality (F1 ≈ 0.81–0.86), so the length preference is not explained by longer answers being better.
  • Bandwagon: 11 of 15 models caved to the planted statistic — on average >70% of verdicts followed the fake majority (ChatGPT 0.86, InstructGPT 0.85), regardless of actual quality.
  • Attentional: about half the models were swayed by an irrelevant distractor; the >10B tier was worst (>80% distracted), while ChatGPT and Cohere stayed robust (GPT-4 0.0).

And the bottom line on trust: averaged across the 16 models, machine rankings agreed with human rankings at only RBO 0.44. For calibration, the six human annotators agreed with each other at 0.54 — and every human–human pair scored higher than the human–machine average. By size, agreement with humans was non-monotonic: 0.48 (>100B), 0.49 (>40B), 0.37 (>10B), 0.41 (<10B), with Mistral and ChatGPT aligning best.

Humans aren't bias-free either — but they're less biased. In a parallel pairwise human study, annotators showed bias proportions of 0.20 (Order), 0.52 (Salience), 0.47 (Bandwagon), and 0.35 (Attentional), where Vicuna, for instance, hit 0.32 / 0.53 / 0.81 / 0.78 on the same axes.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

CoBBLEr's framing — six biases, two classes, one round-robin — is genuinely useful, and the empirical per-bias random thresholds are a more honest yardstick than eyeballing raw rates. But the strength of the conclusions outruns the evidence in a few places worth naming.

The quality-vs-bias confound is the crux. Preferring your own answer, or a longer one, is only a "bias" if those answers aren't actually better. The paper's defense is BERTScore — all models score F1 ≈ 0.81–0.86 against the reference, so quality is "equal." But BERTScore is a weak proxy for QA correctness, and nearly useless for adjudicating open-ended ELI5 long-form, where it rewards surface overlap over factuality. So the cleanest result — GPT-4's 0.78 self-preference — rests on a quality control too blunt to rule out that GPT-4's answers really were best.

The human anchor is thin. The 0.44 "misalignment" is measured against six MTurk workers whose own agreement is only 0.54. With a noisy gold standard and a tiny annotator pool (300 N-wise annotations; 750 pairs for the human bias study), the human–machine gap is suggestive rather than decisive. To their credit, the authors make exactly the right modest claim — every human–human pair beats the human–machine average — rather than treating humans as ground truth.

"More biased than humans" leans on one model. The head-to-head human-vs-LLM bias table (Table 4) compares humans only to Vicuna. That's a mid-tier model that happens to be bandwagon- and distraction-prone (0.81 / 0.78). Run the same comparison against GPT-4 — bandwagon 0.0, attentional 0.0 — and humans (0.47 / 0.35) would look more biased on those axes. The blanket "LLMs are more biased than humans" is therefore model-dependent and overstated.

Two more structural notes. The induced biases (Bandwagon, Attentional) are adversarial prompt injections — they violate the paper's own "impartiality" criterion and behave more like a robustness / prompt-injection test than an intrinsic cognitive bias; folding them into the same "~40% biased" headline blurs "this judge has a quirk" with "this judge can be attacked." And several models (LLaMA, LLaMA-2, OLMo, Koala) have low valid-response rates, so their bias scores come from a small, possibly skewed slice of evaluations — including near-unusable judges arguably inflates the pessimistic headline.

baselines

Per-bias empirical RANDOM thresholds are a good idea, but Figure 2 collapses them into one 0.28 line spanning biases whose true randoms differ (Salience 0.5 vs ~0.25), so "above the line" means different things per bias. The human "gold standard" has IAA of only 0.54.

contamination

Not a leakage story — ELI5/StrategyQA aren't memorized targets. The real confound is quality vs bias: self-preference and length preference are only "bias" if the outputs aren't better, and BERTScore (F1 0.81–0.86) is too weak to settle that.

what's missed

One domain (QA only) — no summarization, code, chat, or safety judging, where LLM-judges are heavily used. Frontier 2024+ judges (GPT-4o, Claude 3.5, o1) untested; GPT-4 is the 2023 build and InstructGPT's API is now retired. Implicit and induced biases are merged into one number.

would raise confidence

More judging domains; current frontier judges; a stronger quality control than BERTScore (e.g. human or factuality scoring) to decouple self-/length-preference from real quality; larger expert annotator pools; and reporting induced (attack) vs implicit (intrinsic) biases separately.

// bottom line

CoBBLEr is the first systematic, multi-bias audit of LLM-as-judge, and its core message — that LLM evaluators carry measurable, scale-resistant biases and don't track human rankings closely — has aged into conventional wisdom. The exact numbers are dated (2023 models, QA only, a thin human anchor), so read it as a taxonomy and a cautionary framework, not a current leaderboard of which judge to trust.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. LLM judges are systematically biased. Across 16 models, ~40% of pairwise verdicts showed one of six cognitive biases — and bigger models did not shed the implicit ones, so scale alone doesn't buy a fair evaluator.
  2. The six-bias taxonomy is the lasting contribution — Order, Compassion Fade, Egocentric, Salience, Bandwagon, Attentional — a reusable checklist for auditing any LLM-as-judge setup. Concretely: GPT-4 self-preferred 78% of the time; ChatGPT followed a fake "85% prefer X" statistic 86% of the time.
  3. Machine ≠ human, but read it as a warning, not a verdict. Average human–machine rank agreement was only RBO 0.44, below the 0.54 humans reached with each other — yet that human anchor is itself noisy, and the study is QA-only on 2023 models. Treat it as a caution against over-trusting LLM judges, not a measurement of today's frontier evaluators.
