AutoDAN: Generating Stealthy Jailbreak Prompts on Aligned Large Language Models

A hierarchical genetic algorithm evolves handcrafted "DAN"-style jailbreak prompts into new prompts that stay fluent, low-perplexity English — reaching 60.77% attack success on Llama2-7b-chat, versus 45.38% for the gradient-based GCG baseline — while GCG's gibberish suffixes collapse to 0% the moment a simple perplexity filter is applied. AutoDAN's fluent prompts are untouched by it.

Xiaogeng Liu, Nan Xu, Muhao Chen, Chaowei Xiao

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

By late 2023, jailbreak attacks against aligned LLMs split cleanly into two families, and each had a fatal weakness. Manual jailbreaks — the "Do-Anything-Now" (DAN) prompt series and its many variants, crowdsourced on forums like jailbreakchat.com — read as fluent, plausible English and reliably fool perplexity-based defenses, but they're hand-crafted: they don't scale, they need continual human re-engineering as models update, and no single person can systematically search the space of possible prompts. Automated, learning-based jailbreaks — chiefly GCG (Zou et al., 2023), which uses greedy coordinate gradient search to build an adversarial suffix — scale perfectly and transfer well, but the suffixes they produce are gibberish token sequences with astronomically high perplexity. A one-line defense (reject any input above a perplexity threshold) neutralizes GCG almost completely.

Neither family is both automated and stealthy. The authors frame this as a discrete-optimization problem where the obvious tool — following the loss gradient token-by-token, as GCG does — is precisely what produces the giveaway gibberish, since gradient-guided token search has no incentive to stay fluent. Their question: can an optimizer search the space of jailbreak prompts while being explicitly seeded and constrained by human-written prototypes, so fluency is preserved by construction rather than imposed as an afterthought?

// how it works

The method

AutoDAN treats stealthy jailbreak generation as a genetic algorithm (GA) problem over discrete text, with the same underlying attack objective GCG uses — force the model's response to begin with an affirmative prefix (e.g. "Sure, here is how to…") — but optimized by evolving whole sentences and paragraphs rather than individual gradient-guided tokens.

1. Population initialization by prototype

Rather than start from random text, the population is seeded from an existing handcrafted jailbreak prompt (a DAN-style prototype), then diversified by asking an LLM to paraphrase it — preserving the prototype's logical structure and meaning while introducing lexical variety. This keeps the search anchored in a region of prompt-space already known to work, rather than searching blind.

2. Fitness evaluation

Each candidate prompt Ji is scored by the same log-likelihood loss GCG uses for its target string — the negative log-probability the target model assigns to an affirmative continuation:

L(Ji) = −log P(rm+1,…,rm+k | t1,…,tm)   fitness S(Ji) = −L(Ji) // t = tokens of (jailbreak prompt + malicious question); r = target affirmative-response tokens. Lower loss on the target ⇒ higher fitness.

3. Hierarchical genetic policy

The key design choice: text has structure at two levels, and AutoDAN searches both. At the sentence level, each word's fitness contribution is scored (with a momentum term averaging the current and prior iteration's score to stabilize noisy signal), and the highest-scoring words are swapped for near-synonyms elsewhere in the population. At the paragraph level, the top N·α individuals (elitism rate α) survive unchanged; the rest are chosen for crossover via softmax selection on fitness score, undergo multi-point crossover (exchanging sentences between two parent prompts at several breakpoints), and are mutated via the same LLM-paraphrasing step used at initialization. The paper calls the vanilla-crossover-only version AutoDAN-GA and the version that alternates sentence- and paragraph-level search AutoDAN-HGA; the latter avoids the local-minimum stagnation the ablations show AutoDAN-GA falls into.

The loop terminates either at a maximum iteration count or as soon as none of a predefined refusal-keyword list (e.g. "I'm sorry," "As a responsible AI") is detected among the top-K words of the model's response.

// what they found

The results

All evaluations run on AdvBench's 520 harmful-behavior requests, against Vicuna-7b, Guanaco-7b, and Llama2-7b-chat (white-box), with GCG (1000 iterations, official settings) as the automated baseline and a single handcrafted DAN prompt as the manual baseline. Two success metrics are reported: keyword-based Attack Success Rate (ASR) and a GPT-based semantic "Recheck" that verifies the response actually engages the malicious request rather than just avoiding refusal phrases. Stealthiness is GPT-2 sentence perplexity (PPL).

On the hardest target, Llama2-7b-chat, AutoDAN-HGA's 60.77% ASR beats GCG's 45.38% by 15 points, while its perplexity (54.38) sits orders of magnitude below GCG's 1027.56 and close to handcrafted DAN's 22.97. Averaged across the three models, the authors report AutoDAN-HGA improves on handcrafted DAN's average ASR by "about 250%" and on GCG's by "about 8%" — the larger relative gain over GCG shows up specifically on the more robustly aligned Llama2, since Vicuna and Guanaco are already near-saturated by GCG.

The perplexity defense (reject inputs above a threshold) is where the two automated methods diverge sharply: it crashes GCG's Llama2-7b-chat ASR from 45.4% to 0.0% and its Vicuna ASR from 97.1% to 39.2%, while AutoDAN-HGA is completely unaffected — 97.7% / 98.5% / 60.8% both before and after the filter is applied, identical to three decimal places in the paper's own tables. Cross-model transferability follows the same pattern: an AutoDAN-HGA prompt crafted on Llama2-7b-chat transfers to Vicuna-7b at 72.9% ASR versus GCG's 13.7%, and to Guanaco-7b at 73.1% versus GCG's 11.5%. Ablations attribute the gains additively — DAN initialization, then LLM-based mutation, then the hierarchical (sentence+paragraph) search each add a further jump in ASR on both Llama2 and a black-box test against GPT-3.5-turbo-0301, where AutoDAN-HGA reaches 65.8% ASR versus GCG's 16.5%. AutoDAN is also modestly faster per sample (715s vs GCG's 922s on an A100).

// does it hold up?

The eval read

GCG is a fair, standard, and well-matched baseline for the moment this paper was written — it's the pioneering automated attack, evaluated inside the same harness (same AdvBench requests, same target models, official 1000-iteration settings), which isolates the optimizer rather than confounding it with a different threat scope. But it is also the only automated baseline; there's no comparison against a broader sweep of handcrafted jailbreaks beyond a single "Handcrafted DAN" representative, and no comparison to concurrent semantic-optimization attacks like PAIR, which shipped around the same time and takes a related "LLM writes the jailbreak" approach.

The headline ASR numbers are keyword-based — a response counts as a successful attack if it avoids a list of refusal phrases like "I'm sorry" — and the paper's own semantic "Recheck" metric shows this inflates the count. On Vicuna-7b, AutoDAN-HGA's ASR (97.7%) sits 6 points above its Recheck score (91.7%); on Guanaco-7b the gap is similar (98.5% vs 93.7%). That means roughly 1 in 16 "successful" attacks in the flagship table is a keyword false positive that the paper's own stricter check doesn't confirm — a small but real overstatement baked into the number reported first. Neither ASR nor Recheck grade the severity of what's elicited (a genuinely dangerous, actionable answer vs. a vague non-refusal); unlike some contemporaneous safety-eval work that scores harm on a graded rubric, AutoDAN's metrics are binary compliance flags.

The evaluation set itself — AdvBench's 520 harmful-behavior requests, all drawn from a single source — is the field's default choice at the time, but it is still one fixed distribution of harmful phrasing; the paper's own Limitation section hedges its generality by pointing to "recent open-source benchmarks" — it cites HarmBench (Mazeika et al., 2024) and the EasyJailbreak framework (Zhou et al., 2024) — rather than claiming AdvBench alone settles the question. The main-text results are also limited to three 7B open-weight targets; the one closed-source data point (GPT-3.5-turbo-0301) appears only in the ablation table with no repeated runs, and a GPT-4 evaluation is deferred entirely to an appendix not reproduced here. As a stochastic genetic algorithm — random initialization, mutation, and crossover throughout — no run-to-run variance or seed count is reported anywhere in the main results, despite GA methods being known to vary run over run.

baselines

GCG is evaluated fairly inside the same harness, but it's the only automated baseline compared — no sweep of other handcrafted jailbreaks and no comparison to concurrent semantic-attack methods like PAIR.

contamination

Not classical leakage, but single-benchmark scope: every main-table number comes from AdvBench's 520 requests alone — the ASRs describe performance against one fixed harmful-request distribution, not "jailbreaking in general."

what's missed

Keyword ASR runs ~5–6 points above the paper's own semantic Recheck metric on Vicuna/Guanaco; neither metric grades harm severity. Only three 7B open models in the main tables; the one closed-source GPT-3.5 result and all GPT-4 results are appendix/ablation-only. No variance across GA random seeds is reported anywhere.

would raise confidence

Repeated runs with variance bars given the method is a stochastic GA, a broader closed-source main-text evaluation (GPT-4, Claude), and a harm-severity rubric alongside the binary ASR/Recheck compliance flags.

// bottom line

AutoDAN's core claim — automated jailbreak generation that stays fluent enough to defeat perplexity filtering — is well supported and reproducible within its own harness, and the perplexity-defense result (GCG: 45.4%→0.0%, AutoDAN-HGA: 60.8%→60.8%, unchanged) is the paper's most convincing single data point. The reported ASR advantage over GCG is real but modest on already-near-saturated targets (Vicuna, Guanaco) and only becomes decisive on the more robustly aligned Llama2 — where the metric's own honesty check (Recheck) shaves several points off the headline number.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. Fluency and automation are not in tension if you seed the search correctly. Initializing a genetic algorithm from handcrafted DAN-style prototypes, then evolving them with hierarchical (sentence + paragraph) crossover and LLM-based mutation, produces jailbreaks that read as ordinary English (PPL ≈ 54 vs GCG's ≈ 1028) without sacrificing much attack strength.
  2. Stealth beats a naive gradient search against a real defense. A perplexity filter drops GCG's Llama2 ASR from 45.4% to 0.0% while leaving AutoDAN-HGA's 60.8% completely untouched — the paper's clearest demonstration that optimizing purely on attack loss, with no fluency constraint, is brittle against the simplest possible content filter.
  3. The headline ASR is keyword-based and runs a few points above the paper's own semantic check. AutoDAN-HGA's Recheck score trails its ASR by roughly 5–6 points on Vicuna and Guanaco — a useful reminder that "attack success rate" in this literature usually means "didn't say a refusal phrase," not "produced verified harmful content."
