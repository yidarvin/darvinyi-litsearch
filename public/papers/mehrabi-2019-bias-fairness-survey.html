A Survey on Bias and Fairness in Machine Learning

A field-defining reference that maps 23 named forms of bias onto a data → algorithm → user-interaction feedback loop, catalogs 10 group/subgroup/individual fairness definitions, and sorts mitigation methods into pre-processing, in-processing, and post-processing across 16 ML subdomains — from classification to word embeddings.

Ninareh Mehrabi, Fred Morstatter, Nripsuta Saxena, Kristina Lerman, Aram Galstyan

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

By 2019, "fairness in machine learning" was no longer a niche concern — COMPAS was flagging Black defendants as higher-risk at nearly twice the false-positive rate of white defendants, an AI beauty-pageant judge had scored darker-skinned contestants lower, and digital cameras were misreading Asian faces as blinking. But the research response to these harms had fragmented badly. Different subfields — classification, NLP, recommender systems, causal inference, community detection — had each independently invented their own vocabulary for "bias" and their own notion of "fairness," with little cross-referencing. A method that satisfied demographic parity in one paper might be called something entirely different, or ignored altogether, in the next paper working on a different task. There was no shared map of where bias enters an ML pipeline, no agreed catalog of what a "fair" outcome even means, and no organized inventory of what had already been tried to fix it.

The authors (Mehrabi, Morstatter, Saxena, Lerman, and Galstyan, all at USC's Information Sciences Institute) set out to build that shared reference: read the scattered literature — 188 references in total — and reorganize it into one taxonomy of bias sources, one catalog of fairness definitions, and one cross-domain map of mitigation techniques. A concrete illustration of the underlying problem: geographic sampling. Two of the field's most widely used image datasets, ImageNet and Open Images, draw the overwhelming majority of their photos from the United States and Great Britain, with countries like India, China, and most of Africa barely represented — meaning a "state-of-the-art" vision model trained on either dataset inherits a Western-skewed notion of what everyday objects and scenes look like before a single line of model code is written.

// how it works

The method

As a survey, the "method" is the taxonomy itself, built around a single organizing idea: bias doesn't just sit in a dataset — it circulates in a feedback loop. Data trains an algorithm; the algorithm's outcomes shape user interactions; user-generated interactions and behavior become the next round of data. The authors place every bias definition they review on one of the three arrows of this loop (data → algorithm, algorithm → user, user → data), which is itself a genuine synthesis contribution — most of the individual bias definitions had prior citations scattered across separate papers on data collection, algorithmic design, and human-computer interaction, but hadn't previously been unified under one cyclical frame.

Counting carefully through the three sections of the taxonomy yields 23 distinctly named forms of bias. Seven sit on the data-to-algorithm arrow — measurement, omitted variable, representation, aggregation (with Simpson's paradox and the modifiable areal unit problem as named subtypes), sampling, longitudinal data fallacy, and linking bias. Five sit on the algorithm-to-user arrow — algorithmic bias, user interaction bias (with presentation and ranking bias as subtypes), popularity bias, emergent bias, and evaluation bias. Seven close the loop on the user-to-data arrow — historical, population, self-selection, social, behavioral, temporal, and content production bias. Each gets a worked real-world example: aggregation bias is illustrated with HbA1c diabetes-marker thresholds that differ by ethnicity and gender, so a model that ignores subgroup structure misdiagnoses fairly even on perfectly balanced training data; evaluation bias is illustrated with the Adience and IJB-A face-recognition benchmarks, which the authors note are themselves skewed toward lighter skin tones, so a system can look strong on the benchmark while still being unfair in deployment.

On top of the bias taxonomy, Section 4 catalogs how "fairness" itself has been formalized. Table 1 sorts 10 named fairness definitions — demographic parity, conditional statistical parity, equalized odds, equal opportunity, treatment equality, test fairness, subgroup fairness, fairness through unawareness, fairness through awareness, and counterfactual fairness — into three families: group fairness (treat protected groups equally in aggregate), individual fairness (give similar predictions to similar individuals), and subgroup fairness (the group criterion held over many overlapping subgroups rather than one binary split). An eleventh, fairness in relational domains, is discussed but left out of the table because it doesn't cleanly fit the group/subgroup/individual axis.

P(Ŷ=1 | A=0, Y=y) = P(Ŷ=1 | A=1, Y=y)  for y ∈ {0, 1} // Definition 1, Equalized Odds — one of the ten group/subgroup/individual definitions catalogued in Table 1; protected and unprotected groups A must see equal true- and false-positive rates

Finally, Section 5 sorts existing mitigation methods into three cross-cutting families regardless of domain: pre-processing (transform the training data itself to remove discrimination before a model ever sees it), in-processing (modify the learning algorithm's objective or constraints during training), and post-processing (treat a trained model as a black box and re-calibrate its outputs afterward using a held-out set). Adversarial learning is one of the in-processing families given its own worked diagram: a predictor network tries to produce an accurate label ŷ while an adversary network simultaneously tries to recover the protected attribute ẑ from that prediction, and the predictor is penalized whenever the adversary succeeds — forcing accuracy and fairness into direct competition during training.

// what they found

The results

The survey's deliverable is coverage, not a single number — but that coverage is large and specific. Section 5 walks 16 distinct ML subdomains where bias-mitigation work has been done: classification, regression, PCA, community detection, clustering, graph embedding, causal inference, variational autoencoders, adversarial learning, word embeddings, coreference resolution, language models, sentence embeddings, machine translation, semantic role labeling, and named entity recognition — each with its own short literature review of what "fair" means there and what's been tried. Table 3 separately catalogs the protected attributes actually recognized in US law (race, color, national origin, religion, sex, familial status, disability, and more, under the Fair Housing Act and Equal Credit Opportunity Act), grounding the abstract fairness definitions in the legal categories they're meant to operationalize.

The appendix adds a practical layer on top of the taxonomy: a table of the eight most widely used fairness benchmark datasets, spanning very different scales and domains.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

Judge this as a taxonomy paper, not an empirical one — the question isn't "are the numbers right" but "did it organize the field correctly, and has that organization lasted." On both counts it holds up unusually well for a fast-moving area. The strongest evidence is this graph itself: six of the specific fairness-definition papers this survey catalogs — Dwork et al.'s individual fairness (2012), Hardt et al.'s equalized odds and equal opportunity (2016), Kleinberg et al.'s impossibility result on risk scores (2016), Chouldechova's calibration/error-rate incompatibility result on COMPAS (2016), Zafar et al.'s disparate-mistreatment framework (2016), and Corbett-Davies et al.'s cost-of-fairness analysis (2017) — are independently tracked as separate nodes here, each with 1,300 to 5,200 citations of its own. A survey that correctly identifies the field's actual foundational papers, rather than padding its reference list with tangential work, is doing its core job well.

The taxonomy is also more honest than it needed to be about its own limits. Rather than presenting the 10 cataloged fairness definitions as complementary options, the paper explicitly foregrounds Kleinberg's and Chouldechova's impossibility results — that calibration and balanced error rates cannot both hold except in narrow special cases — and states plainly in its own future-work section that "having a more unified fairness definition and framework" remains an open problem. That's a more honest posture than most surveys take with an unresolved tension in their own subject matter.

Where it shows its age: this is a pre-generative-AI snapshot. Posted to arXiv in August 2019 (and not formally published in ACM Computing Surveys until mid-2021), its running examples are recidivism scores, credit decisions, and face recognition — classic tabular and computer-vision fairness. It has essentially nothing to say about the bias problems that would come to dominate the field within three years: stereotyped or toxic text generation, RLHF-induced sycophancy and demographic skew, or bias baked into pretraining corpora at LLM scale. A direct 2023 successor, Gallegos et al.'s "Bias and Fairness in Large Language Models: A Survey," exists precisely to fill that gap — this paper is the map of the classical era, not the current one. The taxonomy also stops at cataloging: it doesn't attempt a quantitative meta-analysis of the accuracy-fairness trade-offs reported paper-by-paper across its own references (Corbett-Davies reports 4–9% forgone accuracy across three constraints; Zafar reports cutting a false-positive-rate gap from 0.18 to 0.03–0.06 for under a point of accuracy) — those numbers sit in the papers it surveys, not synthesized into one comparable table here.

coverage rigor

Strong — 188 references, and independently cross-checked: Verma & Rubin's contemporaneous "Fairness Definitions Explained" catalogs an overlapping set of definitions, and this survey correctly flags all six of the fairness-definition papers that would go on to become the field's most-cited work.

currency

Pre-LLM. Written and posted in 2019 (journal version 2021); has nothing on generative-model bias, RLHF, or LLM-scale pretraining-corpus skew — a distinct 2023 survey (Gallegos et al.) exists specifically to cover that era.

what's missed

No cross-paper synthesis of the accuracy-fairness trade-off numbers its own references report; intersectional fairness (combinations of two-plus protected attributes) gets only light treatment relative to single-attribute group fairness.

would raise confidence

An updated edition (or companion table) that (a) folds in the post-2022 LLM-bias literature and (b) tabulates the accuracy-cost numbers already scattered across its own 188 references into one comparable view.

// bottom line

The right paper to cite for "what does the classical (pre-LLM) fairness-in-ML literature look like as a whole" — the data-algorithm-user taxonomy and the 10-definition fairness catalog have proven durable enough that this graph's other fairness nodes were all things this survey already flagged as foundational. Don't reach for it expecting LLM-era bias coverage, and don't expect a ready-made answer to "which of the 10 definitions should I use" — the paper is candid that this remains unresolved.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. The paper's core contribution is a shared map for a fragmented field: a data → algorithm → user feedback loop hosting 23 named bias types, a 10-definition group/subgroup/individual fairness catalog, and a pre-/in-/post-processing taxonomy of mitigation methods spanning 16 ML subdomains, built from 188 references.
  2. Its picks have aged well — six of the specific fairness-definition papers it surveys (Dwork, Hardt, Kleinberg, Chouldechova, Zafar, Corbett-Davies) are independently among the most-cited work in the field, confirming the taxonomy correctly identified what mattered rather than diluting its coverage.
  3. It's a classical-era snapshot: pre-dates the LLM bias literature entirely, doesn't resolve the incompatibility between its own cataloged fairness definitions (and says so explicitly), and leaves the accuracy-fairness trade-off numbers scattered across its sources rather than synthesized into one table.
