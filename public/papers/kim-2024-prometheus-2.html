Prometheus 2: An Open Source Language Model Specialized in Evaluating Other Language Models

Train one evaluator for direct 1–5 scoring and another for pairwise A/B preference on the same base model — then merge their weights. The result is a single open judge that does both formats and lands far closer to GPT-4 and human ratings than any prior open evaluator.

Seungone Kim, Juyoung Suk, Shayne Longpre, Bill Yuchen Lin, Jamin Shin, Sean Welleck, Graham Neubig, Moontae Lee, Kyungjae Lee, Minjoon Seo · KAIST AI · LG AI Research · CMU · MIT · AI2 · UIC

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

Using a strong proprietary model as a judge — “LLM-as-a-judge” — is now the default way to score open-ended generations at scale. But routing every evaluation through GPT-4 or Claude is expensive, opaque, and uncontrollable: you can’t inspect the judge, you can’t pin its version, and you can’t guarantee it’ll be there tomorrow. That pushes hard toward open evaluator LMs you can run yourself.

The trouble in early 2024 was that open evaluators were weak on three fronts at once. First, their scores diverged sharply from humans — the best prior open model (Prometheus 13B) correlated only 0.449 with human FLASK ratings, while GPT-4 reached 0.679. Second, each one did only one format: an evaluator trained for direct assessment (give a single response a 1–5 score) could not do pairwise ranking (pick the better of two), and vice versa — yet both are everywhere in practice. Third, they judged against fixed, generic attributes like “helpfulness” and “harmlessness,” with no way to plug in the custom, instance-specific rubric a real evaluation usually needs.

The paper’s framing of the gap is a single picture: there is a strong evaluator group — humans, GPT-4, Claude-3-Opus — whose judgments correlate tightly with one another, and a weak group — Llama-2-Chat-70B, the original Prometheus, GPT-3.5 — that correlates with neither the strong group nor itself. The goal of Prometheus 2 is to drag an open model out of the weak cluster and into the strong one, on both evaluation formats, with arbitrary user-defined criteria.

// how it works

The method

Prometheus 2 treats evaluation as two related-but-distinct skills and learns each separately before fusing them. Direct assessment maps an instruction, a response, a reference answer, and a custom criterion to verbal feedback plus a 1–5 score. Pairwise ranking maps an instruction, two responses, a reference, and a criterion to feedback plus a decision of which response wins. Both are conditioned on a user-defined criterion — and to the authors’ knowledge this is the first work to study fine-grained, instance-wise criteria in the pairwise setting.

Two datasets, one of them new

Training needs supervision in both formats. For direct assessment the authors reuse the Feedback Collection from the original Prometheus: 1K evaluation criteria, 20K instructions, 20K reference answers, and 100K (response, score 1–5) judgments with GPT-4-written feedback. For pairwise ranking they build the Preference Collection — the first pairwise dataset carrying over 1,000 instance-wise criteria. They derive it from the Feedback Collection: each instruction already has five scored responses, so pairing them gives ten combinations per instruction (≈200K pairs); the existing scores decide the winner, and GPT-4-1106 is prompted to write fresh comparative feedback contrasting the two responses. A human check of that synthetic feedback passed at 99.5% coherence, 98.5% suitability, and an 88% criticality win-rate. The two collections are balanced to have equal numbers of scoring decisions so training doesn’t bias one format.

The trick: merge weights, don’t jointly train

The naive way to get a dual-format evaluator is to fine-tune one model on both datasets at once (joint training). Prometheus 2 instead trains two separate models on the same base — θd on direct assessment, θp on pairwise ranking — and then merges their weights into a single evaluator:

θfinal = α · θd + (1 − α) · θp // linear weight merging of the direct-assessment and pairwise-ranking evaluators

They test six merging recipes — linear, Task Arithmetic, TIES, DARE-TIES, DARE-Linear, and SLERP — and DARE-Linear works best, so it is what ships. The base models are Mistral-7B-Instruct-v0.2 (→ Prometheus-2-7B) and Mixtral-8x7B-Instruct-v0.1 (→ Prometheus-2-8x7B). Direct assessment is run reference-based (the reference answer is appended); pairwise ranking is run reference-free.

// what they found

The results

Prometheus 2 is evaluated on eight benchmarks: four direct-assessment sets (Vicuna Bench, MT Bench, FLASK, Feedback Bench) measured by Pearson correlation with reference judges, and four pairwise sets (HHH Alignment, MT Bench Human Judgment, Auto-J Eval, Preference Bench) measured by agreement accuracy with human labels.

Direct assessment: into the strong group

Across benchmarks the Prometheus 2 models, GPT-4-1106, Claude-3-Opus, and humans all correlate above 0.5 with one another — the cluster the paper was aiming for — while base, single-format, and jointly-trained models mostly sit below 0.5. Prometheus 2 beats the prior open evaluators (Prometheus, Auto-J) by at least 0.2 Pearson across benchmarks. The sharpest single number: on FLASK, where humans correlate 0.679 with GPT-4, the best prior open model (Prometheus-13B) managed only 0.449 with humans; Prometheus-2-8x7B reaches 0.555 — halving the gap.

Pairwise ranking: beating specialists on their home turf

On all four pairwise benchmarks the Prometheus 2 models score highest among open evaluators — and notably they beat PairRM on HHH Alignment (PairRM’s own in-domain set) and Auto-J on Auto-J Eval (Auto-J’s own in-domain set), evidence that a feedback-trained large model generalizes past the specialists’ training distributions. On out-of-domain sets, Prometheus 2 at least halves the accuracy gap to proprietary judges.

Why merging beats the alternatives

The analysis section is the most interesting part. Joint training shows negative task transfer: a model trained on both formats at once is often worse than one trained on a single format. Weight merging shows positive transfer: the merged model beats both the single-format and the jointly-trained models. And the win is not just an ensembling effect — merging two models of the same format (direct+direct, or pairwise+pairwise) hurts; only merging across formats helps. The transfer is also asymmetric: sweeping α, direct-assessment performance peaks at α=0.5 but pairwise peaks at α=0.3, meaning the pairwise-trained weights help direct assessment more than the reverse.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

The engineering result is solid and reproducible: weight-merging two format specialists genuinely beats both joint training and either specialist, and the same-format ablation (Table 6) is a clean control that rules out plain ensembling. The weaker part is what the headline numbers actually mean, because the entire evaluation of this evaluator is by proxy.

Every number above is a correlation or agreement with GPT-4-1106, Claude-3-Opus, or humans. The paper is candid that this is “an indirect method.” The catch is that the training signal is partly GPT-4’s own: the Feedback Collection’s feedback and the Preference Collection’s comparative feedback were both generated by GPT-4. So a high Prometheus-2↔GPT-4 correlation is, in part, successful distillation of GPT-4’s scoring distribution — not independent evidence of judgment quality. The cleanest signal is human agreement, and there the model is clearly weaker: FLASK human correlation is only 0.555 vs GPT-4’s 0.679, and pairwise human agreement on MT-Bench Human Judgment is 71.96 vs GPT-4’s 79.90 and Claude’s 77.65. “Halves the gap” is accurate; “closely mirrors human judgement” oversells what remains a real residual gap.

Two of the eight benchmarks — Feedback Bench and Preference Bench — are in-domain test splits of the very synthetic collections used for training, which is why the model posts 0.88–0.90 there. The paper labels these honestly, but a skim could read those in-distribution numbers as generalization. The genuinely out-of-domain evidence is the FLASK / HHH / MT-Bench-HJ / Auto-J-Eval columns — fewer, and closer to the baselines.

baselines

Fair and current for May 2024 — GPT-4-1106, Claude-3-Opus, Mixtral, Prometheus v1, Auto-J, PairRM, UltraRM — and impressively they beat PairRM and Auto-J on those models’ own in-domain sets. Caveat: the untrained “prompting” baselines were run by “infinitely looping until scores can be parsed,” which the authors call “impractical for regular use” — a charitable handling that muddies the comparison.

contamination

Feedback Bench and Preference Bench are in-domain test splits of the training collections; their high scores are in-distribution, not generalization. Worth noting the α and the merge method were selected by sweeping the same eight evaluation benchmarks (Figure 3), so the merge hyperparameters are lightly fit to the test suite.

what’s missed

Pearson/accuracy on the final scalar says nothing about whether the verbal feedback is correct or useful — a judge can get the right score for the wrong reason, and feedback faithfulness is never scored (only its coherence at dataset-build time). The model is also locked to a 1–5 Likert and A/B pairwise; no 1–10, listwise-of-five, or checklist formats (the paper’s own limitation).

would raise confidence

More human-judgment benchmarks (FLASK is the only human direct-assessment set, ~200 prompts); held-out selection of α and merge method; a direct evaluation of feedback usefulness, not just the score; and a test of whether the merged model retains flexibility to a format it never saw.

// bottom line

Prometheus 2 is the strongest open evaluator of its moment, and merging-format-specialists is a genuinely useful, cheap, reproducible recipe — the negative-transfer-of-joint-training finding alone is worth the read. But “closely mirrors human and GPT-4 judgements” is carried mostly by GPT-4 imitation (partly re-distilling GPT-4-generated training feedback), and against humans — the metric that actually matters — it halves, not closes, the gap. Use it as a fast, controllable first-pass judge; don’t retire your human raters on the strength of these correlations.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. The recipe: train one evaluator for direct 1–5 scoring and one for pairwise A/B preference on the same base, then linearly merge their weights (DARE-Linear). The merged model handles both formats and beats either specialist — positive task transfer you do not get from joint training, which here transfers negatively.
  2. Unifying formats ≠ ensembling. Merging two same-format models hurts; the gain comes specifically from combining absolute and comparative evaluation. The transfer is asymmetric — pairwise helps direct more than vice versa (optimal α = 0.5 for direct, 0.3 for pairwise).
  3. Read the headline carefully. “Matches GPT-4” is anchored to GPT-4/Claude/human correlation on partly-synthetic, partly-in-domain benchmarks, and high GPT-4 agreement partly reflects distilling GPT-4-generated training feedback. The honest read: it halves the human-alignment gap (FLASK human r = 0.555 vs GPT-4’s 0.679), not closes it.
  4. Reusable artifact: the Preference Collection — the first pairwise-preference dataset with 1K+ instance-wise custom criteria — is valuable on its own, beyond the merged model.
