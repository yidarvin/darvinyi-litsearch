LLM Novice Uplift on Dual-Use, In Silico Biology Tasks

Benchmarks ask whether a model knows dangerous biology in one shot. This study asks something scarier: give a non-expert human hours with a fleet of frontier models, and how much better do they get? The answer — 4.16× more accurate than an internet-only control, beating human experts on three of four benchmarks, with almost no one reporting trouble getting dual-use information past safeguards.

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

The case for AI-bio risk has, until now, been built almost entirely on single-shot benchmarks. The Virology Capabilities Test reported that a frontier reasoning model out-troubleshoots 94% of expert virologists; LAB-Bench and others show models matching practitioners on practical life-science tasks. These are real signals, but they all measure the same narrow thing: how a model answers one query, scored in isolation.

That misses the variable that actually governs misuse risk — the human in the loop. A determined actor does not paste one question and walk away. They converse for hours, cross-check answers across several models, troubleshoot dead ends, and iteratively refine a plan. Single-shot scores could under-estimate this (a model that refuses once may comply after rephrasing) or over-estimate it (a benchmark-acing model may be useless to a novice who can't tell a correct answer from a confident hallucination). The quantity that matters is uplift: how much better does a person do with the model than with the internet alone?

Prior uplift studies that did exist — notably Anthropic's internal trials that helped push Claude Opus 4 to ASL-3 — shared two limits: they tested one model at a time, ignoring how an adversary might stitch several models into a "mosaic" to synthesize capability or route around any one guardrail, and they ran on tiny samples (8–10 participants) over short horizons. This paper sets out to measure sustained, multi-model uplift on a diverse battery of biosecurity-relevant tasks, with longitudinal data on how that uplift accrues — and what it means that the people doing it are novices.

// how it works

The method

The design is a controlled human experiment. The authors recruited 57 biology novices in two cohorts: a STEM cohort (N = 47) with Python experience who each completed one long coding/agentic task (between-subjects — each person assigned a single condition), and a non-STEM cohort (N = 10) from backgrounds like English, philosophy, and political science who worked many written tasks (within-subjects — deterministically alternated between conditions to control for individual ability). Everyone was assigned to one of two conditions:

  • Control — internet only. Crucially, AI-powered search was disabled: participants used Google's plain "Web" tab so that "AI Overview" never appeared.
  • Treatment — a tool wired to multiple frontier models (o3, o4-mini, Gemini 2.5 Pro, Claude 3.7 Sonnet, and Claude Opus 4 after its mid-study release), plus Gemini Deep Research capped at one request per hour. Multi-model access is the point: it simulates an actor who cross-validates and plays models against each other.

Tasks ran across eight biosecurity-relevant benchmarks (ten tasks total) spanning multiple-choice knowledge, long-form written reasoning, and agentic coding — from the Virology Capabilities Test (VCT) and Human Pathogen Capabilities Test (HPCT) to LAB-Bench, Humanity's Last Exam (HLE), and two coding suites (Long-Form Virology and the Agentic Bio-Capabilities Benchmark). Time budgets were generous — up to 13 hours for the most involved task — so the study could see uplift accrue rather than catching a single snapshot.

Two reference points were collected for comparison. Human experts answered each question with any resources they liked except LLMs or colleagues, in a 15–30 minute window. Standalone LLMs were run zero-shot through the UK AI Safety Institute's Inspect harness (10 trials each on o3, Claude Sonnet 4, Opus 4, and Gemini 2.5 Pro), with refusals scored as 0. Throughout, the data-collection team was blind to ground-truth answers, and the platform logged every model call to deter Control-group cheating.

Beyond final scores, the study collected longitudinal data: a "best guess" answer and a self-reported confidence at regular checkpoints, plus running Google-Doc notes. A condition-blind qualitative pipeline — LLM annotators, text embeddings, and regex — then catalogued 28 behaviors (deference, verification, sentiment, safety) from those notes, with effects estimated by mixed-effects models and false-discovery-rate control. // what they found

The results

The headline is a single number: novices with LLM access were 4.16× more accurate than internet-only controls (95% CI [2.63, 6.87]). In absolute terms — after adjusting for variability across tasks and people — LLM access lifted novice accuracy from roughly 5% to over 17%.

odds-ratio = odds(correct | Treatment)odds(correct | Control) = 4.16 // 95% CI [2.63, 6.87]; absolute accuracy ≈ 5% → 17%

That advantage held almost everywhere: Treatment beat Control on 7 of 8 benchmarks. On VCT, Treatment scored 0.277 vs 0.051; on HPCT, 0.413 vs 0.104 (nearly 4×). The single biggest gap was on the ABC-Bench fragment-decomposition coding sub-task: 0.778 vs 0.167. The lone exception was Long-Form Virology, where Control (0.582) and Treatment (0.534) were statistically tied — because participants were handed the canonical reverse-genetics paper up front, turning the task from "find the literature" into "interpret this PDF," and erasing the search advantage LLMs provide.

Most striking is what happens against the two reference groups. Against human experts, LLM-assisted novices won on three of the four benchmarks with expert data: HPCT (0.413 vs 0.310) and VCT (0.277 vs 0.222), with experts holding the line only on MBCT (0.325 vs 0.253). Non-experts, given a model and time, matched or beat the credentialed professionals.

Against standalone LLMs, though, the humans usually lost. On MBCT the model alone scored 0.492 vs the Treatment human's 0.253; on LAB-Bench, 0.605 vs 0.422. The interpretation: on well-structured tasks the human is often a bottleneck, adding noise to what the model would have gotten right alone — a sign that novices are still wielding these tools sub-optimally. The exception is telling: on HLE, the open-ended short-answer set, Treatment (0.278) clearly beat both the average (0.107) and best (0.211) standalone LLM. When the task is unstructured, iterative human–LLM collaboration earns its keep.

Confidence, calibration, and how the writing changed

LLM access raised confidence at every time step, and Treatment participants improved over time on most benchmarks while Control stayed flat. Both groups were badly over-confident (calibration curves sit below the diagonal), but Treatment was better calibrated above 40% stated confidence — at 100% confidence they averaged ≈0.45 accuracy vs ≈0.35 for Control.

The qualitative pipeline shows the model didn't just inform answers — it often wrote them. Treatment responses carried far more explicit step-by-step structure and were measurably longer, matching the stylistic fingerprint of "thinking" models. Tellingly, the one effect that did not move was LLM refusal.

Participants didn't blindly defer: 83.1% tried to verify outputs and 51.9% expressed uncertainty when models disagreed — yet greater deference correlated with better scores, hinting that novice under-reliance is part of why humans trailed the standalone models. The most-used forms of help were research (93.5%) and direct answers (80.5%); participants rated conceptual explanations (35%) and information retrieval (25%) as most useful, but error correction (rated useful by only 12%) was statistically a bigger driver of actual performance. LLM use also raised gratitude (+45.0 pp) and, counter-intuitively, frustration (+38.6 pp) — verbose, hard-to-parse output cuts both ways.

// the safety headline

89.6% of Treatment participants gave no indication of any difficulty getting past the models' safeguards. Current safety training, the authors conclude, didn't just fail to block dual-use biology assistance in this realistic setting — it barely registered as an obstacle. They also argue that confident-but-wrong "misleading" responses may deter determined users better than overt refusals, which simply signal where to route around.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

This is an important, well-instrumented study that takes the right target — sustained, multi-model uplift rather than one-shot capability — and the central finding (large relative uplift, novices reaching expert-level on biosecurity knowledge tasks) is credible and consistent across benchmarks. But several of the punchiest comparisons rest on baselines and metrics that don't survive close reading, and the headline numbers need careful framing.

Start with the "novices beat experts" claim, which is the study's most quotable line. The match is profoundly time-asymmetric: experts got 15–30 minutes per question with no LLM, while Treatment novices had 1.5 to 13 hours and a fleet of frontier models. That isn't a test of whether AI makes a novice as good as an expert; it's a test of whether hours-plus-AI beats minutes-without. The honest reading is "AI closes the novice–expert gap on these benchmarks given far more time," which is still alarming — but the symmetric experiment (experts with equal time, or experts with the same AI) was not run.

The "standalone LLMs beat the humans" comparison has the opposite problem: it is cross-harness. The LLM-only numbers come from a clean zero-shot Inspect pipeline with refusals auto-scored 0, while the human numbers come from messy multi-hour transcripts. Those aren't the same measurement, so "the human is a bottleneck" — though plausible — is partly an artifact of comparing an automated grader's view of a model against a person's hand-entered answer. And the most safety-relevant counter-example, HLE (where humans did beat the model), is exactly the benchmark with known contamination: some participants found HLE questions posted online, prompting the authors to end HLE collection early and fall back on a trust-based "please don't look" instruction for LAB-Bench. The one result arguing for human value sits on the shakiest data.

On construct validity: everything here is in silico. The real historical barrier to biological weapons is wet-lab tacit skill, materials acquisition, and organization — none of which a digital uplift study touches, as the authors acknowledge. And the dramatic "4.16×" is a relative odds ratio off a tiny base (≈5% → 17%); absolute Treatment accuracy stays low (mostly 0.25–0.41), and the expert bar it clears is itself only 0.22–0.33. "Super-expert" is doing a lot of work for scores that round to "still wrong most of the time." Finally, the 89.6% safeguard figure is an absence of expressed difficulty in free-text notes, inferred by an LLM annotator — not a measured jailbreak-success rate or a measured acquisition of dangerous capability. Participants weren't necessarily trying to break guardrails, so "didn't mention struggling" is weaker evidence than the framing implies.

The statistical machinery is careful (mixed models, FDR control, Monte-Carlo CIs), but it's straining against small cells: N=10 in the non-STEM cohort, 1–2 participants per task on several static benchmarks, and a single non-blindable treatment that invites subject-expectancy bias. LFV additionally suffered an accidental prompt omission and model refusals. The conclusions are directionally robust; the per-benchmark point estimates are noisier than three-digit precision suggests.

baselines

Asymmetric. Experts capped at 15–30 min with no AI vs novices' 1.5–13 h with a model fleet; the LLM-only baseline is a different (zero-shot Inspect, refusals=0) harness than the human transcripts. "Beats experts / loses to models" both mix unequal conditions.

contamination

Acknowledged and partly uncontrolled. HLE questions were findable online (collection ended early); LAB-Bench leakage handled by a trust-based instruction. The pro-human HLE result rests on the most contaminated set. Off-platform LLM use by Control can't be fully ruled out.

what's missed

All digital — no wet-lab tacit skill or acquisition. The 4.16× is a relative OR off a ~5% base; absolute accuracy stays low. The 89.6% safeguard claim is "no expressed difficulty" in notes, not measured jailbreak success or realized capability.

would raise confidence

Match expert time budgets (and/or give experts the same AI); pre-registered, larger, properly randomized samples; a leakage-clean private-only benchmark; direct measurement of jailbreak success and dangerous-capability realization; a sham-AI or blinded control; and a wet-lab follow-up.

// bottom line

The qualitative direction is hard to argue with: give a motivated non-expert hours with several frontier models and they get dramatically better at biosecurity-relevant digital tasks, with safeguards offering little friction. That's a genuine, policy-relevant signal. Just don't read the specific numbers — "4.16×," "beats experts," "89.6%" — as clean capability measurements; each is shaped by an unequal baseline, a contaminated set, or a proxy metric. The study's real contribution is the method: sustained, multi-model, longitudinal uplift is the right thing to measure, and one-shot benchmarks alone are no longer enough.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. Uplift, not capability, is the risk metric. Over hours of interaction with multiple frontier models, novices became 4.16× more accurate than internet-only controls (≈5%→17%) on biosecurity-relevant tasks — a signal one-shot benchmarks can't see.
  2. AI closed the novice–expert gap. LLM-assisted non-experts beat human-expert baselines on 3 of 4 benchmarks — though experts had only 15–30 minutes and no AI, so this measures hours-plus-AI vs minutes-without, not parity.
  3. The human is often the bottleneck. Standalone LLMs usually out-scored LLM-assisted novices on structured tasks; humans only added value on open-ended HLE. Greater deference correlated with better scores — novices under-rely on tools that already beat them.
  4. Safeguards were a non-event. 89.6% of LLM-users reported no difficulty obtaining dual-use information, and refusal rates didn't move — but this is "no expressed difficulty" in notes, not a measured jailbreak rate.
  5. Read the numbers with the baselines. Time asymmetry, a cross-harness LLM baseline, and known HLE/LAB-Bench contamination all shape the headline figures; the durable contribution is the sustained multi-model uplift protocol itself.
