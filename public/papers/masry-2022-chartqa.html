ChartQA: A Benchmark for Question Answering about Charts with Visual and Logical Reasoning

Every prior chart-QA dataset asked template-generated questions over software-plotted charts with fixed-vocabulary answers. ChartQA instead crowdsources 9,608 human-written questions — plus 23,111 more generated from human-written chart summaries — over 20,882 real-world charts pulled from four different websites, and requires open-ended numeric answers instead of picking from a small label set. Its best model of the day answers only 45.52% of the test set correctly.

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

People ask complex questions about charts — compute a difference between two lines, find the year with the most divergent opinions, name the peak value of a specific series. Answering them takes real perceptual and cognitive work: retrieving values, comparing them, summing or averaging, and often referring to a chart's visual attributes (the "orange line," the "tallest bar," the "leftmost slice") along the way. The paper's own Figure 1 example makes the gap concrete: "Which year has the most divergent opinions about Brazil's economy?" isn't answerable by reading a single label — it requires computing the gap between two series at every year and finding the maximum.

Every existing chart-QA dataset the authors identify falls short of this on at least one axis. FigureQA, DVQA, LEAF-QA and LEAF-QA++ are template-generated — a small, fixed set of question templates instantiated automatically — so they lack the naturalness and linguistic variety of how people actually ask; their charts are also plotted synthetically with a tool like Matplotlib rather than pulled from the real world; and their answers come from a small fixed vocabulary (axis labels, "yes"/"no"), which sidesteps open-ended arithmetic entirely. PlotQA is the one exception with open-vocabulary numeric answers, but its questions are still template-based and it has no visual-reasoning questions at all. The lone prior human-authored effort, a formative study by Kim et al. (2020), collected only 52 charts and 629 QA pairs — far too small to train or benchmark a model on. The consequence on the modeling side: because most datasets only support fixed-vocabulary answers, existing systems treat ChartQA as classification and lean on dynamic encodings tied to a chart's OCR'd text positions (e.g. "x-axis-label-1") — encodings that break down under OCR noise or when a question uses a synonym for a chart label (e.g. "US" vs. "United States").

// how it works

The method

ChartQA is two contributions bundled together: a data-collection pipeline and a family of models built to exploit it. Charts are crawled from four sources chosen for stylistic and topical diversity — Statista (economy, politics, industry), Pew Research (social/demographic issues and public opinion, skewing heavily toward U.S. politics — 45.4% of its charts), Our World in Data (global economic/social/health issues), and the OECD (policy-oriented data analysis). For three of the four sources the underlying data table, metadata, and SVG markup are scraped alongside the chart image; Pew only ever yields the rendered image, since it doesn't publish its data tables.

Questions come from two pipelines that trade cost for scale. The first is fully human: Mechanical Turk workers write two kinds of questions per chart — compositional questions requiring at least two mathematical/logical operations (sum, difference, average, comparison), and visual questions that refer to a mark's visual attributes (color, height, length, position). A second, independent worker answers the same question from scratch; if the two answers match exactly the pair is kept, otherwise it's manually adjudicated. Raw inter-annotator exact-match agreement was 61.04% — but a manual re-check of 500 random pairs found 78.55% agreement once typos and lexical variants ("3$" vs. "3 dollars", "86.33" vs. "86.3") are accounted for, which is itself a signal about how brittle exact-match scoring is for this task (see "does it hold up?" below). This human pipeline produced 9,608 questions over 4,804 charts (ChartQA-H).

The second pipeline is machine-generated, built to add scale cheaply: two T5 models are fine-tuned on SQuAD — one to extract candidate answers from a passage, one to generate an answer-aware question from a (passage, answer) pair — and then applied to the human-written textual summaries Statista publishes alongside its charts. Because the summaries are human-written prose, the generated questions inherit natural, human-like phrasing. A filtering heuristic then discards any generated question whose proposed answer can't be located in the chart's own data table. On a manual check of 1,250 filtered QA pairs, 86.64% were complete, answerable, and correct. This pipeline produced 23,111 questions over 17,141 charts (ChartQA-M) — all sourced from Statista, since it's the only one of the four sources with matching human-written summaries. Combined, and after accounting for charts that supply questions to both pipelines, the benchmark spans 20,882 distinct charts and 32,719 QA pairs.

Since a chart's underlying data table isn't available at test time for most real-world charts, ChartQA also extends an existing chart-digitization model, ChartOCR, into a full data-extraction front end: keypoint-detection networks locate a chart's structural elements (plot area, title) and its data-encoding marks (bars, line points), a CRAFT text-recognition model reads the axis and legend text, and the two are fused using positional and color cues into a fully-structured data table — something the original ChartOCR didn't do (it recovered raw mark values without ever tying them back to axis or legend labels).

The extracted (or gold) data table and the question are then handed to one of four QA models. T5 flattens the table into text and generates the answer directly as a sequence. VL-T5 extends T5 with Mask R-CNN visual object features. TaPas extends BERT with row/column positional embeddings plus two output heads — an aggregation-operation head (SUM, AVERAGE, COUNT, DIFF, RATIO, NONE, …) and a cell-selection head whose selected cells the operation is applied to. And the paper's own contribution, VisionTaPas, adds a third input stream: a ViT encoder over the raw chart image, fused with TaPas's question/table encoding through a 4-block cross-modality encoder (parallel cross-attention in each direction, followed by self-attention and a feed-forward layer, each wrapped in the usual residual-plus-layer-norm). Because ChartQA's questions lean heavily on subtraction and ratio — operations the original TaPas aggregation head doesn't support — the authors extend the head with DIFF and RATIO classes, supervised with a heuristic that searches a chart's own data table for two cells whose difference or ratio matches the gold numeric answer. That heuristic is necessarily noisy: a manual check of 100 such generated labels found 24% were wrong (i.e., the "correct" cell pair the heuristic picked wasn't actually what the question referred to), so a real slice of VisionTaPas's operation-specific training signal is essentially guessed.

Evaluation uses relaxed accuracy: a numeric answer counts as correct if it falls within 5% of the gold value (to absorb noise from automatic data extraction), while non-numeric answers still require an exact string match.

// what they found

The results

The headline result is that ChartQA is unsaturated, unlike its predecessors. VisionTaPas — the paper's own model — is the best performer on the new benchmark, but reaches only 45.52% relaxed accuracy on the test set when the chart's data table has to be extracted automatically (not given as gold), and just 29.60% on the harder, fully human-written ChartQA-H split specifically, versus 61.44% on the machine-generated ChartQA-M split. For comparison, these same architectures are close to solved on the older, template-based, synthetic benchmarks: VisionTaPas reaches 94.54% on DVQA (a 14.5-point margin over the PReFIL baseline) and 91.46%/91.45% on FigureQA's two validation splits. PReFIL itself — a fixed-vocabulary classification baseline that works well on those older datasets (93–95% there) — collapses to 4.8% test accuracy on ChartQA, the clearest evidence that open-vocabulary numeric answering, not just "harder questions" in the abstract, is what actually differentiates this benchmark.

When the gold data table is provided, accuracy rises across the board (VisionTaPas test accuracy reaches 61.84%), confirming that a real chunk of the difficulty is the automatic chart-to-table extraction step rather than the reasoning itself. An ablation removing the DIFF/RATIO operation-head supervision drops VisionTaPas's overall test accuracy from 45.52% to 43.72% (−1.80 points) — and hits the human-written ChartQA-H split harder, 29.60%→24.84% (−4.76 points), since that's where subtraction/ratio questions concentrate. A transfer experiment tells a similar story from another angle: pretraining VL-T5 on PlotQA and fine-tuning on ChartQA lifts it from 41.56% to 51.84% (+10.28), while VisionTaPas gains only marginally (45.52%→47.08%, the authors attribute this to VisionTaPas's lack of support for nested arithmetic operations, which PlotQA's questions don't help it learn). But training on PlotQA and evaluating directly on ChartQA with no fine-tuning at all collapses both models — VisionTaPas falls to 31.96% and VL-T5 to 20.96% — underscoring that whatever these models learn from an older synthetic chart-QA dataset transfers poorly to ChartQA's questions.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

Start with the metric. Relaxed accuracy — within 5% of the gold numeric value counts as correct — was borrowed from PlotQA specifically to absorb noise from automatic chart-to-table extraction, not to forgive reasoning errors. It's a reasonable design for that narrow purpose, but it doesn't fully solve open-ended numeric matching. A fixed 5% relative tolerance behaves very differently at different magnitudes: for a gold answer of "2", ±5% is a band of ±0.1, functionally an exact-match requirement, while for a gold answer in the thousands it's a wide enough window that a model could land inside it by reading the wrong-but-nearby data point rather than by getting the actual arithmetic right. And the metric only relaxes the numeric case — non-numeric answers still require an exact string match, with no tolerance for the "lexical variation" problem the paper documents in its own annotators: its human-agreement study found raw exact-match agreement of just 61.04%, rising to 78.55% only after manually forgiving typos and phrasing differences like "3$" vs. "3 dollars". That gap is real evidence that exact-match under-scores genuinely correct answers — and it's a gap the paper diagnoses in humans but never revisits in how it scores models, so some fraction of every model's reported error rate is almost certainly answer-formatting mismatch rather than reasoning failure.

Next, how much of "logical reasoning" here is genuinely general versus a chart-specific patch. The paper's headline modeling contribution — extending TaPas's aggregation head with DIFF and RATIO operations — is trained with heuristically generated supervision: search the chart's data table for two cells whose difference or ratio equals the gold answer, and treat that pair as the "correct" cells to select. On a 100-question manual check, 24% of these labels were wrong. That's not a small caveat: a quarter of the direct supervision behind the paper's one clearly novel reasoning capability is noisy pattern-matching over coincidental numeric relationships in the table, not a verified reasoning trace. The paper is also explicit that VisionTaPas cannot handle nested operations at all (Figure 4's example: add two numbers, then subtract from a third) — so "logical reasoning" in this system means exactly one arithmetic step, selected via a heuristically-labeled classifier head, and nothing more compositional. The cross-dataset transfer results reinforce the same conclusion from a different angle: a VisionTaPas trained on PlotQA and evaluated directly on ChartQA (no fine-tuning) collapses from 45.52% to 31.96%, and VL-T5 collapses from 41.56% to 20.96% — whatever "reasoning" these models learn is heavily tied to the specific dataset's chart styles and phrasing, not a transferable arithmetic-over-charts competence.

Finally, the composition risk this repo's own graph makes visible: MathVista (Lu et al., 2024) draws directly on ChartQA as one of its constituent chart-reasoning sources when assembling a "new" multimodal-math benchmark. That's a common and reasonable way to build a broader benchmark quickly, but it means any weakness baked into ChartQA — the 24%-noisy DIFF/RATIO supervision, the ~13–21% exact-match disagreement even among independent human annotators, the fact that ChartQA-M's 23,111 questions are all sourced from a single site's (Statista) auto-generated summaries — propagates silently into every downstream benchmark that reuses it, without necessarily being re-audited there. As ChartQA becomes a standard fine-tuning corpus for chart-VQA models (which it plainly has, given citation volume), any newer benchmark assembled by resampling or relabeling ChartQA's own charts also carries a live train/test overlap risk for models that were explicitly trained on ChartQA before being evaluated on the descendant benchmark — a risk that's invisible unless someone tracks chart provenance across the benchmark family.

baselines

Fair and appropriately chosen for 2022 — PReFIL and a faithful PlotQA reimplementation (using the paper's own better data-extraction pipeline, since PlotQA's original extractor doesn't generalize to real-world charts) are the right comparisons, and the four QA models (T5/VL-T5/TaPas/VisionTaPas) are evaluated identically across all four datasets. No modern (post-2023) multimodal LLM is or could be tested, since none existed yet.

contamination

Not classic train/test leakage, but a structural composition risk: ChartQA is reused wholesale as a source dataset by later benchmarks (MathVista among them), so its own annotation noise and single-site (Statista) skew for the machine-generated half propagate into any benchmark built on top of it, and models fine-tuned on ChartQA before being scored on a descendant benchmark carry an unaudited overlap risk.

what's missed

The 5% relaxed-accuracy tolerance and exact-match-for-non-numeric scoring don't account for the lexical-variation gap the paper's own human-agreement study surfaces (61.04% vs. 78.55%), so a real slice of every model's reported error is answer-formatting mismatch rather than reasoning failure; separately, the DIFF/RATIO supervision behind the paper's core reasoning claim is 24%-noisy heuristic labeling, not verified reasoning traces.

would raise confidence

A normalized/paraphrase-tolerant metric for non-numeric answers (mirroring the numeric 5% tolerance); a larger, blind re-annotation of the machine-generated split beyond the 1,250-example spot check; a published noise audit of the DIFF/RATIO heuristic labels beyond the 100-question sample; and provenance metadata on individual charts so downstream benchmarks that resample ChartQA (like MathVista) can be audited for overlap with any model's ChartQA fine-tuning data.

// bottom line

ChartQA correctly diagnosed a real gap — chart QA benchmarks were synthetic, template-based, and fixed-vocabulary — and its human-authored, open-vocabulary reformulation genuinely un-saturates the task: the same architectures that hit 90–98% on FigureQA and DVQA fall to 29.6–45.5% here. That's a meaningful, well-evidenced result. But the benchmark's own numbers show its "logical reasoning" claim is narrower than the title suggests — single-step operations only, learned from 24%-noisy heuristic supervision, that transfer poorly across even a closely related dataset (PlotQA) — and its evaluation protocol (relaxed numeric accuracy, exact-match otherwise) inherits an annotator-disagreement problem the paper documents but doesn't fix. Given how directly later benchmarks like MathVista build on this dataset, those two soft spots are worth tracking rather than assuming solved.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. ChartQA replaced template-generated, fixed-vocabulary chart QA (FigureQA, DVQA, LEAF-QA, PlotQA) with 9,608 human-written plus 23,111 machine-generated questions over 20,882 real-world charts, requiring open-ended numeric answers — and un-saturates the task in the process: models that hit 90–98% on the older synthetic benchmarks fall to 29.6–45.5% here.
  2. Its own model, VisionTaPas — a ViT-encoded chart image fused with a TaPas table/question encoder through a 4-block cross-modality encoder, with a heuristically-supervised DIFF/RATIO extension for subtraction and ratio questions — became a template for the ViT-plus-table-encoder family of chart/document-VQA models that followed.
  3. The 5%-relaxed-accuracy metric (exact match for numeric answers within 5% of gold, strict exact match otherwise) is the evaluation convention this benchmark set for chart QA — one that later chart-reasoning benchmarks, including MathVista, which draws directly on ChartQA as a source dataset, inherited largely unchanged.
