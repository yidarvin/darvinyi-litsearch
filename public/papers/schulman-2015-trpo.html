2015 · ICML · Post-training & RL

Trust Region Policy Optimization

Prove that a KL-penalized surrogate objective guarantees monotonic policy improvement — then approximate that theory into something you can actually run: swap the intractable penalty for a single average-KL trust-region constraint, solve it with conjugate gradient and a line search, and you get a policy-gradient method that reliably learns neural-network policies for both simulated locomotion and Atari from raw pixels. Every later policy-optimization method in this atlas, PPO included, is a simplification of what TRPO proves here.

John Schulman, Sergey Levine, Philipp Moritz, Michael I. Jordan, Pieter Abbeel · UC Berkeley

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

The paper opens by sorting policy optimization into three families: policy iteration (alternate estimating a value function and improving the policy), policy gradient methods (climb an estimated gradient of expected return from sampled trajectories), and derivative-free optimizers like the cross-entropy method (CEM) and covariance matrix adaptation (CMA), which treat return as a black box. The uncomfortable empirical fact motivating the paper: on several classic benchmarks — Tetris for approximate dynamic programming, hand-engineered low-dimensional locomotion controllers for CMA — the gradient-free black-box methods were hard to beat, even though, as the authors put it, "gradient-based optimization algorithms enjoy much better sample complexity guarantees than gradient-free methods." Something in how gradient-based policy search chooses its step size was leaving that theoretical advantage on the table.

The specific failure mode is step-size control. A policy-gradient or policy-iteration update that is too large can collapse the policy's performance with no guarantee of recovery, while a step small enough to be safe is often too small to make useful progress — and there was no principled, generic way to pick the size automatically across different tasks and policy parameterizations. Prior fixes were either restricted to a narrow policy class (Kakade & Langford's conservative policy iteration only has a proven bound for mixture policies, which are "unwieldy and restrictive in practice") or required per-task hand-tuning.

TRPO's goal is to close that gap in two steps: first prove a monotonic-improvement guarantee that holds for any stochastic policy class, not just mixtures; then turn that guarantee into a practical algorithm that scales to neural-network policies with tens of thousands of parameters — validated on tasks as different as continuous-control locomotion and vision-based Atari play.

// how it works

The method

Start from an identity relating any two policies π and π̃ through the advantage function of π: η(π̃) = η(π) + Es,a~π̃[Σt γt Aπ(st,at)]. Rewritten as a sum over states weighted by π̃'s discounted visitation frequency ρπ̃, this says any update with nonnegative expected advantage everywhere is guaranteed not to make the policy worse. The catch is that ρπ̃ depends on the very policy you're trying to solve for, so the paper substitutes the current policy's visitation frequency ρπ instead, defining a local surrogate Lπ(π̃) that matches η to first order at π̃ = π but gives no guidance on step size.

Kakade & Langford's conservative policy iteration supplies that guidance, but only for policies built as an explicit mixture πnew = (1−α)πold + απ′. TRPO's first theoretical contribution is extending their improvement bound to any stochastic policy, by replacing the mixing weight α with a distance measure — the maximum total-variation divergence DmaxTV(π,π̃) between old and new action distributions across all states:

η(πnew) ≥ Lπold(πnew) − 4εγ(1−γ)²·α²,  α = DmaxTV(πold,πnew) // Theorem 1 — since D_TV(p‖q)² ≤ D_KL(p‖q), the same bound holds with a KL penalty in place of α²

Because DTV(p‖q)² ≤ DKL(p‖q), this immediately gives a bound in terms of KL divergence instead: η(π̃) ≥ Lπ(π̃) − C·DmaxKL(π,π̃). Repeatedly maximizing the right-hand side — a minorize-maximize (MM) scheme, the same family as EM — provably produces a non-decreasing sequence η(π₀) ≤ η(π₁) ≤ …. That's the theory: a real, if unglamorous, monotonic-improvement proof for general stochastic policies.

From penalty to trust region

In practice the theoretically-required penalty coefficient C is so large that it forces prohibitively tiny steps. TRPO's practical move is to replace the penalty with a hard constraint instead — a trust region: maximize the surrogate subject to the KL divergence staying under a fixed budget δ. Constraining the divergence at every state (the max-KL form) is theoretically cleanest but "impractical to solve due to the large number of constraints," so the paper relaxes to the average KL divergence under the current policy's own state distribution:

maximizeθ  Lθold(θ)   subject to   Es~ρθold[DKL(πθold(·|s) ‖ πθ(·|s))] ≤ δ // δ = 0.01, held fixed across every experiment in the paper — the one knob

The objective itself is estimated from Monte Carlo rollouts via importance sampling: Lθold(θ) = Es~ρθold, a~q[ (πθ(a|s)/q(a|s))·Qθold(s,a) ]. Two sampling schemes fill in q and the Q-estimate. Single path just runs the current policy forward and uses every state-action pair along the trajectory — the standard scheme, and the only one that needs no ability to reset the simulator, so it's the one compatible with a physical system. Vine instead builds a "rollout set" of states from trunk trajectories, then branches multiple actions from each state and rolls out each branch separately, reusing common random numbers (CRN) across branches to cancel simulation noise. Vine gives lower-variance advantage estimates for the same number of Q-value samples, at the cost of needing many more simulator calls and the ability to reset to an arbitrary state — so it only works in simulation.

Solving the constrained problem itself uses conjugate gradient followed by a line search — "altogether only slightly more expensive than computing the gradient itself." The curvature matrix in that computation is the Fisher information matrix of the policy, but rather than estimate it from the empirical covariance of sampled policy gradients, TRPO computes it analytically as the Hessian of the KL divergence, which "does not depend on the action an that was sampled" and avoids ever forming or storing a dense Hessian — the detail that lets the method scale past small policies. As a byproduct, the paper shows TRPO's update recovers the classic natural policy gradient as a special case (linearizing L and quadratically approximating the KL constraint, with a fixed-penalty stepsize instead of an enforced constraint), and recovers vanilla policy gradient by swapping the KL constraint for an ℓ₂ constraint — a genuinely unifying picture of prior policy-update schemes.

// what they found

The results

On simulated MuJoCo locomotion — cart-pole balancing plus three progressively harder robots (swimmer: 10-dimensional state; hopper: 12-dimensional, terminates on falling; walker: 18-dimensional, with a penalty added for hard foot impacts) — single-path and vine TRPO were compared against natural gradient, empirical-FIM TRPO, CEM, CMA, and reward-weighted regression (RWR), all with δ = 0.01 and averaged over 5 runs. The result, in the paper's words: single-path and vine TRPO "solved all of the problems, yielding the best solutions." Natural gradient (a fixed penalty coefficient instead of an enforced constraint) kept pace on cart-pole and swimmer but "was unable to generate hopping and walking gaits that made forward progress" — direct evidence, per the authors, that constraining the KL divergence is "a more robust way to choose step sizes" than a fixed penalty. CEM and CMA, being derivative-free, scaled poorly as the parameter count grew.

On Atari — 7 games from the Arcade Learning Environment, played from raw screen pixels with a 33,500-parameter convolutional policy (two 4×4 conv layers of 16 filters, one 20-unit fully-connected layer) — TRPO ran for 500 iterations, about 30 hours on a 16-core machine, once per game with no averaging across seeds. By these numbers, TRPO beats DQN's score on Enduro, Pong, and Seaquest (single path) and by a wide margin on Q*bert (vine, 7,732.5 vs. DQN's 1,952), but never beats UCC-I, and single path trails DQN on Beam Rider (1,425.2 vs. 4,092) and Space Invaders. The paper is candid about this: "our method only outperformed the prior methods on some of the games," framing the result as evidence of generality — the same algorithm, essentially unmodified, learns competent policies for both continuous-control locomotion and discrete vision-based game play — rather than a claim of new state-of-the-art scores.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

TRPO's theory section is rigorous — Theorem 1 is a real proof, not an appeal to intuition — but the experimental section is markedly thinner, and the paper is unusually honest about exactly where. Table 1's own caption states it plainly: "Performance varies substantially from run to run…but we could not obtain error statistics due to time constraints." Every Atari number in that table is a single run, on an algorithm the authors elsewhere describe as high-variance across seeds — meaning none of the TRPO-vs-DQN-vs-UCC-I gaps in the table (some as small as a few percent, e.g. Pong's 20.9 vs. 20.0 vs. 21) come with any indication of whether they're noise.

The baselines compound this: DQN's and UCC-I's numbers are copied from their own original papers (Mnih et al., 2013; Guo et al., 2014) rather than reproduced under matched preprocessing, network capacity, or compute budget by the TRPO authors. A 33,500-parameter CNN with two tiny conv layers is also far smaller than DQN's own architecture, so a game where TRPO underperforms could reflect capacity as much as algorithm. The locomotion experiments are more solid — 5 runs per task with learning curves shown for all of them — but still report no confidence intervals, and the natural-gradient baseline's stepsize was chosen by "sweeping through the possible values…in factors of three, and took the best value according to the final performance" directly on the evaluation tasks themselves, a tuning asymmetry that isn't extended to TRPO's own δ (which is fixed at 0.01 everywhere, arguably a stronger and fairer claim, but the comparison protocol itself is uneven).

There's also a theory-practice gap worth naming precisely: the monotonic-improvement proof (Theorem 1, Algorithm 1) applies to the exact penalty-based MM scheme with exact advantage evaluation. What's actually run is a sample-based, average-KL constraint (not penalty) solved approximately via conjugate gradient and a line search with finite rollouts — a heuristic the paper is explicit about ("despite its approximations that deviate from the theory, TRPO tends to give monotonic improvement," an empirical observation, not a corollary of Theorem 1). The one ablation that directly tests how much is lost in that relaxation — "max KL," the per-state constraint from the theory — was "only tractable on the cart-pole problem," i.e., the single easiest task, so the approximation's cost on the harder, higher-dimensional problems where it would matter most is never measured.

baselines

DQN and UCC-I numbers are taken from their original papers, not re-run under matched architecture/compute by the authors; TRPO's own CNN (33,500 params) is much smaller than DQN's.

contamination

N/A in the LLM sense — these are simulated-control and Atari RL benchmarks with no train/test leakage concern of that kind.

what's missed

Single seed per Atari game with no error statistics ("due to time constraints"); no confidence intervals on the 5-seed locomotion curves either; the max-KL vs. average-KL approximation gap is only tested on cart-pole, the easiest task.

would raise confidence

Multiple seeds per Atari game with reported variance; DQN/UCC-I re-run under identical preprocessing and parameter budget; the max-KL ablation extended to hopper/walker/Atari where the relaxation is doing more work.

// bottom line

TRPO's lasting contribution is the theory — a genuine monotonic-improvement guarantee for general stochastic policies, and the demonstration that enforcing a KL trust region rather than a fixed penalty is what makes large, robust steps possible for neural-network policies. The experiments that go with it are era-appropriate but thin by later standards: one seed per Atari game with an explicit admission that variance couldn't be measured, baselines borrowed rather than reproduced, and the one experiment that would show how much the practical algorithm gives up relative to the theory confined to the simplest task. That the field spent the next two years stripping TRPO down to PPO's first-order clip — trading the constraint's rigor for the practicality the 2015 experiments were already straining to show — is itself a comment on how much of TRPO's value was the proof, not the specific numbers in Table 1.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. The proof, not just the algorithm. Theorem 1 extends Kakade & Langford's conservative-policy-iteration bound from restrictive mixture policies to any stochastic policy, via total-variation and then KL divergence — giving a genuine monotonic-improvement guarantee that the practical algorithm only approximates.
  2. Penalty in theory, trust region in practice. The theoretically-justified KL penalty forces tiny steps, so TRPO swaps it for a hard average-KL constraint with budget δ = 0.01 — solved via conjugate gradient + line search using an analytically-computed Fisher information matrix, the detail that lets it scale to neural-network policies.
  3. Single path vs. vine. Single path (one rollout, real-system-compatible) vs. vine (branching rollouts with common random numbers, lower variance but simulator-only) — a tradeoff between sample efficiency and applicability that recurs throughout RL.
  4. The numbers. TRPO solved cart-pole, swimmer, hopper and walker from scratch where natural gradient could not produce forward locomotion; on 7 Atari games it beat DQN on 3-4 of 7 (single run, no error bars) but never beat UCC-I — evidence of generality across two very different domains, not new state-of-the-art scores.
  5. The ancestor of everything downstream. PPO replaces TRPO's conjugate-gradient trust region with a first-order clip that approximates the same idea in a few lines of code; PPO in turn became the RL engine under RLHF, and the entire lineage of GRPO/DAPO-style LLM post-training methods in this atlas traces its trust-region intuition back to Theorem 1 here.
