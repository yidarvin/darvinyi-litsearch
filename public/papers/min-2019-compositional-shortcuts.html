Compositional Questions Do Not Necessitate Multi-hop Reasoning

A BERT reader that scores each context paragraph in complete isolation — no cross-paragraph attention, no way to chain evidence — reaches 67.08 F1 on HotpotQA's distractor setting, matching the multi-hop state of the art. The dataset's compositional questions don't force multi-hop reasoning; its evidence-selection procedure does most of the work of making them easy.

Sewon Min, Eric Wallace, Sameer Singh, Matt Gardner, Hannaneh Hajishirzi, Luke Zettlemoyer

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

HotpotQA (Yang et al., 2018) asks crowd workers to write questions that require reading two linked Wikipedia paragraphs, then pairs each gold pair with eight TF-IDF-selected distractor paragraphs. The dataset's headline claim is that its questions are "multi-hop" — but whether a question actually forces multi-hop reasoning is not a property of the question text alone. It's a property of the question together with the specific ten-paragraph evidence bundle a model is shown. Min et al. argue this distinction has been elided: "even highly compositional questions can be answered with a single hop if they target specific entity types, or the facts needed to answer them are redundant."

Figure 1 makes the point concretely. The question — "What is the former name of the animal whose habitat the Réserve Naturelle Lomako Yokokala was established to protect?" — reads as genuinely two-hop: find what the reserve protects, then find that animal's former name. But among the ten provided paragraphs, only one is about an animal at all. A reader (or model) never has to identify the reserve's purpose from Paragraph 5 first; it can simply scan for the one paragraph about an animal and read off the answer. The compositional structure of the question is real, but the evidence pool doesn't enforce it.

// how it works

The method

The paper's central instrument is single-paragraph BERT: a diagnostic probe, not a proposed architecture. It receives one paragraph at a time — never more than one — concatenated with the question as S = [q₁,...,qₘ, [SEP], p₁,...,pₙ], fed through BERT-base, and max-pooled into four scalars y_span, y_yes, y_no, y_empty indicating whether the answer is a span, "yes", "no", or absent from this paragraph. Run independently over all ten context paragraphs, the model picks its final answer from whichever paragraph got the lowest y_empty (least likely to contain no answer). By construction, this model has no mechanism to combine information across paragraphs — it cannot chain a fact found in one paragraph with a fact found in another. If it performs well, the dataset's evidence pools — not the model's reasoning — must be doing the disambiguating work.

Beyond the probe model, the paper runs two complementary diagnostics. First, a human ablation study: on 200 bridge-type validation questions, one group of annotators sees all ten paragraphs while another sees only nine — the eight distractors plus just one of the two intended gold paragraphs, withholding the other. If the question truly required both gold paragraphs, single-gold-paragraph humans should fail. Second, a manual categorization: the authors hand-label 100 bridge-type validation examples (taking the actual distractor paragraphs into account) into four buckets that explain why a single-hop shortcut exists, tabulated with representative questions.

// what they found

The results

On the distractor setting, single-paragraph BERT scores 67.08 F1 — despite zero cross-paragraph reasoning capacity, this beats several purpose-built multi-hop systems (BiDAF 58.99, GRN 66.71, QFE 68.06) and sits within a few points of the strongest ones (DecompRC 69.63, BERT Plus 69.76), evaluated against the official leaderboard as of March 4, 2019. In the harder open-domain (fullwiki) setting it scores 38.40 F1 — ahead of BiDAF (32.89–34.36) and GRN (36.48), roughly level with QFE's 38.06, but behind MultiQA's 40.23, DecompRC's 40.65, and well behind Cognitive Graph's 48.87.

The human ablation confirms the shortcut is not just a BERT artifact: humans score 87.37 F1 with all ten paragraphs and still 82.06 F1 with only nine — one gold paragraph deliberately withheld. And when questions are truncated to just their first five tokens from the wh-word (stripping most of the compositional content), single-paragraph BERT's F1 drops only from 67.08 to 52.13 — a 15-point fall for discarding most of the question.

The manual bucket breakdown on 100 bridge examples: 27% genuinely require multi-hop reasoning; 35% are single-hop-solvable due to weak distractors (entity-type matching picks out the one relevant paragraph); 26% are compositional but answerable from redundant evidence (part of the question can be ignored); 8% are non-compositional single-hop questions outright (4% were excluded as ambiguous).

The paper also tests whether better distractors fix the problem. Selecting the eight distractors adversarially (using single-paragraph BERT itself to pick the paragraphs most likely to fool it, from the top-50 TF-IDF candidates — with only 9.82% paragraph overlap with the original distractors) drops F1 from 67.08 to 46.84; filtering those candidates further by matching entity type drops it to 40.73. But retraining single-paragraph BERT on these harder distractor distributions recovers most of the loss: 60.10 F1 on the adversarial distractors and 58.42 F1 on the type-filtered ones. In the open-domain setting, retrieval is the bottleneck, not reasoning: with 500 retrieved paragraphs the model reaches only 39.12 F1, but manually adding the two true gold paragraphs to that pool jumps it to 53.12 F1.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

This is a diagnostic paper, not a benchmark or a model paper, and it should be read that way: its contribution is a methodology for testing whether a stated reasoning requirement is actually enforced by a dataset's evidence-selection procedure, using HotpotQA as the case study. Judged as diagnostic methodology, it is careful and multi-pronged — a zero-capacity probe model, a human ablation with a real control condition, and a hand-labeled taxonomy of failure causes, each pointing the same direction. That convergence across three independent measurement methods is the paper's real strength.

The comparison table (BiDAF, GRN, QFE, DFGN+BERT, DecompRC, Cognitive Graph) uses the official leaderboard "as of March 4th, 2019" — contemporary and fair for the paper's moment, though it means the specific 67.08-vs-69.76 F1 gap to the strongest multi-hop model of the day is now a historical snapshot; it says nothing about whether modern retrieval-augmented or long-context LLM multi-hop systems have the same vulnerability (a question later papers like IRCoT and MuSiQue pick up directly).

The paper's own remediation attempt undercuts a simple "just build better distractors" fix: adversarially selected distractors crater accuracy (67.08 → 46.84, or 40.73 with entity-type filtering), but a single-paragraph model retrained on the new distractor distribution recovers most of the gap (60.10 / 58.42) — suggesting the underlying problem (TF-IDF-style distractor selection failing to produce paragraphs that are topically confusable but not answer-bearing) is not a one-shot patch. The paper is explicit about this: "these distractors are still insufficient." The manual 100-example taxonomy also rests on a single annotation pass with no reported inter-annotator agreement — a modest sample for a categorical claim this influential.

baselines

Fair and current for a March 2019 submission — compared against the official HotpotQA leaderboard's top systems of the day, not a strawman.

contamination

Not applicable in the usual sense; the diagnostic model is deliberately capacity-limited (no cross-paragraph attention) rather than trained on held-out data it shouldn't see.

what's missed

No inter-annotator agreement reported for the 100-example manual bridge-question taxonomy (27/35/26/8/4% split), and the adversarial-distractor fix is shown to be only partial without a deeper root-cause remedy proposed.

would raise confidence

A larger, IAA-scored annotation sample for the shortcut taxonomy, and a follow-up testing whether the same single-hop-solvability pattern holds for modern retrieval-augmented multi-hop systems rather than 2019-era readers.

// bottom line

The finding that a zero-capacity single-hop model matches multi-hop state-of-the-art on HotpotQA's distractor setting is well-triangulated across model, human, and manual-annotation evidence, and it correctly locates the cause in evidence-selection (TF-IDF distractors) rather than question design. It set the template — later followed by 2WikiMultihopQA and MuSiQue — for treating "requires reasoning across N documents" as a claim that must be empirically verified against a benchmark's actual evidence pool, not assumed from how the questions were written.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. A single-hop BERT model with no cross-paragraph attention scores 67.08 F1 on HotpotQA's distractor setting — comparable to the era's state-of-the-art multi-hop systems.
  2. Manual categorization of 100 bridge questions finds only 27% genuinely require multi-hop reasoning; 35% are solvable via weak distractors and 26% via redundant evidence.
  3. Humans withheld one of the two gold paragraphs still score 82.06 F1 (vs. 87.37 F1 with both) on 200 bridge questions — the dataset barely taxes single-hop humans either.
  4. Adversarially selected distractors crater single-hop F1 to 46.84, but retraining on the new distractor distribution recovers most of it (60.10) — the fix is shallow, not structural.
