2019 · ICLR · Benchmarks & Evals

ImageNet-trained CNNs are biased towards texture; increasing shape bias improves accuracy and robustness

A well-powered psychophysics study (97 lab observers, 48,560 trials) pits shape against texture using style-transfer cue-conflict images and finds humans vote shape 95.9% of the time while four standard ImageNet-trained CNNs vote texture 57–83% of the time. Retraining the identical ResNet-50 on a stylized ImageNet then flips its shape bias from 22% to 81% — and buys accuracy, detection, and corruption-robustness gains along the way.

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

The dominant intuition about how CNNs recognize objects is what the authors call the shape hypothesis: low-level edge detectors compose into parts, parts compose into shapes, and the object is read off from shape — Kriegeskorte (2015) writes that high-level units "appear to learn representations of shapes occurring in natural images," and LeCun et al. (2015) describe intermediate layers detecting "objects as combinations of [object] parts." That story is backed by real evidence: deconvolutional visualizations tend to highlight object parts, and CNNs are among the best predictive models of primate ventral-stream shape responses.

But a set of separate, disconnected findings had started to point the other way. Gatys et al. (2015) found that a linear classifier on top of a CNN's texture (Gram-matrix) representation loses almost no ImageNet accuracy relative to the full network. Brendel & Bethge (2019) showed that BagNets — CNNs restricted to a tiny receptive field, unable to represent any long-range shape — can still solve ImageNet to a high accuracy. And standard CNNs are conspicuously bad at classifying sketches (Ballester & de Araújo, 2016), where shape is fully intact but texture is stripped away. Nobody had put the two hypotheses through a direct, controlled test on the same images. Worse, the obvious way to test it — show a network a silhouette or an edge map — confounds "texture removed" with "an image statistic the network has literally never seen during training," so a network's failure on those stimuli could just be domain shift, not evidence about what cue it prefers. A clean test needs images that keep natural image statistics intact while making shape and texture disagree about the answer.

// how it works

The method

The paper's central instrument is the texture-shape cue conflict stimulus: take a natural "content" image (say, a tabby cat) and a "texture" image from a different category (a patch of elephant skin), and run iterative neural style transfer (Gatys et al., 2016) between them so the output keeps the cat's silhouette but is rendered entirely in elephant-skin texture. A standard ResNet-50 that sees the pure texture patch says "Indian elephant" (81.4%); the same network on the plain cat photo says "tabby cat" (71.1%); on the cue-conflict composite it says "Indian elephant" again (63.9%) — following the texture, not the shape, of the very same underlying cat.

The full stimulus set has 1,280 such cue-conflict images (80 per category) across 16 coarse, ImageNet-adjacent "entry-level" categories, built only from texture and content images that all four test networks (AlexNet, GoogLeNet, VGG-16, ResNet-50) already classified correctly on their own — so any confusion on the composite is attributable to the conflict itself, not to a bad source image. Five further non-conflict manipulations (greyscale, filled silhouette, Canny edges, and pure texture crops) round out nine experiments in total.

Human data came from a genuinely well-controlled psychophysics setup rather than a crowdsourcing platform: 300 ms fixation, 200 ms stimulus presentation, a 200 ms pink-noise mask, then a 1,500 ms forced choice among 16 category icons arranged in a 4×4 response grid, with entirely neutral instructions ("click on the object category that you see... guess if unsure — there is no right or wrong answer"). In total, 97 observers completed 48,560 trials, paid or given course credit, with a performance-linked bonus used specifically to discourage careless answers.

The second methodological contribution is Stylized-ImageNet (SIN): every image in ImageNet's training set is stripped of its own texture and re-skinned with the style of a randomly chosen painting, using fast AdaIN style transfer (Huang & Belongie, 2017) at stylization coefficient α = 1.0 and a painting drawn from Kaggle's Painter-by-Numbers set (79,434 paintings), each source image stylized exactly once. Note the deliberate mismatch: SIN is built with fast AdaIN transfer, while the cue-conflict eval images are built with the slower, iterative Gatys et al. (2016) optimization — different algorithms for training-time texture removal and test-time texture removal, so a network cannot simply learn to invert the one procedure used to build its own exam.

// sanity check: did SIN actually remove the texture shortcut?

The authors verify SIN can't just be "solved" the same lazy way as ImageNet using BagNets — CNNs whose receptive field is capped so small they can only integrate local texture, no long-range shape. A BagNet with a 9×9-pixel receptive field reaches 70.0% top-5 accuracy trained and tested on ImageNet, but collapses to 10.0% top-5 trained and tested on SIN — confirming SIN genuinely forces a network away from local-texture shortcuts.

// what they found

The results

On the non-conflict manipulations, CNNs track humans closely on original, greyscale, and pure-texture images (all ≥90% for both) but fall well behind on silhouettes (CNN average 44–54% vs. 75% human) and further behind on edge drawings (18–40% vs. 87% human) — suggestive, but confounded by the domain-shift problem described above. The decisive test is the cue-conflict experiment, and it is stark: human observers answer with the shape category on 95.9% of correctly-attributable trials. All four standard, ImageNet-trained CNNs show the mirror-image pattern — a texture bias: VGG-16 answers texture 82.8% of the time (17.2% shape), GoogLeNet 68.8% (31.2% shape), ResNet-50 77.9% (22.1% shape), and even the least texture-biased network, AlexNet, still answers texture 57.1% of the time (42.9% shape).

Retraining the identical ResNet-50 architecture on Stylized-ImageNet — no architectural change at all — lifts its shape-bias score from 22% (IN-trained) to 81% (SIN-trained), in many categories approaching the human level. So the fully-recipe'd "Shape-ResNet" edges out the vanilla ImageNet-only ResNet-50 on classification (76.72% vs. 76.13% top-1) while also transferring better as a Faster R-CNN backbone (Pascal VOC 2007 mAP50 70.7→75.1; MS COCO mAP50 52.3→55.2). On distortion robustness — uniform noise, contrast, high-/low-pass filtering, three "eidolon" perturbations, and phase noise — the SIN-trained ResNet-50 beats the IN-trained one on nearly every distortion type and approaches human-level curves despite never training on any of these specific degradations, with low-pass/blurring the sole distortion where SIN-trained networks trail (plausibly because paintings over-represent high-frequency brushwork).

The paper's one externally-validated robustness number comes from an appendix check against ImageNet-C (Hendrycks & Dietterich, 2019) — an independent, 15-corruption-type benchmark built by a different lab. Joint SIN+IN training improves 13 of the 15 corruption types there, and cuts mean corruption error from 76.7 (vanilla ResNet-50) to 69.3.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

This is a more careful "human vs. CNN" comparison than most of the deep-learning literature manages: an in-lab (not crowdsourced) psychophysics setup, 97 incentivized observers, neutral instructions designed to avoid experimenter-demand effects, and a sample size (48,560 trials, Table 3) built for statistical power rather than a handful of qualitative examples. That rigor is genuine and it's why this paper became foundational. But the abstract's two headline claims — "CNNs are texture-biased" and "fixing that buys general robustness" — each carry a scope caveat worth stating plainly.

On the bias claim: the diagnostic images are synthetic style-transfer composites, and the 16 categories tested are a coarse, ImageNet-adjacent grouping reused from the authors' own 2018 paper, deliberately filtered to only the texture/content pairs every network already classified correctly. That filtering keeps the comparison clean, but it also means the result is demonstrated on prototypical, easily-separable categories — not on fine-grained distinctions (dog breeds, bird species) where texture may be a genuinely diagnostic cue rather than an artifact of the training distribution.

On the robustness claim, there's a circularity worth flagging even though the authors partially guard against it: the in-house distortion battery (uniform noise, contrast, high-/low-pass, eidolons, phase noise) is inherited wholesale from the same lab's own earlier work (Geirhos et al., 2018), so "robustness generalizes to unseen distortions" is initially measured against a benchmark authored by the people making the claim. The one arm's-length check — ImageNet-C, from an outside group — is the strongest evidence in the paper, and it's telling that it's tucked into an appendix rather than the main results: it's exactly the piece that the field's later citations of this paper lean on (ImageNet-C's own benchmark paper evaluates Stylized-ImageNet training as a baseline defense). And even there, the gain is 13 of 15 corruption types, not a clean sweep, and SIN-only training tanks clean accuracy by 16 points (60.18% vs. 76.13% top-1) — the version that actually preserves accuracy is the specific SIN+IN-then-fine-tune-on-IN recipe, not "shape bias" as an abstract, portable property. The resulting 0.6-point top-1 classification gain (76.72% vs. 76.13%) is also small enough that seed-to-seed variance isn't reported, so it's hard to know how much of it is signal.

baselines

AlexNet, GoogLeNet, VGG-16, and ResNet-50 were a reasonable architecture-diversity spread for 2018, but all four share the same ImageNet training recipe, and there is no comparison to a prior debiasing method — none existed yet, since this paper originates the texture-bias framing.

contamination

Not leakage in the benchmark-contamination sense (this is psychophysics plus retraining, not scores on a static test set), but there is a related circularity: the eval stimuli and the fix both come from style transfer. The authors mitigate this by using a different stylization algorithm for training (fast AdaIN) than for evaluation (iterative Gatys et al.), which is a real safeguard but doesn't remove the shared dependence on style-transfer image statistics altogether.

what's missed

No test of whether shape-bias training helps modern architectures (ViTs, ConvNeXt), which already show different texture/shape profiles than 2018-era CNNs; no full ImageNet-1k version of the cue-conflict test, only 16 coarse categories; no ablation isolating "learning shape" from "training on harder/more diverse augmented images" as the actual source of the robustness gain.

would raise confidence

An independent replication of the cue-conflict psychophysics on a larger, finer-grained category set; a robustness benchmark built and scored by a group with no lab overlap (ImageNet-C moves partway there); and reported seed-to-seed variance for the 0.6-point classification gain.

// bottom line

The texture-bias finding is well-supported by a genuinely rigorous psychophysics design, and it identified — years before it became a load-bearing plank of the corruption-robustness literature — that a lot of what looked like a CNN modeling failure was actually a data-distribution artifact. But "shape bias" is best read as this paper's own name for a broader intervention (retraining on domain-diversified, texture-scrambled images), whose benefits its own numbers show are partial (13 of 15 ImageNet-C corruptions, not 15), demonstrated on one architecture (ResNet-50), and bundled with a specific SIN+IN+fine-tune recipe rather than shape bias in isolation.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. A rigorous, well-powered psychophysics paradigm (97 observers, 48,560 trials, in-lab) shows humans resolve texture-shape cue conflicts by voting shape 95.9% of the time, while four standard ImageNet-trained CNNs vote texture 57–83% of the time — a strategy gap, not just an accuracy gap.
  2. The bias is a property of the training data, not the architecture: retraining the identical ResNet-50 on Stylized-ImageNet — every training image re-skinned in a random painting's texture via AdaIN style transfer — lifts its shape bias from 22% to 81%.
  3. Shape-biased training pays off beyond the diagnostic task itself: a modest ImageNet accuracy gain (76.72% vs. 76.13% top-1), a larger object-detection transfer gain (MS COCO mAP50 52.3→55.2), and — the paper's strongest, most externally-validated result — a corruption-error drop on the independent ImageNet-C benchmark from 76.7 to 69.3, seeding the training-data-augmentation branch of the corruption-robustness literature.
