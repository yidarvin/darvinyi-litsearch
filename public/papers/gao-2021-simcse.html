SimCSE: Simple Contrastive Learning of Sentence Embeddings

Pass the same sentence through the encoder twice, let the standard dropout masks differ, and call the two embeddings a positive pair. That one-line trick gave unsupervised sentence embeddings that matched prior supervised systems — and with NLI supervision it set a new STS state of the art.

Tianyu Gao, Xingcheng Yao, Danqi Chen — Princeton NLP / Tsinghua IIIS

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

A sentence embedding is a single fixed vector meant to place semantically similar sentences near each other — the substrate for semantic search, clustering, retrieval and de-duplication. The frustrating fact circa 2021 was that a powerful pre-trained encoder did not give you a good one for free. Averaging BERT base's token vectors scores only 56.7 average Spearman correlation across the seven standard STS tasks — worse than averaging 2014-era GloVe word vectors (61.3).

The culprit is anisotropy: pre-trained embeddings collapse into a narrow cone of the vector space, so cosine similarity between even unrelated sentences runs high and carries little signal. Two families of fixes had emerged, each with a catch. Post-processing methods (BERT-flow, BERT-whitening) bolt a normalizing transform onto frozen embeddings to spread them out — they help, but degrade how well genuinely-paired sentences align. Supervised Siamese fine-tuning (Sentence-BERT) trains on natural-language-inference pairs and works well — but needs labeled data, and was itself outscored by simpler tricks. The open question SimCSE asks is blunt: can a single, almost trivial contrastive objective beat all of these — and can the unsupervised version, with no labels at all, close the gap to supervised systems?

// how it works

The method

Contrastive learning pulls semantically close pairs together and pushes everything else apart. SimCSE uses the standard in-batch objective: for a pair (xi, xi+) encoded to hi, hi+, with the other sentences in a mini-batch of N serving as negatives, minimize the cross-entropy (InfoNCE) loss over cosine similarities at temperature τ:

ℓi = −log e sim(hi, hi+)/τ Σj=1N  e sim(hi, hj+)/τ // in-batch contrastive loss; sim(·,·) is cosine similarity, τ the temperature

Everything hinges on how you build the positive pair (xi, xi+). In vision you crop or rotate the same image; in text, discrete edits (delete a word, swap a synonym) are clumsy and hurt. SimCSE's idea is almost embarrassingly simple:

Unsupervised: dropout is the augmentation

Take a sentence and predict itself — set xi+ = xi. The only thing that makes the two copies differ is that a Transformer applies standard dropout (default p = 0.1) on its fully-connected layers and attention. Feed the same sentence through the encoder twice and the two independently-sampled dropout masks yield two slightly different vectors — a minimal-noise positive pair, with no data, no labels, and no discrete editing (Figure 1a). Other sentences in the batch are the negatives.

The fragility of this is itself the proof it works. If you remove the noise — no dropout (p = 0), or fixed 0.1 (the same dropout mask on both passes) — the two embeddings become identical and the representation collapses: STS-B dev falls from 82.5 to 71.1 and 43.6 respectively. Even "delete one word" (75.9) underperforms pure dropout noise.

Supervised: NLI entailment as positives, contradiction as hard negatives

When labels are available, SimCSE feeds the contrastive objective with natural-language-inference data (SNLI + MNLI, 314k pairs). Entailment pairs become positives. The extra twist: each premise's contradiction hypothesis is added as an explicit hard negative (Figure 1b), extending the loss to a triplet (xi, xi+, xi−) with the contradiction term in the denominator. Why NLI? Entailment pairs are human-written and have low lexical overlap — 39% bag-of-words F1, versus 60% for Quora question pairs and 55% for the ParaNMT paraphrase set — so the model is forced to learn meaning rather than surface form.

Why it works: alignment and uniformity

To explain the gains, the authors borrow Wang & Isola's (2020) two diagnostics. Alignment measures how close positive pairs sit; uniformity measures how evenly all embeddings spread over the hypersphere (the opposite of anisotropy). Tracking checkpoints during training (Figure 2), unsupervised SimCSE steadily improves uniformity while holding alignment roughly constant — dropout noise is exactly what keeps the paired copies from collapsing onto each other. The "no dropout" and "fixed 0.1" variants improve uniformity too, but their alignment craters. The paper also proves, via a singular-spectrum argument, that the contrastive objective "flattens" the singular-value distribution of the embedding matrix, making representations more isotropic.

// what they found

The results

Across the seven STS tasks (Spearman correlation, the "all" aggregation setting, no STS training data used), SimCSE is the best in every encoder class. The unsupervised model, with only 1M random Wikipedia sentences and dropout, posts 76.25 average with BERT base — a +4.2 jump over the previous best unsupervised method (CT-BERT, 72.05) and good enough to match a supervised baseline (SBERT, 74.89). Add NLI supervision and SimCSE-BERT base reaches 81.57, +2.2 over the prior supervised best (CT-SBERT, 79.39). The gains compound on RoBERTa, topping out at 83.76 with RoBERTa large.

The supervised-data ablation (STS-B dev) is revealing about which signal matters. NLI entailment pairs (84.9) beat Quora question pairs (81.8), Flickr30k caption pairs (81.4), and ParaNMT (78.7) — the last of which actually underperforms the label-free unsupervised model (82.5), a pointed reminder that more "supervision" isn't automatically better. Adding contradiction pairs as hard negatives lifts NLI from 84.9 → 86.2, the final supervised recipe.

The alignment–uniformity map (Figure 3) is the clearest single picture of why SimCSE wins: raw pre-trained BERT has decent alignment but disastrous uniformity (bottom-right, anisotropic); BERT-flow and whitening fix uniformity but slide down in alignment; SimCSE occupies the good corner on both axes, and the supervised version improves alignment further still.

One more contribution is methodological rather than numerical: the authors flag an "incoherent evaluation issue" in the prior STS literature — papers silently differed on whether an extra regressor was fit, on Spearman vs. Pearson correlation, and on how the seven tasks were aggregated — making published numbers not directly comparable. SimCSE fixes a single protocol (no regressor, Spearman, "all" aggregation), re-evaluates the baselines itself, and calls for the field to unify on it.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

SimCSE is, deservedly, one of the most-cited NLP papers of its year, and part of why is that it took evaluation seriously — the call-out of the STS "incoherent evaluation issue" and the decision to re-run every baseline under one fixed protocol is more rigor than most 2021 embedding papers offered. So the question isn't whether the result is real; it's how much weight the headline deltas can bear.

Start with the proxy. The entire headline rests on STS, and STS is a narrow, somewhat artificial target: gold scores are averaged human similarity judgments on short, mostly out-of-domain sentence pairs, and "semantic similarity" with no downstream task attached is genuinely underspecified. The paper itself concedes STS is "the main result" — but the transfer tasks (SentEval classification), which is where sentence embeddings actually get consumed, show only on-par gains and lean on an auxiliary MLM objective to look strong. Downstream retrieval appears only as a handful of cherry-picked Flickr30k anecdotes (Table 8), not a real benchmark. STS gains do not automatically become retrieval gains — a lesson the dense-retrieval line that grew out of this work (BEIR, Contriever) had to relearn at scale.

There is also a quiet genre-overlap concern. Supervised SimCSE trains on SNLI+MNLI and is then evaluated on SICK-Relatedness — and SICK, SNLI and the Flickr caption pairs all descend from the same image-captioning lineage. The paper never quantifies that train/eval distributional overlap, and it plausibly flatters the supervised number on at least one of the seven tasks. Separately, every ablation (the 82.5s and 86.2s) is reported on the STS-B development set that the model is tuned against — fine for relative comparisons, but not held-out.

Finally, the result comes with no variance reporting. The STS datasets are small, the main table shows single runs, and a swing of one or two points of average Spearman is well within plausible seed noise — yet the comparisons that order CT, DeCLUTR and SimCSE turn on exactly that margin. The mechanism story ("dropout = minimal augmentation") is elegant but partly post-hoc: it is tied to a specific architectural artifact (where Transformers happen to place dropout), and the alignment/uniformity analysis is descriptive, not predictive. The singular-spectrum proof leans on an empirical positivity assumption.

baselines

Commendably re-run under one fixed protocol, so the comparison is apples-to-apples. But "+4.2 / +2.2 over previous best" is measured against the strongest contrastive baselines (CT 72.05 / CT-SBERT 79.39) under SimCSE's own re-evaluation — the giant gaps are over averaged GloVe/BERT; the gap over the real competitors is solid but incremental, and CT/DeCLUTR were concurrent.

contamination

No explicit test-set leakage, but an unquantified genre overlap: NLI training data (SNLI/MNLI) and the SICK-R eval set share an image-caption lineage. Every ablation number is STS-B dev, the tuning set — relative-only, not held out.

what's missed

STS measures a proxy (averaged human similarity on short, out-of-domain pairs), not downstream usefulness. Transfer-task gains are only on-par; retrieval is shown as anecdotes, not a benchmark. No seed variance on small datasets where a 1–2 point margin decides the ranking.

would raise confidence

Report seed variance and significance tests; quantify the NLI↔SICK/STS genre overlap; evaluate on a real retrieval benchmark (e.g. BEIR) rather than STS plus anecdotes; ablate the dropout mechanism across architectures with different dropout schemes.

// bottom line

A genuinely important, beautifully simple result: dropout-as-augmentation became a default and the alignment/uniformity lens reframed how the field reasons about embedding quality. But the headline lives on STS — a proxy of shaky construct validity — under a (admirably) self-standardized protocol that still leaves NLI↔eval genre overlap and seed variance unaddressed. Read the specific STS deltas as "clearly better in this regime," not as precise, load-bearing rankings.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. Dropout is enough. Passing one sentence through a Transformer twice, with different dropout masks, is a sufficient positive pair — no labels, no discrete augmentation. Remove the noise and the representation collapses (82.5 → 43.6 on STS-B dev).
  2. NLI is unusually good supervision for embeddings because entailment pairs are human-written with low lexical overlap (39% F1), and contradiction hypotheses make excellent hard negatives (84.9 → 86.2).
  3. Alignment + uniformity is a portable diagnostic. Good embeddings need both; pre-trained models fail uniformity (anisotropy), post-processing trades away alignment, and contrastive learning improves both at once.
  4. The number is on a proxy. A +4.2/+2.2 STS jump is real, but STS is a narrow construct; treat the result as evidence the recipe is better, and validate downstream (retrieval, transfer) before trusting the exact ranking.
