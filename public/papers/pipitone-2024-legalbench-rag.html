LegalBench-RAG: A Benchmark for Retrieval-Augmented Generation in the Legal Domain

The first benchmark that scores the retrieval half of legal RAG — 6,889 expert-traced query-to-span pairs over ~80M characters of contracts, NDAs, M&A agreements and privacy policies — built by retracing LegalBench's gold clauses back to their exact location in the source documents.

Nicholas Pipitone, Ghita Houir Alami · ZeroEntropy

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

A retrieval-augmented generation system has two halves: a retriever that pulls relevant text out of a corpus, and a generator that writes an answer from it. In the legal sector — where firms are racing to ship RAG over contracts and filings — almost all the public evaluation effort had gone into the second half. LegalBench (Guha et al., 2023), the field's reference benchmark, hands the model the exact gold clause and asks whether it reasons about it correctly. It never tests whether a system can find that clause in the first place, inside a 350,000-character merger agreement.

That is the gap. Production legal RAG lives or dies at retrieval, and nothing scored it. General-purpose RAG benchmarks (RGB, RECALL, MultiHop-RAG) measure retrieval in generic contexts, but they reward returning large, broadly-relevant chunks and don't distinguish that from pinpointing the small, exact span that actually answers a query. In law that distinction is the whole game:

  • Cost and latency. Long retrieved contexts cost more per call and add latency; stuffing a whole contract into the window to be safe is expensive at scale.
  • Hallucination. The more irrelevant text rides along in the context window, the more an LLM forgets or hallucinates — an unacceptable failure mode in legal work.
  • Verifiability. A precise span lets the model cite its source, and lets a lawyer-in-the-loop check the claim against the original text in seconds.

Legal documents also carry idiosyncratic structure, terminology and clause relationships that generic benchmarks don't capture. LegalBench-RAG argues a domain-specific retrieval benchmark is therefore necessary — and that it should reward minimal, highly relevant snippets, not document IDs or broad chunks.

// how it works

The method

The clever move is that the authors don't annotate from scratch — they retrace an existing benchmark. LegalBench is 162 expert-built legal-reasoning tasks; each task pairs a question with a gold clause and a yes/no label. LegalBench-RAG inverts that: take the gold clause, search the original source corpus to find the exact character span it came from, and convert the task's annotation category into a natural-language interrogative. The benchmark is assembled from four expert-annotated source datasets — CUAD (private commercial contracts), ContractNLI (NDAs), MAUD (M&A agreements), and PrivacyQA (consumer privacy policies).

Every query takes a canonical form, "Consider (document description); (interrogative)". For example, the CUAD "Affiliate License-Licensee" classification task becomes:

// example query

Consider the Cologuard Promotion Agreement between Exact Sciences Corporation and Pfizer Inc.; Does this contract include any right of first refusal, right of first offer, or right of first negotiation?

Label: ExactSciencesCorp_…_Promotion Agreement.txt, span [51520, 52804]

Critically, the label is not a yes/no and not a document ID — it is an array of (file path, character index span) tuples marking the minimal text that answers the query (a single annotation can map to several non-adjacent spans). The document descriptions were generated by GPT-4o-mini from each file's name and first/last paragraphs, format-checked with regex, manually inspected, and de-duplicated by flagging the most embedding-similar description pairs. Quality control ran at three decision points: mapping annotation categories to interrogatives (categories with inconsistent annotation precision were dropped), mapping document IDs to descriptions, and selecting which categories to include at all.

The result is a corpus of 79,704,214 characters across 714 documents, paired with 6,889 query-answer pairs, every pair annotated by legal experts. The authors note that the labour behind this is enormous: CUAD alone took a year and 40+ lawyers to produce 13,000 annotations — at $500/hour for legal expertise they estimate it would cost roughly $2,000,000 to replicate. To make iteration cheap, they also ship LegalBench-RAG-mini: 194 queries from each of the four datasets (776 total, 72 documents, 8.68M characters). Every experiment in the paper is run on -mini.

Scoring is span-level Precision@k and Recall@k: a pipeline retrieves its top-k chunks, and the overlap between retrieved text and the gold spans is measured against, respectively, what was retrieved and what should have been retrieved.

Precision@k = | retrievedk ∩ gold || retrievedk |    Recall@k = | retrievedk ∩ gold || gold | // overlap of top-k retrieved text with the gold spans — precision falls and recall rises as k grows (the paper reports both but never formalizes the exact unit of overlap)
// what they found

The results

The authors benchmark a small grid of pipeline choices on -mini: OpenAI's text-embedding-3-large embeddings, a SQLite-Vec vector store, and Cohere's rerank-english-v3.0 reranker, crossed over two chunkers — a naive fixed 500-character splitter (no overlap) and LangChain's Recursive Character Text Splitter (RCTS) — and two post-processors (no reranker vs. the Cohere reranker). Metrics are weighted equally across the four datasets.

The headline is that this is a hard benchmark. Precision is in the single digits on three of four datasets even at k=1, because the ground truth is so concise — a correct system that returns a slightly-too-large chunk is penalised. The clearest, most robust finding is the spread between datasets: PrivacyQA (questions written by non-lawyers about consumer policies, plain language) is by far the easiest, while MAUD (dense M&A jargon, 351K-character average documents) is by far the hardest.

Under the configuration the paper highlights as best — RCTS, no reranker — the per-dataset numbers are:

DatasetPrecision@1Recall@64
PrivacyQA (easiest)14.38%84.19%
CUAD1.97%74.70%
ContractNLI6.63%61.72%
MAUD (hardest)2.65%28.28%
Equal-weighted mean6.41%62.22%

Two design conclusions are drawn. First, the paper reports that RCTS without a reranker is best on both precision and recall. Second — and presented as the surprise — the Cohere reranker hurt performance rather than helping it, which the authors attribute to a general-purpose reranker being mismatched to specialized legal text. Recall climbs with k and precision falls, as expected. (As the next section shows, the first of these conclusions does not survive a recomputation of the paper's own per-dataset numbers — but the reranker result holds up.)

// does it hold up?

The eval read

The dataset is a real, expensive, well-motivated contribution. The leaderboard built on top of it is the weak half, and it has concrete arithmetic problems that a reader should know about before quoting the rankings.

The aggregate result rows are internally inconsistent

The naive-splitter table (Table 4) reports an aggregate "ALL" Precision@1 of 2.40% — but that figure is below every one of its four constituent datasets (the minimum is MAUD's 3.36%). An average cannot fall beneath all of its inputs, so the row is simply wrong. A correct equal-weighted mean of the four cells is 9.23%. The same row's Recall@64 prints 76.39%, where the equal-weighted mean is 63.49% and even the paper's own aggregate figure shows the naive curve topping out near 66% — three irreconcilable numbers for one quantity. (On -mini each dataset contributes exactly 194 queries, so "equal-weighted" is just a simple mean; there's no weighting subtlety to hide behind.)

// the headline ranking flips

The paper concludes RCTS+no-reranker is best on both precision and recall. Recompute the equal-weighted average straight from the per-dataset cells, and the naive 500-char splitter actually edges it on both: Precision@1 9.23% vs 6.41%, and Recall@64 63.49% vs 62.22%. The "RCTS wins" story rests on the corrupted Table 4 aggregate; the honest read is that the two chunkers are within a point and trade wins across datasets (RCTS wins PrivacyQA and MAUD; naive wins ContractNLI and CUAD on recall).

Two "different" pipelines report identical numbers

Table 5 (RCTS, no reranker) and Table 6 (naive splitter, Cohere reranker) describe genuinely different pipelines, yet their numbers are near-identical across all cells — both aggregate to Recall@64 = 62.22%, and their per-dataset rows match almost cell for cell (most differ only in the final digit). That is almost certainly a copy/paste or labelling error; at minimum it means one of the four reported pipelines can't be trusted as printed. There's also a smaller hygiene tell: the abstract and Table 1 say 6,858 query-answer pairs while the body and Table 2 say 6,889 (the gap is ContractNLI, 946 vs 977).

The metric is never formally defined

Precision@k and Recall@k carry the entire results section, but the paper never writes the formula or states whether overlap is counted over characters, spans, or retrieved chunks. With 500-character chunks scored against character-range ground truth, that choice materially changes the numbers — and it's exactly the kind of detail a benchmark exists to standardize.

A thin pipeline matrix carrying a broad claim

Every experiment uses a single embedding model, a single reranker, a single vector store, and two chunkers — all on the 776-query -mini, never the full 6,889. The dramatic "rerankers hurt" conclusion generalizes from one general-purpose reranker; it's good evidence that Cohere's English reranker mismatches legal text, not that reranking is a bad idea. There's no BM25 or hybrid baseline, no legal-domain embedder or reranker, and no fine-tuned retriever — so the benchmark ships without a strong reference point on its own leaderboard.

baselines

One embedding model (OpenAI text-embedding-3-large) + one general-purpose reranker (Cohere v3.0) + two chunkers, all on the 776-query -mini. No BM25/hybrid, no legal-tuned retriever; the "reranker hurts" claim rests on a single reranker.

contamination

The corpus is four public datasets (SEC filings, NDAs, privacy policies) almost certainly inside the embedding models' pretraining, and queries are templated from the source datasets' own annotation categories. Scoring retrieval (not memorized generation) blunts the leakage, but tuning on these public sets could flatter results.

what's missed

A formal Precision/Recall@k definition; an internally consistent aggregate row (Table 4 is arithmetically impossible; Tables 5 and 6 are duplicates); cross-document and multi-hop retrieval — by construction every query is answered by exactly one document — plus numeric/tabular and medical-record parsing.

would raise confidence

Re-run on the full 6,889 pairs; add BM25/hybrid and a legal-domain embedder + reranker; publish the exact metric; fix and re-derive the aggregate rows; release per-query scores so others can recompute the rankings independently.

// bottom line

LegalBench-RAG fills a genuine gap: it's the first public benchmark to isolate the retrieval step of legal RAG, and retracing expert annotations to exact character spans is a valuable, hard-to-replicate piece of dataset engineering. But the experiments are shakier than the dataset — one aggregate table is arithmetically impossible, two are duplicates, and the headline "RCTS beats naive" conclusion reverses once you recompute the paper's own per-dataset numbers. Trust the corpus and the difficulty ordering (PrivacyQA easy → MAUD hard); treat the pipeline ranking as unproven. The contribution is the benchmark, not the leaderboard.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. It scores the retrieval half of legal RAG, which nobody had. 6,889 expert-traced (query → exact character span) pairs over ~80M characters of contracts, NDAs, M&A agreements and privacy policies, built by retracing LegalBench's gold clauses back to their source documents — plus a 776-query "-mini" for cheap iteration.
  2. The task is genuinely hard. Even the best configuration lands single-digit Precision@1 on three of four datasets; MAUD (dense M&A jargon, 351K-char documents) bottoms out at 2.65% Precision@1 / 28.28% Recall@64, while PrivacyQA is easiest at 14.38% / 84.19%.
  3. Trust the dataset, not the leaderboard. The result tables are internally inconsistent (one aggregate row is arithmetically impossible, two tables are duplicates), and the "Recursive splitter wins, Cohere reranker hurts" conclusion only half survives recomputation: the reranker really does hurt, but the naive splitter actually edges RCTS once you average the paper's own per-dataset cells.
