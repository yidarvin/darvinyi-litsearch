Measuring the Impact of Early-2025 AI on Experienced Open-Source Developer Productivity

METR ran a randomized controlled trial: 16 seasoned open-source developers completed 246 real tasks on mature repositories they personally maintain. Early-2025 AI tooling made them 19% slower — even though the developers forecast a 24% speedup, still believed they'd been sped up 20% afterward, and economics and ML experts predicted speedups of 39% and 38%.

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

Every signal in 2025 pointed the same way: AI makes software developers faster. Frontier models post impressive numbers on coding benchmarks — SWE-bench, SWE-Lancer, RE-Bench, MLE-bench — and controlled productivity studies on synthetic tasks reported large gains (Peng et al. found a 56% speedup with GitHub Copilot, Paradis et al. 21%, Weber et al. a 65% jump in requirements satisfied). But those tasks buy scale by sacrificing realism: they're self-contained, need little prior context, and are graded by automatic test cases that may resemble training data. It's hard to read real-world impact off them.

The field experiments that do use real work have a different problem: their outcome measures aren't fixed in advance. Studies that count lines of code, commits, or merged pull requests report 14–51% "productivity" increases — but AI can move those numbers without doing more actual work, by writing more verbose-but-equivalent code or splitting one task into several PRs. So the question that matters for forecasting AI's economic impact was still open: when an experienced developer works on a large, mature codebase they know intimately, with a fixed unit of work, does AI actually save time?

METR built an experiment specifically to close that inferential gap — and got a result nobody, including the developers themselves, saw coming.

// how it works

The method

This is a true RCT, not a survey. METR recruited 16 experienced developers — typically over a decade of experience and, crucially, regular maintainers of the specific repositories used in the study. On average each developer had 5 years and ~1,500 commits on their repo, spanning 59% of that project's lifetime. The repos themselves are large and demanding: averaging 23,000 stars, ~1.1M lines of code, ~10 years old, 710 committers, with strict contribution standards. Developers were paid $150/hour; 93% had used LLMs before, but only 44% had prior experience with the Cursor IDE.

Each developer supplied a list of 246 real issues — bug reports, feature requests, work items — from their own backlog. The key design move: every issue is defined and time-forecast before randomization. Only then is it randomly assigned to AI-allowed or AI-disallowed. Because the unit of work is fixed before treatment, AI can't inflate the outcome by being verbose or by splitting the task — the measured quantity is simply how long the same predefined issue takes. In the AI-allowed condition developers primarily used Cursor Pro with Claude 3.5/3.7 Sonnet, the February–June 2025 frontier.

Speedup is estimated by regressing the log of implementation time on whether AI was allowed (controlling for the forecasted difficulty of each issue), so a slight post-randomization imbalance in difficulty doesn't drive the result:

log(timei) = α + β·AIallowedi + γ·log(forecasti) + εi // speedup = 1 − eβ;  here eβ ≈ 1.19, i.e. ~19% more time when AI is allowed

To understand why, METR went past the headline number. They collected screen recordings (which also verify compliance), then manually labeled 143 hours of recordings — 29% of all developer-hours — at roughly 10-second resolution, decomposing exactly how time was spent with and without AI. They also surveyed and interviewed participants, pulled source-control statistics, and gathered independent speedup forecasts from 34 economics experts and 54 ML experts.

// what they found

The results

Across 136 AI-allowed and 110 AI-disallowed issues, allowing AI increased completion time by 19%. The slowdown holds across nearly every quantile of the time distribution — it isn't a few catastrophic tasks dragging an average. (The raw ratio of average times is even larger, 34%; the regression trims it to 19% because the AI-allowed issues happened to draw slightly harder after randomization.)

The most striking part is the calibration failure. Developers forecast a 24% speedup before the study and still estimated a 20% speedup after living through it — they were essentially unable to feel the slowdown. Yet their forecasts weren't noise: the correlation between forecast and actual time was 0.64 (AI-allowed) and 0.59 (AI-disallowed), so they read relative task difficulty well; they just had the sign of AI's effect backwards. The experts did worse, not better, predicting 39% (economics) and 38% (ML) speedups.

The screen-recording labels show where the time goes. With AI allowed, developers spend a smaller share of time actively coding and reading/searching for information, and more time prompting the AI, waiting on generations, and reviewing and cleaning its output — plus a bit more time idle. Roughly 9% of time goes to reviewing/cleaning AI output and 4% to waiting on generations, and developers accept fewer than 44% of the AI's suggestions, with most reporting they make major edits to the rest.

Why did this happen? METR enumerated 21 candidate factors a priori and weighed the evidence for each: they found support for 5, mixed/no evidence for 10, and evidence against 6. The five with real support:

Just as important are the factors they ruled out, since each one is a way the result could have been an artifact: alternative outcome measures and alternative estimators give similar slowdowns; developers weren't cheating or under-using AI (it was used in all but 16.4% of labeled allowed cases); issue dropout didn't bias it; the IDE switch didn't explain it; and they were mostly using genuine frontier models. The slowdown survives all of these checks.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

This is one of the most carefully constructed measurements in the AI-impact literature, and its core strength is exactly the thing synthetic studies lack: a fixed, pre-treatment outcome. Because issues are defined and forecast before randomization, the result can't be gamed by AI writing more code or splitting work — the failure mode that makes lines-of-code and PR-count studies hard to trust. The robustness work is unusually disciplined too: enumerating 21 confounders and actively defeating six of them (estimator choice, outcome choice, cheating, dropout, IDE familiarity, model frontier) is what separates a finding from an anecdote. And the headline isn't really "+19%" — it's that even people doing the work, after hours of it, were off by 39 percentage points on the sign of the effect.

So what should temper how far you carry it? Mostly scope, and the paper is admirably explicit about this. The setting is narrow by construction — 16 self-selected elite maintainers working on codebases they've lived in for years — and METR's own factor analysis says the two biggest drivers (deep repo familiarity, large mature repos) are precisely the conditions that don't hold for greenfield code or unfamiliar projects. The title says "experienced open-source developer productivity," not "developer productivity," and that qualifier is load-bearing.

Two more cautions. First, currency: the tools are the Feb–Jun 2025 frontier (Claude 3.5/3.7 Sonnet, Cursor Pro), and the authors flag that future models, better elicitation (more inference tokens, better scaffolding, repo-specific finetuning), or simply more Cursor practice could flip the sign — only 44% of developers had used Cursor before the study, so most came to it new. They check the first 30–50 hours and see no learning curve, but that null comes from a small sample. Second, power: the factor analysis is explicitly not powered for the multiple comparisons it makes, so the five "contributing factors" are suggestive, not established, and the per-developer estimates have wide intervals — a handful of developers were genuinely sped up.

baselines

The right comparison — the same developer, same predefined task, AI off — is built into the RCT, and the forecast control handles post-randomization difficulty imbalance. Strong. The weakness is the sample: 16 developers, self-selected from the authors' networks and outreach.

contamination

Not a leakage study, but the analogue — outcome gaming — is the central threat, and the fixed pre-treatment outcome plus screen-recording compliance checks neutralize it. Cheating and AI under-use were explicitly measured and ruled out.

what's missed

The +19% average hides heterogeneity (some devs sped up), measures only time (not code quality, learning, or enjoyment), and is a snapshot of early-2025 tools — a forthcoming note says autonomous agents (Claude 3.7 Sonnet) can often implement the core functionality of issues on several of these repos, though they still fail to satisfy all requirements.

would raise confidence

A larger, more representative developer pool; pre-registered factor hypotheses with adequate power; replication on the same repos with mid/late-2025 agentic tools; and downstream quality/review outcomes alongside time.

// bottom line

The most rigorous field evidence to date that benchmark prowess is not the same as real-world uplift — for expert developers on code they know cold, early-2025 AI was a net drag, and nobody could feel it. The slowdown is robust within its setting; the live question is how narrow that setting is, and how fast better-elicited or agentic models close the gap.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. In a real RCT, early-2025 AI tooling made 16 experienced open-source maintainers 19% slower on 246 real tasks in their own repos — the opposite of the speedup everyone expected.
  2. Everyone misjudged it: developers forecast a 24% speedup and still estimated 20% after the study; economics and ML experts predicted 39% and 38%. Perceived productivity and measured productivity pointed in opposite directions.
  3. Screen recordings show the mechanism — with AI, developers spent less time coding and reading and more time prompting, waiting on, and cleaning up AI output, accepting under 44% of its suggestions.
  4. The slowdown is setting-specific by the authors' own analysis: deep repo familiarity, large mature codebases, and low AI reliability in those codebases drive it — they do not claim it generalizes to greenfield or unfamiliar work.
  5. It's a snapshot, not a ceiling. Better elicitation, future agentic models, or less-expert developers could flip the sign — making the result a benchmark to beat rather than a verdict on AI coding tools.
