Kimi k1.5: Scaling Reinforcement Learning with LLMs

Moonshot AI's recipe for o1-level reasoning from a deliberately simple RL loop — long-context rollouts, a variant of online mirror descent, and no MCTS, value functions, or process reward models — plus "long2short" methods that fold long-CoT skill back into cheap short-CoT models.

Kimi Team — Angang Du, Bofei Gao, Bowei Xing, Changjiu Jiang, Cheng Chen, Cheng Li, Chenjun Xiao, Chenzhuang Du, et al. (Moonshot AI)

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

Pretraining with next-token prediction scales beautifully with compute, but it is fundamentally capped by the amount of high-quality text that exists. The scaling-law line of work (Kaplan et al. 2020; Hoffmann et al. 2022) tells you how to spend a compute budget; it does not manufacture new data, and forecasts that the web's stock of useful tokens is finite. Reinforcement learning offers a different axis entirely: if a model can explore with rewards, it generates its own training signal and is no longer pinned to a static corpus.

The catch, as the paper bluntly puts it, is that "prior published work has not produced competitive results." OpenAI's o1 had demonstrated that RL-trained long chain-of-thought could reach competition-math and competitive-programming levels, but the recipe was closed. Open attempts to reproduce that capability tended to reach for heavy machinery — Monte Carlo tree search, learned value functions, and process reward models (PRMs) that score every intermediate step — on the assumption that o1-style reasoning needed all of it.

Kimi k1.5 is Moonshot AI's counter-argument: a full, multimodal, o1-class reasoning model trained with a framework that throws all three of those components away. Its central empirical claim is that scaling the RL context window — letting the model think across up to 128k tokens — is the load-bearing ingredient, and that once you can do that efficiently, a simple policy-gradient-style update is enough.

// how it works

The method

The training pipeline is: pretrain a multimodal base → a light long-CoT supervised warm-up that teaches the shape of reasoning (planning, branching, self-correction) → then a single, long RL phase that does the real work. The RL stage rests on four ideas.

1 · Long-context scaling via partial rollouts

The headline finding is that performance keeps climbing as you grow the RL context window, which they push to 128k tokens. Intuitively, a longer context buys the model more "search steps" inside a single rollout — room to plan, hit a dead end, notice the error, and recover. The engineering problem is that generating 128k-token rollouts on-policy is brutally expensive and a single long trajectory can monopolize a worker. Their answer is partial rollouts: cap each rollout at a fixed token budget; if a trajectory runs over, stash the unfinished tail in a replay buffer and continue it next iteration. Only the current segment needs on-policy generation; earlier segments are reused. Asynchronous workers mean short rollouts keep flowing while others grind through long ones.

2 · Policy optimization without a value network

At each iteration i the current policy πθi is frozen as a reference, and the model optimizes a relative-entropy-regularized objective — a variant of online mirror descent:

maxθ  E(x,y*)~D  [  E(y,z)~πθ [ r(x, y, y*) ]  −  τ · KL( πθ(x) ‖ πθi(x) )  ] // reward the verified answer, but stay close to the reference policy. τ tunes the leash.

This problem has a closed-form optimum, and taking logs yields a surrogate ℓ2 loss whose gradient looks like an ordinary policy gradient that uses the mean reward of k sampled responses as the baseline (à la REINFORCE-with-baseline), plus an off-policy regularization term. The two deliberate departures from the textbook setup: responses are sampled from the frozen πθi (so the data is off-policy and reusable), and there is no value/critic network. They argue a value function is actively wrong here: an exploratory wrong step that the model later recovers from is exactly what you want long-CoT to learn, yet a critic would assign it negative advantage and suppress it. The optimizer is reset at the start of each iteration. Notably, the paper never mentions PPO or GRPO — its only head-to-head policy-optimization baseline is ReST.

3 · A length penalty against "overthinking"

Left alone, RL inflates response length — accuracy rises but so does token cost, and humans dislike the rambling. So a per-response length reward is added, normalized within each group of k samples (λ = 0.5 − (len − min_len) / (max_len − min_len)): among correct answers, shorter ones are rewarded and longer ones penalized; incorrect long answers are penalized outright. It is warmed up gradually, because switching it on too early stalls training.

4 · Sampling, prompts, and rewards

Curriculum sampling trains on easy problems first and ramps to hard ones (spending compute on impossible problems early just wastes rollouts); prioritized sampling draws each problem in proportion to 1 − success_rate, focusing effort where the model is weak. The RL prompt set is curated for diverse coverage, balanced difficulty, and accurate evaluability — the last to blunt reward hacking. For math, where "a²−4" and "(a+2)(a−2)" should score the same, a Chain-of-Thought reward model that reasons before judging reached 98.5% agreement on spot checks versus 84.4% for a classic value-head RM — so the CoT RM is what drives RL feedback. Coding problems without test cases get auto-generated ones as the reward.

5 · long2short — and the infrastructure

Long-CoT models are accurate but token-hungry. The long2short methods transfer that thinking prior into cheaper short-CoT models: model merging (literally averaging a long-CoT and short-CoT model's weights, no training), shortest rejection sampling (sample a problem n=8 times, fine-tune on the shortest correct answer), DPO (shortest correct response as the positive, longer ones as negatives), and a dedicated long2short RL phase that re-applies the length penalty with a much shorter max rollout. Underneath, training runs on a hybrid system: a Megatron sidecar for the policy/training step and a vLLM sidecar for rollout generation, linked by a checkpoint engine that hands updated weights across shared memory (Figure 2).

// what they found

The results

The long-CoT model lands squarely in o1 territory: 96.2 on MATH-500, 77.5 on AIME 2024 (Pass@1), the 94th percentile on Codeforces, and 74.9 on MathVista — at or above OpenAI o1's reported 94.8 / 74.4 / 94 / 71.0 on the same tasks. o1 still leads on MMMU (77.3 vs. 70.0) and LiveCodeBench (67.2 vs. 62.5), so it is a tie-with-edges rather than a clean sweep.

The more surprising result is the short-CoT model, where long2short distillation lets a non-thinking model punch far above other non-thinking models. It reaches 94.6 on MATH-500, 60.8 on AIME 2024, and 47.3 on LiveCodeBench — versus GPT-4o's 9.3 on AIME and Claude 3.5 Sonnet's 16, a gap the abstract advertises as "up to +550%." On general-knowledge tasks it is competitive — leading on IF-Eval (87.2) and C-Eval (88.3), though its MMLU (87.4) trails LLaMA-3.1-405B (88.6), DeepSeek-V3 (88.5), and Claude 3.5 Sonnet (88.3).

The long2short trade-off is the cleanest demonstration that the method does something specific. Plotting accuracy against average response length, the k1.5-short variants sit far to the left of DeepSeek-V3 at equal or higher accuracy: on MATH-500, k1.5-short-w/-rl reaches ~94.6 in ~1,200 tokens where DeepSeek-V3 needs ~1,400 for ~90; on AIME the long2short-RL model matches the full long-CoT model's accuracy at a fraction of the tokens.

Two ablations back the method choices: context length and response length both rise together throughout training on a mid-sized model (the "thinking longer" effect is learned, not prompted), and curriculum sampling beats uniform sampling once the hard-problem phase begins (Figure 1).

// does it hold up?

The eval read

This is an unusually honest recipe report — and that framing is also its biggest evaluation limitation. The model's parameter count and architecture are never disclosed. The scaling and ablation studies are run on "small" and "mid-sized" variants, while every headline number comes from an undisclosed flagship. That makes it impossible to separate "the simple-RL recipe works" from "a very large model plus a lot of RL compute works" — the paper's whole thesis (you don't need MCTS/PRMs/value nets) would be far more convincing with a controlled, ablation against those components at fixed model size, which it does not provide.

The comparisons lean on numbers reported by closed competitors. The o1 column is OpenAI's own published figures, not a controlled re-evaluation, and o1's decoding/test-time-compute budget is unknown — so "matching o1" is a same-benchmark claim, not a same-conditions one. Conspicuously, Kimi k1.5 was released the same day as DeepSeek-R1 (Jan 22, 2025), the other open o1-class RL model, yet R1 is not cited or compared; DeepSeek appears only as the "DeepSeek-V3" non-reasoning baseline in the tables and scatter plots.

The "+550%" framing is real but flattering: it is driven by GPT-4o and Claude 3.5 Sonnet scoring near-floor on AIME (9.3 and 16), where any genuine reasoning model looks enormous in relative terms. The fairer read is the absolute short-CoT gap over the strongest non-reasoning baseline, DeepSeek-V3 (60.8 vs. 39.2 on AIME) — still excellent, far less cinematic. Credit where due on contamination: they evaluate code on date-windowed LiveCodeBench slices (24.08–24.11 for short-CoT, a 24.12–25.2 v5 window for long-CoT) specifically to dodge training-cutoff leakage — better hygiene than most reports. Math benchmarks (MATH-500, AIME 2024) get no such windowing, and the CoT reward model that scores RL is itself a Kimi model, so its 98.5% spot-check accuracy is the single load-bearing number the entire RL signal depends on.

baselines

Strong and current (o1, o1-mini, GPT-4o, Claude 3.5, DeepSeek-V3, Qwen2.5/Qwen2-VL, LLaMA-3.1-405B) — but o1's are reported, not re-run, and concurrent rival DeepSeek-R1 is absent. Only ReST is ablated as a policy-optimization alternative; no PPO/GRPO head-to-head.

contamination

Handled well for code (date-windowed LiveCodeBench); untreated for the static math sets. The verifier is an in-house CoT RM, so reward quality is assumed, not independently audited.

what's missed

No parameter count, no architecture, no training-compute figure. Headline results are on an undisclosed flagship while ablations use small/mid models — so "simple beats complex" is asserted at scale, not isolated.

would raise confidence

A fixed-size ablation pitting this recipe against MCTS/value-net/PRM variants; a controlled re-eval of o1 and R1 under matched test-time budgets; disclosure of model size and compute; reward-model robustness numbers on held-out adversarial cases.

// bottom line

A credible, well-engineered demonstration that o1-class reasoning is reachable with a simple RL loop — the long-context + partial-rollout + mirror-descent stack is the real contribution, and long2short is a genuinely useful deployment trick. But it is a practitioner's recipe, not a controlled scientific comparison: the "you don't need the complex machinery" claim is supported by an existence proof at undisclosed scale rather than by an apples-to-apples ablation.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. Context length is the scaling axis. Kimi k1.5's core claim is that letting RL think across up to 128k tokens — more "search steps" per rollout — is what unlocks o1-level reasoning, and that partial rollouts make training at that length affordable.
  2. Simple RL is enough. A KL-regularized online-mirror-descent update with a mean-reward baseline and no value network, MCTS, or process reward model reaches MATH-500 96.2 / AIME 77.5 / Codeforces 94th percentile — a deliberate rejection of the heavy machinery the field assumed was necessary.
  3. long2short turns thinking into efficiency. Merging, shortest-rejection-sampling, DPO, and a length-penalized RL phase transfer long-CoT skill into short-CoT models, yielding 60.8 AIME / 94.6 MATH-500 at a fraction of the token cost — the most reusable idea in the paper, and the one easiest to verify from its token-vs-accuracy frontier.
