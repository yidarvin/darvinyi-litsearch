A Comprehensive Survey of Reward Models: Taxonomy, Applications, Challenges, and Future

A beginner's map of the reward-model literature: how preference data gets collected, how reward is modeled (discriminative, generative, implicit), how the resulting RM gets used across the LLM lifecycle, and where the dedicated evaluation benchmarks and open failure modes sit.

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

Reward models (RMs) are the load-bearing component behind almost every LLM alignment pipeline: trained on human or AI preference data, an RM turns "which response is better?" into a scalar (or implicit) training signal that RLHF, DPO, and process-supervised reasoning methods all optimize against. By 2025 the RM literature had sprawled in several directions at once — preference-collection tricks, at least three structurally different ways to build the reward head, an outcome-vs-process split borrowed from the reasoning literature, half a dozen incompatible RM benchmarks, and a growing bestiary of failure modes (overoptimization, evaluator bias) — with no single place that organized it.

The authors are explicit about the gap they're targeting: prior surveys either cover human-in-the-loop RL broadly (Cruz & Igarashi, 2020; Najar & Chetouani, 2021) or treat RLHF/alignment as the unit of analysis (Ji et al., 2023; Kaufmann et al., 2023; Casper et al., 2023), with the reward model itself discussed only in passing. This paper's claim is to be "the first comprehensive survey specifically focused on RMs in the LLM era," organizing the field around the RM's own life-cycle — how preference is collected, how reward is modeled, how the RM gets used — rather than around a downstream training algorithm.

// how it works

The taxonomy

The organizing picture is a single pipeline: a task instruction plus candidate responses go through preference collection, get scored by reward modeling, and the resulting signal drives usage — optimizing a policy model. Figure 1 (the paper's own running example) shows this end to end: two LLM responses to a "weakest points in human anatomy" prompt are ranked by an RM trained to prefer the honest-harmless-helpful one, and that preference pair becomes the optimization target for the policy.

Preference collection

Preferences come from two sources. Human preference is the default (Christiano et al.'s original deep-RL-from-human-preferences setup; InstructGPT's RLHF), refined by active-learning query selection (Biyik & Sadigh, 2018) and annotator-quality fixes (demonstrations, active annotator selection, fine-grained rules). AI preference substitutes an LLM judge to cut annotation cost: Bai et al.'s RLAIF trains on a mix of LLM-generated harmlessness labels and human-generated helpfulness labels, and UltraFeedback (Cui et al., 2024) scales this to large synthetic preference sets.

Reward modeling: three mechanisms, two granularities

The survey's core taxonomic move is a 3×2 grid. By type, following Liu et al. (2024b) and Lambert et al. (2024)'s taxonomy: discriminative RMs attach an MLP head to a base model and output a scalar or pairwise preference probability (InternLM2-Reward, PairRM); generative RMs use an LLM's own generation ability to produce a judgment in text or extract next-token probabilities as a score (LLM-as-a-Judge, CriticGPT); implicit RMs skip an explicit reward head altogether and derive reward from generation probabilities directly — this is exactly what DPO and SLiC-HF do. By granularity: outcome-level RMs (ORMs) score only the final answer, while process-level RMs (PRMs) score each intermediate reasoning step, motivated by catching "a model arriving at a correct answer through an incorrect reasoning path."

The underlying assumption behind almost every discriminative RM in the survey is the Bradley-Terry pairwise-preference model (Appendix A.2): for prompt x and responses yw (preferred) and yl (rejected),

P(yw ≻ yl | x) = 11 + exp(r(x,yw) − r(x,yl)) // as printed in Appendix A.2 — fit by maximizing E[log σ(r(x,yw) − r(x,yl))] over the preference dataset (see the critique: these two lines are not actually consistent with each other)

Usage of the trained RM splits three ways: data selection (RAFT ranks LLM-generated responses and fine-tunes on the highest-reward ones; RRHF adds a ranking loss), policy training (the RM supplies the reward term in the standard KL-regularized RLHF objective, with fixes like length-controlled rewards and RM ensembling to fight overoptimization), and inference (RM-guided tree search and best-of-N reranking, mostly with PRMs).

// what they found

Where RMs get used, and how they're graded

The applications survey (§3) spans dialogue (harmlessness, professionalism, "dialogue impression" such as empathy and enthusiasm), reasoning (math and code, where PRMs dominate), retrieval & recommendation, and a growing "other" bucket — text-to-audio, text-to-image, text-to-video, robotic manipulation, and games — that the authors flag as evidence RMs are becoming a general-purpose alignment tool, not a chat-specific one.

The more consequential finding is that RM evaluation itself remains unsettled. The paper's own diagnosis (§4): training a full RL policy to check an RM's quality is prohibitively expensive, RM quality is entangled with the policy it's tested against, and RMs are sensitive to input style/domain/format in ways a simple pairwise-comparison test set won't surface. The result has been a proliferation of dedicated RM benchmarks rather than convergence on one:

BenchmarkTargetsWhat it checks
RewardBench (Lambert et al.)ORMchat, reasoning, safety, prior test sets + an RM-auditing toolkit
RM-Bench (Liu et al.)ORMchat, code, math, safety, run across publicly accessible RMs
RMB (Zhou et al.)ORM49+ real-world scenarios; documents generalization defects in earlier benchmarks
PPE (Frick et al.)ORMproxy tasks tied to actual downstream RLHF outcomes via an end-to-end RLHF run
ProcessBench (Zheng et al.)PRMstep-by-step annotated solutions on competition math
PRMBench (Song et al.)PRMthousands of stepwise-labeled problems, evaluating RMs across multiple dimensions

Layered on top of domain-general benchmarks, the survey also lists domain-specific ones — VL-RewardBench and Multimodal RewardBench for vision-language, M-RewardBench for multilingual settings, MJ-Bench, and RAG-RewardBench — which the authors read as "collectively mitigat[ing] the need for a more comprehensive and fine-grained evaluation of RMs," i.e. no single benchmark is trusted to stand alone.

On challenges (§5), three threads recur: data (annotator disagreement and noisy synthetic labels), training — specifically reward hacking / overoptimization, where a policy exploits the RM's blind spots via reward tampering, misleading, or sycophancy — and bias in evaluation, where top-ranking RMs and benchmarks show biases toward specific format patterns (Zhang et al., 2024f), evaluator-side biases toward length and concreteness (Park et al., 2024a), and "preference leakage" when the same model family generates both the synthetic training data and later serves as the judge (Li et al., 2025). The future-directions section (§6) bets on combining rule-based rewards (for math/code, where ground truth exists) with model-based RMs (for open-ended tasks) — already standard practice in o1-like reasoning training — plus reward design for long-horizon agent tasks and multi-modal RMs.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

Judge this as a survey, not an empirical paper — and on that basis, its biggest weakness is currency, not synthesis. The arXiv record shows exactly one revision: posted 2025-04-12, never updated since. The companion resource the abstract points readers to ("the resources are publicly available at github") — JLZhong23/awesome-reward-models — shows the same pattern: its last real commit landed 2025-05-28, about six weeks after the paper posted, and has sat static since (170 stars, only 3 open issues, no further pushes as of this writing). For a field this fast-moving, a survey that stopped six weeks after publication is a snapshot of April 2025, not a living map.

That staleness has a concrete, checkable symptom: the word "rubric" does not appear anywhere in the paper. Rubric-based / structured-criteria reward modeling — decomposing a scalar reward into named, independently-scored criteria (OpenAI's RBR, RLCF, Rubrics-as-Rewards, and the broader "rubric reward RL" line) — is exactly the kind of granularity-level innovation this survey's own taxonomy predicts (it already distinguishes outcome vs. process granularity), yet the entire branch is absent, because it only took off after this paper's writing window closed. A reader picking this up today gets a clean map of 2023–early-2025 reward modeling and zero signal about what's arguably the most active branch of the field right now.

The taxonomy itself is also asserted rather than validated: there's no stated inclusion/exclusion methodology for which of the 226 references made the cut, no inter-rater check on where a given system lands in the 3×2 (type × granularity) grid, and Table 1's pros/cons list for ORM vs. PRM reads as informed opinion rather than a claim backed by numbers pulled from the very benchmarks §4 catalogs — the survey never actually tabulates a score from RewardBench, RM-Bench, or PRMBench to let a reader see how today's RMs stack up, or how much the benchmarks disagree with each other (which RMB's own "generalization defects" finding suggests they do).

There's also a small but concrete copy-editing slip worth flagging for anyone tempted to lift a formula straight from the appendix: Appendix A.2 prints the Bradley-Terry preference probability as P(yw≻yl|x) = 1/(1 + exp(r(x,yw) − r(x,yl))) — but the standard Bradley-Terry/sigmoid derivation, and the paper's own very next equation (the MLE objective, which maximizes E[log σ(r(x,yw)−r(x,yl))]), both imply the denominator should instead read 1 + exp(r(x,yl) − r(x,yw)). As printed, the two adjacent equations on the same appendix page are mutually inconsistent — the first one, taken literally, computes P(yl≻yw|x) instead. It's a minor slip in an appendix rather than a claim central to the survey's argument, but it's exactly the kind of formula a reader would copy verbatim into their own code.

baselines

N/A in the usual sense — this is a synthesis piece, not an experiment. But the synthesis itself has no stated search methodology (no inclusion/exclusion criteria, no count of papers screened vs. included) and no inter-rater check on the 3×2 taxonomy assignments.

contamination

Not a leakage issue, but a currency one: one arXiv revision since 2025-04-12, and the companion GitHub tracker's last real commit was 2025-05-28. The word "rubric" appears zero times, despite rubric-based reward modeling becoming a major branch of the field within a year of this paper's posting.

what's missed

No cross-benchmark score table: §4 names six-plus dedicated RM benchmarks but never reports what any specific RM scores on them, so a reader can't see whether RewardBench, RM-Bench, and RMB actually agree with each other (RMB's own paper reportedly documents "generalization defects" in earlier benchmarks — a finding this survey cites but doesn't quantify).

would raise confidence

A stated systematic-review protocol; an actual leaderboard table pulling real scores across the RM benchmarks it lists; and either continued arXiv revisions or a genuinely maintained companion repo, given how quickly the RM literature (rubric rewards included) has moved since April 2025.

// bottom line

A clean, useful glossary and taxonomy for someone getting oriented in reward modeling as of early-to-mid 2025 — the preference-collection / reward-modeling / usage split is a genuinely helpful organizing frame. But it's frozen in time: no revisions since April 2025, an abandoned tracker repo, and a total blind spot on rubric-based rewards mean it should be read as a historical snapshot to pair with more current sources, not a live reference.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. Reward models decompose along three largely-orthogonal axes worth keeping as vocabulary: preference source (human vs. AI-generated), modeling mechanism (discriminative head / generative judge / implicit-DPO-style), and supervision granularity (outcome-level vs. process-level).
  2. RM evaluation is itself an open problem, not a solved prerequisite — the survey catalogs at least six general-purpose RM benchmarks (RewardBench, RM-Bench, RMB, PPE, ProcessBench, PRMBench) plus several domain-specific ones, precisely because no single benchmark is trusted alone.
  3. The survey is a snapshot, not a living map: one arXiv revision since April 2025, an abandoned companion repo, and zero mention of rubric-based reward modeling — the paradigm that took over much of the field's attention within about a year of this paper's release.
