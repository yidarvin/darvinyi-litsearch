EnigmaEval: A Benchmark of Long Multimodal Reasoning Challenges

Take 1,184 problems from real puzzle hunts — no instructions, mixed text and image, answers buried behind hidden chains of inference — and frontier vision-language models solve 7.0% of the easy ones and a clean 0% of the hard ones. Lower than Humanity's Last Exam, and not even adversarially curated.

Clinton J. Wang, Dean Lee, Cristina Menghini, Johannes Mols, Jack Doughty, Adam Khoja, Jayson Lynch, Sean Hendryx, Summer Yue, Dan Hendrycks

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

Reasoning benchmarks keep getting solved. MMLU, GPQA, MATH, and OlympiadBench test multi-step problem-solving, but they live inside well-defined problem spaces — a clear question, a clear domain, a clear notion of the right kind of answer — and frontier models are rapidly saturating them. The multimodal benchmarks that exist (MMMU, MathVista, VISTA) test vision-language understanding, but again on structured tasks. What no benchmark stresses is the integration: long, unstructured, multimodal problems that arrive without instructions, where the whole challenge is to discover hidden connections between seemingly unrelated pieces of information and to invent the solution path yourself.

Puzzle hunts are exactly that, and they are a genuinely untapped repository. A single puzzle-hunt problem might weave together wordplay, an acrostic hidden in a poem, a grid that has to be read diagonally, a pop-culture reference, and a final indexing step — with no statement telling you any of this is happening. Teams of skilled human solvers take hours to days per hunt. The catch for benchmark builders is that prior puzzle benchmarks are narrow by construction: PUZZLES, PuzzlePlex, PuzzleBench, GridPuzzle, and RiddleSense each fix a single puzzle type and a consistent format (sudoku, crosswords, grid logic), often text-only. That makes them tractable but defeats the point — the thing puzzle hunts test is precisely format diversity and lateral reasoning, the ability to figure out what kind of problem you're even looking at.

EnigmaEval's bet is that this unstructured, cross-domain, multimodal inference is the cognitive frontier current benchmarks miss — and that puzzle hunts, with their unambiguous final answers, give you a way to measure it cheaply and verifiably.

// how it works

The method

EnigmaEval is 1,184 puzzles scraped from eight archived puzzle-hunt events, split by difficulty into a Normal set (949) and a Hard set (235). The Normal split is dominated by PuzzledPint (838) — a monthly, beginner-friendly pub event whose own guideline is that a team of inexperienced solvers should finish in under two hours — plus CS50x Puzzle Day (41), Puzzle Potluck (34), Mark Halpin's Cryptic Crosswords (30), and CRUMS (6). The Hard split is Mark Halpin's Labor Day Extravaganza (153, multi-day events for experienced solvers), MIT Mystery Hunt (72), and Grandmaster Puzzles (10). Hard puzzles typically require five or more non-trivial steps with minimal verification, where intermediate answers are only thematically hinted by flavor text.

The clever part of the design is that each puzzle exists in two formats, which lets the benchmark separate two things that are usually confounded:

  • Raw format — a PNG of the original source PDF, or for web puzzles an automated full-page screenshot. This is the end-to-end test: the model has to parse the document and solve it.
  • Transcribed format — a human-curated, standardized text-and-image representation that preserves layout and visual structure but strips source-identifying headers and footers. This isolates reasoning from OCR and document parsing.

Building the transcribed format was a serious human-annotation effort (curated by Scale AI): automating transcription with LLMs proved impractical given the format diversity, so annotators hand-transcribed each puzzle, preserved grid structure, removed shortcut-enabling headers, and a separate set of reviewers verified both puzzle and solution. The team also filtered the raw material: advanced meta-puzzles were dropped unless solvable from prior answers and titles alone (77 meta-puzzles survive, all in Normal); audio/video and interactive puzzles were excluded; and only puzzles under Creative Commons licenses or with explicit author consent were included.

Grading is deliberately cheap and unambiguous. Every solution is a word or short phrase, annotated into one of three answer patterns (single answer, a pair, or a comma-separated list), so model outputs are compared to ground truth by string matching — no LLM judge, no rubric. Models are prompted with a format-specific system prompt that requires a step-by-step solution plus a final answer in a fixed Answer: <answer> format, and — notably — hands the model an elaborate list of puzzle-hunt heuristics (look for acrostics; try indexing; convert numbers to letters; check for pop-culture references). Accuracy is reported at default temperature, averaged over three runs (all standard deviations < 0.6%). For the 77 meta-puzzles, the model is given the correct component answers, so the score isolates meta-reasoning from performance on the underlying puzzles.

// what they found

The results

Models barely register. The best model, OpenAI o1, solves 7.0% of the Normal split and 0.0% of the Hard split. Every other model lands around 1% on Normal, and all nine models score 0% on Hard. o1's reasoning training buys it a roughly 5× lead over the rest of the field — the next best, Gemini 2.0 Flash Thinking, manages just 1.4% — but in absolute terms even the leader is failing 93% of the easy puzzles. The authors stress that this difficulty is organic, not adversarial: nothing was curated to exploit model weaknesses; the puzzles are simply hard, and the failures emerge from genuine demands for lateral, multi-step, cross-domain reasoning.

The dual-format design pays off as a diagnostic. Comparing transcribed vs. raw accuracy separates a parsing problem from a reasoning problem: o1 barely moves (7.0% → 6.1%), so it parses complex documents well and its failures are genuinely about reasoning. But weaker parsers lose most of their already-tiny score when handed the raw document — Pixtral Large and Claude 3 Opus both drop from 1.0% to 0.3%, and Gemini 2.0 Pro from 0.9% to 0.5% — revealing that, for some models, OCR and layout parsing is a wall reached before reasoning even begins. (GPT-4o is the lone exception, ticking up 1.0% → 1.1%.)

Two more findings sharpen the picture. On the 77 meta-puzzles, where the model is handed the correct component answers and only has to combine them, o1 still solves just 8.7% and everyone else ≤ 2.0% — the models neglect to attend to information sitting right there in their prompt, a long-context failure rather than a reasoning one. And on contamination: puzzle solutions are public online, so some models may have seen them, yet the uniformly near-zero scores show no detectable inflation. As a check, the team used o1 to audit its own correct answers for plagiarism against the official walkthroughs; every flagged case turned out to be a false positive (the auditor hallucinated evidence), suggesting the model reached those answers on its own. To keep it that way, EnigmaEval is held private and continuously updated rather than released.

The framing the authors land on: EnigmaEval joins Humanity's Last Exam as a new class of deliberately unsaturated benchmark — but where HLE probes structured academic knowledge, EnigmaEval probes the integration of reasoning in creative, instruction-free problem-solving, and it produces even lower scores.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

As a difficulty probe EnigmaEval is convincing — the scores are real, the design separates parsing from reasoning, and the leakage story is reasonable. But three things complicate reading the headline number as "models can't reason laterally."

The numbers are a frozen February-2025 snapshot, and a low-floor one. The slate is o1, Gemini 2.0, Claude 3.5 Sonnet, GPT-4o, Claude 3 Opus, Pixtral, and Llama 3.2 — no o3/o4, GPT-5, Claude 4.x, or Gemini 2.5/3. Given that o1's reasoning training alone produced a 5× lead, the 7.0%/0.0% in the paper is almost certainly stale; the maintained artifact is the live Scale leaderboard, not the table here. And because every model scores 0% on Hard, the Hard split currently has no discriminative power — it can't rank anything until some model clears the floor. Even Normal mostly separates "reasoning model" (o1 at 7%) from "everything else" (~1%); it's not yet resolving fine distinctions.

String-match-on-the-final-answer measures end-to-end success, nothing else. There's no partial credit and no signal on reasoning quality: a model that nails nine of ten steps and fumbles the last scores identically to one that produced garbage. That's the deliberate trade for cheap, unambiguous grading, but it means the benchmark can't tell you why a model failed or how close it got — exactly the gradient you'd want on a benchmark where everyone is near zero (contrast process-graded benchmarks like MoReBench).

Is it measuring lateral reasoning, or puzzle-hunt fluency? Puzzle hunts are a niche subculture with learned conventions — English wordplay, acrostics, indexing, US-centric pop culture — and the system prompt has to hand the model a tutorial of those conventions just to make the task legible. Low scores may partly reflect unfamiliarity with that idiom rather than a general reasoning deficit, which is a construct-validity question the paper doesn't fully separate. The human baseline doesn't settle it either: "teams of skilled solvers, hours to days" is asserted, not a measured human accuracy on the same items, so the "far short of humans" gap is qualitative (unlike HLE's quantified human baselines).

baselines

A Feb-2025 frontier slate (o1, Gemini 2.0, Claude 3.5/3 Opus, GPT-4o, Pixtral, Llama 3.2) — no o3/o4, GPT-5, Claude 4.x, or Gemini 2.5/3. The paper's table is a snapshot; the live Scale leaderboard is the maintained number.

contamination

Solutions are public online, but uniformly near-zero scores show no detectable inflation; kept private and continuously refreshed. The leakage audit uses o1 to police o1 — a same-family judge — though manual review of every flag is a fair backstop.

what's missed

End-to-end string match: no partial credit, no reasoning-quality signal. 0% on Hard = no gradient to rank by. No measured human baseline. "Normal" is 88% PuzzledPint, so aggregate ≈ one beginner source.

would raise confidence

Rerun on current reasoning models; a quantified human accuracy on the same items; per-source breakdowns; and step-level or partial-credit grading to recover signal once Hard is at the floor.

// bottom line

EnigmaEval is a genuinely hard, leakage-resistant probe of the one thing saturated benchmarks miss — instruction-free, multimodal, lateral inference — and the 0% Hard floor is honest proof that real headroom exists. But it's a snapshot of a niche skill graded only on the final answer: the live leaderboard, not the 7.0%/0.0% in the paper, is the number to trust, and until something cracks the Hard floor that split ranks nothing.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. Frontier vision-language models solve only 7.0% of "easy" puzzle-hunt puzzles and 0% of hard ones — organically hard, not adversarially curated, and lower than Humanity's Last Exam.
  2. The dual raw vs. transcribed format cleanly separates reasoning from OCR: o1 barely drops (7.0% → 6.1%), but weaker parsers lose two-thirds of their score on raw documents, exposing a parsing wall hit before reasoning starts.
  3. Even handed the correct sub-answers, models solve ≤ 8.7% of meta-puzzles — they don't reliably attend to information already in their context.
  4. Because solutions are public, the benchmark treats privacy as a contamination defense: it's kept unreleased and continuously updated against the latest models rather than published.
  5. The real artifact is the maintained leaderboard, not the paper's table — and the all-zero Hard split is a feature: it's a benchmark built to stay unsaturated.
