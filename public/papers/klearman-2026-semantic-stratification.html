Coverage, Not Averages: Semantic Stratification for Trustworthy Retrieval Evaluation

A single nDCG@10 number quietly averages over a corpus's semantic structure — and standard query sets leave 40–50% of a corpus's topic regions completely untested, precisely where retrieval is weakest. This paper reframes retrieval evaluation as a stratified estimation problem, shows the resulting bias is irreducible, and builds a coverage-aware curation loop that exposes the gaps.

Andrew Klearman, Radu Revutchi, Rohin Garg, Rishav Chakravarti, Samuel Marc Denton, Yuan Xue · Scale AI

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

Retrieval is the bottleneck of retrieval-augmented generation: whatever the retriever fails to surface, the generator never sees. So how we measure retrieval quality determines which systems get deployed. The standard practice — inherited from TREC and crystallized in BEIR — is to take a benchmark's fixed query set, compute nDCG@10 / Recall@100 / MAP@10 on each query, and report the mean with a bootstrap confidence interval.

That single number silently assumes the evaluation queries are an i.i.d. sample from the "natural" query distribution over the corpus. In offline, cold-start, or enterprise settings, that distribution doesn't exist — query sets are heuristically constructed, and they cluster on whatever topics the benchmark authors happened to write questions about. The corpus, meanwhile, covers far more ground.

The authors make this concrete on NFCorpus (a 3.6K-document medical IR benchmark). Organizing the corpus into 326 entity-based semantic clusters and mapping each query to the cluster of its relevant document reveals a stark compositional mismatch: 26 clusters that together hold 17.3% of the corpus are exercised by only 1.1% of queries. Medically central topics are effectively invisible — the "immune cells and their functions" cluster has 122 documents and exactly 1 query. Worse, the under-covered regions are systematically the hardest ones: in Figure 1, the dense band of warm-colored points sitting below the diagonal is high-document-count, near-zero-query, low-nDCG topics. Aggregate metrics are dominated by a handful of over-sampled, easy clusters and mask failures in prevalent but rarely-tested domains — so the headline score overestimates true retrieval quality.

// how it works

The method

The paper has two halves: a statistical reframing that says why aggregate metrics mislead, and a construction pipeline that fixes evaluation-set composition.

Retrieval evaluation as stratified estimation

Partition the query space into retrieval regimes {Sk} — coherent semantic or structural regions of the corpus. Each regime has a conditional mean performance µk and population mass wk, so true performance is the mixture µ = Σ wkµk. The usual empirical average estimates this only if the evaluation set's regime proportions ŵk match the true wk. They don't — and the conditional bias is exact:

Bias(µ̂naive) = Σk ( ŵk − wk ) µk // if a regime is missing entirely (ŵk = 0 but wk > 0), its term is dropped — irreducible bias, regardless of evaluation-set size

The key consequence: collecting more queries from the same skewed distribution never fixes the bias. The paper also shows the law of total variance is mangled — when extreme-µk regimes are missing, the between-regime variance term is attenuated, so confidence intervals look reassuringly tight while being systematically wrong. The takeaway is to treat the per-regime profile {µk} as the primary target, since it doesn't depend on the unknowable global mixture weights.

Building a coverage-aware evaluation set

To make regimes concrete without favoring any particular retriever, the pipeline grounds them in corpus structure (Figure 2):

  • Semantic structure. An LLM (GPT-4o-mini) extracts atomic (name, description) entities from every document — ~24,000 unique entities for NFCorpus's 3,633 docs, >109,000 for FiQA's 57,000. Entities are embedded (text-embedding-3-large, 256-dim), connected into a sparse semantic graph (k=50 nearest neighbors, cosine ≥ 0.5, FAISS), and clustered with the Leiden community-detection algorithm (resolution γ tuned per dataset: 25 / 40 / 10). Each document inherits the clusters of its entities.
  • Structural strata. Beyond which topic a query touches, two signals capture how hard it is. Relevance dispersion (∆) measures how widely a query's relevant documents spread across clusters; query–document alignment (J) is the Jaccard overlap between the clusters the query's own entities touch and the clusters its relevant documents occupy.
∆(q) = | ⋃d∈D(q) C(d) |Σd∈D(q) |C(d)|       J(q) = | C(q) ∩ C(D(q)) || C(q) ∪ C(D(q)) | // high ∆ = relevance scattered across topics (broad/ambiguous need); low J = query says one thing, answers live elsewhere (semantic mismatch)

Discretizing ∆ and J into tertiles yields a 3×3 difficulty grid inside each semantic cluster. The final step is an iterative curation loop: detect coverage gaps across semantic and structural strata, sample seed documents from under-covered regions, prompt an LLM to generate candidate queries, pool relevant documents from dense (KNN) + sparse (BM25) retrieval, verify relevance with an LLM judge, score candidates for how much new coverage they add, and repeat until the target set size is hit — producing an evaluation set with explicit semantic, structural, and difficulty-balance guarantees.

// what they found

The results

Experiments span three BEIR datasets — NFCorpus (medical), FiQA (finance), SciDocs (scientific) — three retrievers (dense text-embedding-3-large, BM25, and RRF hybrid), and three metrics. Three findings hold throughout.

1 · The strata are real, and they hide up to 3× performance swings

Within-stratum standard deviation (σwithin) is consistently below the overall σ across every metric, system, and dataset — confirming the clusters are internally homogeneous evaluation units. But across strata, performance varies enormously. The structural signals separate cleanly: on NFCorpus, dispersion governs ranking (it explains 12.3% of nDCG@10 variance, p < 10⁻⁸⁰; low-∆ queries average nDCG 0.56 vs 0.30 for high-∆), while alignment governs coverage (23.5% of Recall@100 variance, p < 10⁻¹⁰⁰; high-J Recall 0.60 vs 0.25 for low-J). The diagonal from "high ∆ / low J" to "low ∆ / high J" spans nDCG 0.25 → 0.62 — a nearly 3× difference between the hardest and easiest regimes inside the same benchmark. Per-cluster boxplots (Figure 3) show the same retriever can be best-in-class on one topic and worst on another: "Nutrition Expert" is the weakest cluster for all three retrievers, while "Prostate Enlargement" ranks top for dense but not for sparse or hybrid — retriever-specific strengths that the aggregate average erases.

2 · Standard benchmarks leave 40–50% of the corpus untested

Three coverage metrics quantify the gap: Minimum Semantic Coverage (MSC, fraction of clusters touched by ≥1 query), Sufficient Corpus Coverage (SCC, fraction of documents in clusters with ≥5 queries), and Zero-Query Clusters (ZQC, count of untested clusters). The original BEIR query sets fare poorly; the coverage-aware generated sets close most of the gap at comparable query counts.

The gaps are systematic, not random: NFCorpus's zero-query clusters are predominantly methodological topics — statistical methods (Cox regression, 470 docs), clinical-trial designs (450 docs), multivariate analysis (325 docs) — spanning 1,700+ documents that receive no evaluation query at all. A retriever could fail completely on "what is a Cox proportional hazards model?" and the benchmark would never notice. Generating queries into two such zero-query clusters confirms the danger cuts both ways: the "cancer cell lines" cluster retrieves strongly (nDCG 0.795 / 0.854 / 0.872 for dense / BM25 / hybrid) — a strength invisible to the benchmark — while "statistical methods" is a genuine blind spot (0.365 / 0.336 / 0.483).

3 · Coverage changes which system you ship

This is the punchline. Comparing OpenAI's text-embedding-3-small against Cohere's embed-english-v3.0 on NFCorpus, neither dominates: across clusters, OpenAI wins 94, Cohere wins 91, and 56 tie (Figure 4). When neither wins outright, the aggregation protocol decides the verdict — and it flips. Over 1,000 bootstrapped evaluation sets (Figure 5), text-embedding-3-small wins just 32.4% of the time under the conventional overall mean (which implicitly weights clusters by query volume), but 62.5% under a macro-average that weights every semantic region equally — and 85.8% on worst-case (P10) performance. Same models, same data, opposite decision. Stratified evaluation makes the weighting choice — and the practitioner's actual priorities — explicit instead of an accident of dataset composition.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

This is an evaluation-methodology paper, so the critique is meta: does its own measurement apparatus support the claim of "more trustworthy" evaluation? The statistical core is clean and basically unarguable — if you define target performance as a mixture over regimes and your query set misses regimes, you have irreducible bias. The diagnosis of BEIR's coverage gaps is genuinely useful and well-visualized. But the load-bearing claim that stratified evaluation is more accurate, rather than merely differently weighted, rests on softer ground.

First, there is no ground-truth query distribution to validate against — the paper says so itself ("in offline and cold-start settings, such a distribution is ill-defined"). So when stratified evaluation "corrects" the aggregate, it isn't shown to be closer to truth; it substitutes one weighting (query-volume / micro-average) for another (uniform-over-clusters / macro-average). The headline decision-flip (32.4% → 62.5%) is, underneath, the textbook micro-vs-macro averaging debate — macro-averaging up-weights small clusters by construction. Whether equal-per-cluster weighting matches "the practitioner's actual priorities" is asserted, not measured.

Second, the whole evaluation is mediated by LLMs end-to-end. Entities are extracted by GPT-4o-mini; the generated queries that "expose" the gaps are LLM-written; and their relevance labels — the qrels that define the new ground truth — are LLM judgments, with no human calibration (the authors flag this as a limitation). The "true" performance in zero-query regions is therefore measured against a synthetic, potentially noisy yardstick. There's also a representation-overlap risk: text-embedding-3-large both builds the semantic graph that defines the strata and is the dense retriever under evaluation. The authors argue entity-level embedding is system-independent and report low sensitivity to the embedding choice, which helps — but using the same model family on both sides leaves a seam.

Third, the magnitudes lean on a hand-tuned knob. Cluster granularity γ is tuned per dataset, and the resolution sensitivity table shows the per-cluster nDCG spread widens from roughly 0.44–0.63 at γ=10 to 0.40–0.86 at γ=60. The reassuring news is that σwithin and the variance-reduction ratios stay stable across a 4× change in cluster count; the caveat is that the eye-catching "3× variation" partly scales with how finely you slice.

baselines

Fair comparison point (BEIR aggregate vs stratified), but only three small/medium specialized corpora (3.6K–57K docs); no web-scale, multilingual, or long-document settings. Retrievers are current (OpenAI/Cohere embeddings, BM25, RRF hybrid).

contamination

Not a training-data issue (no model is trained). The analogous risk is label quality: generated queries and their relevance judgments are entirely LLM-produced and LLM-verified, with no human spot-check reported.

what's missed

No demonstration that stratified scores predict downstream RAG accuracy better than aggregate; no ablation isolating "more queries" from "coverage-aware queries"; the decision-flip reduces largely to micro-vs-macro averaging; γ is hand-tuned.

would raise confidence

Human relevance judgments on a sample of generated qrels; strata built from a different embedding family than the one evaluated; a downstream task where stratified selection beats aggregate selection; broader corpora and languages.

// bottom line

A sharp, well-formalized diagnosis that BEIR-style aggregate retrieval metrics hide systematic, predictable coverage gaps — and a practical, corpus-grounded curation loop to surface them. As a diagnostic and reframing tool it is convincing and immediately useful. As a new measurement standard it is one step short: its "more trustworthy" estimates are validated against LLM-generated queries and labels, and much of the headline instability is a weighting choice plus a tunable clustering resolution rather than a recovered ground truth.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. Averaging hides structure. A single nDCG number is a mixture Σ wkµk; if the query set misses corpus regions, the bias Σ(ŵk−wk)µk is irreducible — more queries from the same skewed distribution won't fix it.
  2. BEIR has large blind spots. NFCorpus tests only 50.9% of its semantic clusters; 26 clusters hold 17.3% of documents but 1.1% of queries; 1,700+ methodology documents get zero queries — and under-tested regions are systematically the hardest to retrieve.
  3. Two structural signals predict difficulty. Relevance dispersion (∆) drives ranking quality (nDCG) and query–document alignment (J) drives coverage (Recall); together they span a ~3× performance range inside a single benchmark.
  4. Coverage changes the decision. OpenAI vs Cohere on NFCorpus flips from a 32.4% to a 62.5% win rate just by switching from query-weighted to cluster-weighted aggregation — system selection is an artifact of how you average.
  5. The fix is corpus-grounded curation, but LLM-mediated. Entity → Leiden clustering → ∆/J strata → coverage-aware generation lifts semantic coverage to ~90–100%; the open question is validating the synthetic queries and labels it depends on.
