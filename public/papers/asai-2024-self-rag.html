Self-RAG: Learning to Retrieve, Generate, and Critique through Self-Reflection

Trains a single LM to decide for itself when to retrieve, then critique its own retrieved passages and generations with special reflection tokens — turning indiscriminate top-k RAG into an on-demand, self-graded pipeline a 7B model can run without a separate reranker.

Akari Asai, Zeqiu Wu, Yizhong Wang, Avirup Sil, Hannaneh Hajishirzi

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

Retrieval-Augmented Generation reduces the factual errors LLMs make when they rely purely on parametric knowledge, but the standard recipe — retrieve a fixed number of passages and prepend them to every prompt — has two structural flaws the paper targets directly. First, it retrieves indiscriminately: even a prompt like "write an essay about your best summer vacation," which needs no external fact at all, gets the same top-k passages jammed in front of it, which can dilute the model's own creativity and versatility. Second, the generator is never trained to actually verify that its output is consistent with what it was handed — "the output is not guaranteed to be consistent with retrieved relevant passages since the models are not explicitly trained to leverage and follow facts from provided passages," as the paper puts it — so RAG can still produce fluent claims that the retrieved evidence doesn't actually support.

Prior fixes only chip at one side of this. Adaptive-retrieval work wraps a proprietary LLM with a decision of when to call a retriever, but doesn't train the underlying model or grade its own output. Toolformer trains a model to emit API calls, but doesn't evaluate whether the resulting generation is actually grounded in what came back. What's missing is a single trained model that both decides, per segment, whether retrieval is worth it, and grades its own candidate outputs — relevance, factual support, overall usefulness — well enough that a decoder can use those grades to pick the best one.

// how it works

The method

Self-RAG trains one arbitrary LM (Llama2 7B or 13B in the paper) to generate ordinary text interleaved with four types of special reflection tokens, added to the model's vocabulary so they're produced just like any other token:

TokenInputOutput valuesWhat it decides
Retrievex / x,yyes / no / continueWhether to retrieve at all, before generating the next segment
IsRelx, drelevant / irrelevantWhether a retrieved passage d is useful for solving x
IsSupx, d, yfully / partially / no supportWhether the generated segment y is actually supported by d
IsUsex, y1 – 5Overall usefulness of the response y to x

Training in two stages

A critic model C is trained first: GPT-4 is prompted with type-specific instructions to label sampled (input, output) pairs with the appropriate reflection token, producing 4k–20k labeled examples per token type, and C (initialized from the same base LM) is trained on these labels with a standard next-token objective. The paper reports the resulting critic reaches "a higher than 90% agreement with GPT-4-based predictions on most reflection token categories" — meaning C approximates, but doesn't perfectly replicate, GPT-4's judgment.

C is then used offline to annotate a 150k-instance instruction corpus (drawn from Open-Instruct plus knowledge-intensive sets including Wizard of Wikipedia, Natural Questions, FEVER, OpenBookQA, ARC-Easy, and ASQA): for each segment, C decides if retrieval helps, the retriever (Contriever-MS MARCO, top-10 by default) fetches passages, and C labels each passage's relevance and support. This reflection-token-augmented corpus (with retrieved passages masked out of the loss) trains the final generator model M with the ordinary LM objective — so at inference, M produces reflection tokens itself, with no critic model needed at serving time.

Segment-level tree decoding

At each generation step where retrieval is triggered, the retriever returns K passages in parallel, and M generates one candidate continuation per passage plus its own critique tokens for that candidate. A segment-level beam search then scores each candidate by combining its language-model probability with a weighted sum of its reflection-token probabilities:

f(yₜ, d, Critique) = p(yₜ | x, d, y<ₜ) + S(Critique),    S(Critique) = ΣG wG·sGt // G ranges over {IsRel, IsSup, IsUse}; s^G_t is the normalized probability of the most desirable token in group G; default weights are IsRel=1.0, IsSup=1.0, IsUse=0.5

Because those weights are set at inference time, not baked in during training, a practitioner can raise the IsSup weight to prioritize citation-grounded output over fluency, or lower it the other way — and a retrieval-probability threshold (0.2 by default, 0 for the citation-heavy ASQA task) controls how eagerly the model retrieves at all, all without any additional fine-tuning.

// what they found

The results

Across six tasks — PopQA and TriviaQA (short-form QA), PubHealth and ARC-Challenge (closed-set), and biography generation and ALCE-ASQA (long-form, with citations) — Self-RAG 7B/13B beats every open baseline of comparable or larger scale, including retrieval-augmented Llama2 and Alpaca variants. Against ChatGPT specifically (no retrieval), Self-RAG wins on PopQA (54.9/55.8 vs. 29.3), PubHealth (72.4/74.5 vs. 70.1), and biography FactScore (81.2/80.2 vs. 71.8) — but ChatGPT still leads on TriviaQA accuracy (74.3 vs. Self-RAG's best of 69.3) and ARC-Challenge (75.3 vs. 73.1).

On ASQA, the citation-precision/recall numbers show a split result the paper's summary prose glosses over: Self-RAG 13B beats Ret-ChatGPT on citation precision (70.3 vs. 65.1) — "even outperforming ChatGPT in citation precision," as the paper puts it — but Ret-ChatGPT still leads on citation recall (76.6 vs. 71.3). And on ASQA's Rouge-based correctness metric, the 7B model (35.7) actually trails plain ChatGPT (36.2), even though the 13B model edges ahead (37.0); the paper's claim of outperforming ChatGPT on "PubHealth, PopQA, biography generations, and ASQA (Rouge and MAUVE)" only cleanly holds for ASQA's Rouge metric in the 13B variant, not the 7B one.

The ablation is the clearest evidence for what actually drives the gains: dropping the trained critic (so the model just always retrieves the top-1 passage, matching how SAIL works) is far more damaging than dropping the retriever outright on the ASQA task — ASQA-em falls from 32.1 to 18.1, worse than removing retrieval from training altogether (31.0). Naively retrieving the top-1 document at inference time, as plain RAG does, also costs several points on PopQA (41.8 vs. 45.5) and ASQA (28.6 vs. 32.1). The self-reflection and selection mechanism, not retrieval by itself, is doing most of the work.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

The comparison set is broad and mostly fair within scale tiers — Self-RAG 7B/13B against similarly-sized Llama2/Alpaca baselines run by the authors themselves, plus proprietary references (ChatGPT, Ret-ChatGPT) reported for context. But a few of the more favorable-sounding baselines aren't reproduced at all: the paper's own footnote states: "We report numbers using the results reported in the paper as the implementations are not available" for SAIL and Toolformer, meaning those rows (marked with an asterisk in Table 2) come from each method's own paper under its own eval setup, not a controlled rerun. The retrieval setup also isn't held constant across tasks — Self-RAG uses Contriever-MS MARCO by default but switches to an added web-search retriever for biography/QA tasks and to author-provided GTR-XXL passages for ASQA specifically "for a fair comparison" with prior ASQA work — a sensible per-task choice, but it means the retrieval corpus itself differs from task to task in ways that complicate any single, unified read of "how good is the retriever."

On contamination: PopQA is deliberately built from long-tail entities with low Wikipedia page views specifically to reduce the chance a model memorized the answer during pretraining, which is a genuinely good design choice baked into the benchmark itself. But nothing in the paper checks whether the 150k-instance training corpus (which pulls in Natural Questions and other knowledge-intensive sets via KILT) overlaps with the evaluation sets for PopQA or TriviaQA-unfiltered.

The self-supervision loop is also worth naming directly: the critic model that grades relevance and support during training is itself distilled from GPT-4 labels, and its output is then used to train the generator that Self-RAG evaluates against GPT-4-adjacent systems like ChatGPT. The paper's own check on those GPT-4 labels is a single sentence: "Manual assessment reveals that GPT-4 reflection token predictions show high agreement with human evaluations" — no sample size given. A separate, more rigorous check exists later, but it validates something different: a 50-sample human evaluation (drawn from PopQA and Bio outputs only) of whether the trained generator's own predicted IsRel/IsSup tokens match human inspection, not whether the original GPT-4 labels used to supervise the critic were themselves correct. Any systematic bias in GPT-4's own judgments of "supported" or "relevant" would propagate through the critic and into the generator's behavior largely unchecked by either measurement.

baselines

Mostly reproduced by the authors within a scale tier; SAIL and Toolformer numbers are pulled from their original papers, not rerun, by the paper's own admission.

contamination

PopQA's long-tail-entity design mitigates memorization by construction, but no explicit train/eval overlap check is reported for the KILT-sourced training data against NQ or TriviaQA.

what's missed

The GPT-4 labels used to supervise the critic are checked against human judgment only via an unquantified "manual assessment"; the paper's one sample-sized human check (50 instances, PopQA + Bio) instead validates the downstream generator's own predicted tokens, not those original labels.

would raise confidence

Reproducing SAIL/Toolformer under one shared harness; a sample-sized, quantified check of the original GPT-4 labels against human judgment (not just the downstream generator's tokens); and an explicit contamination check between the KILT/Open-Instruct training mix and each eval benchmark.

// bottom line

The ablations make a genuinely convincing case that self-reflection — not retrieval alone — is what drives Self-RAG's gains: dropping the trained critic hurts more than dropping the retriever outright. But the headline "outperforms ChatGPT" framing smooths over real splits in the data (the 7B model trails ChatGPT on ASQA Rouge; both sizes trail on TriviaQA and ARC-Challenge), and the training pipeline's dependence on GPT-4-distilled critique labels — checked against human judgment only via an unquantified "manual assessment," with the paper's one sized human study (50 instances) validating the downstream generator's tokens rather than those original labels — is a chain of trust the paper doesn't fully close.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. Self-RAG trains a single LM to emit reflection tokens (Retrieve, IsRel, IsSup, IsUse) as ordinary vocabulary, so at inference it needs no separate critic or reranker — the critic model only exists offline, to generate training labels.
  2. The ablations show the trained critic matters more than the retriever itself: removing it and falling back to always-retrieve-top-1 (à la SAIL) costs more on ASQA-em (32.1→18.1) than removing retrieval from training entirely (32.1→31.0).
  3. Inference-time weights on the reflection-token score are tunable without retraining — raising the IsSup weight buys citation precision at the cost of MAUVE fluency, letting one trained model serve different task priorities.
  4. The "outperforms ChatGPT" framing is broadly true but not universal: ChatGPT still leads on TriviaQA and ARC-Challenge, and the 7B model specifically trails ChatGPT on ASQA's Rouge metric even though the 13B model edges ahead.
