FinanceBench: A New Benchmark for Financial Question Answering

The first ecologically-valid test of LLMs on open-book financial QA: 10,231 expert-written (question, answer, evidence) triplets over real SEC filings. With a realistic retrieval stack, GPT-4-Turbo answers just 19% correctly — leaving 81% wrong or refused.

Islam, Kannappan, Kiela, Qian, Scherrer, Vidgen · Patronus AI / Contextual AI / Stanford

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

Financial analysts spend their days doing one core loop: find a figure buried in a 250-page 10-K, summarize it, and reason about it to support a decision. By late 2023 the industry was eager to hand parts of that loop to LLMs — but there was almost no way to measure whether a model was actually good enough to trust on a live, high-stakes desk.

The benchmarks that existed didn't fit. Generic QA suites — SQuAD, NarrativeQA, HellaSwag, TruthfulQA — contain few or no financial questions, and strong scores on an open-domain test don't transfer to a specialist domain. The finance-specific datasets that did exist (FiQA's sentiment-flavored QA, FinQA and TAT-QA's numeric reasoning over curated tables) handed the model the exact passage it needed, collapsing the task into closed-book reading comprehension. That erases the part analysts actually find hard: retrieval. Pull the right page and most of the work is done; the benchmark that grades a model with the answer already in front of it is measuring the wrong thing.

FinanceBench's bet is ecological validity: questions a real analyst would actually ask, over the real documents they'd actually read, graded open-book so the model has to locate its own evidence. The domain is unusually unforgiving — it demands up-to-date company knowledge, mixes free text with tables, spans multiple long filings, and leans heavily on numerical reasoning, exactly where LLMs are known to slip. A wrong number delivered with a confident explanation isn't a harmless miss; it's a hallucination that can move a position.

// how it works

The dataset & the test harness

FinanceBench is 10,231 (question, answer, evidence) triplets covering 40 US publicly-traded companies across 361 public filings — 10-Ks, 10-Qs, 8-Ks and earnings reports released between 2015 and 2023. Every entry carries the question, the gold answer, an evidence string with the exact text that justifies it, and a page number; many add a human "justification" showing how a number was computed. The triplets come in three flavors, built by a vetted team of finance annotators:

(question, answer, evidence)  ×  10,231
domain-relevant 925 · novel-generated 1,323 · metrics-generated 7,983 // 25 reusable expert questions · analyst-written one-offs · template-built metric questions over 8 years of statements
  • Domain-relevant (925): 25 hand-built questions an analyst asks of any company (e.g. dividend history, margin consistency), posed to 37 of 40 companies.
  • Novel-generated (1,323): analyst-written, company-specific questions, deliberately kept realistic and reasoning-heavy rather than purely extractive — ~36 per company.
  • Metrics-generated (7,983): the bulk. Annotators extracted 18 "base metrics" from the three financial statements over 2015–2022, then templates derive metrics (e.g. net-income margin from net income ÷ revenue) into questions — some purely extractive, some requiring multi-statement arithmetic.

A secondary taxonomy labels each question by skill: of 8,908 labeled questions, 28% are information extraction, 66% numerical reasoning, 6% logical reasoning — a benchmark dominated by getting numbers right. Quality control was heavy: 20 finance-credentialed annotators were recruited, 5 dropped for quality, and a senior analyst reviewed ~10–20% of each annotator's work weekly with corrections.

Because manually reviewing every model response is expensive, evaluation runs on a 150-case slice — 50 domain-relevant, 50 novel, 50 metrics — and only those 150 cases are open-sourced. The team tests 16 model configurations built from four models (GPT-4, GPT-4-Turbo @128k, Claude 2 @100k, Llama 2) across five setups, then manually labels all 2,400 responses (16 × 150) into one of three buckets:

  • Closed book — model gets the question and nothing else (most naive).
  • Oracle — model is handed the exact evidence page(s); retrieval removed. Unrealistic, an upper bound.
  • Single / shared vector store — a Chroma + OpenAI ada-002 RAG stack, either one store per document (easy) or one shared store over all ~360 filings (realistic, hard).
  • Long context — the whole filing dumped into a 100–128k window (truncated when it overflows).

Grading is three-way and deliberately captures the safety-relevant distinction: correct answer, incorrect answer (a wrong number, a fabricated fact, the wrong direction of an effect — a hallucination), or failure to answer (the model declines). Refusing is treated as safer than confidently inventing.

// what they found

The results

The headline is bleak and specific. Closed-book GPT-4-Turbo answers 9% of questions correctly — but, to its credit, refuses 88% rather than guessing. The realistic retrieval setup is the one that matters for enterprises, and it's where the famous number lives: GPT-4-Turbo on a shared vector store answers just 19% correctly — incorrectly answering or refusing 81% of questions.

The full spread shows that retrieval, not reasoning, is the wall. Giving each document its own vector store (a much easier search) jumps GPT-4-Turbo from 19% → 50%; the same move takes Llama 2 from 19% → 41%. Dropping the whole filing into a long context window reaches 76% (Claude 2) and 79% (GPT-4-Turbo), and the unrealistic Oracle — evidence page handed over, retrieval deleted — tops out at 85%. The closer you get the right page to the model, the more the gap closes.

The failure mode differs by model, and that distinction is the safety story. GPT-4-Turbo tends to refuse when it can't find evidence (e.g. 68% failures vs 13% wrong in the shared store) — annoying but honest. Llama 2 does the opposite: it confidently hallucinates, posting 70% and 54% incorrect answers against single-digit refusal rates. In a live setting a wrong, well-justified number is the more dangerous outcome, because nothing flags it.

Two more findings sharpen the picture. By question type, models are weakest on the metrics-generated questions — the numeric, multi-statement ones — except in the long-context setting, confirming the bottleneck is fetching the right figures rather than computing with them. And prompt order matters enormously in long context: putting the filing before the question (Context-First) versus after it (Context-Last) swings GPT-4-Turbo from 78% → 25% and Claude 2 from 76% → 37% — the model loses track of the question after thousands of evidence tokens.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

FinanceBench's lasting contribution is methodological: it grounds financial-QA eval in real filings, grades open-book, and separates retrieval failure from reasoning failure — the single-store / shared-store / oracle ladder is a clean ablation that pins most of the gap on getting the right page, not on the model's arithmetic. The three-way correct / incorrect / refusal labeling is also exactly right for a high-stakes domain, surfacing the hallucination-vs-honesty split that a single "accuracy" number would hide.

But the headline "81% wrong or refused" deserves three asterisks. First, the models are a late-2023 cast — GPT-4-Turbo, Claude 2, Llama 2 — and the absolute numbers are a snapshot of that generation and that 2023 RAG stack (Chroma + ada-002, naive chunking). The shared-store result is as much an indictment of the embedding/retrieval pipeline as of the model; a modern agentic retriever would move these numbers a lot, so read the 81% as a 2023 watermark, not a standing fact about frontier capability. Second, only 150 of the 10,231 cases are open-sourced, and that public slice has since become one of the most-used finance evals — so the very numbers people quote come from a 150-case sample that is now a strong contamination candidate for any post-2023 model. Third, response grading was done once-over by a single researcher (spot-checked, with allowances for "minor deviations" and rounding) — no inter-annotator agreement is reported for the response labels, despite 66% of questions being numeric where the correct/incorrect line is precisely where judgment calls cluster.

baselines

Four late-2023 models (GPT-4/Turbo, Claude 2, Llama 2) on a 2023 Chroma+ada-002 RAG stack. Fair for its moment, badly dated now — the realistic configs partly measure the retriever, not the model.

contamination

Filings are public SEC docs almost certainly in pretraining, but the open-book framing blunts that. The real risk: the public 150-case set is now widely reused and likely leaked into newer training — and the headline numbers come from exactly those 150.

what's missed

Single-turn only, single-company only (no cross-document comparison), full 10,231-set unreleased, no response-grading IAA, and a numeric-heavy task graded with subjective "minor deviation" tolerance.

would raise confidence

Re-run on current frontier models + modern agentic retrieval; release the full set; report grader agreement; add multi-turn and cross-filing tasks; auto-grade numeric answers to remove rater subjectivity.

// bottom line

A landmark for putting financial-QA evaluation on real documents and for proving the wall is retrieval, not reasoning. Just don't treat the "81% wrong" as a fact about today's models — it's a once-graded, 150-case, late-2023 measurement whose enduring value is the framing, not the number.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. FinanceBench reframed financial-QA eval as open-book and ecologically valid: 10,231 expert (question, answer, evidence) triplets over 361 real SEC filings from 40 US public companies (2015–2023), with a 150-case human-eval slice open-sourced.
  2. On the realistic shared-vector-store setup, GPT-4-Turbo answered only 19% correctly — 81% wrong or refused; even the best realistic config (long-context, 79%) and the unrealistic oracle (85%) fall short of production trust.
  3. The dominant failure is retrieval, not reasoning: single-store (50%) crushes shared-store (19%), oracle hits 85%, and models are weakest on numeric metrics questions — put the right page in front of the model and most of the gap closes.
  4. The failure mode is the safety signal: GPT-4-Turbo refuses (honest), Llama 2 confidently hallucinates (70%+ incorrect) — and in long context, evidence-before-question vs after swings accuracy by 40+ points.
