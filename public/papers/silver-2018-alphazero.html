2018 · Science · Post-training & RL

A general reinforcement learning algorithm that masters chess, shogi, and Go through self-play

AlphaGo Zero proved that tabula rasa self-play plus Monte Carlo tree search could reach superhuman Go without a single human game — but Go is unusually convenient for a neural network: symmetric, binary-outcome, one simple action type. AlphaZero takes the same recipe, strips out every Go-specific convenience (symmetry-based data augmentation, "best player" checkpointing, per-game hyperparameter search), and trains three separate instances — for chess, shogi, and Go — from random play and nothing but the rules. Each one defeats a world-champion program within hours to a day, searching thousands of times fewer positions per second than the hand-tuned engines it beats.

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

Chess is the oldest grand-challenge domain in AI — Babbage, Turing, Shannon and von Neumann all worked on it — and by 2017 the state of the art was a mature, decades-deep engineering pipeline: Stockfish and its peers evaluate positions with a linear combination of handcrafted features (piece-square tables, king safety, pawn structure, mobility) refined by human grandmasters, wrapped in an alpha-beta search sped up by quiescence search, transposition tables, killer-move heuristics, and endgame tablebases. None of that transfers to a new domain without significant human effort. AlphaGo Zero, published earlier the same year, showed a wholly different path was possible in Go: a single deep network trained purely by self-play reinforcement learning, guided by MCTS, with zero human game data — but Go's rules happen to be extremely friendly to that recipe. The board is translationally invariant (matching a convolutional network's weight sharing), rotationally and reflectionally symmetric (so training data can be augmented 8× for free), the action space is a single move type (place a stone), and outcomes are binary wins or losses with no draws.

Chess and shogi share almost none of that structure. Their rules are position-dependent (pawns move two squares only from the second rank, promote only on the eighth) and asymmetric (pawns move one direction only, castling differs kingside vs. queenside), include long-range interactions (a queen can traverse the whole board, or deliver checkmate from across it), and — critically — both can end in a draw, with chess widely believed to be a theoretical draw under optimal play. Shogi adds a further twist Go and chess don't have: a captured piece changes sides and can be dropped back onto the board anywhere by its new owner, which inflates the action space and search complexity well beyond chess's. So the open question this paper poses is sharp: was AlphaGo Zero's tabula rasa success a general property of self-play reinforcement learning, or an artifact of Go's convenient symmetries — and can one algorithm, given nothing but each game's rules, actually beat the accumulated hand-engineering of Stockfish and Elmo (the reigning 2016 TCEC and 2017 CSA world-champion programs) from scratch?

// how it works

The method

AlphaZero keeps AlphaGo Zero's core architecture intact: a single deep neural network fθ(s) = (p, v) maps a board position s to a policy p — a probability over moves — and a scalar value v estimating the expected outcome. Self-play games are generated by MCTS, which uses fθ to guide simulations toward moves with high prior probability, high estimated value, and low visit count, and returns a search policy π over moves at the root. Games are played to completion using π at every step; the terminal outcome z (+1 win, 0 draw, −1 loss) is then used to train θ by minimizing the same joint loss as AlphaGo Zero:

l = (z − v)²  −  π⊤log p  +  c‖θ‖² // value error (mean-squared) + policy cross-entropy + L2 weight regularization — identical to AlphaGo Zero's objective

What's actually new is everything the paper removes to make the algorithm domain-agnostic, each one tied directly to a Go-specific convenience the gap above identifies:

  • Draws, not just win/loss. AlphaGo Zero optimized the probability of winning under a binary outcome assumption. AlphaZero instead estimates and optimizes the full expected outcome z ∈ {−1, 0, +1}, since chess and shogi can end drawn and Go cannot.
  • No symmetry exploitation. Go's rotational/reflectional symmetry let AlphaGo Zero augment training data with 8 symmetric copies of every position, and average MCTS evaluations over randomly rotated/reflected board views. Chess and shogi rules are asymmetric (pawns move one way, castling differs by side) — AlphaZero does neither, training and evaluating on the raw board only.
  • No best-player checkpointing. AlphaGo Zero periodically froze the current network as the "best player," measured a freshly trained candidate against it, and only started generating self-play games from the candidate once it won by a margin. AlphaZero drops this entirely: self-play games are always generated by the single, latest set of network parameters, continuously updated, with no evaluation step and no best-player selection.
  • No per-game hyperparameter search. AlphaGo Zero tuned its MCTS hyperparameters by Bayesian optimization specifically for Go. AlphaZero reuses one fixed set of hyperparameters, network architecture, and algorithm settings across chess, shogi, and Go without game-specific tuning — the sole exception is the Dirichlet exploration noise added at the root, scaled to each game's typical branching factor (α = 0.3 for chess, 0.15 for shogi, 0.03 for Go).

Everything else is domain knowledge limited strictly to encoding the rules, never to evaluation: spatial input planes matched to each board's grid (17 feature planes for Go, 119 for chess, 362 for shogi, each repeated over an 8-step history), a policy output as a stack of planes over legal move types (4,672 possible moves in chess including underpromotions; 11,259 in shogi including the piece-drop moves unique to that game), the game's termination rule for scoring simulations, and the typical legal-move count used only to scale exploration noise. AlphaZero trained one separate instance per game for 700,000 mini-batch steps (batch size 4,096) from randomly initialized parameters, using up to 5,000 first-generation TPUs to generate self-play games and 64 second-generation TPUs to train the network — but the actual compute-per-game still varied a great deal: 9 hours and 44 million self-play games for chess, 12 hours and 24 million games for shogi, 34 hours and 21 million games for Go (Table S3), reflecting each game's different board size and typical game length rather than a truly uniform budget.

// what they found

The results

All three instances cross their respective world-champion baseline in well under a day of self-play from scratch: AlphaZero outperformed Stockfish after just 4 hours (300k steps) in chess, Elmo after less than 2 hours (110k steps) in shogi, and AlphaGo Lee after 8 hours (165k steps) in Go (Figure 1). The fully-trained instances were then evaluated in dedicated 100-game tournament matches at 1 minute per move against Stockfish (chess), Elmo (shogi), and the previously published 3-day AlphaGo Zero (Go), with AlphaZero and AlphaGo Zero each restricted to a single machine with 4 TPUs while Stockfish and Elmo ran on 64 CPU threads with a 1GB hash table:

AlphaZero lost zero of its 100 games against Stockfish and lost 8 of 100 to Elmo, decisively defeating both, and won 60 of 100 against the previously published AlphaGo Zero. It did this while evaluating a small fraction of the positions its opponents did: Table S4 reports AlphaZero at just 80,000 positions/second in chess and 40,000/second in shogi, versus 70 million/second for Stockfish and 35 million/second for Elmo — roughly three orders of magnitude fewer evaluations, compensated by using the network to search far more selectively, "arguably a more human-like approach to search" the paper explicitly frames as closer to Shannon's original 1950 proposal than brute-force alpha-beta. Figure 2 shows AlphaZero's MCTS also scales better with additional thinking time than either alpha-beta engine, closing and then exceeding the gap as time per move grows — evidence against the widely held belief that alpha-beta search is inherently superior for these domains.

A separate analysis of the 12 most popular human chess openings (each played over 100,000 times in an online database) found that AlphaZero independently rediscovered and played every one of them during self-play, and — starting from each opening as either color in dedicated 100-game matches — still convincingly beat Stockfish overall (242 wins, 353 draws, 5 losses as White across the 12 openings; 48 wins, 533 draws, 19 losses as Black), suggesting the mastery wasn't narrowly tuned to a particular opening repertoire.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

The Stockfish/Elmo comparison is the part most worth pressure-testing, and it drew real pushback from the computer-chess community at the time. AlphaZero and AlphaGo Zero each ran on a single machine with 4 TPUs; Stockfish and Elmo were capped at 64 CPU threads and a 1GB hash table — a hardware and configuration choice DeepMind made, not a neutral, independently audited tournament setting. TPUs and CPU threads aren't fungible units, so "who had the bigger machine" isn't a clean question, and critics pointed out that Stockfish's engineers would have configured it very differently (larger hash tables were cheap and standard by 2017, and Stockfish's own developers noted the version and settings used weren't necessarily its strongest). The paper does report evaluation speed transparently (Table S4: 80k/40k positions-per-second for AlphaZero in chess/shogi vs. 70M/35M for Stockfish/Elmo), which is the honest way to let a reader judge the trade-off — but the head-to-head win/draw/loss numbers alone, without that context, invite reading the match as more hardware-neutral than it was.

"General reinforcement learning algorithm" is a claim about the procedure, not about a shared model: AlphaZero trains three completely separate networks, one from-scratch run per game, each seeing only that game's rules and never transferring anything learned in one domain to another. That's a meaningfully weaker claim than "one model masters three games," and the paper is careful never to claim the latter — but the framing in both the title and abstract ("a single AlphaZero algorithm") can be read as implying more unification than the training procedure delivers. The four simplifications relative to AlphaGo Zero (draw-aware value target, no symmetry augmentation, no best-player checkpointing, no per-game hyperparameter search) are also presented as a bundle with no ablation isolating which one, if any, mattered most to generalizing beyond Go — so it's not possible from this paper alone to tell whether continuous updating or dropping augmentation was the load-bearing change.

The compute footprint is also easy to undersell: up to 5,000 first-generation TPUs for self-play generation plus 64 second-generation TPUs for training, per game, is an industrial-scale resource commitment unavailable to essentially anyone outside a few labs at the time, which made independent reproduction slow — the open-source Leela Chess Zero project took many months of volunteer-donated compute to approach comparable strength, since DeepMind released no reference implementation alongside the paper.

baselines

Genuinely the reigning world-champion programs (2016 TCEC Stockfish, 2017 CSA Elmo, the previously published 3-day AlphaGo Zero) — strong choices in principle, but evaluated on hardware and configuration terms DeepMind selected rather than a neutral third-party tournament standard.

contamination

Not applicable in the usual data-leakage sense (pure self-play, no human game corpus to train on) — though the chess-opening analysis (Table 2) does draw on a human game database purely for identifying which openings to test against, not for training.

what's missed

No ablation isolating which of the four AlphaGo-Zero simplifications actually drove generalization to chess/shogi; the "single algorithm" framing can read as stronger than "three independently trained instances of one procedure"; and the training compute (thousands of TPUs) makes the specific Elo numbers hard to independently verify outside a similarly resourced lab.

would raise confidence

A released reference implementation at publication time (the gap community efforts like Leela Chess Zero later filled); a controlled, compute-normalized hardware comparison against Stockfish/Elmo; and an ablation isolating each of the four changes from AlphaGo Zero individually.

// bottom line

The central claim holds up well: the same self-play-plus-MCTS recipe, with Go-specific conveniences stripped out and almost no per-game tuning, does generalize to two structurally very different games and beats world-champion engines doing so — that's a real and important result, not an artifact of Go's symmetry. But "general" describes the procedure, not a shared model (three separate from-scratch runs), the Stockfish/Elmo hardware comparison was contested rather than neutral, and the massive TPU footprint made the result hard for anyone outside DeepMind to reproduce at the time. The paper's own framing — "the same algorithm... without any additional domain knowledge except the rules of the game" — was itself surpassed within two years by MuZero, which generalized one step further by discarding even the rules.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. AlphaGo Zero's tabula rasa self-play recipe was not a Go-specific trick: stripped of symmetry augmentation, best-player checkpointing, and per-game hyperparameter tuning, the same algorithm reaches superhuman play in chess and shogi too — games with draws, asymmetric rules, and (in shogi) a drop-and-reuse action space Go doesn't have.
  2. AlphaZero won every head-to-head match decisively — 28W/72D/0L vs. Stockfish, 90W/2D/8L vs. Elmo, 60W/0D/40L vs. AlphaGo Zero, all in 100-game tournaments — while evaluating roughly a thousand times fewer positions per second (80k/40k vs. 70M/35M) than the alpha-beta engines it beat, by searching far more selectively.
  3. "General" here means one procedure trained three separate times from scratch, not one shared model; the Stockfish/Elmo hardware comparison (TPUs vs. 64 CPU threads) drew real contemporaneous criticism as not fully neutral; and the result required an industrial TPU footprint that made independent reproduction slow until community efforts like Leela Chess Zero caught up months later. MuZero (2019) pushed the same lineage one step further, dropping the rules of the game entirely.
