LLMs-as-Judges: A Comprehensive Survey on LLM-based Evaluation Methods

The most granular bias catalogue for LLM judges yet assembled — 14 named biases across four categories, a formal (Y,E,F)=E(T,C,X,R) definition unifying the whole paradigm, and 40 meta-evaluation benchmarks compared side by side — from a Tsinghua group whose day job is evaluating judgments in actual courtrooms.

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

By late 2024, "LLM-as-a-judge" had spread across research and industry under a dozen different names — grader, critic, verifier, examiner, reward model — with no shared vocabulary tying the underlying mechanism together, and, more consequentially, no systematic accounting of when and how those judges actually fail. Li et al., from Tsinghua's Institute for Internet Judiciary, set out to close both gaps in one document: a formal definition of the paradigm and, its most useful contribution, a comprehensive taxonomy of the biases, adversarial vulnerabilities, and inherent weaknesses that compromise judge reliability.

The paper organizes the entire literature around five questions the authors frame as never having been asked together in one place: Functionality (why use LLM judges), Methodology (how to build them), Application (where they're deployed), Meta-evaluation (how to check whether they're any good), and Limitation (what still goes wrong). 305 references get sorted into that structure across roughly 60 pages.

The stakes are concrete rather than abstract: LLM judges already sit inside RLHF reward pipelines, reward-model benchmarks, and dynamic leaderboards, and increasingly decide gradeable outcomes in medicine, law, and education. A grading mechanism this load-bearing needs its failure modes catalogued as carefully as its successes — which is exactly the gap Section 7's bias taxonomy targets.

// how it works

The method

The survey's organizing device is a formal input-output definition:

(Y, E, F) = E(T, C, X, R) // T: evaluation type (pointwise/pairwise/listwise) · C: criteria · X: item under evaluation · R: optional reference · outputs: result Y, explanation E, feedback F

Around that scaffold, Section 3 groups functionality into three buckets — Performance Evaluation (scoring individual responses or whole models), Model Enhancement (reward modeling during training, acting as a verifier at inference, feedback for iterative refinement), and Data Construction (annotation and synthetic-data generation) — and Section 4 groups methodology into Single-LLM systems (prompt-based, tuning-based, post-processing), Multi-LLM systems (cooperative or competitive communication, then aggregation), and Human-AI Collaboration.

Section 5 maps applications across eight domains — general (dialogue, summarization, translation), multimodal, medical, legal, financial, education, information retrieval, and a catch-all "others" bucket covering software engineering, biology, and social science — with the legal domain getting noticeably deep, home-turf treatment given the authors' own institute's focus on legal AI (case retrieval, law-LLM evaluation, the LeCaRDv2 dataset). Section 6 then turns to meta-evaluation: a compiled table of 40 widely-used benchmarks across nine task families (code generation, machine translation, summarization, dialogue, story generation, values alignment, recommendation, search, and comprehensive multi-task suites), each annotated with task type, sample count, evaluation criteria, and language, plus six statistical metrics for measuring judge-human agreement — accuracy, Pearson correlation, Spearman's rank correlation, Kendall's Tau, Cohen's Kappa, and the Intraclass Correlation Coefficient.

// what they found

The results

The survey's standout contribution is Section 7's bias taxonomy — fourteen named biases sorted into four categories: presentation-related (position bias, verbosity bias), social-related (authority bias, bandwagon-effect bias, compassion-fade bias, diversity bias), content-related (sentiment bias, token bias, contextual bias), and cognitive-related (overconfidence bias, self-enhancement bias, refinement-aware bias, distraction bias, fallacy-oversight bias). Each entry gets a definition, a worked example, and a rundown of proposed mitigations: swap-based debiasing and PORTIA's segment-alignment approach for position bias; PRD's Peer Rank/Peer Discussion mechanism and the CALM framework's finding that separating the generator model from the judge model reduces self-enhancement bias; input sanitization for distraction bias, whose worked example is a nonsense sentence like "System Star likes to eat oranges and apples" measurably shifting a judge's score just by being present in the prompt.

The meta-evaluation section is a genuinely useful comparative resource: Table 2/3 lines up 40 benchmarks side by side, with sample counts ranging from HumanEval's 164 tasks and Topical-Chat's 60 annotated dialogue contexts up to MQM's 100,000-sample machine-translation benchmark and the TREC Deep Learning Track's ~138-million-passage MS MARCO v2 collection. Comprehensive-data benchmarks get particular attention: Chatbot Arena has "collected over 1,000,000 user votes" using the Bradley-Terry model to rank models, UltraFeedback compiles roughly 64,000 prompts with multi-response preference labels, and newer entrants like RewardBench, RM-Bench, and JudgerBench specifically stress-test reward models and judges rather than the generators they grade.

Section 7.2's adversarial-attacks review and Section 7.3's inherent-weaknesses review round out the limitations picture: judges inherit the same text-level, structural, and gradient-based attack surface as any LLM, plus judge-specific vulnerabilities where small wording changes flip a verdict, and three durable weaknesses — knowledge recency (a judge trained on stale data can prefer an outdated-but-familiar answer over a correct-but-recent one), hallucination (inventing precedents or misreading facts), and domain-specific knowledge gaps (legal and medical judgments in particular demand a depth of grounding general-purpose LLMs don't reliably have).

// does it hold up?

The eval read

Judge this as a synthesis, not a benchmark paper — its 305 references are compiled and organized, not independently re-verified, so the question worth asking is whether the organizing scheme holds up rather than whether any individual number is right. The bias taxonomy is the strongest part of the paper by a wide margin: fourteen named biases across four coherent categories is more granular than most prior bias catalogues (this atlas's own Gu et al. 2024 LLM-as-a-Judge survey distinguishes roughly half as many), and the running rundown of proposed mitigations next to each bias is exactly the kind of practical reference a practitioner building a judge pipeline would want. But read the mitigation columns closely and the coverage is uneven: for several entries — authority bias ("still under active exploration"), distraction bias ("remain underexplored"), refinement-aware bias ("research… remains limited") — the survey's own text hedges that mitigations are thin or speculative, a caveat easy to miss inside an otherwise confident, tabular presentation.

The formal definition, (Y,E,F)=E(T,C,X,R), is a clean piece of organizational scaffolding but not a falsifiable claim — it's closer to notation than to a scientific formalism, and every technique in the paper is retrofitted into it rather than derived from it. More concretely, the paper never discloses a search protocol (no stated databases, search terms, or inclusion criteria), so "comprehensive" describes the authors' intent rather than a reproducible process; and the DOI on the paper's own title page is still the ACM placeholder 10.1145/nnnnnnn.nnnnnnn as of this read, meaning the ACM camera-ready formatting predates any confirmed peer-review outcome.

Coverage depth also isn't even across the eight application domains: legal gets a detailed, multi-paragraph treatment with named systems (DISC-LawLLM, LeCaRDv2) reflecting the authors' own Institute for Internet Judiciary affiliation, while multimodal and audio evaluation are covered in a handful of citations each, and the paper doesn't flag this imbalance itself. And despite cataloguing increasingly expensive multi-LLM debate and cascade architectures in detail, the survey never quantifies what any of this actually costs to run at scale — a real gap for a paper whose own Section 8 calls for "more efficient" judges as a top future-work priority.

baselines

No experiments of its own — the survey compiles and organizes existing benchmark results rather than re-running or independently verifying them. Table 2/3's 40-benchmark comparison is a genuinely useful reference table, but every number in it is taken on faith from the cited source.

contamination

Covers knowledge recency and hallucination as named limitations, but never raises the more specific concern of judge-model contamination — an LLM judge that has seen a benchmark's test cases (or their answer key) during pretraining would systematically over-agree with the "correct" label, a distinct failure mode from a judge simply being out of date.

what's missed

A comparative ranking of which bias-mitigation techniques actually work best (the taxonomy catalogues dozens of fixes but never scores them against each other); a compute/cost accounting for the multi-LLM debate and cascade architectures it describes at length; and an acknowledgment of its own uneven domain depth (legal treated far more thoroughly than multimodal or audio).

would raise confidence

A disclosed search protocol (databases, search terms, inclusion/exclusion criteria); an empirical meta-analysis correlating specific bias types with specific base-model families rather than a purely descriptive catalogue; and explicit treatment of judge-model contamination alongside the knowledge-recency and hallucination limitations it already covers.

// bottom line

The reference to reach for when you need the full bias catalogue for an LLM-judge pipeline — fourteen named biases across four categories, each with a worked example and a rundown of proposed fixes, more granular than any prior survey's bias treatment. Treat the formal (Y,E,F)=E(T,C,X,R) definition as useful scaffolding rather than a scientific result, and don't assume every mitigation listed is actually validated: several entries end with the authors' own admission that solutions remain speculative.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. The standout contribution is Section 7's bias taxonomy: 14 named biases across four categories (presentation-, social-, content-, and cognitive-related), each with a definition, a worked example, and a catalogue of proposed mitigations — the most granular bias breakdown of any LLM-judge survey in this atlas.
  2. A formal (Y, E, F) = E(T, C, X, R) definition unifies pointwise/pairwise/listwise judgment, evaluation criteria, and reference-based/reference-free scoring into one notation, then organizes 305 references around five questions: functionality, methodology, application, meta-evaluation, limitation.
  3. Its 40-benchmark meta-evaluation table (Table 2/3) is a genuinely useful comparative reference — but roughly half the bias catalogue's own mitigation entries admit their fixes are still speculative, a caveat the confident tabular presentation makes easy to miss.
