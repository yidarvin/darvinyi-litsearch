LLM Defenses Are Not Robust to Multi-Turn Human Jailbreaks Yet

Four state-of-the-art LLM defenses report near-zero attack success against automated single-turn attacks. Hand them to expert human red teamers in a multi-turn chat box and success rates jump to 51.7–87.9% — and the same humans pull unlearned biosecurity knowledge back out of a model that was supposed to have forgotten it.

Nathaniel Li, Ziwen Han, Ian Steneker, Willow Primack, Riley Goodside, Hugh Zhang, Zifan Wang, Cristina Menghini, Summer Yue · Scale AI · UC Berkeley

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

A wave of recent LLM defenses — representation rerouting / "circuit breakers", latent adversarial training, decoupled refusal training — report headline robustness numbers that look like the problem is nearly solved. On standardized robustness benchmarks they drive attack success rate (ASR) to single digits, sometimes ~0%, by withstanding a battery of automated attacks: GCG, AutoDAN, PAIR, GPTFuzzer, AutoPrompt, and others.

The catch is in the threat model those numbers are measured under. Almost every automated attack jailbreaks the model in a single turn of conversation — one optimized prompt, one response. But that is not how people actually use chat assistants, and it is not how a determined malicious user would behave. Real usage is multi-turn: a person probes, backtracks, reframes, and steers the model across a conversation. The paper's central claim is that the field has been certifying robustness against the wrong threat model, and that "~0% single-turn automated ASR" is simply not evidence that a defense is safe to deploy.

There is a reason the multi-turn human setting is underexplored: it is expensive. Running expert red teamers is orders of magnitude costlier than running an automated attack, so researchers default to automated benchmarks. Scale AI — which runs commercial red-teaming engagements at volume — was unusually positioned to actually pay for the human study and to release the artifact that comes out of it: a dataset of real human jailbreaks plus the tactics taxonomy its red teamers developed across "dozens of commercial red teaming projects."

// how it works

The method

The study pits multi-turn human red teamers against six automated attacks on four defenses, all applied to the same base model (llama-3-8b-instruct) under a single shared system prompt: "You are a helpful AI Assistant!". The four defenses are RR (representation rerouting / circuit breakers), LAT (targeted latent adversarial training), DERTA (decoupled refusal training), and CYGNET (Gray Swan's closed-source circuit-breaker model, attacked through its API). Only attack-agnostic defenses are used — nothing trained against a specific attack — to avoid overfitting comparisons.

The threat model

Red teamers interact with a black-box chat interface, mirroring ChatGPT-style usage. They cannot see model internals or output probabilities, cannot prefill the assistant's response, and cannot access the assistant token. What they can do is converse over multiple turns and toggle the sampling temperature among three values (0, 0.5, 1). This is deliberately more realistic — and in some ways more restrictive — than the API-access assumptions many automated attacks rely on. The six automated baselines split into four black-box attacks (AutoDAN, GPTFuzzer, PAIR, Zero-Shot) and two white-box attacks that assume weight/gradient access (AutoPrompt, GCG); the white-box attacks actually exceed the human threat model, and are kept as a conservative stress test of the defenses.

The red-teaming pipeline

To turn messy human attempts into a defensible ASR, every harmful behavior runs through a multi-stage pipeline (Figure 1) built to suppress false positives. Up to two independent red teamers each get 30 minutes to attempt a jailbreak and self-rate it "failed", "partially successful", or "fully successful". Successful attempts are checked by a human reviewer; if rejected, a third independent red teamer re-attempts from scratch, followed by a second reviewer. Finally a GPT-4o classifier (gpt-4o-2024-05-13, with the HarmBench classifier prompt) adjudicates whether the behavior was genuinely elicited. The same classifier is used to score the automated attacks, keeping the success criterion consistent across attack types.

A taxonomy of human tactics

Every successful jailbreak is labeled with one dominant tactic from a 7-category taxonomy the red teamers built organically across commercial engagements: Direct Request, Echoing, Hidden Intention Streamline (a chain of individually-innocuous turns that add up to a harmful request), Injection, Obfuscation (burying the request in symbols, synonyms, or look-alike Unicode), Output Format, and Request Framing (fictional/urgent/emotional pretext). Several of these — Hidden Intention Streamline in particular — only make sense across multiple turns, which is the whole point.

Two evaluation wrinkles matter. First, the Ensemble Automated Attack is an upper bound on automated ASR: a behavior counts as broken if any of the six attacks breaks it. So when humans beat the ensemble, they beat the best-case union of all automated attacks, not just the average. Second, the unlearning experiment uses a different setup: red teamers attack an RMU-unlearned model on 43 WMDP-Bio questions converted to free-response, are given unlimited time (the experiment ran to 240 total red-teaming hours), and submissions are hand-graded because the HarmBench classifier can't judge dual-use biosecurity content. ASRs across the two settings are therefore not directly comparable.

// what they found

The results

Across all four defenses, multi-turn human red teamers beat every automated attack — and beat the ensemble of all six. On HarmBench (n=240), human ASR was 51.7% against RR, 75.4% against LAT, 87.9% against DERTA, and 70.4% against CYGNET. The ensemble automated upper bound was 8.3%, 10.0%, 68.3%, and 0.0% respectively. That is a human-over-ensemble margin of between +19.6% (DERTA) and +65.4% (LAT) on the three open-source defenses — and a 70.4%-vs-0% gap on the closed-source CYGNET, whose automated ASRs are cited from the circuit-breakers paper rather than re-run here.

The per-attack breakdown (Figure 4) makes the gap vivid: against RR and LAT, every individual automated attack sits near the floor (≤5.8%), while humans clear 50–75%. DERTA is the one defense automated attacks dent meaningfully (ensemble 68.3%), yet humans still push it higher to 87.9%. Notably, robustness to automated attacks is not a proxy for robustness to humans: CYGNET is more automated-robust than RR, but red teamers found CYGNET easier to break than RR.

Unlearning is not safe either

The most consequential result is that human red teaming recovers unlearned knowledge. On an RMU-unlearned model evaluated on 43 free-response WMDP-Bio questions, humans reached 51.2% ASR — versus 25.6% for the automated ensemble and just 11.6% for the best single automated attack (AutoPrompt / GPTFuzzer). That is +25.6% over the ensemble and +39.6% over the best single attack. Unlearning that looks robust to automated probes is therefore not robust to a person — the hazardous knowledge was obfuscated, not removed. (Recovery was still harder than ordinary jailbreaking: red teamers averaged 20.5 minutes per successful biosecurity submission and the authors suspect domain expertise would push ASR higher.)

It really is the multi-turn that matters

Post-hoc, 91.62% of successful HarmBench jailbreaks and 85.7% of successful WMDP-Bio jailbreaks required more than one turn — direct evidence that the tactics are optimized for, and depend on, the conversational setting that single-turn benchmarks never test. The authors attribute the gap partly to defenses being trained on short, single-turn refusals — "shallow" safety that is "only a few tokens deep." All of this is compiled into the released MHJ dataset: 2,912 prompts across 537 multi-turn jailbreaks, with per-submission metadata (tactic, time taken, temperature, and the red teamer's own notes), after legal review removed sensitive/export-controlled content.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

The headline "70%+ vs single-digit" comparison is doing a lot of rhetorical work, and to the authors' real credit they spend an entire Discussion section and Appendix A.3 dismantling it themselves. The honest reading is that this is a confounded comparison wrapped around a genuine finding. Two things differ at once between the human and automated columns: human vs automated, and multi-turn vs single-turn. The paper cannot separate them, so it cannot tell you whether a multi-turn automated attack (Crescendo, or the concurrent Gibbs et al. work it cites) would close most of the gap. The 91.62% "needed >1 turn" statistic strongly hints the turn-count is the dominant factor — which, if true, means the contribution is less "humans are special" and more "your benchmark omits the turn dimension."

The comparison is also not cost-controlled. Humans get 30 minutes and temperature-toggling against a black box; automated attacks get a fixed compute budget and (for the white-box ones) gradient access the humans never had. The authors are explicit that "any cost-controlled robustness analysis may yield different results." The CYGNET column is the weakest of all: its automated ASRs are cited from a different paper, possibly under a different system prompt, on a closed model attacked via a different channel — the 70.4%-vs-0% gap there should be read as suggestive, not measured.

And the ASRs themselves rest on small, noisy adjudication. HarmBench n=240; WMDP-Bio n=43 hand-graded free-response. Success is decided by a GPT-4o classifier whose own error rate is why the pipeline bolts on two human reviewers and re-audits 464+100 responses — and "fully" vs "partially successful" is a judgment call that moves the number. The red-team pool is small (≥6 per defense), expert, and demographically narrow (native-English North-American degree-holders), so ASR estimates this team, not a defense-intrinsic constant; a more skilled or better-resourced adversary would presumably score higher, not lower. Finally, all four defenses sit on an 8B model under a single generic system prompt — some weren't originally evaluated on HarmBench — so whether the gap survives at frontier scale, with guard models and rate limits the authors deliberately omitted, is untested.

baselines

Six automated attacks including the strongest public ones (GCG, PAIR, AutoDAN), and an ensemble upper bound — a fair and current automated suite. But humans and automated attacks run under different threat models (multi-turn vs single-turn, time-bounded vs compute-bounded), so the comparison isn't like-for-like, and the multi-turn automated baseline that would isolate the real variable is absent.

contamination

Not a leakage story — the risk here is adjudication. ASR hinges on a GPT-4o HarmBench classifier (mitigated by human review) and on a "fully successful" threshold that is partly subjective; WMDP-Bio is hand-graded with no reported inter-rater agreement.

what's missed

The number conflates "human" with "multi-turn"; the 91.62% >1-turn rate suggests turns, not humans, drive most of the gap. CYGNET's automated baseline is borrowed from another paper. Cost asymmetry and a small, expert red-team pool mean ASR isn't a defense-intrinsic quantity.

would raise confidence

Run a multi-turn automated attack (Crescendo / Gibbs et al.) on the same defenses to disentangle turns from humans; a compute/cost-matched comparison; larger n with classifier-validation and inter-rater numbers; and a frontier-scale defended model rather than llama-3-8b.

// bottom line

Read as a precise "humans are 8× better than machines" benchmark, it overclaims and the authors say so. Read as its actual thesis — that "~0% single-turn automated ASR" is not evidence a defense is deployment-safe, and that unlearned knowledge can be talked back out of a model — it is convincing and important. The lasting contribution is the reframed threat model plus the released MHJ dataset and tactics taxonomy, not the exact size of the deltas.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. Defenses reporting single-digit (even ~0%) ASR against automated single-turn attacks fell to 51.7–87.9% ASR under multi-turn human red teamers — a gap of +19.6% to +65.4% over the best-case automated ensemble.
  2. Machine unlearning is breakable too: humans recovered 51.2% of "unlearned" WMDP-Bio knowledge from an RMU model vs 25.6% for the automated ensemble — the knowledge was obfuscated, not removed.
  3. It's the conversation, not just the human: 91.62% of successful jailbreaks needed more than one turn, exposing single-turn benchmarks as the wrong threat model.
  4. The artifact is the point — MHJ (2,912 prompts, 537 multi-turn jailbreaks) plus a 7-tactic taxonomy from real commercial red-teaming — but the human-vs-automated ASR gap is confounded by threat model and cost, as the authors themselves stress.
