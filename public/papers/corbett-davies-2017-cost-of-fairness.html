Algorithmic Decision Making and the Cost of Fairness

Reformulates the 2016 COMPAS fairness fight as constrained optimization — maximize public safety subject to a formal fairness constraint — and prices out, on the same Broward County data everyone was already arguing about, exactly what each fairness definition costs in released-and-rearrested defendants.

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

By late 2016 the COMPAS controversy had produced two kinds of results, but not the one policymakers actually needed. One kind was impossibility: Kleinberg, Mullainathan & Raghavan and, separately, Alexandra Chouldechova had each shown — from the same Broward County numbers ProPublica used in its Machine Bias investigation — that calibration and error-rate balance cannot both hold whenever recidivism base rates differ by race. The other kind was construction: Feldman et al.'s disparate-impact repair, Zafar et al.'s decision-boundary-covariance constraints, Kamiran & Calders's "massaging," and Hardt, Price & Srebro's equalized-odds post-processing all showed how to build a classifier that provably satisfies one formal fairness definition or another. What neither strand supplied was an answer to the question a judge, legislator, or pretrial-services director actually has to weigh: if we adopt this fairness constraint instead of the rule that maximizes public safety, how many additional violent crimes does that choice cost us?

Existing fairness papers treated "the algorithm satisfies definition X" as the finish line. This paper treats it as the start of a trade-off analysis — putting every fairness definition on the same footing as a candidate policy with a measurable public-safety price tag, rather than a checkbox to be satisfied.

// how it works

The method

The paper casts pretrial release as constrained optimization rather than classifier design. A decision rule d(x) maps a defendant's visible attributes x — age, sex, prior convictions, COMPAS score — to a probability of detention d(x) ∈ [0,1]. Writing pY|X for a defendant's true probability of committing a violent crime if released, and c ∈ (0,1) for the cost of detaining one person in units of crime prevented, the immediate utility of a rule is the expected benefit of crimes prevented minus the expected cost of detention:

u(d,c) = E[Yd(X) − cd(X)] = E[d(X)(pY|X − c)] // utility is maximized pointwise by detaining exactly those with risk above c

Because utility decomposes this way, the unconstrained utility-maximizing rule is provably (Theorem 3.2) a single, uniform threshold on pY|X applied to everyone, irrespective of race — "maximize public safety" and "hold every defendant to the same standard" turn out to be the same objective. The paper then asks what happens when a rule must also satisfy one of three formal fairness properties: statistical parity (equal detention rates across race groups), predictive equality (equal false-positive rates — the specific pattern ProPublica's investigation flagged), and conditional statistical parity (equal detention rates within strata of "legitimate" factors such as binned prior-conviction count). For all three, the utility-maximizing rule that also satisfies the constraint is still a threshold rule on pY|X — but with a different threshold per race group (and, for conditional statistical parity, per stratum of legitimate factors too).

A single uniform threshold and a set of race-specific thresholds are structurally different policies. Whenever the two groups' risk distributions differ in shape — not necessarily in mean — the two rules diverge, and the constrained one sacrifices some of the utility the unconstrained one would have captured. That gap is the paper's namesake "cost of fairness." Figure 1 makes the mechanism concrete: it shows the actual Broward County risk distributions by race (left) alongside a simulated pair of populations with identical means but different tail shapes (right) — demonstrating that a single threshold produces racial disparities in detention and false-positive rates even in a case Kleinberg et al.'s base-rate argument alone doesn't cover.

// what they found

The results

The empirical section reruns this logic on the same data ProPublica compiled: 3,377 Broward County, Florida defendants assigned COMPAS scores within 30 days of arrest, tracked for two-year violent recidivism. The authors first re-fit a risk model (L1-regularized logistic regression with Platt scaling, excluding race) that edges out COMPAS's own accuracy — AUC 0.75 versus COMPAS's 0.73 — then, for each fairness constraint, find the utility-maximizing rule that both satisfies the constraint and detains the same 30% of defendants COMPAS itself flags as medium-or-high risk, so the comparison isolates the constraint's cost while holding total detention fixed. Averaged over 100 random 70/30 train-test splits:

Fairness constraint% of detainees who are low-riskEst. increase in violent crime
Statistical parity17%9%
Predictive equality14%7%
Conditional statistical parity10%4%

Read straight: enforcing statistical parity means 17% of the defendants who end up detained are actually low-risk (they'd be released under the safety-maximizing rule), and releasing an equal number of high-risk defendants in their place produces an estimated 9% increase in violent recidivism relative to the unconstrained optimum. Predictive equality is somewhat cheaper (14% low-risk detainees, 7% recidivism increase); conditional statistical parity — conditioning on binned prior-conviction count — is the cheapest of the three (10%, 4%).

The flip side is that the safety-maximizing single-threshold rule is not race-neutral in its outcomes. Detaining the same 30% of defendants with one threshold means detaining 40% of black defendants versus 18% of white defendants; and among defendants who did not go on to reoffend — the crux of ProPublica's original complaint — 32% of black defendants are detained versus 14% of white defendants, a clear predictive-equality violation produced by a rule that never looks at race. The paper ties this to "infra-marginality" in the econometric discrimination literature: detention rate and false-positive rate are statistics about people above a threshold, so they can differ across groups even when the threshold itself — the actually observable, directly comparable quantity — is identical.

Section 6 adds a sharper caution about the field's dominant fairness-of-scores criterion, calibration: COMPAS scores are shown to be well-calibrated by race (among defendants scoring 7, 61% of black and 60% of white defendants reoffended), yet calibration alone cannot certify that a score is free of engineered discrimination. Figure 3 constructs a hypothetical score for a "favored" group by adding Gaussian noise to true risk estimates and refitting: the resulting scores remain perfectly calibrated (right panel) while guaranteeing, by construction, that no one in that group crosses the detention threshold (left panel) — the same logic Hardt et al. raise in their discussion of "oblivious" fairness measures.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

The paper is candid about a foundational choice it makes rather than derives: it takes "maximize public safety," operationalized as violent-crime arrests within two years of release, as the objective function, and treats every fairness definition as a side-constraint against that objective. That framing is not neutral — whose safety is maximized, at what cost to whom, is exactly what the underlying policy fight is about — and the paper's own Discussion (Section 7) concedes the sharpest version of the problem: arrest data is an imperfect proxy for offending, and heavier policing of minority neighborhoods can inflate black defendants' arrest rates relative to white defendants committing the same underlying crime at the same rate. If that bias is present, the "safety-maximizing" single threshold is partly optimizing against a policing-intensity signal rather than true risk, and the entire cost-of-fairness computation inherits that same distortion — likely understating the real cost of the disparity the fairness constraints are trying to correct. The paper flags this candidly but does not attempt to bound it, so the 9%/7%/4% figures should be read as costs relative to arrest-based public safety specifically, not to some unbiased ground truth of who would actually have reoffended.

This paper needs to be read as the third leg of a near-simultaneous 2016–17 trio already on this graph. Kleinberg, Mullainathan & Raghavan prove calibration and balanced error rates are jointly satisfiable only when base rates match across groups — a purely structural, distribution-agnostic result. Chouldechova derives the same tension as an algebraic identity specifically for COMPAS's own numbers. Corbett-Davies et al. add the piece neither supplies: an actual price tag, in released-and-rearrested defendants, for choosing a fair rule over the safety-maximizing one on this same dataset — turning an impossibility result into a policy-relevant cost curve. But the paper's implicit claim that a single race-blind threshold is thereby the fair default deserves the same scrutiny it applies to the alternatives: Hardt, Price & Srebro's "oblivious measures" critique — which this paper explicitly invokes and which anchors its own Figure 3 — shows a facially race-blind rule can still discriminate by design if the inputs feeding it are engineered or degraded per group. The constitutional argument the paper offers in its favor (a uniform threshold likely survives strict-scrutiny review; race-specific thresholds might not) is a genuine legal-risk consideration, but it is an argument about litigation exposure, not evidence that the uniform rule is the substantively fairer or safer policy — a distinction the paper's own rhetoric sometimes blurs.

baselines

No ML "baseline" in the usual sense — this is a theoretical reformulation plus an empirical audit, and its comparison set (statistical parity, predictive equality, conditional statistical parity, the unconstrained optimum) is the complete relevant set from the papers it cites. The COMPAS-vs-refit accuracy comparison (AUC 0.75 vs. 0.73) is fair: both are evaluated on the same held-out Broward County split.

contamination

Not applicable in the leakage sense, but the analogous concern is real: the paper reuses ProPublica's exact Broward County extract and inclusion criteria, so every downstream number inherits that dataset's small size (3,377 defendants), single-county scope, and ProPublica's own construction choices.

what's missed

Table 1's 9%/7%/4% and 17%/14%/10% figures are point estimates averaged over 100 random splits with no reported confidence intervals in the main result, so it's unclear whether conditional statistical parity is really cheaper than predictive equality or within noise of it on a dataset this size. The comparison is also pinned to COMPAS's own 30% detention rate rather than showing how the fairness-cost ranking moves at other operating points.

would raise confidence

Uncertainty intervals (not just point estimates) around every number in Table 1; a sensitivity sweep across detention rates other than 30%; and an explicit bounding exercise for how much of the measured "cost of fairness" is attributable to arrest-based measurement bias in the outcome variable itself, given the paper's own acknowledgment that policing intensity varies by neighborhood race composition.

// bottom line

A genuinely useful piece of shared vocabulary — "here is the safety price of each fairness constraint, on the same numbers everyone was already arguing about" — but the price is denominated in an objective the paper itself flags as a biased proxy, and the implicit case for the race-blind threshold as the fair default earns the same skepticism the paper directs at its fairness-constrained alternatives.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. Fairness becomes a cost, not a checkbox. The paper reformulates algorithmic fairness as constrained optimization — maximize expected public safety subject to a formal fairness constraint — turning "does this algorithm satisfy definition X" into "what does satisfying X cost."
  2. A concrete price tag, on real data. On Broward County COMPAS data, enforcing statistical parity, predictive equality, or conditional statistical parity costs an estimated 9%, 7%, and 4% more violent recidivism respectively, while detaining 17%, 14%, and 10% low-risk defendants who'd be released under the safety-maximizing rule.
  3. The "fair" default has its own disparities. The safety-maximizing rule is a single, race-blind threshold — yet it still produces a 40%-vs-18% detention-rate gap and a 32%-vs-14% false-positive-rate gap between black and white defendants, because the two groups' underlying risk distributions differ in shape, not just mean ("infra-marginality").
  4. Calibration doesn't certify non-discrimination. Figure 3 shows a score can be strategically noised for one group, remain perfectly calibrated, and still guarantee no one in that group crosses the detention threshold — echoing Hardt et al.'s "oblivious measures" critique.
  5. The objective itself is contestable. "Public safety" here means arrest-based recidivism, which the authors themselves note can be inflated by heavier policing of minority neighborhoods — a caveat that applies directly to how literally the headline cost-of-fairness percentages should be taken.
