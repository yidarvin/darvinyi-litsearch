2023 · arXiv · Multimodal

Set-of-Mark Prompting Unleashes Extraordinary Visual Grounding in GPT-4V

GPT-4V can barely point at a dog in a photo when asked to output raw coordinates — 25.7% referring-expression accuracy. Overlay numbered marks on segmented image regions first, and the same frozen model, with no fine-tuning, jumps to 86.4%: beating specialist detectors and every open-source multimodal model tested.

Jianwei Yang, Hao Zhang, Feng Li, Xueyan Zou, Chunyuan Li, Jianfeng Gao · Microsoft Research · HKUST · University of Wisconsin-Madison

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

GPT-4V, when it arrived, showed unmistakably strong high-level vision-language understanding: it could describe a scene, answer questions about it, reason about relationships between objects. What it could not reliably do was fine-grained visual grounding — point precisely at a specific region and be right. The paper opens with concrete failure cases from public reports: GPT-4V "can hardly produce a sequence of accurate coordinates for a dog in the image," and the same failure extends to something as simple as a few traffic lights.

The obvious fix — train or prompt the model to emit numeric pixel coordinates directly — has two problems the authors identify. It hurts the natural fluency LLMs already have as text generators (coordinate sequences are an unnatural output mode for an autoregressive language model), and it discards spatial understanding the vision encoder already has rather than exploiting it, per the reasoning in Chen et al.'s Pix2Seq work. Prior visual-prompting approaches — a hand-drawn red circle (RedCircle), a highlighted region (FGVP), circles-and-arrows in an early qualitative GPT-4V exploration ("The Dawn of LMMs") — sidestep the coordinate problem, but only for one or a few objects at a time, and their marks (an arbitrary circle, a color wash) aren't cleanly "speakable": there's no natural way for the model's text output to unambiguously refer back to "that circle" versus another one. The gap, then, is a prompting method that can mark many regions of an image at once with symbols the model can both recognize and reliably reproduce in its own text — turning grounding into something closer to multiple-choice than to freehand pointing.

// how it works

The method

Set-of-Mark (SoM) prompting is entirely a change to the input image — no fine-tuning, no change to GPT-4V itself. Given an image I, SoM produces a marked image Im by overlaying interpretable symbols on a set of automatically-detected regions, then feeds that marked image (plus an unchanged text prompt) to the model:

To = F(SoM(I), Ti) // F = the frozen LMM; SoM augments only the image input I, leaving the text prompt Tⁱ untouched

There are three moving parts.

1. Image partition

An off-the-shelf toolbox of segmentation models — SEEM and SAM for open-vocabulary, promptable segmentation, Semantic-SAM for multi-granularity part/object masks, and MaskDINO where strong closed-set precision is needed — divides the image into K semantically meaningful binary-mask regions. Both automatic (whole-image) and interactive (user-clicked) modes are supported, and granularity is adjustable, from whole objects down to their parts.

2. Mark type and placement

Each region gets a mark — numbers, letters, masks, or boxes — chosen for interpretability: numeric marks are avoided on an image that's already full of numbers (an arithmetic problem), and alphabetic marks are avoided on a page of text, so the marks never collide semantically with the image's own content. Placing marks naively at each region's centroid causes collisions in dense scenes, so the paper's mark-allocation algorithm sorts regions by ascending area, excludes area already covered by smaller (already-placed) regions from consideration, then runs a distance transform to find the point of each remaining region that is maximally far from its boundary — pushing marks toward open space instead of overlapping neighbors.

3. Prompting

The marked image can be paired with a plain text prompt (no explicit reference to marks — the model spontaneously grounds its answer in them, e.g. "the lamp, which is numbered as 12") or an interleaved prompt that injects mark references directly into the question. Because each mark is tied 1:1 to a mask, any mark GPT-4V mentions in its text output can be traced straight back to a pixel-level region — giving a purely text-generating model a path to producing effectively pixel-grounded answers.

// what they found

The results

The headline comparison is stark and appears in the paper's own framing as verification of its hypothesis: prompted for coordinates directly, GPT-4V scores just 25.7% accuracy on RefCOCOg referring expression comprehension (REC). With SoM — same frozen model, zero additional training — it reaches 86.4%, ahead of every specialist and open-source LMM tested, including Grounding DINO (86.1%*), Ferret (85.8%*) and PolyFormer (85.8%*), MiniGPT-v2 (84.4%*), and Shikra (82.6%*) (starred numbers are as reported in each model's own paper on the full validation set; SoM's own numbers, like most rows in the table, are measured on a GPT-4V-quota-constrained subset).

The pattern holds across every task in Table 2: on open-vocabulary segmentation, zero-shot GPT-4V+SoM (75.7 COCO, 63.4 ADE20K) closes in on fully fine-tuned MaskDINO (80.7 COCO) and dramatically outperforms the zero-shot specialist OpenSeeD on ADE20K (23.4) — evidence the authors read as strong cross-domain generalization. On referring-expression segmentation (RefCOCOg, mIoU), SoM's 75.6 beats specialist PolyFormer's 67.2 "by a large margin." On video object segmentation, GPT-4V+SoM's 78.8 J&F is the best of any generalist model tested, ahead of SegGPT's 75.6. Only phrase grounding on Flickr30K is essentially a tie with the strongest specialist (89.2 vs. Grounding DINO's 90.5).

Two ablations isolate why it works. Adding boxes on top of numbered masks lifts Flickr30K R@1 from 84.4 to 89.2 — extra shape information helps. And swapping the toolbox's predicted masks for ground-truth masks on RefCOCOg lifts mIoU from 75.6 to 90.1, a 14.5-point jump — meaning a meaningful share of SoM's remaining error is simply upstream segmentation quality, not a limit of GPT-4V's grounding itself.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

SoM's central claim — marks unlock grounding GPT-4V otherwise can't do — is well supported by the coordinate-vs-SoM gap (25.7 → 86.4) and by two clean ablations. But the surrounding evaluation has real gaps the paper itself is candid about in places, and a few it doesn't dwell on.

1. Every number depends on an upstream segmentation model the paper isn't evaluating. SoM's own "ceiling" experiment shows exactly how much: swapping predicted masks for ground truth lifts RefCOCOg mIoU by 14.5 points (75.6 → 90.1). That means the reported 75.6 conflates GPT-4V's grounding ability with MaskDINO's segmentation quality, and the two are not separated anywhere else in the results — a reader can't tell how much of any headline number is "GPT-4V got it right" versus "the segmenter proposed a good enough region to begin with."

2. Most evaluation is on small, GPT-4V-quota-constrained subsets, not full validation sets. Table 1 shows the actual sample sizes: 100 images (567 instances) for COCO, 100 images (488 instances) for ADE20K, 100 images (274 instances) for Flickr30K, 100 images (177 instances) for RefCOCO, 71 videos (157 instances) for DAVIS. The paper is explicit that this is a "limited quota" constraint from the ChatGPT interface (no GPT-4V API access at the time) — an honest limitation, but it means every comparison against specialists benchmarked on full validation sets (marked with * in Table 2) is comparing a ~100-image subsample against a full-dataset number, not an apples-to-apples evaluation.

3. The evaluation pipeline was manual, one chat window per instance. The authors describe "a divide-and-conquer strategy" of authors manually running instances through the ChatGPT interface, one new chat window per instance to avoid context leakage. This is a defensible workaround for the tooling available in October 2023, but it introduces no formal inter-annotator check on how model outputs were parsed and scored, and no reported variance across repeated runs of the same prompt — a real concern for anything downstream that wants to reproduce these exact numbers today.

4. No contamination discussion. COCO, RefCOCOg, and Flickr30K are old, extremely well-known, and near-certainly within GPT-4V's training distribution in some form; the paper doesn't address whether high performance partly reflects memorized associations between these specific images/captions and their labels rather than purely the SoM mechanism generalizing. The DAVIS2017 and ADE20K results, being less textually captioned in typical web corpora, are somewhat more likely to reflect genuine grounding, but this isn't discussed either way.

5. "Extraordinary" is doing some of the paper's own argumentative work. The title's framing and the Discussion section's "mystery in GPT-4V" language lean into presenting the capability jump as an emergent surprise rather than working through the more mundane alternative explanation the ablations themselves partly support: that a meaningful share of the gain is simply giving an already-strong OCR-capable model a well-posed multiple-choice task instead of an open-ended coordinate-regression one.

baselines

Broad and current for late 2023 — specialist detectors/segmenters (MaskDINO, Grounding DINO, PolyFormer, SegGPT, SEEM) and contemporaneous open LMMs (Shikra, LLaVA-1.5, MiniGPT-v2, Ferret) — but most specialist numbers are pulled from their own papers' full-dataset runs, compared against SoM's ~100-image subset.

contamination

Not discussed. COCO, RefCOCOg, and Flickr30K are canonical, widely-scraped datasets almost certainly represented in GPT-4V's training data in some form.

what's missed

No decomposition of GPT-4V grounding error from upstream segmentation error, despite the paper's own ablation showing a 14.5-point swing from mask quality alone; small evaluation subsets throughout; no formal reproducibility check on the manual, per-instance ChatGPT-interface evaluation process.

would raise confidence

Full-validation-set evaluation once API access allowed it; an explicit upper/lower bound separating segmentation-toolbox error from GPT-4V grounding error; a contamination check or a held-out, post-cutoff image set; repeated-run variance on a sample of prompts.

// bottom line

The core mechanism is real and it stuck: SoM's numbered-region-overlay idea became a standard building block almost immediately (SeeAct tests it directly as a web-agent grounding strategy three months later, in January 2024; WebVoyager builds its entire action space around a DOM-derived variant of it). But the specific numbers in this paper — measured on ~100-image subsets, compounded with an unevaluated upstream segmenter, and gathered through a manual one-chat-per-instance process — should be read as a proof of concept that a much larger, better-controlled follow-up study would need to firm up, not as settled benchmark results.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. Make the mark speakable, not just visible. The reason SoM works where a red circle or highlighted region didn't is that a number or letter is something GPT-4V can output verbatim in its own text — turning open-ended pointing into a task the model was already good at (multiple-choice / OCR) instead of one it wasn't (coordinate regression).
  2. The gain is entangled with an unevaluated upstream segmenter. The paper's own ground-truth-mask ablation shows a 14.5-mIoU swing on RefCOCOg from mask quality alone — a caution against reading any single SoM number as purely "how good is GPT-4V's grounding."
  3. The idea generalized faster than the specific numbers were stress-tested. Built on ~100-image subsets and a manual evaluation pipeline (no GPT-4V API access yet in late 2023), the paper is best understood as the proof of concept that made numbered-mark overlays a default building block for later web- and GUI-agent grounding work — not as a fully hardened benchmark result in its own right.
