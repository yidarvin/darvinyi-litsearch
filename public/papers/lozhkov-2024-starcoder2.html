StarCoder 2 and The Stack v2: The Next Generation

A 619-language, 67.5 TB rebuild of the BigCode training corpus on Software Heritage's full archive, paired with a 3B/7B/15B code-model family whose 15B model matches or beats CodeLlama-34B and DeepSeek-Coder-33B — models more than twice its size — while shipping the training-data provenance behind every one of its tokens.

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

By early 2024, the strongest open-weight code models — Code Llama, DeepSeek-Coder — released weights but not training data. Nobody outside those labs could check what the models had memorized, whether the eval sets were contaminated, or whose code was actually in the corpus. The BigCode project's own first attempt at doing better, The Stack v1, was a genuine step forward on governance (opt-out tooling, permissive-license-only filtering) but was small and dated next to the trillion-token corpora backing the newer closed-data entrants: 6.4 TB across 384 languages, harvested purely by file extension.

The gap, then, was less "can an open corpus train a competitive model" — StarCoder had already answered that in 2023 — and more "can a fully-transparent, opt-out-respecting, license-audited pipeline scale to the size needed to stay competitive with 3–4 trillion-token training runs, without the transparency becoming a performance tax." StarCoder2 and The Stack v2 are BigCode's answer: rebuild the corpus on Software Heritage's much larger archive, keep every governance guarantee from v1 (and add a few), and see whether the resulting 3B/7B/15B models still land near the top of their size classes.

// how it works

The method

The Stack v2 is built on top of the Software Heritage (SWH) graph dataset — a fully deduplicated Merkle DAG of the SWH archive's 2023-09-06 snapshot — rather than a fresh GitHub crawl. The team extracts the latest revision of each repository's main branch, deduplicates by content hash, and detects language with a classifier (go-enry) rather than by file extension, surfacing 658 distinct languages before filtering down to a final 619. The raw result is 67.5 TB, roughly ten times The Stack v1's 6.4 TB.

License handling is the paper's most distinctive engineering artifact: a cascading decision procedure (shown below) that falls back from GitHub's own repo-level license metadata, to file-level ScanCode detection over files matching a license-reference regex, to propagation of a detected license across files sharing its base path. Only permissively-licensed and unlicensed files are kept; copyleft and commercial-licensed code is excluded outright — a stricter policy than "grab everything and let downstream users sort it out."

Source code is only one of eight ingested sources. The pipeline also pulls in GitHub issues and pull requests (~300M PRs, backed by 15 TB of base code, rendered with username pseudonymization), Jupyter notebooks converted to scripts and structured notebook form (near-deduplicated down to roughly 4M and 4.6M examples respectively) plus a separate Kaggle notebooks pipeline, scraped code documentation across dozens of sites, and math/coding/NL corpora (OpenWebMath, arXiv, Wikipedia, StackExchange, and a small licensed high-quality text set). Every source with substantial reuse risk is run through five preprocessing stages: near-deduplication (MinHash, following the SantaCoder pipeline), PII redaction (the StarPII model, plus username masking on PRs/issues/notebooks), decontamination against evaluation benchmarks, malware scanning (ClamAV plus community malware signatures), and opt-out removal driven by BigCode's "Am I in The Stack" governance tool.

After filtering, deduplication, and subsampling, the actual training set is ~3 TB, 900B+ unique tokens — 4× the first StarCoder's training set, still a fraction of the 67.5 TB raw archive. On top of this corpus, the team trains dense transformer models at 3B, 7B, and 15B parameters, replacing StarCoderBase's learned positional embeddings with RoPE (θ=1e5) and its Multi-Query Attention with Grouped-Query Attention (2 KV heads at 3B, 4 at 7B/15B). Base training runs at a 4k context window for 3.1–4.1 trillion tokens (3.3–4.3 trillion once the long-context stage is included) — roughly 4.5 to 5.3 training epochs over the unique-token pool, deliberately well past the Chinchilla compute-optimal token budget — followed by long-context fine-tuning to 16k tokens with a 4,096-token sliding window and FlashAttention-2. Repository examples are also transformed with repo-context, file-level fill-in-the-middle (FIM): 50% of repositories are selected as FIM candidates, and within those, each chunk is FIM-transformed with 50% probability, so the models learn to infill as well as complete.

// what they found

The results

The headline comparison is code completion on HumanEval/HumanEval+ and MBPP/MBPP+ (EvalPlus's stress-tested variants), evaluated with greedy decoding, grouped by size class against StarCoderBase, Code Llama, DeepSeek-Coder, and StableCode:

ModelHumanEvalHumanEval+MBPPMBPP+
StarCoder2-3B31.727.457.447.4
StarCoder2-7B35.429.954.445.6
DeepSeekCoder-6.7B47.639.670.256.6
StarCoder2-15B46.337.866.253.1
CodeLlama-34B48.244.365.452.4
DeepSeekCoder-33B54.346.373.259.1

StarCoder2-3B is the strongest small model tested on every one of the four columns, and even edges out the previous-generation StarCoderBase-15B on HumanEval/HumanEval+ — a 60.2% relative gain over StarCoderBase-3B on HumanEval+ and 32.4% on MBPP+. StarCoder2-15B clears CodeLlama-13B by a wide margin (46.3 vs. 37.8 on HumanEval) and comes close to — sometimes exceeds — CodeLlama-34B and DeepSeek-Coder-33B, both more than twice its parameter count; DeepSeek-Coder-33B still leads on raw high-resource-language completion. StarCoder2-7B is the one outlier: it beats CodeLlama-7B and its own StarCoderBase-7B predecessor but trails DeepSeek-Coder-6.7B by a wide margin, and — as the authors themselves flag — "it is not clear to this report's authors why StarCoder2-7B does not perform as well as StarCoder2-3B and StarCoder2-15B for their size."

The pattern holds beyond completion. On 8-shot GSM8K math reasoning (via the PAL program-synthesis prompting method), StarCoder2-15B scores 65.1% — ahead of both CodeLlama-34B (54.2%) and DeepSeek-Coder-33B (58.7%) despite being under half their size. On CRUXEval (predicting a Python function's input or output), the paper reports StarCoder2-15B likewise surpassing DeepSeek-Coder-33B. The consistent story: StarCoder2-15B's edge over larger models shows up specifically on reasoning-flavored evaluations (math, execution simulation, low-resource languages), while DeepSeek-Coder-33B keeps the lead on raw high-resource code completion.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

The baselines are the right ones and evaluated fairly on the surface — all are non-instruction-tuned base models compared like-for-like, and the size-class grouping (small/medium/large, plus two "extra-large" bonus comparisons) is transparent about which models are more than twice StarCoder2-15B's size before the headline "matches or beats" claims land. But the framing does real work: DeepSeek-Coder-33B is the strongest model on the table for high-resource code completion, and that fact sits inside the same report that opens by advertising StarCoder2-15B matching or outperforming "extra-large" models — true on math and reasoning benchmarks, not true on the benchmark most practitioners actually care about first (HumanEval/MBPP completion).

The bigger issue is decontamination scope. Section 3.3 is explicit: the pipeline removes files containing docstrings or solutions from HumanEval and MBPP, docstrings from APPS, questions from GSM8K, and prompts from DS-1000 — five benchmarks, matched by string search with whitespace stripped. But StarCoder2 is evaluated on well over a dozen benchmarks (MultiPL-E, CRUXEval, CanItEdit, RepoBench, CrossCodeEval, HumanEvalFix, the "Asleep at the Keyboard" security suite, BOLD/WinoBias/HONEST bias suites), and the paper is silent on whether any of those were swept for leakage. Worse, the decontamination step itself explicitly excludes docs, LHQ, arXiv, and Wikipedia — exactly the natural-language sources where a HumanEval or MBPP problem (or a discussion of it) is most likely to appear in prose rather than as a literal code solution, and where simple docstring/solution string-matching wouldn't catch it anyway.

Almost no pass@1 number in the report carries a variance estimate — HumanEval/MBPP (Table 9) and GSM8K (Table 14) are single greedy-decoding runs with no seed range reported. The one partial exception is CRUXEval, where the authors borrow the original CRUXEval paper's noise estimate (~0.2% sampling noise for 10 samples) to caveat a couple of close calls — but that's someone else's prior estimate applied after the fact, not the authors' own multi-seed measurement, and it isn't extended to any other benchmark. That's a defensible default for headline comparisons with large gaps (StarCoder2-15B's 8.5-point lead over CodeLlama-13B on HumanEval is not going to be noise), but it leaves the closer calls on the ungapped benchmarks unverifiable: StarCoder2-7B vs. CodeLlama-7B is 35.4 vs. 33.5 on a 164-problem benchmark, well within the range where a different sampling seed could move the ranking. The unexplained 7B anomaly — worse relative to its own model family than either sibling size, with the authors admitting they don't know why — is exactly the kind of result an ablation or a second training run would help disentangle, and neither appears in the report. The paper also discloses, to its credit, two implementation bugs that affect controlled comparison within its own model family: StarCoder2-15B trained with RoPE θ=1e4 instead of the intended 1e5 (a training-config parsing bug), and repo-level FIM ran at a lower rate than intended for most of training (Table 16), which the authors say explains StarCoder2-15B's comparatively weak FIM-benchmark score.

baselines

Fair and size-labeled (Code Llama, DeepSeek-Coder, StableCode, StarCoderBase, all non-instruct base models), but the "matches/beats models twice its size" framing is true on math/reasoning benchmarks and false on the highest-resource completion benchmarks, where DeepSeek-Coder-33B leads outright.

contamination

Decontamination is real but narrow: string-match removal covers only 5 of the ~15+ benchmarks evaluated, and explicitly exempts docs, LHQ, arXiv, and Wikipedia — the natural-language sources most likely to carry paraphrased or discussed benchmark content.

what's missed

No seed/variance reporting on the headline HumanEval/MBPP/GSM8K numbers (CRUXEval borrows someone else's noise estimate, not extended elsewhere); the unexplained StarCoder2-7B underperformance is flagged but not investigated; two disclosed training bugs (15B's RoPE θ, FIM rate) complicate clean within-family comparison.

would raise confidence

Multi-seed pass@1 with confidence intervals; a decontamination coverage report spanning every evaluated benchmark, not just the five named in §3.3; an ablation isolating the 7B anomaly; an n-gram/canary overlap audit specifically on the docs/arXiv/Wikipedia slices exempted from decontamination.

// bottom line

This is a genuine advance in training-data transparency and governance for open code models — the license cascade, opt-out tooling, and SWHID traceability are unusually rigorous for a model release — and the headline results (especially StarCoder2-3B and -15B) are real, verifiable state-of-the-art claims within their size classes. But the paper's own contamination controls don't reach most of the benchmarks it reports numbers on, and nearly every comparison is a single greedy-decoding run with no variance reporting, so "punches above its weight class" should be read benchmark-by-benchmark rather than as a blanket property of the model family.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. The Stack v2 rebuilds BigCode's training corpus on Software Heritage's full archive: 67.5 TB raw (10× The Stack v1), reduced to ~3 TB / 900B+ unique training tokens across 619 programming languages plus PRs, notebooks, docs, and math/NL sources, all under an opt-out and license-cascade governance pipeline.
  2. StarCoder2-15B, trained on 3.3–4.3T tokens well past Chinchilla-optimal, matches or beats CodeLlama-34B and DeepSeek-Coder-33B — both more than twice its size — on math reasoning (GSM8K 65.1% vs. 54.2%/58.7%) and code-execution reasoning, while DeepSeek-Coder-33B keeps the lead on high-resource code completion.
  3. The paper's decontamination pipeline names exactly five benchmarks (HumanEval, MBPP, APPS, GSM8K, DS-1000) and explicitly excludes docs/arXiv/Wikipedia from the sweep, leaving most of the dozen-plus benchmarks the model is actually evaluated on — and the natural-language sources most likely to leak benchmark content in prose — outside its own stated contamination controls.
