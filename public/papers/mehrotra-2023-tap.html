Tree of Attacks: Jailbreaking Black-Box LLMs Automatically

TAP turns PAIR's single-chain iterative refinement into a branching, pruned search tree: an attacker LLM proposes several rewrites of a harmful prompt at once, an evaluator LLM prunes the off-topic ones before they're ever sent to the target, and only the survivors get queried. The result jailbreaks GPT4-Turbo and GPT4o for 84–94% of prompts — on GPT4o specifically, a 16-point higher success rate than the prior black-box state of the art (PAIR) while sending 60% fewer queries.

Anay Mehrotra, Manolis Zampetakis, Paul Kassianik, Blaine Nelson, Hyrum Anderson, Yaron Singer, Amin Karbasi

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

By late 2023, jailbreaking a production LLM through nothing but its chat API was a solved-in-principle problem, but every practical route to it had a real weakness. Hand-crafted jailbreaks (the DAN-style prompts, persona modulation) needed human ingenuity and didn't scale. White-box optimizer attacks like GCG could run without human effort, but required weights and a tokenizer the attacker doesn't have for GPT-4 or Claude — and the strings they produce are gibberish, trivially caught by a perplexity filter. The one method that was automated, black-box, and produced natural, hard-to-detect prompts was PAIR (Chao et al., 2023) — Prompt Automatic Iterative Refinement — which uses an attacker LLM to rewrite a single candidate prompt, chat-style, until it either jailbreaks the target or runs out of turns.

The gap is that a single refinement chain is a narrow search: PAIR commits early to whichever rewrite direction looks promising and, when that direction fails, it can only keep iterating along that same line of conversation rather than branching off and trying several rewrite strategies at once. PAIR's own authors explored this — their recommended fix is to divide a fixed query budget across several independent chains run in parallel — but that only spends more queries on more chains; it doesn't let one chain learn from another or systematically discard bad candidates before they cost a query. Nobody had yet applied the two ideas that make tree search useful — branching to explore multiple directions, and pruning to kill bad ones early — to black-box jailbreaking specifically.

// how it works

The method

TAP — Tree of Attacks with Pruning — is initialized with two auxiliary LLMs, an attacker A and an evaluator E, plus the black-box target T being attacked. It maintains a tree whose nodes are candidate prompts, starting from the root query Q (the harmful request in plain language). At every iteration it runs four steps:

  1. Branch. For each current leaf, the attacker A generates b variations of that leaf's prompt (the branching factor), conditioned on the full conversation history of past attempts and their outcomes.
  2. Prune, phase 1. The evaluator E checks each new variation against an Off-Topic function — does it still ask for the same information as the original query? — and deletes any that have drifted off-topic, before they cost a query to the target.
  3. Attack and assess. Only the surviving on-topic variations are sent to the target T. The evaluator then runs a Judge function (scored 1–10 by an LLM) on each response; a score of 10 means the target fully complied, and TAP returns that prompt immediately.
  4. Prune, phase 2. If no jailbreak is found, only the top-w highest-scoring leaves (the width) are kept for the next iteration; the rest are discarded to keep the tree's growth in check.

This repeats until a jailbreak is found or the tree reaches a maximum depth d. In their main experiments the authors fix depth d = 10, width w = 10, and branching factor b = 4, chosen so TAP's total query and token usage roughly matches PAIR's under the same budget. Framed against PAIR directly: PAIR is the special case of TAP with b = 1 and no pruning at all — a single chain with no branching and no filtering of off-topic candidates. The paper is explicit that TAP is "simple to implement: only requiring a few additional lines of code over PAIR," and frames its own contribution as showing that branching plus pruning, not a fundamentally new attack idea, is what closes the gap.

Both attacker and evaluator roles are filled by carefully system-prompted LLMs (Vicuna-13B-v1.5 as the attacker in the main experiments, matching PAIR's own default, GPT-4 as the evaluator), rather than any fine-tuned or specialized model — the entire method rides on prompting off-the-shelf LLMs to play red-team roles. Success is measured two ways: an automated GPT4-Metric (the same Judge-style scoring used internally) and a manual Human-Judgement pass by the authors, following Wei, Haghtalab & Steinhardt's (2023) grading guidelines with the generating method anonymized to reduce bias.

// what they found

The results

On the AdvBench Subset (50 harmful requests across 32 categories, following Chao et al.'s benchmark), TAP beats PAIR on jailbreak rate on almost every target while using fewer queries. The headline comparison, using GPT-4o — "the latest LLM from OpenAI as of May 2024" — TAP finds jailbreaks for 94% of prompts against PAIR's 78%, a 16-point improvement, while sending 60% fewer queries to the target. On GPT-4 specifically, TAP reaches 90% success with a mean of 28.8 queries, versus PAIR's 60% at 39.6 queries. Across the full model sweep (Vicuna, Llama-2-7B-Chat, GPT-3.5, GPT-4, GPT-4-Turbo, GPT-4o, PaLM-2, Gemini-Pro, Claude-3-Opus), TAP wins on jailbreak rate for every target except Llama-2-7B-Chat (both methods near-zero — 4% vs 0%) and, on Claude-3-Opus, TAP's higher success rate (60% vs 24%) comes at the cost of more queries (116.2 vs 55.0). Against GCG — the white-box attack that can only run on open-weight models — TAP roughly matches GCG's success rate on Vicuna-13B (98% both) while using orders of magnitude fewer queries (GCG needs 256K model evaluations; TAP needs 11.8 on average).

TAP also holds up against a guardrail layer: with responses filtered through Llama-Guard, TAP's success rate stays close to its unprotected numbers (e.g. GeminiPro: 90% protected vs 96% unprotected) and remains well above PAIR's protected numbers on most models, though the number of queries needed rises more than PAIR's does. Transferability — whether a prompt that jailbroke one target also jailbreaks another — comes out roughly even between TAP and PAIR; both clearly beat GCG's transfer rate, whose gibberish suffixes the authors attribute to "not carry[ing] natural meaning."

The paper's own ablation isolates why the method works. Removing branching (TAP-No-Branch, b = 1 but pruning kept) drops GPT-4-Turbo's success rate from 84% to 48% despite using more queries (33.1 vs 22.5) — branching is what drives the success-rate gain. Removing pruning instead (TAP-No-Prune, branching kept but no off-topic filtering) keeps success close to full TAP (72% vs 84%) but very nearly doubles the query cost (55.4 vs 22.5) — pruning is what keeps the method query-efficient. Neither ablation alone reproduces full TAP; the paper argues both mechanisms are needed together.

On judge reliability specifically, the authors report their GPT-4 judge's own false-positive and false-negative rates against a scale of 1–10 (10 = jailbreak): 13% false positives and 0% false negatives (Appendix A.2) — a check on the automated metric that most black-box jailbreak papers of this era simply don't run.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

The PAIR comparison is the paper's central claim, and it's mostly fair: TAP and PAIR share the same attacker model (Vicuna-13B-v1.5), the same evaluator (GPT-4), the same AdvBench Subset, and — per the paper's own framing — PAIR is literally the b=1, no-pruning special case of TAP, so the ablations (Table 4) double as an apples-to-apples decomposition of where the gain comes from. One caveat the authors flag themselves and deserves to be taken seriously: Table 1's own note states that "the success rate of PAIR in our evaluations differs from those in" the original PAIR paper (Remark A.1) — their own PAIR numbers are a re-run, not a citation of the original paper's reported numbers, and that re-run is what TAP is measured against throughout. That's the right way to do it (same harness, same judge), but it also means the reported gap between TAP and PAIR is partly a statement about this paper's PAIR implementation, not necessarily the published PAIR result.

The success metric leans heavily on an LLM judge scoring 1–10, and while the reported 13% false-positive / 0% false-negative rates (Appendix A.2) are a genuinely useful piece of self-auditing that many contemporaries skip, a 13% false-positive rate is not small when the headline numbers are being read to two significant figures — a 94% success rate with a 13% FP rate on the positive class leaves real room for the true jailbreak rate to be meaningfully lower than reported, and the paper doesn't propagate that uncertainty into its headline table. The Human-Judgement cross-check (Section D.1) is the right instinct, but as author-graded rather than independently annotated, it doesn't fully substitute for inter-rater reliability.

The benchmark itself is narrow: AdvBench Subset is 50 prompts across 32 categories, and the "new dataset" of 123 prompts is generated by asking an uncensored open model (WizardVicuna30B-Uncensored) to generate variants of those same 50 prompts — so the two eval sets are not independent; a defense or judge quirk specific to AdvBench-style phrasing could inflate both. The transferability study (Table 3) is also scoped only to prompts that succeeded on Vicuna-13B/GPT-4/GPT-4-Turbo, which biases the transfer sample toward "already strong" attacks rather than a random sample of attempts. Cost is reported only qualitatively via query counts, not dollars — a meaningful gap given the method depends on multiple LLM calls (attacker + evaluator + target) per branch per iteration, and the authors' own broader-impact section acknowledges the method could lower the resource bar for real-world misuse, which is precisely the kind of claim a cost table would substantiate or temper.

baselines

Well-controlled against PAIR — same attacker/evaluator/benchmark, and PAIR is formally TAP's b=1 special case — but the authors' own PAIR numbers are a re-run that differs from the original paper's reported results (flagged in Remark A.1), so the headline gap is partly a statement about this re-implementation.

contamination

Not classical leakage, but the two eval sets aren't independent: the "new" 123-prompt dataset is generated by paraphrasing the same 50 AdvBench Subset prompts used as the primary benchmark.

what's missed

No dollar-cost or wall-clock reporting despite the method's multi-call-per-branch cost structure; the 13% judge false-positive rate isn't propagated into headline-number uncertainty; transferability is measured only on prompts that already succeeded, biasing toward strong attacks.

would raise confidence

Independent (non-author) human annotation for a subset of Human-Judgement scores; a genuinely held-out third benchmark beyond AdvBench-derived prompts; reported cost in dollars/latency alongside query counts; confidence intervals around the headline success rates given the known judge error rate.

// bottom line

The core empirical claim — branching plus pruning meaningfully improves on PAIR's single-chain search, at both higher success and lower query cost — is well-supported by the paper's own controlled ablation (Table 4), which is the strongest piece of evidence here precisely because it isolates the mechanism rather than just beating a black-box baseline. The headline success-rate numbers deserve a slightly more skeptical read given the judge's own reported 13% false-positive rate and the non-independence of the two benchmark sets, but the directional finding — search structure matters more than raw attacker cleverness for automated black-box jailbreaking — is credible and, per the citation graph, became a reference point other tree/graph-search jailbreak methods built on directly.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. PAIR is TAP with the branching and pruning switched off. Formally, PAIR is the b=1, no-pruning special case of TAP's tree search — which makes TAP's ablation (Table 4) a clean decomposition: dropping branching costs 36 points of success rate (84%→48% on GPT-4-Turbo) despite using more queries; dropping pruning keeps success nearly intact but roughly doubles query cost (22.5→55.4).
  2. Search structure, not a smarter attacker, closes the gap to the black-box state of the art. Both TAP and PAIR use the same attacker model (Vicuna-13B-v1.5) and evaluator (GPT-4); the entire performance jump — 94% vs 78% on GPT-4o with 60% fewer queries — comes from how candidate prompts are explored and discarded, not from a better prompt-generation model.
  3. Interpretable, black-box jailbreaks resist a guardrail layer better than expected. TAP stays close to its unprotected success rate even behind Llama-Guard (e.g. 90% vs 96% on GeminiPro) — a reminder that a response-filtering guardrail is a different, and not obviously sufficient, layer of defense from the alignment training of the underlying model itself.
