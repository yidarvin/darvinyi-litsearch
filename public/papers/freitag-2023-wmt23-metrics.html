Results of WMT23 Metrics Shared Task: Metrics Might Be Guilty but References Are Not Innocent

The annual meta-evaluation that validates automatic MT metrics against professional human judgment (MQM), redesigned this year into a streamlined 10-task weighted score. XCOMET-Ensemble wins outright, a synthetic "best-of-N" reference recovers correlation the official human reference was actively destroying, and a calibration study finds that the folk-wisdom "2 BLEU points is meaningful" is off by up to 5.5×.

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

Automatic MT metrics are only as trustworthy as the process used to validate them, and that process has two soft spots the field routinely ignores. First: last year's WMT22 meta-evaluation scored metrics across 201 separate tasks and then averaged per-task ranks — a scheme the organizers themselves call out as unwieldy, since adding one new metric to the comparison meant re-running expensive pairwise significance tests against every other metric on every task, and could shuffle the whole leaderboard's rankings as a side effect. Second, and more fundamental: reference-based metrics (BLEU, chrF, COMET, BLEURT) all assume the human reference translation they're compared against is good. Nobody routinely checks that assumption — but if the reference itself is bad, the metric's correlation with human judgment collapses regardless of how good the metric is.

There's a third, more practical gap: practitioners don't have a reliable intuition for what a metric score difference actually means. "Model B is 2 BLEU points better than Model A" gets treated as a real, human-noticeable improvement, but nobody had rigorously connected metric-score deltas to whether human annotators would actually judge the difference as significant.

// how it works

The method

The task evaluates both reference-based and reference-free (QE) metrics on three language pairs with fresh professional MQM (Multidimensional Quality Metrics) annotations collected specifically for this shared task: English→German (paragraph-level), Hebrew→English (low-resource, sentence-level), and Chinese→English (high-resource, sentence-level). Every submitted metric scores the same pool of competing MT systems, and its scores are compared against the human MQM ratings.

A streamlined, weighted meta-evaluation

Instead of 201 tasks and rank-averaging, this year uses exactly 10 tasks spanning three correlation/accuracy statistics — system-level pairwise ranking accuracy (pooled across all three language pairs, weight 3), segment-level pairwise accuracy with tie calibration (the acc*eq variant from Deutsch et al., 2023, which gives metrics credit for correctly predicting when human raters called two translations a tie), and Pearson correlation at both system and segment level for each language pair (weight 1 each) — and simply takes the weighted average.

overall score = Σt=1..10 wt · scoretΣt=1..10 wt // system-level accuracy carries weight 3; the other 9 tasks carry weight 1 each

This makes a new metric's overall score cheap to compute (no re-ranking against every existing metric) and puts scores on an absolute, comparable scale — at the cost of two limitations the authors disclose themselves: correlation statistics with different dynamic ranges get averaged together (mean segment-level Pearson is far lower than mean system-level Pearson, so system-level performance is implicitly upweighted), and the 10 tasks aren't statistically independent, since several share the same underlying MQM data.

Fixing a bad reference with a synthetic one

The Chinese→English human reference (refA) turned out to score worse on MQM than almost every submitted MT system — an obvious problem for any metric that compares translations against it. The fix: for every source segment, pick whichever candidate (any competing MT system's output, or the original human reference) got the lowest MQM error score, and stitch those together into a synthetic reference. The original human reference is eligible to "win" a segment like any other candidate.

Calibrating score differences to human significance

For every pair of competing MT systems, the organizers run a one-sided paired t-test on segment-level MQM scores to get a p-value, then fit an isotonic regression mapping a metric's score difference between the two systems to Pr(pmqm < 0.05 | ΔM) — the probability that the human-judged difference is statistically significant, given the metric's score gap.

// what they found

The results

XCOMET-Ensemble wins the overall ranking with an average correlation of 0.825 across the 10 tasks, ahead of the reference-free XCOMET-QE-Ensemble and MetricX-23 (both 0.808) and the LLM-prompting metric GEMBA-MQM (0.802). The established baselines trail clearly: COMET scores 0.779, BLEURT-20 scores 0.776, and the classic overlap metrics land at the bottom of the neural pack — BLEU at 0.696 and chrF at 0.694, both still comfortably ahead only of the weakest entries.

Correlation skill doesn't transfer across granularity: a metric that separates whole MT systems almost perfectly can still struggle to rank individual segments. Figure 5 shows nearly every metric's correlation climbing steeply from "overall" to "sys" (system-level) — several metrics exceed 0.9 — and then dropping sharply back down at "seg" (segment-level), often below 0.5. A metric earning a top system-level ranking is not automatically reliable for judging a single sentence pair.

The reference-quality experiment is the paper's most concrete finding. The official zh→en human reference scored an MQM error of 4.83 — worse than the best MT system (2.10) — while the synthetic best-of-N reference cut that down to 0.66, an average of well under one minor error per segment. Swapping in the synthetic reference produced a dramatic jump in every reference-based metric's correlation for zh→en, at both segment and system level. For en→de, where the original reference was already decent (2.96 MQM error vs. 0.87 synthetic), the swap barely moved segment-level correlation and had only a mixed effect at the system level — confirming the effect is specifically about reference quality, not an artifact of the construction method itself.

Finally, on the magnitude-of-difference calibration: contrary to the common belief that "2 BLEU points" signals a meaningful improvement, the paper finds the actual 80%-confidence threshold ranges from 2.2 BLEU (zh→en) to 3.5 BLEU (he→en) to 11 BLEU (en→de) — while the matching COMET thresholds for that same 80% confidence of a human-judged significant difference are far smaller in absolute terms: 0.013 (zh→en), 0.014 (he→en), and 0.030 (en→de).

// does it hold up?

The eval read

This is itself a meta-evaluation study, so its own rigor is the whole point — and it holds up better than most, mainly because the organizers are unusually candid about where it's shaky. The BLEU/chrF baselines are computed with SacreBLEU using disclosed signatures (Post, 2018), sidestepping the classic "which BLEU implementation" reproducibility trap. The weighting scheme (system-level accuracy at 3×, everything else at 1×) is disclosed rather than hidden, but it's also not really justified beyond "last year's approach did something similar" — a reader can't tell whether a different weighting would have reordered the top of the leaderboard.

The clearest structural wrinkle is a disclosed conflict of interest: several shared-task organizers are also metric developers competing in their own task (Ricardo Rei submits COMET/CometKiwi variants; Tom Kocmi co-develops other entries). The Ethics section addresses this directly — organizer-affiliated metrics used "frozen stable versions... dated before this year's shared task," and newer COMET versions were "developed without using any of the test set, test suite or challenge sets" — but there's no separate comparison excluding organizer-affiliated submissions, so a skeptical reader has to take the disclosure at face value rather than see it demonstrated in the rankings.

The synthetic-reference result is the paper's most useful finding and also its most self-aware limitation: the organizers explicitly flag it as "cherry-picking" — the synthetic reference is built by selecting, per segment, whichever candidate (including competing MT systems) scored best on the very MQM ratings later used to validate metrics against it, which is circular by construction. They caution readers not to over-read the correlation jump for exactly this reason, but they also don't quantify how much of the "impact on metrics" effect is genuine reference-quality signal versus this circularity. And the significance-calibration study, while a genuinely useful contribution, explicitly doesn't account for cross-task non-independence — many of the 10 meta-evaluation tasks "are based on the same underlying data," which the authors admit "violate[s] the assumptions of our hypothesis tests," deferring a fix to future work.

baselines

Fair and current: BLEU/chrF use disclosed SacreBLEU signatures, and the neural comparison set (COMET, BLEURT-20, BERTscore, MetricX-23, GEMBA-MQM) reflects the actual state of the art that year, not a stale strawman.

contamination

No train/test leakage in the classic sense (fresh MQM ratings each year), but a disclosed organizer/participant conflict of interest — several task organizers also submit competing metrics — mitigated by using frozen pre-existing metric versions, not eliminated by a blinded comparison.

what's missed

No sensitivity analysis on the task-weighting scheme (why system-level accuracy gets 3× weight and not 2× or 4×); the synthetic-reference circularity isn't quantified, only flagged; cross-task statistical dependence is acknowledged but not corrected for.

would raise confidence

A leaderboard variant excluding organizer-affiliated metrics, an ablation over alternative task-weighting schemes, and a held-out human reference (never used to build the synthetic reference) to measure how much of the correlation gain from Section 8 is circularity versus real reference-quality signal.

// bottom line

One of the more self-auditing evaluation papers in the MT space — it names its own weak points (weighting choices, cross-task dependence, cherry-picked synthetic references, organizer conflicts of interest) rather than burying them, which is exactly what a paper whose entire purpose is validating other people's metrics should do. The magnitude-calibration table (Table 10 in the original) is the single most immediately useful artifact for anyone reporting MT results: a BLEU or COMET delta means nothing on its own without a threshold like this attached to it.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. XCOMET-Ensemble topped the streamlined 10-task, weighted meta-evaluation at 0.825 average correlation, with neural metrics (COMET 0.779, BLEURT-20 0.776) clearly ahead of overlap-based BLEU (0.696) and chrF (0.694) — but a metric's system-level ranking skill doesn't guarantee segment-level reliability.
  2. Reference quality can dominate metric choice: the zh→en human reference scored a worse MQM error (4.83) than most MT systems it was meant to grade against, and a synthetic best-of-N reference (0.66 MQM error) recovered much of the lost correlation — though the authors flag this construction as inherently "cherry-picked."
  3. "2 BLEU points" is not a reliable rule of thumb: the actual 80%-confidence-of-significance threshold ranges from 2.2 to 11 BLEU depending on language pair, versus 0.013–0.030 for COMET — use the paper's calibration table, not folklore, when deciding whether a reported score gap is real.
