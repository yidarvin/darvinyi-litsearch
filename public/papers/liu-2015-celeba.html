2015 · ICCV · Benchmarks & Evals

Deep Learning Face Attributes in the Wild

The dataset behind a decade of face-attribute, face-verification, and face-generation research — 200,000 celebrity photos, each hand-labeled with 40 binary attributes — paired with a two-stage CNN cascade (LNet + ANet) that learns to find the face from nothing but image-level attribute tags, no bounding boxes or landmarks required.

Ziwei Liu, Ping Luo, Xiaogang Wang, Xiaoou Tang · The Chinese University of Hong Kong

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

By 2014, attribute recognition ("is this face smiling? wearing lipstick? bald?") split into two camps, and both had a ceiling. Global methods ran a classifier over the whole image and were robust to localization noise but couldn't exploit part structure. Local methods — Kumar et al.'s FaceTracer, Zhang et al.'s PANDA — first detected facial landmarks or parts, extracted HOG-like or CNN features from each aligned patch, and concatenated them; this was more discriminative in principle, but it made every downstream prediction hostage to the upstream detector. The paper's own Figure 1 shows the failure mode directly: when a HOG+SVM pipeline mis-localizes or misaligns a face under pose, lighting, or occlusion, several attribute predictions flip to wrong outright, even though the true labels ("Arched Eyebrows," "Smiling," "Young") are visually obvious to a human. Face localization and alignment "in the wild" — as opposed to frontal, studio-lit portraits — were, in the authors' words, "still not well solved problems," so any attribute pipeline built on top of them inherited that fragility.

The second half of the gap was data. Every attribute-recognition method up to that point trained and evaluated on datasets far smaller than what deep CNNs need to generalize across real-world pose and lighting variation, and none paired that scale with dense, per-image attribute annotation. Landmark-based training data is also expensive to collect — bounding boxes and keypoints require far more annotator effort per image than a checklist of yes/no attributes. So the paper attacks both problems in one release: a weakly-supervised localization method that needs only image-level tags, and a face-attribute corpus — CelebA — an order of magnitude larger than anything publicly available at the time.

// how it works

The method

The framework cascades two AlexNet-derived CNNs that are pre-trained on completely different tasks before ever seeing an attribute label. LNet (split into LNeto and LNets) handles localization: LNeto is pre-trained to classify the 1,000 object categories of ILSVRC 2012 (1.2 million training images) — nothing to do with faces — and is then fine-tuned using only image-level attribute tags, never a bounding box or landmark. The surprising result: the averaged response map of LNeto's fifth convolutional layer lights up on the head-and-shoulders region anyway, purely because attribute tags correlate with "there is a face here." Thresholding that response map yields a face proposal window directly — a form of weakly-supervised localization emerging from classification-style fine-tuning, years before this became a standard trick. LNets repeats the same recipe one level in, localizing the tighter face crop from the head-shoulder region LNeto found.

ANet then takes the localized, resized face crop and predicts the 40 attributes. It is pre-trained not on ImageNet but by classifying massive face identities — the same identity-discrimination pretext used by contemporaneous face-verification work (DeepFace, DeepID) — before being fine-tuned with attribute tags. Its four conv layers mix globally-shared filters (C1, C2) with locally-shared filters (C3, C4), which the authors find sharpens face-specific features but breaks the usual trick of computing one feature map for the whole image and pooling per-patch. Their fix is an "interweaved operation": instead of convolving each overlapping patch independently (redundant compute) or padding one shared map for all patches, they build a separate interweaved feature map per local filter's receptive-field group so every filter still runs once over the full image. The result is a single feed-forward pass that extracts features for every patch at once — a measured 6× speedup over naïve patch-by-patch scanning. Per-patch SVMs (via LIBLINEAR) predict each attribute; the final call is the average across patches, which absorbs residual localization jitter.

L = Σᵢ [ yᵢ log p(yᵢ|x) + (1−yᵢ) log(1−p(yᵢ|x)) ],  p(yᵢ=1|x) = 11 + exp(−f(x)) // LNet fine-tuning loss — cross-entropy over each binary attribute tag, no localization supervision anywhere in the objective

Underneath the method is the CelebA release itself: face images drawn from the CelebFaces and LFW corpora — celebrity photographs taken by professional photographers, as the paper's own footnote is careful to distinguish from the ordinary-user phone photos in its separate "MobileFaces" generalization test set — annotated by a professional labeling company with 40 binary attributes and 5 landmark locations per image.

// what they found

The results

The dataset itself, in the paper's own words: CelebA contains ten thousand identities, twenty images each — two hundred thousand images in total — each annotated with forty binary face attributes and five landmark points by a professional labeling company, totaling over eight million attribute labels. (CelebA's public release, which became the field's default benchmark, is documented at 202,599 images across 10,177 identities — a slightly finer count than this paper's own rounded totals.) A companion set, LFWA, carries 13,233 images of 5,749 identities and over five hundred thousand attribute labels. CelebA is split identity-disjointly: 8,000 identities / 160,000 images pretrain and fine-tune LNet and ANet; a separate 1,000 identities / 20,000 images train the per-attribute SVMs; a final held-out 1,000 identities / 20,000 images are test-only.

On attribute prediction (Table 1, averaged over all 40 attributes), LNets+ANet reaches 87% on CelebA and 84% on LFWA, against FaceTracer's 81%/74%, PANDA-w's 79%/71%, and PANDA-l's 85%/81% — all three baselines retrained on the identical CelebA/LFWA splits for a fair comparison. The PANDA-l gap matters most: it is handed ground-truth landmark boxes that LNets+ANet never receives, and still loses by 3 points. An ablation swapping out ImageNet pre-training (LNet trained from scratch on attribute tags alone) drops localization recall by 74 percentage points at an IoU overlap of 0.5 — the paper's sharpest evidence that it's the object-classification pre-training, not the attribute fine-tuning, that teaches LNet where faces are. On a held-out, differently-distributed 3,876-image "MobileFaces" test set (ordinary users' phone photos rather than celebrity press photography), LNet still gains 7 points over the best baseline at FPPI=0.1, evidence the weakly-supervised localizer isn't just overfitting to CelebA's photographic style.

On a 19-attribute extended set evaluated under the POOF protocol (Table 2), LNets+ANet averages 83% versus FaceTracer's 73% and POOF's 76% — 10- and 7-point gains — with individual attributes like "Gender" at 94% and "Blond Hair" at 89%. On a third, manually-labeled 30-attribute extension (LFWA+, designed to test generalization to attributes never trained on until a lightweight SVM re-fit), LNets+ANet still gains 8, 10, and 3 points on average over FaceTracer, PANDA-w, and PANDA-l respectively. Wall-clock: 35ms to localize plus 14ms to extract ANet features per 300×300 image on GPU, versus roughly 80ms for naïve patch-by-patch scanning alone — the interweaved feed-forward operation earns its keep.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

As a methods paper, the evaluation is careful in exactly the ways that are easy to skip: every baseline (FaceTracer, PANDA-w, PANDA-l) is retrained on the identical CelebA/LFWA splits rather than quoted from its original paper, PANDA-l is handed ground-truth landmark boxes LNets+ANet never gets and still loses, and the pre-training ablation (LNet with vs. without ImageNet pre-training) isolates which specific design choice buys the accuracy rather than reporting one entangled number. LFWA+'s 30 attributes, manually labeled after the fact and never seen during CNN training, is a legitimate transfer test, not just more of the same 40 attributes reshuffled.

The dataset side of the release is where the scrutiny thins out. The 40 (and later 19 + 30) attributes are compound, subjective judgment calls — "Attractive," "Chubby," "Young," "Big Nose" — made once by a single professional labeling vendor; the paper discloses no inter-annotator agreement, no adjudication protocol, and no estimate of label noise, even though several of these attributes have no ground truth to check against beyond the labelers' own aesthetic and demographic assumptions. More consequentially: CelebA's images come from CelebFaces and LFW, both built from existing press and internet photography of celebrities — footnote 3 of the paper itself notes these are "professional photographers'" images, explicitly contrasted with the ordinary-user phone photos in its own MobileFaces control set. That collection pipeline structurally over-represents whoever entertainment media chose to photograph in that era — disproportionately white, Western, young, conventionally attractive faces — and the paper reports zero demographic breakdown of its own identities to let a reader check how skewed the corpus actually is. None of the depicted individuals consented to their likeness becoming ML training/eval data; that provenance gap is common to essentially every press-scraped face corpus of the period (LFW itself included), but CelebA's decade-long run as the default face-attribute, face-verification, and GAN-training benchmark makes the gap unusually consequential rather than a one-off.

This is not a hypothetical downstream risk — it is precisely the gap surfaced by later work built on this exact dataset. Mitchell et al.'s Model Cards for Model Reporting paper builds its worked example on a smiling-face classifier trained and evaluated on CelebA, disaggregated by the gender/age attributes CelebA already provides — and its own Caveats section states plainly that CelebA's annotations "don't cover race or Fitzpatrick skin type — which has been reported as a source of disproportionate errors," and that its own gender attribute is treated as strictly binary. The broader Gender-Shades-style audit literature on commercial face-analysis systems found exactly the accuracy gaps by skin tone and gender that a CelebA-only evaluation, lacking those annotations from the outset, has no mechanism to detect.

baselines

Fair: FaceTracer, PANDA-w, and PANDA-l are all retrained on the paper's own CelebA/LFWA splits rather than quoted from prior publications, and PANDA-l is given ground-truth landmarks LNets+ANet doesn't get — the toughest apples-to-apples comparison available at the time.

contamination

Not an issue in 2015 — CelebA's own identity-disjoint 8,000/1,000/1,000 split is the release's whole design point. The real leakage risk arrived later, once CelebA became the default pretraining corpus for face-recognition and face-generation systems subsequently benchmarked on overlapping face pools.

what's missed

A single 40-attribute average hides both per-attribute variance the paper's own Table 1 already shows (some attributes score in the high 60s, others in the high 90s) and per-subgroup variance that's never reported at all — no accuracy breakdown by the race/gender/age labels CelebA itself supplies.

would raise confidence

Disaggregated accuracy by CelebA's own demographic-adjacent attributes; disclosed inter-annotator agreement for a sample of the 40 labels; and a stated collection/consent protocol for the celebrity photographs, rather than silently inheriting CelebFaces/LFW's scraped provenance.

// bottom line

The CNN methodology holds up well under its own tests — fair retrained baselines, a clean ablation isolating pre-training's contribution, and a genuine held-out generalization check. But the paper's lasting influence is the dataset, not the cascade, and it's precisely the dataset's undisclosed demographic composition and absent consent protocol that later work — Gender Shades, and the Model Cards paper this survey traces the connection to directly — had to reconstruct from the outside.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. CelebA — ~200,000 celebrity face images (202,599 / 10,177 identities in its public release), 40 binary attributes, 5 landmarks — is the paper's lasting contribution; a decade later it's still a default training/benchmark corpus for face-attribute prediction, face verification, and GAN-based face generation.
  2. The LNet+ANet cascade shows a usable face localizer can emerge from image-level attribute tags alone, with no bounding box or landmark ever labeled — by pre-training on unrelated ImageNet object categories and reading off where conv5's response map happens to light up.
  3. Every reported gain (2–10 points depending on the split) comes from baselines retrained on the exact same data as the proposed method — a fair same-data comparison, not a borrowed-number leaderboard entry, and the paper's ablations correctly isolate pre-training as the source of the localization gain.
  4. The dataset's blind spots — no race/skin-tone annotation, no disclosed consent or demographic makeup for its celebrity subjects, no inter-annotator agreement for any of its 40+19+30 attributes — are exactly what Mitchell et al.'s 2018 Model Cards paper had to name explicitly when it built a worked smiling-classifier example on top of this same data.
