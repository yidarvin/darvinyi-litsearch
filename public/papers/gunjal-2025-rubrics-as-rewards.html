Rubrics as Rewards: Reinforcement Learning Beyond Verifiable Domains

RLVR works when an answer can be checked — math, code, a boxed letter. RaR asks: what reward do you use when there is no single right answer? Its bet is to have an LLM write a per-prompt checklist, then let a cheap judge score against it and feed that score straight into GRPO.

Anisha Gunjal, Anthony Wang, Elaine Lau, Vaskar Nath, Yunzhong He, Bing Liu, Sean Hendryx · Scale AI

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

Reinforcement Learning with Verifiable Rewards (RLVR) is the engine behind the recent reasoning leap — DeepSeek-R1, DeepSeekMath, the o-series. It works because in math and code the reward is free and exact: run the test cases, check the boxed answer, return 1 or 0. No reward model, no human raters, nothing to overfit.

That engine stalls the moment you leave verifiable domains. Ask a model the most likely diagnosis for a patient with a pH of 7.05 and a base deficit of −40 mEq/L, and there is no single string to match against — a good answer is judged on accuracy and completeness and safety and whether it avoids a common misdiagnosis. The usual workaround is a preference-based reward model trained on pairwise comparisons, but those are expensive to collect and notorious for latching onto superficial artifacts: response length, formatting quirks, annotator idiosyncrasies.

Meanwhile, evaluation benchmarks had quietly solved a related problem. HealthBench grades free-form medical answers against thousands of instance-specific, physician-written rubrics — explicit checklists of what a good answer must contain. RaR's observation is simple: if a rubric is a good enough signal to grade a model, it should be a good enough signal to train one. The gap is that rubrics had only ever been used for evaluation; nobody had closed the loop and turned them into the reward for on-policy RL.

// how it works

The method

RaR has two halves: generate a rubric per prompt, then use that rubric as the reward inside GRPO. There is no trained reward model anywhere in the pipeline.

1. Synthesize the rubric

For each training prompt, a strong LLM (they use o3-mini and GPT-4o) writes 7–20 self-contained criteria, conditioning on the dataset's golden reference answer as a stand-in for expert supervision. Each item gets a categorical weight — Essential, Important, Optional, or Pitfall (a negative criterion, e.g. "avoids a common misdiagnosis", phrased positively so satisfying it adds to the score). The authors hold up four desiderata for a good rubric: grounded in expert guidance, comprehensive coverage, criterion importance, and — crucially for automation — each item must be self-contained, judgeable in isolation without extra context.

2. Turn the rubric into a scalar reward

Two aggregation strategies. Explicit asks the judge to score each criterion independently as a 0/1 satisfied check, then takes a weighted, normalized sum (weights mapped from the categorical labels: Essential 1.0, Important 0.7, Optional 0.3, Pitfall 0.9):

r(x, ŷ) = Σj wj · cj(x, ŷ)Σj wj // explicit aggregation — cj ∈ {0,1} is the judge's verdict on criterion j; normalization keeps rewards comparable across prompts with different rubric counts

Implicit hands the whole rubric — all criteria and their labels — to the judge at once and asks for a single holistic 1–10 score, normalized to [0,1]. This delegates the weighting to the model and sidesteps hand-tuning entirely. The judge in all training runs is the cheap gpt-4o-mini.

A tidy framing the paper makes: RLVR is just the k=1 special case of RaR — one criterion, weight 1, where the binary check is exact-match or test execution. Rubric rewards generalize that to multi-dimensional, partially-subjective supervision.

3. Train

Everything runs on a single 8×H100 node. The base policy is Qwen2.5-7B, optimized with GRPO (batch 96, lr 5×10⁻⁶, 16 samples per prompt, temperature 1.0). Two datasets are released: RaR-Medicine (20k medical-reasoning prompts, rubrics by GPT-4o) and RaR-Science (~20k prompts aligned to GPQA-Diamond categories, rubrics by o3-mini).

// what they found

The results

The headline: the best variant, RaR-Implicit, beats the Direct-Likert reward baseline by up to 31% relative on HealthBench and 7% relative on GPQA-Diamond. Concretely, on HealthBench overall score it moves a Qwen2.5-7B policy from 25.5 → 31.2, and on GPQA-Diamond from 34.8 → 37.6. The GPQA gain matters for a subtle reason: the model was trained with rubric rewards on free-form science prompts, yet the skill transfers to a multiple-choice test it never saw in that format — rubric supervision isn't just teaching the model to game rubrics.

Three findings beyond the headline are worth carrying:

  • Instance-specific rubrics are the whole point. RaR-Predefined — a fixed generic checklist ("response is concise", "response is correct") applied to every prompt — collapses to 12.5 on HealthBench, far below even the untuned instruct model. Generic criteria produce a misaligned reward; the value is in per-prompt synthesis.
  • Rubrics rescue small judges. In a separate alignment study (Figure 3), giving the judge the rubric lifts pairwise preference accuracy at every judge size, with the largest gains for the smallest judges — a 7B judge with a rubric closes much of the gap to gpt-4o. Rubric rewards are also lower-variance across judge scales than plain Likert scoring.
  • Expert grounding ≈ human rubrics. In-domain on HealthBench-1k, synthetic rubrics written with reference answers (35.9) essentially match human-authored rubrics (34.8) and crush synthetic rubrics written without references (32.0). The reference answer is doing real work; pure-synthetic rubrics fall short.
// does it hold up?

The eval read

RaR is a clean, well-motivated idea and the paper is admirably honest about its own ablations — which is exactly where the seams show. The first thing to notice is that the impressive-sounding "up to 31%" is a relative figure over the weakest reasonable baseline (Direct-Likert). The more honest comparison is against Reference-Likert — the baseline that also gets to see the gold answer — and there the gap shrinks to "small but consistent": 28.9 → 31.2 on HealthBench, 36.5 → 36.9/37.6 on GPQA. On GPQA the RaR variants sit inside or beside Reference-Likert's 95% CIs. Since the rubrics are themselves generated from the reference answers, much of what RaR buys you is "have a good reference answer," with the checklist decomposition adding a modest increment on top.

The sharpest self-inflicted wound is the rubric-design ablation. The method's whole visual identity — categorical weights, Essential/Pitfall labels — barely earns its keep: dropping the categorical labels entirely scored 38.8, higher than the full rubric's 37.2, and removing pitfall criteria changed nothing. The authors say so plainly. So the working mechanism isn't the weighting machinery in Figure 1; it's "decompose the reference into a checklist and let a judge holistically score it" (RaR-Implicit, which discards the explicit weights anyway). That's a simpler — and more defensible — claim than the framing suggests.

There is also a contamination smell around the ablations. The main Figure 2 result is reasonably clean (train on RaR-Medicine's 20k diverse prompts, evaluate on HealthBench). But Tables 1–3 train on a HealthBench-3.5k subset and evaluate on HealthBench-1k — same source distribution, with reward rubrics derived from HealthBench's own reference answers and graded against HealthBench's own rubric axes. The training judge (gpt-4o-mini) differs from HealthBench's GPT-4 grader, so it isn't fully circular, but the reward signal and the eval signal are drawn from the same well, which inflates the in-domain numbers the ablations rely on.

baselines

Fair set — Direct-Likert, Reference-Likert, a generic-rubric control, plus off-the-shelf base and instruct. But the headline number is quoted against the weakest one; against the reference-aware baseline the margin is slim and, on GPQA, within the CIs.

contamination

Main result is clean; the ablation tables train and test on the same HealthBench pool with rubrics sourced from its reference answers. In-domain gains should be read with that overlap in mind.

what's missed

One 7B base policy, one cheap judge, two carefully-chosen domains. No reward-hacking stress test over longer training — the exact failure mode later rubric-RL papers (PaW3R, Reward Hacking in Rubric-Based RL) go after. The weights/pitfalls the method advertises don't measurably help.

would raise confidence

Scale the base model and vary the judge; report against Reference-Likert as the primary baseline with significance tests; train long enough to see whether the judge gets gamed; show transfer to a domain whose references weren't used to write the rubrics.

// bottom line

A genuinely useful recipe — rubrics are a practical bridge from RLVR into unverifiable domains, and the small-judge result is the most reusable finding. Just read the win as "a good reference answer, decomposed into a checklist and scored holistically," not as evidence that the weighted-pitfall rubric machinery is what's doing the lifting.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. Rubrics as a reward, not just a ruler. RaR closes the loop by feeding LLM-written, per-prompt checklists into GRPO — extending RLVR into medicine and science where no single answer can be matched. RLVR is recovered as the one-criterion special case.
  2. Implicit > Explicit, and instance-specific is non-negotiable. Handing the whole rubric to the judge for one holistic score beats hand-weighted sums; a generic fixed rubric is actively harmful (12.5 vs 31.2 on HealthBench). The categorical weights and pitfall criteria the method advertises barely move the needle.
  3. The reference answer is the real ingredient. Synthetic rubrics grounded in a reference answer match human-written ones; without that grounding they fall off. The cleanest standalone win is that rubric structure lets small, cheap judges align like much larger ones.
