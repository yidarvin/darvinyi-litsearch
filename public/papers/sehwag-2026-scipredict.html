SciPredict: Can LLMs Predict the Outcomes of Scientific Experiments?

A 405-task benchmark drawn from physics, chemistry, and biology papers posted after the models' training cutoff. Frontier LLMs score just 14–26% — barely matching ~20% human experts — and, unlike experts, have no idea which of their own predictions to trust.

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

The single most expensive decision in empirical science is which experiment to run next. Beam time on a particle accelerator, a months-long protein-crystallization screen, a materials-synthesis campaign — each commits real money and real time before any data comes back. A system that could reliably predict what an experiment would show, before it's run, would let researchers prune dead-ends and concentrate resources on the directions most likely to pay off. This is exactly the niche where an LLM that has read most of the scientific literature ought to shine.

But the existing "AI for science" benchmarks measure almost everything except this. They test literature review and paper drafting, reproducing the code and results of a known paper, or generating hypotheses — tasks about summarizing or re-deriving what is already published. None of them ask the forward-looking question: given a fully specified experimental setup that has never been run by this model before, what will the measurement be?

That gap is hard to close for one structural reason: data leakage. Any experiment old enough to appear in a benchmark is old enough to be in the training data, so a "prediction" collapses into recall. SciPredict's central design move is to side-step this entirely — every task is extracted from a paper first posted after March 31, 2025, postdating the cutoff of every frontier model evaluated, with web search disabled so models can't look the answer up. What's left is genuine predictive reasoning from first principles and provided context. The benchmark then asks two questions in sequence: (a) can models predict experimental outcomes accurately, and (b) even where they can, do they know when their prediction is trustworthy enough to act on?

// how it works

The method

SciPredict is 405 expert-curated prediction tasks spanning 33 specialized sub-fields — 9 in physics, 10 in chemistry, 14 in biology (task mix: ~50% biology, 25% physics, 25% chemistry). Each task hands the model the skeleton of a real experiment — the system under study, the conditions imposed, the measurements taken, any interventions — and asks it to predict the outcome. To keep the task hard but graded fairly, questions come in three formats: multiple-choice (MCQ, ~40%), free-form (~32%), and numerical value (~28%).

Grading is format-specific. MCQ is exact match against the expert key. Numerical answers count as correct if they fall inside an expert-set acceptable range [Li, Ui] that absorbs measurement noise. Free-form answers are scored by an LLM judge against a 1–10 expert-written rubric — checking whether the reasoning is scientifically correct rather than whether it matches the reference word-for-word:

AccFF = 1|DFF| Σi si,   si = Jθ(ŷi, yi) ∈ {0,1}   |    AccNUM = 1|DNUM| Σi 1[Li ≤ ŷi ≤ Ui] // free-form judged by rubric (Gemini-3-Pro, cross-checked with GPT-5.2); numerical scored against an expert tolerance band

The benchmark's second axis is the part that makes it more than an accuracy leaderboard. Alongside every prediction, each model (and each human) must report three reliability self-assessments on a 1–5 scale: confidence (is my answer correct?), difficulty (how hard was this?), and feasibility (could this outcome be predicted by reasoning at all, without actually running the experiment?). If a system is well-calibrated, accuracy should climb with confidence and feasibility and fall with difficulty. To probe what knowledge actually helps, SciPredict also varies the context across five conditions — no background knowledge (NBK), expert-curated background knowledge (BK), model self-generated background (SBK), the two combined (SABK), and a filtered version (FBK) that strips out any background fact the model can already answer on its own.

The human baseline is deliberately strong: a separately recruited pool of domain experts — 75% with doctoral degrees — answers questions within their own active research subdomain, under the same NBK/BK conditions and giving the same confidence/difficulty/feasibility ratings as the models. The authors are explicit that this baseline is a calibration reference point, not an upper bound: the whole premise is that a model integrating cross-domain knowledge could, in principle, beat any single human.

// what they found

The results

Across 15 state-of-the-art models — OpenAI o3 / o4-mini / GPT-5.2, Anthropic Claude Sonnet 4.5 / Opus 4.1 / Opus 4.5, Google Gemini 2.5 Pro / 3 Flash / 3 Pro, Meta Llama, Alibaba Qwen, DeepSeek v3 — accuracy lands between 14% and 26%, essentially on top of the ~20% human-expert baseline. Gemini 3 Pro leads at ~25.3%, with Claude Opus 4.5 (~23.0%) and GPT-5.2 (~20.6%) behind it. Yes, the best models nominally exceed the human panel — but at this level "superhuman" means clearing a ~20% bar by a couple of points, nowhere near the reliability an experimentalist would need to reallocate a budget.

Context helps, but only when an expert wrote it. Supplying expert-curated background knowledge lifts accuracy for every model — by ~3% on average (1.2–5.8% depending on the model). Strikingly, restating facts the model already knows still helps: in the filtered-background condition, most models sit above the y=x line, so foregrounding known priors in context beats relying on parametric memory alone. But models cannot supply this context themselves — self-generated background (SBK) usually lowers accuracy below the no-background baseline, and bolting it onto expert background (SABK) rarely improves on expert background alone. Models don't know which scientific facts matter, and confidently add ones that mislead.

Format moves the number more than the model does. MCQ is consistently the easiest format, free-form lower, numerical lowest. When the authors rewrite a multiple-choice item as a matched open-ended prompt (MCQ→FF) — same experiment, options removed — accuracy drops across essentially every model. That gap is the tell that headline MCQ scores reflect recognition, not the open-ended generation a real research workflow demands.

But the headline result isn't the accuracy — it's the calibration gap. Human experts are sharply calibrated: their accuracy climbs from ~5% on questions they judge infeasible to predict (feasibility = 1) to ~80% on questions they judge feasible (feasibility = 5). They know which outcomes follow from established principles and which genuinely require running the experiment. Models show none of this structure: their accuracy is a flat ~20% regardless of whether they self-report confidence 1 or 5, feasibility 1 or 5. A model claiming feasibility 5 is no more likely to be right than one claiming feasibility 1.

An LLM-judge error analysis confirms the failure mode is substantive, not superficial. Failures concentrate in logical & reasoning flaws (avg 87.4%) and factual & extraction errors (avg 80.1%) — chiefly information fabrication (avg 54.0%) and factual contradiction (avg 52.3%) — while basic comprehension errors are rare (avg 10.0%). Within "scientific rigor," the dominant sub-error is false certainty (avg 43.6%): high confidence (4–5) attached to wrong answers, which is exactly the mechanism behind the broken calibration. Chemistry is the hardest domain — for the human baseline, accuracy falls to 8.82% in chemistry versus 23.15% (biology) and 26.00% (physics).

// does it hold up?

The eval read

SciPredict gets the hardest part right: leakage control is genuinely strong. The post-March-2025 cutoff plus disabled web search is the correct, almost necessary design for a prediction benchmark, and it's enforced at curation time rather than assumed. The model roster is current to the month (Gemini 3 Pro, Claude Opus 4.5, GPT-5.2), the human baseline is a real subdomain-matched expert panel rather than crowdworkers, and the calibration framing is a meaningful contribution that most "AI for science" leaderboards never attempt. The signature finding — models can't tell their good predictions from their bad ones — is a relative, structural claim that survives even if the absolute accuracy numbers are noisy.

The accuracy story, though, is shakier than the headline "models exceed human experts" suggests — and the paper's own figures show why. With 3–4 options, random guessing on MCQ is ~25–33%, yet Figure 9's MCQ accuracies span only ~20–36%: all but the top two or three models are sitting at or below chance on the format the paper calls easiest. That reframes "14–26% overall" as near a floor, where a 2–5 point lead over a ~20% human baseline (reported over 3 trials with ±1σ error bars) is plausibly within noise. "Superhuman" is technically true and practically meaningless here. The leakage guard is also narrower than it looks: a post-cutoff paper can still describe an outcome that follows from textbook principles the model already knows — and the BK/FBK results confirm models already hold >70% of the "required" background — so the cutoff blocks memorizing the specific result, not the underlying science.

Two grading choices deserve more scrutiny than they get. Free-form scoring uses Gemini-3-Pro as the rubric judge — one of the graded models — a self-preference risk only partly defused by the GPT-5.2 cross-check (also a graded model). And numerical correctness hinges entirely on the expert-set tolerance band [L, U]: band width is a curator judgment call that directly sets the accuracy number, and there's no sensitivity analysis on it. Finally, 405 tasks across 33 subfields is ~12 tasks per subdomain — thin for the per-domain claims (e.g. the chemistry-is-hardest result rests on a small slice), and no inter-annotator agreement or rubric-reliability figure is reported for the free-form judgments.

baselines

Current and fair on the model side (frontier + open, to the month); the human panel is a strong subdomain-matched expert reference, explicitly framed as a calibration point, not a ceiling. But the ~20% human floor makes "beats humans" a weak claim.

contamination

Best-in-class: post-March-2025 papers + web search disabled, enforced at curation. Caveat — it blocks memorizing the specific result, not the textbook science the outcome may follow from.

what's missed

MCQ accuracy (~20–36%) is near random chance (~25–33%) for most models; headline MCQ numbers overstate skill, and the 2–5 pt "win" over humans is plausibly within the reported ±1σ noise.

would raise confidence

A judge outside the graded set (or human re-grading) for free-form; sensitivity analysis on numerical tolerance bands; explicit MCQ chance baselines; inter-annotator / rubric-reliability stats; more tasks per subdomain.

// bottom line

Read it as a calibration paper, not an accuracy leaderboard. The accuracy numbers sit near a noisy floor where "models beat experts" barely means anything, but the structural result is robust and important: today's frontier models predict experimental outcomes at chance-adjacent rates and have no internal signal for when they're right. For the stated use case — deciding which experiments to fund — that second failure is the disqualifying one.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. On 405 outcome-prediction tasks from post-cutoff physics, chemistry, and biology papers, frontier models score 14–26% — barely above the ~20% human-expert baseline, and (per the paper's own Figure 9) near random chance on even the easiest MCQ format.
  2. The real gap is calibration, not accuracy: experts' accuracy climbs from ~5% to ~80% as they judge a question more predictable, while models stay flat at ~20% regardless of their own confidence — so a correct model prediction is indistinguishable from a wrong one, which is fatal for experiment selection.
  3. Restating background a model already knows still helps (~+3%), but models can't generate useful context themselves (self-generated background usually hurts), and answer format swings accuracy more than model identity — so MCQ leaderboards overstate real predictive skill.
