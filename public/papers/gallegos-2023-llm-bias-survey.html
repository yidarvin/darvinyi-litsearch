Bias and Fairness in Large Language Models: A Survey

The LLM-bias literature had exploded into hundreds of one-off metrics, datasets, and debiasing tricks with no shared vocabulary. This survey imposes one: three taxonomies, each organized not by what kind of bias a technique targets, but by what a practitioner can actually touch — embeddings, token probabilities, generated text, or nothing but a black-box API.

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

Algorithmic fairness has a mature, decade-old theory: given a classifier and a protected attribute, criteria like equalized odds (Hardt, Price, and Srebro 2016) or individual fairness (Dwork et al. 2012) tell you precisely what "unbiased" means, and Kleinberg, Mullainathan, and Raghavan's impossibility result tells you which of those criteria you can't all satisfy at once. None of that theory assumes the model's output is free text. An LLM doesn't emit a label with a true/false-positive rate — it emits a paragraph, and "biased" can mean the paragraph is toxic, that it stereotypes a group, that it responds differently to "he" versus "she," or that it just answers less accurately for one dialect than another. The classical machinery has no native way to talk about any of that.

So a parallel literature grew up around word embeddings and then LLMs, starting from Caliskan, Bryson, and Narayanan's WEAT — itself an NLP transplant of the psychologist's Implicit Association Test — and fanning out into dozens of embedding tests, masked-token probability tests, generated-text classifiers, and lexicon scans, each introduced in isolation with its own notation, its own toy benchmark, and often no stated relationship to the others. By the authors' own account, the closest existing survey, Chang et al. (2023)'s Survey on Evaluation of Large Language Models, covers LLM evaluation broadly but "does not focus on the evaluation of fairness and bias in such models." Mehrabi et al. (2019)'s earlier Survey on Bias and Fairness in Machine Learning covers the classical, mostly-classification setting this paper explicitly builds past. Nothing yet unified the LLM-specific metrics, the datasets they run on, and the mitigation techniques meant to fix what they find — and reviewers, replicators, and practitioners were left comparing incommensurable numbers across incompatible setups.

The paper also pins down what is being measured, not just how: it catalogs nine distinct representational and allocational harms (Table 1) — derogatory language, disparate system performance, erasure, exclusionary norms, misrepresentation, stereotyping, and toxicity on the representational side; direct and indirect discrimination on the allocational side — because "bias" as a blanket term was doing too much work across papers that meant very different things by it.

// how it works

The method: three taxonomies, one organizing principle

Rather than group techniques by which social bias they target (gender, race, religion, …) — the field's default, and the reason it fragmented — the survey groups them by what level of model access a practitioner has, since that determines which techniques are even usable. The result is three parallel taxonomies covering evaluation metrics, evaluation datasets, and mitigation techniques.

Taxonomy 1 — metrics, by data structure

For an arbitrary evaluation dataset D, only a subset of the space of all metrics Ψ is actually applicable — the compatible subset ψ(D) ⊆ Ψ depends on the dataset's structure and on what the metric needs from the model. Figure 2 makes this decomposition explicit: a dataset is chosen for a task, and only then does the space of possible metrics narrow.

On that basis, Table 3 of the paper sorts every metric into three families: embedding-based (word-embedding WEAT and its variant WEAT*, plus sentence-embedding SEAT, CEAT, and the Sentence Bias Score — all requiring access to the model's internal vector representations), probability-based (masked-token metrics like DisCo, the Log-Probability Bias Score, and the Categorical Bias Score, plus pseudo-log-likelihood metrics like the CrowS-Pairs Score, Context Association Test, All Unmasked Likelihood, and Language Model Bias — all requiring access to token probabilities but not necessarily embeddings), and generated-text-based (distribution metrics, classifier metrics, and lexicon metrics — the only family usable against a pure black-box API that returns text and nothing else). WEAT's own effect size is the family's founding equation:

WEAT(A₁,A₂,W₁,W₂) = meana₁∈A₁ s(a₁,W₁,W₂) − meana₂∈A₂ s(a₂,W₁,W₂)stda∈A₁∪A₂ s(a,W₁,W₂) // s(·) is the mean cosine-similarity gap between an attribute word and two target-word sets — the effect size a larger LLM-era literature of SEAT/CEAT variants generalizes to sentence embeddings

Taxonomy 2 — datasets, by structure

Datasets split the same way the metrics that consume them do: counterfactual inputs (masked tokens, where the model fills a blank, or unmasked sentences, where the model scores a whole sentence) versus prompts (sentence completions or question-answering, where a generative model produces free text). Table 4 catalogs 23 such datasets across those four sub-buckets — from 118-example Grep-BiasIR up to the 5,712,066-pair Bias-NLI — annotated by which of the nine Table-1 harms and which social groups each one actually targets.

Taxonomy 3 — mitigation, by pipeline stage

Mitigation techniques are organized by where in the LLM's lifecycle they intervene, since that determines what access they require: pre-processing (5 subtypes — data augmentation, filtering and reweighting, generation, instruction tuning, and projection-based mitigation) changes inputs before training; in-training (4 subtypes — architecture modification, loss function modification, selective parameter updating, and filtering model parameters) needs gradient access; intra-processing (3 subtypes — decoding strategy modification, weight redistribution, and modular debiasing networks) needs the trained weights but no further training; and post-processing (1 subtype — rewriting) needs nothing but the model's output text, making it the only option against a closed API.

// what they found

What the taxonomies actually contain

Because this is a survey, its "results" are the shape of the literature it consolidates, made legible via a shared notation (Table 2) and 12 formal definitions — from group-fairness borrowings like fairness through unawareness to five new desiderata the authors introduce for open-ended generation, such as invariance (model output shouldn't change under a social-group substitution) and replicated distributions (a generated corpus's demographic term frequencies should match a reference distribution). Figure 1 shows invariance concretely: substituting "male" for "female" in a question-answering prompt changes the model's advice (a violation), while substituting a pronoun in a completion prompt does not (compliance).

// does it hold up?

The eval read

As a survey rather than an empirical paper, the right question isn't "are the numbers right" but "does the map match the territory, and does it connect to what came before." On the second question, this paper does real work: it doesn't treat LLM bias as a green field. Section 2 explicitly imports group-fairness definitions from Hardt, Price, and Srebro (2016), Chouldechova (2017), and Kamiran and Calders (2012), and individual fairness from Dwork et al. (2012), before explaining exactly where that machinery breaks: "for cases when simple classification may not be useful, we present general desiderata of fairness for NLP tasks that generalize notions in the LLM bias evaluation and mitigation literature." That's the correct move — inherit the theory where it still applies (an LLM used as a classifier can still be judged by equalized odds) and extend it where it can't (free-text generation has no natural positive/negative class). Caliskan et al.'s WEAT is the load-bearing bridge node in exactly this sense: the survey traces the entire embedding-metric family (SEAT, CEAT, Sentence Bias Score) back to it.

Where it's weaker is in what its own §6 admits about the literature it just organized. The taxonomy's neatness can read as if the field has convergent, comparable measurements once you've filed a metric into the right bucket — but the authors themselves report that "several metrics are inconsistent with one another," that mitigation papers "often claim to successfully debias a model if any metric demonstrates a decrease in bias" (a cherry-picking risk the tidy taxonomy doesn't prevent), and that "most works leverage PerspectiveAPI for detecting toxicity despite [its] known flaws." Kleinberg, Mullainathan, and Raghavan's impossibility result — that calibration and both balance conditions can't all hold except in degenerate cases — is exactly the kind of result that should make a reader suspicious of any single bias score treated as ground truth, yet it isn't in this paper's reference list at all, and its spirit (that fairness criteria can trade off against each other, not just fail to be satisfied) gets little airtime next to the invariance-style desiderata the paper foregrounds.

There's also a coverage-cutoff problem inherent to any survey of a fast-moving field: the catalog is anchored to metrics and datasets built almost entirely around base and single-turn completion models (StereoSet, CrowS-Pairs, BOLD, BBQ — all pre-ChatGPT or contemporaneous with it), not the RLHF-tuned, multi-turn chat assistants that now dominate deployment, where bias can hide behind refusals, sycophancy, or persona-conditioned responses that these mostly-template-based benchmarks weren't built to probe.

baselines

Not applicable in the empirical sense, but the survey's implicit baseline — comparing itself against Chang et al. (2023)'s broader LLM-eval survey and Mehrabi et al. (2019)'s classical ML-fairness survey — is fair and explicitly scoped: it correctly stakes out the bias/fairness-specific, LLM-specific gap between the two.

contamination

Not a leakage risk in the usual sense, but a temporal one: the catalog is frozen at datasets and metrics that predate widespread RLHF chat-model auditing, so its coverage skews toward base/single-turn-completion evaluation.

what's missed

The paper's own admission that group-fairness metrics dominate "with little emphasis towards individual or subgroup fairness" undercuts how much practical traction Dwork's and Kleinberg's classical results actually have in the literature it surveys, despite being cited as foundations.

would raise confidence

A living, versioned companion (the kind of periodically-updated benchmark table this graph itself tries to be) plus a meta-analysis of which cataloged metrics actually correlate with each other or with human-judged harm, rather than a static taxonomy of what's been proposed.

// bottom line

A genuinely useful unification — it correctly threads LLM-era bias metrics back through WEAT to the Implicit Association Test and forward through generalized fairness desiderata, giving a previously balkanized literature one shared vocabulary. But by its own account, the underlying metrics it organizes still disagree with each other more than the clean three-taxonomy structure suggests, and the classical fairness results it name-checks (Hardt's oblivious-audit limits, Kleinberg's impossibility) are cited as ancestry more than applied as ongoing discipline on the LLM-era work it catalogs.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. Three taxonomies — metrics, datasets, mitigation — all organized by the same principle: what level of model access a practitioner actually has (embeddings, token probabilities, generated text only; or gradients, weights, outputs only). That's why the structure generalizes past any one bias axis.
  2. It explicitly rebuilds on classical group and individual fairness (Hardt, Dwork, Kleinberg) rather than treating LLM bias as an unrelated new field — WEAT is the traced lineage from the Implicit Association Test through SEAT/CEAT to today's embedding metrics.
  3. Its own §6 is as much a warning as a summary: most cataloged work leans on a narrow set of contested tools (PerspectiveAPI toxicity chief among them) and group-level metrics, so filing a technique into the right taxonomy box doesn't mean its numbers agree with a technique in a different box.
