Fairness Beyond Disparate Treatment & Disparate Impact: Learning Classification without Disparate Mistreatment

Existing fairness law recognizes two failure modes — disparate treatment and disparate impact — but neither one asks whether a classifier's mistakes fall unevenly across groups. This paper names that gap disparate mistreatment and shows how to train against it directly, as a convex-concave constraint on the decision boundary itself.

Muhammad Bilal Zafar, Isabel Valera, Manuel Gomez Rodriguez, Krishna P. Gummadi

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

By 2016, anti-discrimination law and the fair-ML literature recognized two notions of unfairness. Disparate treatment holds when a decision changes with the sensitive attribute itself — P(ŷ|x,z) ≠ P(ŷ|x) — and disparate impact holds when outcomes disproportionately favor one group over another — P(ŷ=1|z=0) ≠ P(ŷ=1|z=1) — regardless of whether those outcomes are actually correct. That independence from ground truth is disparate impact's whole appeal in settings like hiring or lending, where historical decisions can't be trusted as labels. But it becomes a liability once trustworthy ground truth exists: forcing proportional outcomes when the true qualification rate genuinely differs by group risks penalizing accuracy for its own sake — a form of reverse discrimination courts have already ruled against (Ricci v. DeStefano, 2009).

The paper's target is the failure mode neither existing notion catches: a classifier can treat identical feature vectors identically (no disparate treatment) and produce proportional outcomes (no disparate impact), yet still be wrong about one group far more often than another, or wrong in a different direction. Figure 1 makes the three notions' independence concrete with three toy stop-and-frisk classifiers deciding whether to search a pedestrian for a weapon. C3 stops Male 2 and Female 2 differently despite identical clothing-bulge and crime-proximity features (disparate treatment), yet has identical false positive and false negative rates for men and women (no disparate mistreatment). C1 does the reverse: it treats identical feature vectors identically, but its false negative rate is 0.0 for men versus 0.5 for women — a case neither disparate treatment nor disparate impact would flag as unfair. The paper names this third failure disparate mistreatment: unequal misclassification rates across groups — exactly the pattern Goel et al. had just documented in NYPD's stop-and-frisk data (higher false-positive weapon-suspicion stops for Black pedestrians) and what ProPublica's concurrent COMPAS investigation was alleging for recidivism-risk scores.

// how it works

The method

Section 3 formalizes all three notions side by side, and disparate mistreatment isn't one equation but a choice of which misclassification rate must be equalized across the sensitive groups z=0 and z=1: the overall misclassification rate (OMR), false positive rate (FPR), false negative rate (FNR), false discovery rate (FDR), or false omission rate (FOR). The method built in Section 4 targets OMR, FPR and FNR — the three that condition on the ground-truth label y and turn out to be tractable. FDR and FOR condition on the predicted label ŷ instead, which the paper flags as significantly harder to optimize and leaves as future work.

Directly constraining, say, P(ŷ≠y|z=0) = P(ŷ≠y|z=1) inside a training objective is intractable, because the constraint is non-convex in the classifier parameters θ. The paper's fix is a proxy: define gθ(y,x) = min(0, y·dθ(x)), where dθ(x) is the signed distance from x to the decision boundary, then measure disparate mistreatment as the covariance between the sensitive attribute z and this quantity.

Cov(z, gθ(y,x)) ≈ 1N  Σ(x,y,z)∈D (z − z̄) gθ(y,x) // g_θ(y,x) = min(0, y·d_θ(x)) targets overall misclassification rate; swapping in min(0, (1−y)/2·y·d_θ(x)) or min(0, (1+y)/2·y·d_θ(x)) targets FPR or FNR specifically

Because gθ is convex in θ and the zero-mean term E[(z−z̄)] cancels out, this covariance sits close to zero exactly when the corresponding rate-equality constraint approximately holds. Splitting the sum over the two z-groups turns the constraint |Cov| ≤ c into a difference of two convex functions — a Disciplined Convex-Concave Program (DCCP) — which the paper solves with the heuristic of Shen, Diamond, Gu & Boyd (2016) rather than requiring a fully convex solver. Because the sensitive feature z never has to enter the feature vector x used at decision time, the same training run can also guarantee no disparate treatment simply by keeping x and z disjoint — the paper's stated point of departure from Hardt, Price & Srebro's concurrent post-processing method (also late 2016), which must use z at prediction time and therefore cannot avoid disparate treatment by construction.

// what they found

The results

On synthetic data engineered so the two sensitive groups have deliberately different false-positive rates, an unconstrained logistic regression classifier reaches 85% accuracy with FPR 0.25 (z=0) vs. 0.06 (z=1) — DFPR=0.19. Tightening the covariance threshold toward zero (Figure 2) brings the group FPRs together while accuracy falls to about 0.80 — roughly a 5-point cost purely from equalizing false positives. A second synthetic scenario (Figure 3, Case I) shows a more useful dynamic: when DFPR and DFNR have opposite signs, constraining only FPR also rotates the boundary enough to bring FNR into near-balance for free, whereas when the two gaps share a sign (Case II, not pictured), fixing one measure can make the other worse, and closing both simultaneously costs far more accuracy (0.80 → 0.69).

On ProPublica's Broward County COMPAS data — 5,278 offenders restricted to the Black/white subset, with 2-year-recidivism ground truth and the same five features ProPublica used (age category, gender, race, priors count, charge degree) — recidivism prevalence is 52% for Black defendants versus 39% for white defendants (Table 3). An unconstrained logistic regression classifier reaches 66.8% accuracy but shows the same opposite-direction pattern as synthetic Case I: false positive rates of 0.35 (Black) vs. 0.17 (white) — DFPR=0.18 — and false negative rates of 0.31 vs. 0.61 — DFNR=−0.30 — disadvantaging Black defendants on false positives and white defendants on false negatives at the same time.

Every constrained variant costs under one accuracy point relative to the unconstrained baseline while cutting the FPR gap by two-thirds to five-sixths and the FNR gap by over half — but never to exactly zero, which the authors attribute to the training set's modest size (2,639 rows) limiting how reliably the Monte Carlo covariance estimate can be computed. Against the two comparison methods, results are close but instructive: a naive reweighting baseline (iteratively up-weighting the worse-off group's misclassified points) lands in a similar range on COMPAS (0.643–0.660 accuracy), but on synthetic Case II it collapses to 38% accuracy — worse than a coin flip — when forced to satisfy both constraints at once, versus 69% for the paper's own method and 67% for Hardt et al. under the identical requirement. Once the paper's own method is also allowed to use race as a feature (dropping the disjoint-features constraint, the variant actually reported for COMPAS), it ties or slightly edges out Hardt et al. at every fairness level there (0.660–0.662 vs. 0.645–0.659) — the real accuracy cost of the paper's headline guarantee shows up only when it insists on keeping race out of the feature vector, as in the synthetic setting-1 comparison, where that disjoint-features variant trails both Hardt et al. and its own race-using variant by about 5 points (0.80 vs. 0.85) for exactly that reason.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

The proxy at the center of the method is a genuine trade: turning a non-convex rate-equality constraint into a covariance makes training tractable, but a near-zero covariance is neither necessary nor sufficient for exactly equal misclassification rates — it's an approximation the paper itself never fully closes even on its own showcase dataset (DFPR bottoms out at 0.03 on COMPAS, not 0). The paper is candid about this and about the DCCP solver's lack of global-optimality guarantees, but the evaluation doesn't probe how loose the proxy gets as class imbalance or dataset size varies — COMPAS, the only real dataset used, is the sole data point for that question.

More consequential is what the evaluation never checks: calibration. The paper cites Kleinberg, Mullainathan & Raghavan and Chouldechova — both published within about six weeks of this one — for the general result that equalized false-positive/false-negative rates and calibration cannot both hold once base rates differ by group, exactly the situation here (52% vs. 39% recidivism prevalence). That means every one of this paper's own "fair" COMPAS classifiers has, by construction, given up calibration — a cost the paper never reports, even though it cites the very results proving it must be paying it.

There's also a scope gap between the headline claim and what's actually demonstrated. The paper's distinguishing promise over Hardt et al. is removing disparate treatment and disparate mistreatment simultaneously — but on COMPAS, footnote 7 quietly concedes that "since race is one of the features in the learnable set, we additionally assume that all the methods have access to the sensitive attributes," and Table 2's COMPAS block reports only the disparate-treatment-permitting variant of the method, not the disjoint-features version the rest of the paper is built around. The dual guarantee is validated only on synthetic data; the one real dataset tests exactly the single-guarantee case Hardt et al. already covers.

baselines

Compared against a self-constructed reweighting heuristic (which sometimes fails to converge, or collapses to 38% accuracy under both constraints in synthetic Case II) and the concurrent Hardt et al. post-processing method — a fair comparison in that it's honest about Hardt et al.'s structural advantage (access to z at decision time), but it omits then-available alternatives cited only in related work, such as Kamishima et al.'s prejudice-remover regularizer or Zemel et al.'s learned fair representations.

contamination

No train/test leakage in the usual sense, but the analogous risk is label bias: COMPAS's 2-year re-arrest outcome and the NYPD SQF "has weapon" ground truth are both taken as trustworthy targets, and the paper never audits whether the labels themselves already encode the disparities the method is meant to remove.

what's missed

Calibration is never reported, despite the paper's own citations proving that equalizing FPR/FNR under COMPAS's differing base rates necessarily breaks it; FDR/FOR-based mistreatment is explicitly deferred; and the "no disparate treatment + no disparate mistreatment together" headline is demonstrated only on synthetic data, not on COMPAS.

would raise confidence

Reporting calibration (or PPV) alongside DFPR/DFNR on COMPAS so the impossibility trade-off is explicit; running the disjoint-features variant of the method on a real dataset, not just synthetically; validating on a second real dataset beyond one 2,639-row training split; and reporting variance, not just the 5-split average, given how sensitive the covariance estimate is said to be to sample size.

// bottom line

A well-motivated, genuinely novel fairness notion — the first to formalize "proportional mistakes" as distinct from "proportional outcomes" — paired with an elegant convex-concave training trick that plugs into any margin-based classifier. But the empirical case rests on one real, modest-sized dataset, never checks the calibration cost its own cited impossibility results guarantee it's paying, and validates its most distinctive claim — avoiding disparate treatment and disparate mistreatment at once — only in simulation. Read alongside Hardt et al., Kleinberg et al., and Chouldechova (all published within weeks of each other in late 2016), it's less an escape from the fairness-notions-conflict these concurrent papers were simultaneously proving than a new, trainable point inside it.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. A third fairness failure mode, named and formalized. Disparate treatment (decisions vary with z) and disparate impact (outcomes vary with z) don't capture unequal misclassification rates across groups. Table 1's five measures — OMR, FPR, FNR, FDR, FOR — give disparate mistreatment a precise, ground-truth-dependent definition for the first time.
  2. A covariance proxy makes it trainable. Cov(z, gθ(y,x)) with gθ(y,x)=min(0, y·dθ(x)) turns a non-convex rate-equality constraint into a Disciplined Convex-Concave Program, so any margin-based classifier can be trained fair-by-construction — and because z never enters the feature vector, the same training run also avoids disparate treatment, something the concurrent Hardt et al. post-processing method structurally cannot do.
  3. Near-free on COMPAS, but incomplete as evidence. The false-positive-rate gap between Black and white defendants (0.35 vs. 0.17 unconstrained) falls to 0.03–0.06 for under one point of accuracy loss — yet the demonstration uses race as a raw feature, skips a calibration check the paper's own cited impossibility results (Kleinberg et al., Chouldechova) say it must be failing, and never tests the "no treatment + no mistreatment together" claim outside simulation.
