LegalBench: A Collaboratively Built Benchmark for Measuring Legal Reasoning in Large Language Models

162 tasks, hand-built by 40 legal professionals and organized around the same IRAC framework lawyers are trained on — turning "can LLMs do law?" into a question you can actually measure, one type of reasoning at a time.

Guha, Nyarko, Ho, Ré, Chilton, … Henderson, Goel — 40 co-authors across 20 institutions

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

By mid-2023 lawyers and administrators were openly debating whether LLMs could draft briefs, review contracts, and help close the United States' access-to-justice gap. But the field had no good way to answer the question underneath all the excitement: what kinds of legal reasoning can these models actually perform? The risk is asymmetric — a hallucinated rule or a botched contract clause does real harm, often to the people least able to absorb it — so guessing was not an option.

The legal-NLP benchmarks that existed were a poor fit. Most were built for the supervised / fine-tuning era — BERT-style models trained on large annotated corpora — and they centered on the things that make legal text hard for that paradigm: document length, jargon, span extraction. They measured whether a specialized model could be trained on law, not whether a general model could reason about it in-context. And because legal annotation is expensive, the field had only a handful of datasets, each a "natural" source of labels or a one-off megaproject like CUAD.

The deeper problem is a vocabulary gap. AI researchers and lawyers had no shared language for talking about which reasoning a task requires. "GPT-4 scores 78% on legal tasks" is meaningless to a practitioner: 78% of what — spotting issues? recalling the rule? applying it to facts? Without a typology grounded in how lawyers actually describe their own work, model scores couldn't translate into anything a legal professional could act on.

// the bet

Don't scrape a benchmark — convene one. Put legal professionals in the lead, have them hand-craft tasks that measure reasoning they find practically useful or genuinely interesting, and organize every task by the IRAC framework (Issue, Rule, Application, Conclusion) that lawyers already use. The result is a benchmark whose scores mean the same thing to an ML researcher and a litigator.

// how it works

The method

LegalBench is 162 tasks drawn from 36 distinct corpora, assembled through an interdisciplinary collaboration in which the subject-matter experts were co-authors, not contractors. Tasks come from three sources: existing legal datasets restructured for the few-shot prompting paradigm (e.g., the long-document extraction corpus CUAD is recast as a set of binary clause-classification tasks); datasets hand-coded by legal scholars for prior empirical projects but never released; and brand-new tasks built specifically for LegalBench by the lawyers on the author list.

Because the few-shot regime needs only a handful of labeled demonstrations rather than a full training set, the effort that would have gone into building large training corpora could instead be spent on diversity of tasks — which is exactly what a benchmark of legal reasoning needs.

The typology: IRAC, plus the skills IRAC leaves out

The organizing spine is IRAC, the framework taught in every first-year law class: a lawyer spots the issue, recalls the relevant rule, applies the rule to the facts, and reaches a conclusion. LegalBench operationalizes each step as a task type, then adds two skills that don't fit neatly inside IRAC — interpreting legal text and understanding legal argument. Crucially, each task evaluates a single reasoning type in isolation; the benchmark doesn't (yet) ask a model to run the whole IRAC chain end-to-end.

Reasoning typeWhat the task asksExample task
Issue-spottingDo these facts raise a legal question / implicate an area of law?learned_hands_benefits
Rule-recallState the correct legal rule for a jurisdiction (a hallucination probe)rule_qa
Rule-applicationExplain how a given rule applies to the facts — graded on correctness and analysisdiversity_jurisdiction
Rule-conclusionPredict the legal outcome under a rule supplied in the promptucc_v_common_law
InterpretationParse a legal text — extract, classify, or answer about its contentcuad_audit_right
Rhetorical-understandingReason about legal argumentation (what role a sentence plays)definition_classification

The split between rule-application and rule-conclusion is the subtle part. Both run on the same fact-pattern tasks, but conclusion only checks whether the predicted outcome is right, while application checks the reasoning: the model must verbalize how the rule maps onto the facts. An explanation that restates the rule and the answer but skips the inferences is "correct but contains no analysis" — exactly the conclusory writing that earns a low grade on a law exam.

Grading: balanced-accuracy, and a law-trained human for the hard part

Most tasks are classification or extraction. Following HELM, classification is scored by exact-match, but because many legal tasks have heavy label imbalance, the metric is balanced-accuracy — the mean of per-class recall, so a model can't win by always guessing the majority label:

balanced-acc = 1|C| Σc∈C TPcNc // average recall across classes c — chance ≈ 50% on a balanced binary task, not the majority-class rate

Extraction tasks normalize for tense/casing/punctuation; multi-label tasks (e.g., naming every defendant) use F1. The expensive exception is rule-application: there is no automatic grader for whether an explanation contains the right inferences, so those tasks were graded manually by a law-trained individual against a released answer-guide — and only for the three large commercial models, since smaller models rarely produce gradable explanations at all.

The models and the prompts

The empirical study covers 20 LLMs from 11 families. Three are commercial API models — GPT-4, GPT-3.5 (text-davinci-003), and Claude-1 (v1.3) — evaluated between May and August 2023. The other 17 are open-source models at 3B / 7B / 13B scales (Incite, OPT, Falcon, MPT, Vicuna, Flan-T5, LLaMA-2, WizardLM, BLOOM); none larger than 13B. Each task got a hand-written instruction and zero-to-eight in-context demonstrations. The authors are explicit that prompts were not heavily optimized, so the reported scores are a deliberate lower bound on what each model can do.

// what they found

The results

The headline isn't a single number — it's the spread. Performance varies enormously across the six reasoning types and across model families, which is the whole point: legal competency must be assessed task-by-task, not with one leaderboard score. GPT-4 leads almost everywhere, but the gap to the next model and the shape of its strengths differ sharply by reasoning type.

Four findings worth keeping

  • GPT-4 is best, and issue-spotting is where it pulls away. Its largest margin over the other commercial models is on issue-spotting — a 20+ point absolute gap — while GPT-3.5 and Claude-1 sit within ~2 points of each other.
  • Rule-recall is the one place Claude-1 rivals GPT-4. Claude even beats GPT-4 on three tasks (rule_qa, international_citizenship_questions, nys_judicial_ethics). This is the category most like factual recall, and it doubles as a hallucination probe: small open models collapse here, echoing the legal community's complaints that LLMs misstate the law.
  • Open-source can match commercial — selectively. Flan-T5-XXL (11B) beats both GPT-3.5 and Claude-1 on issue-spotting and rhetorical-understanding despite the parameter gap. On rhetorical extraction, open Incite-Base essentially ties GPT-4 (80.6% vs 81.8%). The closed/open gap is widest on rule-conclusion, the most multi-step-reasoning-like category.
  • Reasoning quality lags answer accuracy. On the manually-graded rule-application tasks, GPT-4 scores 82.2 correctness but 79.7 analysis; GPT-3.5 drops from 58.5 to 44.2 — its explanations are far more often right-but-conclusory than wrong.

The kind of analysis LegalBench unlocks

Because tasks are sliced by fact-pattern type, the benchmark explains why a model improves, not just that it does. On the abercrombie trademark-distinctiveness task, GPT-4's jump over GPT-3.5 is concentrated in the hard middle categories — suggestive marks 38%→70% and arbitrary marks 41%→82% — while both models sit at ~72–73% on descriptive marks and ace the easy ends (generic, fanciful). That's a far more useful read than a single accuracy delta.

The prompt-engineering micro-studies surface practical, sometimes counterintuitive guidance. Spelling out the legal rule in the prompt (a "description" prompt) instead of just naming it helps on some tasks but not others — evidence that a model's latent knowledge of a rule depends on how often that rule appears in pretraining text. And switching from technical legalese to plain language helped GPT-3.5 on four of five tested tasks, by up to 21 points — except on one contract-NLI task, where the precise technical phrasing won instead.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

LegalBench's lasting contribution is the methodology — experts-as-authors, a typology lawyers recognize, scores that mean something to both communities — more than any specific number. Read as a 2023 capability snapshot, it has the limits the authors themselves flag, and a few they soft-pedal.

The most important honesty is upfront: the commercial models are believed to be hundreds of billions of parameters with unknown training data, "so it is thus possible that some LegalBench tasks leaked into pretraining data." Many tasks are reformatted from public corpora (CUAD, the overruling dataset, ToS clauses) that almost certainly sit in web-scale pretraining. Appendix D documents each task's prior online availability — which is the right move — but documentation isn't decontamination, and the strongest scores (commercial models, interpretation/rhetorical tasks built from public legal text) are exactly where leakage would inflate results most.

Two design choices cap how far the scores generalize. First, every task isolates a single reasoning type: a task can test issue-spotting or rule-recall, never the full IRAC chain a real matter demands, where each step compounds the last. Second, LegalBench deliberately restricts itself to questions with objectively correct answers — but the legal reasoning lawyers are actually paid for lives in the ambiguous cases where "reasonable minds may differ," which the benchmark explicitly defers. High scores here are necessary but nowhere near sufficient for "can do law."

Finally, the headline metric and the grading both deserve scrutiny. Balanced-accuracy on largely binary classification means chance is ~50%, so several mid-50s scores are barely above guessing — and a benchmark of yes/no questions rewards pattern-matching over the structured argument that is legal reasoning. The most interesting metric, rule-application's "analysis" score, rests on manual grading by a single law-trained individual for only three models; it's the richest signal in the paper and the least reproducible.

baselines

Broad for 2023 — 20 models, 11 families, 4 size points — but every commercial model (GPT-4, GPT-3.5, Claude-1) is now superseded, no open model exceeds 13B, and prompts are intentionally unoptimized (a stated lower bound).

contamination

Openly acknowledged and partially mapped (Appendix D logs each task's prior web availability), but many tasks are reformatted from public corpora and commercial training data is unknown — leakage is plausible exactly where scores are highest.

what's missed

Single-reasoning-type-per-task (no end-to-end IRAC); unambiguous-answer-only (skips the contested reasoning lawyers do); mostly binary classification under balanced-accuracy; rule-application "analysis" graded by one human, three models.

would raise confidence

Re-run on current frontier models with tuned prompts; contamination-controlled held-out tasks; multi-step joint-IRAC tasks; broader jurisdictions beyond US law; multiple expert graders with reported agreement on the generative tasks.

// bottom line

Treat LegalBench as the field's vocabulary and infrastructure, not its scoreboard. Its durable idea — domain experts co-author the benchmark and a discipline-native typology makes the scores legible — is the template later expert-rubric benchmarks (PRBench's Legal split, HealthBench, ResearchRubrics) scaled up. The 2023 numbers are a dated snapshot; the framework is the contribution.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. Experts as co-authors, not annotators. 162 tasks across 36 corpora, hand-built by 40 legal professionals, organized by the IRAC framework lawyers already use — so a model score means the same thing to an ML researcher and a litigator.
  2. Legal ability is not one number. GPT-4 leads but unevenly (conclusion 89.9, rule-recall 59.2); Claude-1 rivals it only on rule-recall; open Flan-T5-XXL beats GPT-3.5/Claude-1 on issue-spotting and rhetorical tasks. Assess task-by-task.
  3. Right answer ≠ good reasoning. On rule-application, models score lower on "analysis" than "correctness" (GPT-3.5: 44.2 vs 58.5) — they reach conclusions without verbalizing the inferences, exactly the conclusory writing that fails a law exam.
  4. Prompting law is its own craft. Plain language beat legalese by up to 21 points on most tasks (but not all); spelling out the rule helps only when the rule is rare in pretraining; in-context-demo choice swings scores 20+ points.
  5. The template, not the scoreboard, endured. The 2023 models are obsolete and binary classification under balanced-accuracy is a blunt instrument — but the expert-built, typology-organized benchmark is the blueprint later rubric benchmarks scaled.
