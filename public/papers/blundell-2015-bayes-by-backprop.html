Weight Uncertainty in Neural Networks

Bayes by Backprop puts a learnable Gaussian on every weight instead of a single number, then uses the reparameterization trick to make sampling from that distribution differentiable — turning ordinary backprop into a scalable, unbiased Monte Carlo approximation to Bayesian neural network inference.

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

An ordinary neural network trained by backprop learns one point estimate per weight — a single number, chosen by gradient descent to fit the training set. That single number carries no sense of how sure the network is that this particular weight setting is right. Two very different sets of weights might explain the training data almost equally well, but a point-estimate network reports only the one gradient descent happened to find, with total confidence, on every input — including inputs unlike anything it saw during training.

That missing uncertainty shows up as three concrete failures. First, overconfidence: nothing in a standard network's forward pass distinguishes "I've seen thousands of examples like this" from "I've never seen anything like this and am guessing." Second, ad hoc regularization: L2 weight decay, L1 sparsity, and dropout all act as stand-ins for a proper prior over weights, chosen for convenience rather than derived from a probabilistic model. Third, and most concretely testable, no principled exploration signal: in a contextual bandit or RL setting, deciding whether to try an under-explored action requires knowing which of the network's own predictions it's still unsure about — a point-estimate net has no such notion, forcing hand-tuned heuristics like ε-greedy instead.

Fully Bayesian neural networks — which keep a whole posterior distribution over the weights rather than a point estimate — have existed since the early 1990s (MacKay's Laplace-approximation framework, Neal's Hamiltonian Monte Carlo), but none of the classical approaches scale to networks and datasets of modern size. The most promising scalable proposal, Graves (2011)'s variational treatment, got part of the way there but needed a biased approximation to a key gradient term. The gap this paper closes is a way to train a genuinely Bayesian neural network — a learned distribution, not a point, per weight — using nothing more exotic than ordinary backprop and SGD, at the same asymptotic scale as any other deep net.

// how it works

The method

The idea is to replace every scalar weight w with a random variable governed by a learned Gaussian q(w|θ) = N(w | μ, σ²). Concretely, each weight now needs two learnable numbers, μ and ρ, with the standard deviation obtained as σ = log(1 + exp(ρ)) — a softplus transform that keeps σ positive for any real-valued ρ, so unconstrained gradient descent can never push the variance negative.

Fitting this distribution is variational inference: instead of computing the true (intractable) posterior P(w|D) exactly, find the θ that makes q(w|θ) as close as possible to it, measured by minimizing the variational free energy

F(D, θ) = KL[q(w|θ) ∥ P(w)] − Eq(w|θ)[log P(D|w)] // KL-to-prior term (regularizer) minus expected data log-likelihood (fit term)

The first term pulls the learned distribution toward a prior P(w) — this is where regularization enters, principled rather than bolted on. The second term is the ordinary training-data fit, except now it's an expectation over weight samples rather than a single evaluation. That expectation is the obstacle: it's an integral over a distribution that itself depends on the parameters θ being optimized, so naively you can't differentiate through the sampling step to get a gradient on θ.

Bayes by Backprop's core trick is a specific reparameterization: rather than sampling w ~ N(μ, σ²) directly, sample a parameter-free noise variable ε ~ N(0, I) and compute the weight deterministically as w = μ + log(1 + exp(ρ)) · ε. Now w is an ordinary differentiable function of (μ, ρ, ε), so the gradients ∂F/∂μ and ∂F/∂ρ can be computed by standard backprop through the network exactly as if w were a normal activation. The full Monte Carlo cost that gets minimized, averaged over a handful of sampled weight draws w(i) per minibatch, is

F(D, θ) ≈ Σi [log q(w(i)|θ) − log P(w(i)) − log P(D|w(i))] // one term per sampled weight vector; the paper proves this estimator is unbiased at any sample count, even one

That unbiasedness is the paper's key theoretical claim: earlier variational approaches to Bayesian nets (including Graves 2011) worked with a biased gradient estimate. Because every piece of this objective is differentiable end-to-end, no new inference algorithm or sampler is required — it's trained with the same SGD loop as any other network, and the authors note it parallelizes across machines exactly like standard async SGD. The paper tries both a single Gaussian prior and a two-component scale-mixture prior (a mixture of two zero-mean Gaussians, one narrow and one wide), which lets most weights be cheaply pulled toward zero while still permitting a heavy-tailed subset to grow large where the data demands it — this mixture prior turns out to matter a lot for the results below.

// what they found

The results

The paper tests Bayes by Backprop (BBB) on three tasks: MNIST classification, a nonlinear regression problem, and a contextual-bandit exploration task — each isolating a different claimed benefit (regularization, calibrated predictive uncertainty, and directed exploration, respectively).

MNIST classification and automatic pruning

On feedforward nets with two hidden layers of rectified-linear units (400 / 800 / 1200 units per layer, trained on 50,000 digits with 10,000 held out for validation), plain SGD reaches 1.83% / 1.84% / 1.88% test error at the three sizes; SGD with dropout reaches 1.51% / 1.33% / 1.36%. BBB with a single Gaussian prior does worse than dropout — 1.82% / 1.99% / 2.04% — but switching to the scale-mixture prior flips the result to 1.36% / 1.34% / 1.32%, matching or slightly beating dropout at every width, with the 1200-unit, 1.32% result the best in the table among comparable plain feedforward nets. The cost: each BBB training iteration runs "around two times slower" than dropout's, and the network needs a mean and a variance per weight during training — effectively double the parameters, even though the point comparison above uses matched weight counts (400/800/1200 units → 500K/1.3M/2.4M weights) for all methods.

A second experiment probes what the learned per-weight uncertainty is actually good for: rank all weights in a trained 1200-unit BBB network by their signal-to-noise ratio |μ|/σ, then zero out the noisiest ones. Starting from 1.24% test error at 2.4M weights, removing 50% of weights (1.2M left) leaves error unchanged at 1.24%; removing 75% (600K left) still holds at 1.24%; only at 95% removed (120K left) does it rise to 1.29%, and at 98% removed (48K left) to 1.39%. The network's own uncertainty estimates correctly flag which weights it never needed to pin down precisely.

Regression: knowing what you don't know

Fitting y = x + 0.3·sin(2π(x+ε)) + 0.3·sin(4π(x+ε)) + ε (with ε ~ N(0, 0.02)) to a bounded range of training x, a standard point-estimate network's predictive confidence interval stays narrow everywhere, including well outside the training range — it confidently extrapolates one particular continuation of the curve. The BBB network's interquartile range widens sharply exactly where training data runs out, because Bayesian model averaging over the many weight settings consistent with the data disagrees more the further it extrapolates.

Contextual bandits: uncertainty as an exploration signal

The UCI Mushroom dataset is recast as a bandit: an agent can eat or not eat a mushroom described by a feature vector (the context). Eating an edible mushroom pays +5; eating a poisonous one pays +5 or −35 with equal probability (expected −15); not eating always pays 0. Four agents — all built on the same two-hidden-layer, 100-ReLU-unit network trained online from a 4096-tuple replay buffer — are compared: a purely greedy policy, ε-greedy at 1% and 5%, and Bayes by Backprop with no ε at all (it samples its own weights twice per decision and averages, letting posterior weight uncertainty itself decide when to explore — the neural-net analogue of Thompson sampling). Over 50,000 steps, BBB accrues the lowest cumulative regret of the four; the 5%-greedy agent, which explores the most indiscriminately, accrues the most. The purely greedy agent does badly for its first ~1,000 steps (eating nothing) before abruptly discovering mushrooms are usually safe — illustrating exactly the failure mode principled exploration is meant to avoid.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

The reported numbers are honestly framed and the pruning experiment is a genuinely good demonstration that the learned uncertainty carries real signal rather than being cosmetic. But the empirical scope is narrow by design, and it's worth being precise about what is and isn't tested.

The MNIST comparisons hold the architecture fixed (plain feedforward ReLU nets, no convolutions, no data augmentation) and vary only the training objective — a fair, controlled test of the regularization claim, but nowhere near the state of the art even at the time: the paper's own Table 1 cites 1.6% for unregularized SGD, ≈1.3% for dropout, and 1.2% for SGD+DropConnect using a 5-network ensemble — already beating BBB's best single-model result of 1.32%. No convolutional or ensembled variant of Bayes by Backprop is tried, so it's untested whether the benefit compounds with a stronger backbone or is architecture-specific. The bandit task is a single, low-dimensional, tabular-feature benchmark (repurposed UCI Mushroom data); it cleanly illustrates that posterior weight uncertainty can substitute for hand-tuned ε, but says nothing about whether the effect survives in a higher-dimensional or sparse-reward RL setting. And the paper reports only a passing "roughly two times slower than dropout" per-iteration cost, with no wall-clock- or parameter-count-normalized comparison table against either the classical Bayesian NN baselines it cites (MacKay 1992, Graves 2011) or, naturally, against methods published after it.

baselines

SGD (no regularization), SGD+dropout, and SGD+DropConnect (5-net ensemble) on one feedforward MNIST architecture family — fair within that family, but no ConvNet and no post-2015 uncertainty method existed yet to compare against.

contamination

Not applicable in the leakage sense — MNIST and the UCI Mushroom bandit are closed, static evaluations reporting same-distribution test performance; the open question is generalization to settings the paper never tries (larger nets, other modalities), not train/test overlap.

what's missed

No ImageNet/ConvNet-scale experiment; no comparison to Monte Carlo dropout (published the following year, and arguably BBB's most consequential rival, since it extracts similar calibrated uncertainty from a network you were likely already training); no normalized compute/memory comparison against the classical Bayesian NN methods it does cite.

would raise confidence

A head-to-head against MC-dropout and Deep Ensembles on an identical OOD/calibration protocol at ConvNet scale, plus an ablation isolating how much of the MNIST gain comes from the scale-mixture prior specifically versus the variational training machinery in general.

// bottom line

Bayes by Backprop is the cleanest worked example of turning a fully Bayesian neural network into something trainable end-to-end by ordinary SGD — the same reparameterization trick Kingma & Welling and Rezende, Mohamed & Wierstra used for latent variables, applied directly to the weights themselves — and it still reads as the reference formulation of variational BNNs. But as a production recipe it was quickly out-competed: Gal & Ghahramani's Monte Carlo dropout got comparable calibrated uncertainty a year later from a network you'd probably train anyway, and Lakshminarayanan et al.'s Deep Ensembles (2017) — co-authored by Blundell himself — explicitly frames plain deterministic-network ensembling with proper scoring rules as a simpler, equally-or-better-calibrated non-Bayesian alternative that needs none of this paper's KL machinery. Its lasting contribution is the formulation, not the adoption.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. Bayes by Backprop replaces every point-estimate weight with a learned Gaussian N(μ,σ²), and makes the whole thing differentiable via the reparameterization w = μ + log(1+exp(ρ))·ε — so a genuinely Bayesian neural network trains with ordinary backprop and SGD, no new inference machinery required.
  2. The learned uncertainty isn't decorative: with a scale-mixture prior it matches or beats dropout on MNIST (1.32% vs. 1.33–1.36% test error), lets 95% of weights be pruned by signal-to-noise ratio with almost no accuracy loss (1.24%→1.29%), widens predictive intervals exactly where training data is sparse, and drives Thompson-sampling-style exploration in a contextual bandit with no hand-tuned ε.
  3. The cost is real — roughly double the parameters during training and about 2x slower per step than dropout — and the field mostly moved past it in practice: MC-dropout and Deep Ensembles get comparable uncertainty far more cheaply, leaving Bayes by Backprop's legacy as the clean differentiable-Bayesian-NN formulation rather than a production default.
