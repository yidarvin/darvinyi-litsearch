DeepResearch Bench: A Comprehensive Benchmark for Deep Research Agents

The first benchmark built specifically for Deep Research Agents — 100 PhD-level tasks across 22 domains, with the task mix grounded in 44,019 filtered real user queries. Two human-aligned frameworks grade it: RACE scores report quality against a reference, and FACT scores citation trustworthiness. Gemini-2.5-Pro Deep Research leads (48.88) and averages 111 verified citations per report, but Perplexity cites most accurately and the RACE judge's agreement with humans (71.33%) edges out the experts' agreement with each other (68.44%).

Mingxuan Du, Benfeng Xu, Chiwei Zhu, Xiaorui Wang, Zhendong Mao · USTC / Metastone

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

Deep Research Agents (DRAs) — OpenAI Deep Research, Gemini Deep Research, Grok Deeper Search, Perplexity Deep Research — autonomously orchestrate multi-step web exploration, targeted retrieval, and higher-order synthesis, turning a vague research request into an analyst-grade, citation-rich report and compressing hours of desk research into minutes. By mid-2025 they were among the most widely deployed LLM agents. Yet there was no standard way to measure whether any of them was actually good.

Grading a DRA is genuinely hard for two reasons. First, the agent's internal reasoning and retrieval are opaque — the final report is the only interface you can score. Second, for a real research question there is no definitive ground-truth answer; a good report can take many valid forms, run thousands of words, and depend on a live web that shifts between runs.

Existing benchmarks miss in two opposite directions. Browsing / retrieval evals — BrowseComp, WebArena, BrowseComp-ZH — and QA datasets like GAIA, Humanity's Last Exam, and WebWalker reward short, verifiable factual answers; they never test the long-form, multi-source synthesis that is the deep-research task. Generation benchmarks — LongWriter, HelloBench, WritingBench — test writing quality but are disconnected from real-time information acquisition. And the few efforts that do grade long reports lean on LLM-as-judge without a structured framework or any verification of human consistency. The gap DeepResearch Bench targets: a dedicated, end-to-end DRA benchmark grounded in authentic user demand, paired with evaluation methods — for both report quality and retrieval — that are demonstrably aligned with expert human judgment.

// how it works

The method

The benchmark is 100 tasks — 50 in English, 50 in Chinese — each authored and iteratively refined by a domain expert (a PhD or a senior practitioner with 5+ years of relevant experience) and then manually screened by the team for quality, clarity, complexity, and fit to the deep-research definition. Crucially, the number of tasks per domain isn't arbitrary: the authors collected 96,147 real queries from a web-search-enabled chatbot, used DeepSeek-V3-0324 to filter them down to 44,019 true deep-research queries and classify those into 22 domains, then proportionally compressed that distribution to 100 tasks so the benchmark mirrors what people actually ask DRAs to do.

On top of the tasks sit two complementary evaluation frameworks — one for the report, one for the citations.

RACE — grading the report

RACE (Reference-based Adaptive Criteria-driven Evaluation with Dynamic Weighting) scores report quality with an LLM judge, but fixes the two failure modes of naïve LLM-as-judge. It starts from four orthogonal, human-defined dimensions: Comprehensiveness (COMP), Insight/Depth (DEPTH), Instruction-Following (INST), and Readability (READ). For each task, the judge first derives task-specific dimension weights by averaging over T trials, so a "write a literature review" task weights Comprehensiveness more than a "draft a quick brief" task:

Wd = 1T Σj=1T wd(j) // (Eq. 1) dimension weight for d ∈ {COMP, DEPTH, INST, READ}, averaged over T judge trials. Within each dimension the judge then writes Kd tailored criteria whose weights sum to 1.

The second fix is reference-based scoring. Scoring a report in isolation produces uniformly high, undiscriminating scores, so RACE judges the target report and a high-quality reference report against the same aggregated criteria list, then reports a relative score — the target's quality as a fraction of (target + reference):

Sfinal(Rtgt) = Sint(Rtgt)Sint(Rtgt) + Sint(Rref) // (Eq. 3) Sint is the criterion-weighted, then dimension-weighted, intermediate score. A report exactly as good as the reference scores 0.5 (≈ 50 on the 0–100 scale).

The judge is Gemini-2.5-pro; the reference reports were produced by Gemini-2.5-pro-based Deep Research (April 2025). Because everything is relative to that reference, the authors stress that scores compress into a narrow band and should be read as rankings and proportions, not absolute grades — a 48.88 here maps onto something like a human 90+.

FACT — grading the citations

FACT (Factual Abundance and Citation Trustworthiness) audits whether the report's sourcing is real. A judge extracts every statement–URL pair from the report and deduplicates pairs that cite the same URL for the same fact. Each unique pair is then verified: the cited page's text is fetched via the Jina Reader API, and a judge (the cheaper Gemini-2.5-flash) issues a binary verdict on whether that page actually supports the statement. Two metrics fall out: Citation Accuracy (C. Acc.) — the precision of the agent's citations — and Average Effective Citations per Task (E. Cit.) — how much verifiably-supported information the agent surfaces per report.

// what they found

The results

Across four early-release DRAs and a dozen search-augmented LLMs, the specialized Deep Research Agents clearly separate from chat models with a search tool. On RACE Overall, Gemini-2.5-Pro Deep Research leads at 48.88, with OpenAI Deep Research at 46.98 — and OpenAI actually edges Gemini on Instruction-Following (49.27 vs 49.18), a hint the four dimensions are genuinely decoupled. Perplexity Deep Research (42.25) and Grok Deeper Search (40.24) trail. The best non-DRA is Claude-3.7-Sonnet with Search at 40.67, which actually outscores Grok's dedicated agent — the authors credit Claude being allowed multi-turn search.

FACT tells a sharper story. Gemini-2.5-Pro DR is in a league of its own on volume — 111.21 effective citations per task, versus 40.79 for OpenAI DR and 31.26 for Perplexity DR — consistent with its top Comprehensiveness score. But volume isn't precision: on Citation Accuracy, Perplexity DR leads at 90.24%, ahead of Gemini (81.44%) and OpenAI (77.96%). Among search-augmented LLMs, Claude-3.7-Sonnet posts the highest accuracy of all at 93.68%. So the headline split is real: Gemini retrieves the most, Perplexity grounds the most reliably.

Does the grader track humans?

The benchmark's real load-bearing claim is that RACE agrees with people. On 50 Chinese tasks scored by 70+ Master's-level annotators (three experts per report), RACE(Full) hits a 71.33% Pairwise Agreement Rate with humans — higher than the 68.44% the human experts agree with each other — alongside a 99.54% overall Pearson correlation, versus just 58.89% / 60.46 for a vanilla direct-scoring prompt. Ablations confirm the design earns its complexity: removing the reference report is the single biggest hit (PAR 71.33 → 66.56), and swapping adaptive criteria for static ones drops it to 68.33. On the judge-LLM bake-off, Gemini 2.5 Pro Preview gives the best agreement (72.56 overall) at a competitive $0.13 per query, which is why it's the default judge.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

DeepResearch Bench did something genuinely useful and was rewarded for it — 168 citations and a live Hugging Face leaderboard make it the de-facto DRA benchmark of 2025. RACE and FACT are pragmatic, and the human-consistency study is more than window dressing: a pairwise agreement rate that beats inter-expert agreement is a real result. But the framework has a structural bias baked into its core, and the leaderboard rests on a thin experimental base.

The deepest issue is circularity around the Gemini family. RACE's judge is Gemini-2.5-pro, the reference report every target is scored against was written by Gemini-2.5-Pro Deep Research, and the top-ranked system is… Gemini-2.5-Pro Deep Research. A relative score (Eq. 3) anchored to a Gemini-authored reference, adjudicated by a Gemini judge, systematically flatters Gemini-style outputs — and the paper never isolates self-preference (e.g. by grading with an off-family judge or using references from multiple model families). This is exactly the objection sibling benchmark ResearchRubrics later raised: leaning on LLM-generated reference reports and LLM-generated criteria "raises circularity and oversight concerns." Here the adaptive criteria are also judge-written, and the ablation shows they buy only ~3 points of human agreement over static criteria — modest payoff for the added opacity.

Then the headline numbers. The relative-scoring trick compresses everything into a 40–49 band, so the marquee 48.88-vs-46.98 gap is 1.9 points with no confidence intervals, and the human study that validates the grader is only 50 Chinese tasks, four agents, three raters — with the filtered correlations (FAP/FAS) computed on just 37 tasks after dropping low-agreement ones. The benchmark itself is 100 tasks and exactly four real DRAs, all early-release commercial black boxes whose retrieval stacks and version histories are unknown (the authors candidly note they "cannot confirm" whether Gemini DR iterated during reference collection). That makes every score a snapshot of a moving, non-reproducible target — the live web changes between runs, and the 100 public tasks invite future contamination.

Finally, FACT is only half-validated. The human-consistency work covers RACE; the citation framework rests on single-judge Gemini-2.5-flash verdicts plus whatever Jina Reader can fetch, so link rot, paywalls, and JS-heavy pages bias C. Acc. downward and no human check is reported. And E. Cit. rewards sheer volume — Gemini's 111 citations is as easily read as citation-padding as thoroughness, since the metric counts supported citations but not whether they were needed.

baselines

Only four early-release DRAs, all closed commercial products; no open-weight or controlled-model variant to separate "architecture" from product/retrieval-config differences. The LLM-with-search comparison is apples-to-oranges (single-turn search vs multi-turn agents).

contamination

100 tasks are public, inviting future training leakage; live-web retrieval makes runs non-reproducible; commercial DRAs iterate without version transparency. Scores are a dated snapshot, not a stable measurement.

what's missed

RACE judges Gemini against a Gemini-written reference with a Gemini judge — self-preference is never isolated. Relative scoring hides effect sizes (1.9-pt top-two gap, no CIs); FACT is unvalidated against humans and its E. Cit. metric rewards volume over necessity.

would raise confidence

Diversify the judge off the Gemini family and use references from multiple families (or human-written ones); report confidence intervals and run-to-run web variance; human-validate FACT; hold out a private split; add open-weight DRAs.

// bottom line

DeepResearch Bench is the right idea at the right time — the first end-to-end DRA benchmark, grounded in real demand, with a grader that provably tracks experts. Trust the broad picture: dedicated DR agents clearly beat search-augmented chat models, Gemini and OpenAI lead, and "retrieves most" (Gemini) is a different skill from "cites most accurately" (Perplexity). Hold loosely the things downstream of a Gemini-grades-Gemini-against-Gemini pipeline: the exact 48.88-vs-46.98 ordering, and the 111-citation flex.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. The first dedicated Deep Research Agent benchmark: 100 PhD-level tasks across 22 domains (50 English / 50 Chinese), with the per-domain task counts grounded in 44,019 filtered real user queries (from 96,147 raw).
  2. Two frameworks do the grading: RACE (reference-based, dynamically-weighted, adaptive-criteria report scorer) and FACT (citation accuracy + effective-citation count). RACE's 71.33% pairwise agreement with humans beats the experts' 68.44% agreement with each other; the reference report is its most important component (removing it drops agreement to 66.56%).
  3. Gemini-2.5-Pro Deep Research leads RACE Overall (48.88) and dominates on volume with 111.21 effective citations per report — but Perplexity DR cites most accurately (90.24% vs Gemini's 81.44%), and OpenAI DR (46.98) edges Gemini on Instruction-Following.
  4. Dedicated DR agents clearly beat search-augmented LLMs (best non-DRA: Claude-3.7-Sonnet w/Search, 40.67 RACE / 93.68% accuracy). The catch worth remembering: the entire RACE pipeline is Gemini grading against a Gemini-written reference — read the rankings, not the absolute numbers.
