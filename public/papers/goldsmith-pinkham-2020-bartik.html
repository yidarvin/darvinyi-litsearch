Bartik Instruments: What, When, Why, and How

For three decades, applied economists interacting local industry shares with national industry growth rates — a "Bartik" or shift-share instrument — to study everything from labor supply to trade shocks to immigration, without a settled answer to what, exactly, makes the design valid. This paper shows the instrument is numerically identical to a GMM estimator built from the underlying shares, and hands researchers a two-line diagnostic — the Rotemberg weight — that reveals which of the many shares are actually doing the identifying work. Run on the field's most famous shift-share design, Autor-Dorn-Hanson's China-shock instrument, it finds over half the identifying weight sits on five industries that are not the ones the original paper's own narrative points to.

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

The "Bartik instrument" — named after Bartik (1991) and popularized by Blanchard and Katz (1992) — interacts a location's baseline industry employment shares with national industry growth rates to predict local labor demand, and then uses that prediction as an instrument. By 2018 it (or a formally identical variant) had been used across labor, public, development, trade, macro, and finance economics for three decades: to estimate the elasticity of labor supply, the local effects of Chinese import competition, the wage effects of immigration, the effects of credit-supply shocks, and dozens more applications. Yet, as the authors put it, "it is not plausible... that the Bartik instrument always provides a valid identification strategy" — and there was no common, formal understanding of exactly which assumption makes a given use of it valid, nor any tool for a reader to check what variation in the data is actually driving a published estimate. Practitioners typically motivated Bartik designs with a two-industry toy example and moved on, leaving open whether the identifying assumption was really about the exogeneity of the industry shares (a differential-exposure, difference-in-differences-style story) or of the national growth rates ("shocks," which requires many independent, idiosyncratic industry shocks averaging out). With dozens of industries or origin-country shares combined into a single number, it was practically impossible to tell — from the coefficient alone — whether a headline result rested on broad-based variation or on one or two idiosyncratic industries doing all the work. This graph already contains two well-known instances of exactly this design: autor-2013-china-syndrome's commuting-zone exposure to Chinese imports, and acemoglu-2020-robots-and-jobs's commuting-zone exposure to industrial robots — both black boxes this paper is built to open.

// how it works

The method

The paper makes two moves. First, an equivalence result: employment growth is the inner product of industry shares zlk and industry growth rates glk, and the Bartik instrument Bl = Σk zlkgk combines the same shares with only the national component of growth. The authors show that two-stage least squares with the Bartik instrument is numerically identical to a generalized method of moments (GMM) estimator that instead uses each industry's share zlk as its own separate instrument, weighted by a specific matrix built from the growth rates. The practical consequence: the identifying assumption a researcher is actually leaning on is exogeneity of the shares — a pooled-exposure design, formally equivalent to a difference-in-differences setup, where locations with more of an industry are compared to locations with less — not exogeneity of the national growth rates, which is how many papers frame it.

Second, and more useful in practice: because Bartik is equivalent to combining many just-identified share-instruments, it can be decomposed into a weighted average of them. Building on Andrews, Gentzkow, and Shapiro (2017), the authors derive the exact weight each industry share contributes — the Rotemberg weight — and show it is a sensitivity-to-misspecification elasticity: how much bias in that one share's exogeneity assumption translates into bias in the overall Bartik estimate. The weights sum to one but individual weights can be negative, and in every application the authors study, a small number of industries (or origin countries) carry a large share of the total — meaning the "397-industry" or "722-commuting-zone" instrument a paper reports is often functionally much closer to a comparison of a handful of specific places.

β̂Bartik = Σk α̂k β̂k,  α̂k = gk·Z′kX⊥Σk′ gk′·Z′k′X⊥ // β̂ₖ is the just-identified IV estimate using only industry k's share as the instrument; α̂ₖ (the Rotemberg weight) is how much of the overall Bartik estimate that one industry is responsible for — Σₖ α̂ₖ = 1, Proposition 3.1

Around this decomposition the paper builds a short checklist for auditing any shift-share design: (1) report the shares with the largest |α̂k| and check whether they match the industries the paper's own narrative emphasizes; (2) check how much of the variance in the Rotemberg weights is explained by the growth-rate component versus the share-variance component, which tells you whether the "shocks" framing is even doing any work; (3) where a pre-period exists, test for parallel pre-trends specifically among the high-weight shares (not just the aggregate instrument); and (4) compare the Bartik TSLS point estimate against overidentified TSLS, LIML, jackknife (MBTSLS), and heteroskedasticity-robust (HFUL) alternatives, and run overidentification tests — divergence across estimators, or a rejected overidentification test, signals that the many just-identified estimates being averaged are not all estimating the same thing.

// what they found

The results

The paper runs its diagnostic on three published Bartik designs. The one most relevant to this graph is a replication of autor-2013-china-syndrome (Autor, Dorn, and Hanson 2013, "ADH"): using their specification (a $1,000/worker rise in import exposure predicted by lagged industry shares interacted with import growth from China to other high-income countries), the authors reproduce ADH's own column-6 TSLS coefficient of a 0.60 percentage-point decline in manufacturing employment share. But despite 397 underlying industries, the top five instruments — electronic computers, games and toys, household audio and video, telephone apparatus, and computer equipment — receive over half (0.532/1.067) of the total absolute Rotemberg weight. Except for games and toys, these are higher-skill, technologically innovative industries — not the low-skill, labor-intensive industries (apparel, footwear, furniture, textiles) that ADH's own narrative motivates the design around. The growth-rate component (imports to other high-income countries) explains less than 20% of the variance in the weights, meaning the growth rates are a poor guide to which industries are actually driving the estimate. Alternative estimators diverge sharply — from half the baseline Bartik-TSLS size (MBTSLS) to several times larger (LIML) — and the overidentification tests reject the null that all 397 instruments are jointly valid.

In the second application (a re-analysis following Card 2009's immigrant-enclave design, estimating the elasticity of substitution between immigrants and natives), the picture is different in an instructive way: for high-school-equivalent workers the Rotemberg weights are "almost completely explained" by the immigrant inflows themselves (the growth-rate analogue), and the share of Mexican immigrants living in a city in 1980 alone carries almost half the total weight — a concentration Card (2009, p.9) himself acknowledges. Unlike the other two applications, most overidentification tests here fail to reject, and alternative estimators agree closely — the paper's clearest example of a shift-share design where the diagnostic comes back reassuring rather than alarming.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

This is a paper whose entire contribution is a diagnostic for evaluating other papers' identification strategies, so the natural "eval read" is: how much does the diagnostic itself teach us once pointed back at this graph's own shift-share designs? Applied to autor-2013-china-syndrome, the sharpest finding is not just that five industries carry over half the weight — it's what happens when you check those five industries for pre-trends. Figure 2 below plots each high-weight industry's reduced-form effect on manufacturing employment, indexed to 1970. Telephone apparatus shows a fairly steady decline from 1980 onward — well before the 2000s, when Chinese import competition was actually largest — and household audio/video and computer equipment show substantial pre-2000 movement too. That is precisely the pattern a valid differential-exposure design should not show: if places with more telephone-apparatus employment were already diverging in the 1980s, some of the "China effect" attributed to 2000–2007 may really be a pre-existing trend the design cannot separate from the trade shock. The paper is candid about this rather than papering over it, which is itself notable, but it stops short of concluding what it means for ADH's headline 0.60pp estimate.

baselines

Not applicable in the usual sense — the paper doesn't compete against alternative estimators to win, it audits what any given estimator (Bartik-TSLS) is doing internally. Its own internal validity is unusually strong: the core equivalence result (Proposition 3.1) is an exact algebraic identity verified by construction, not a simulation claim, and the two-industry / two-period special cases are worked by hand before the general panel result — about as close to a formally verified "unit test" as an econometrics paper gets.

contamination

Not leakage in the ML sense, but a structurally identical concern: a headline "397-industry" or "722-commuting-zone" instrument can be functionally a single-industry natural experiment. This graph's own acemoglu-2020-robots-and-jobs node is the sharpest illustration — its explainer already notes the automotive industry alone explains 67% of cross-commuting-zone variation and that Rotemberg weights run 50–90% in its main specifications, a diagnostic that paper ran precisely because this one exists. The ADH replication here is milder (top 5 of 397 industries, ~50% of weight) but the same logic applies: the design's effective sample size is much smaller than its headline industry count suggests.

what's missed

The diagnostic is descriptive, not a hypothesis test — it cannot itself confirm or reject whether a share is exogenous, only tell you where to look. It also can't resolve the deeper "shares vs. shocks" debate: Borusyak, Hull, and Jaravel's competing shocks-based inference framework, circulating the same year, argues that under their assumptions a smaller number of "important" shocks (not shares) can still deliver valid, if less precise, inference — and the two frameworks can disagree about which papers' designs are more or less credible. Nor does finding a high-weight industry with a pre-trend tell a reader how much of the headline estimate to discount; the paper flags the problem without re-estimating a "corrected" ADH effect.

would raise confidence

Routine reporting — now increasingly standard in applied micro precisely because of this paper — of the Rotemberg-weight table and a pre-trends plot restricted to the top-weight shares in every published shift-share paper, rather than only the aggregate instrument's pre-trend. Cross-checking a design against both this paper's shares-based diagnostic and Borusyak-Hull-Jaravel's shocks-based inference would pin down whether a given result is robust to which of the two identifying stories a reader finds more plausible.

// bottom line

This paper doesn't overturn any specific published estimate, and it is careful to say so. What it does is give every reader of a shift-share paper — including this graph's own China-shock and robots-and-jobs nodes — a two-line diagnostic that converts "trust the authors' framing of which industries matter" into "check which five industries are actually carrying the number, and whether those five have clean pre-trends." Run on the field's most cited shift-share design, the answer was: the industries doing the identifying work were not the ones the original paper's narrative was built around.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. A Bartik / shift-share instrument is numerically identical to a GMM estimator built from the underlying industry (or origin-country) shares as separate just-identified instruments — so "is this instrument valid" really means "are these specific shares exogenous," not "are the national growth rates exogenous."
  2. The Rotemberg weight tells you exactly how much of a published Bartik estimate each underlying share is responsible for (weights sum to 1, but individual weights can be negative) — report the top-weight shares and you've told the reader what comparison your estimator is actually making.
  3. Run on Autor-Dorn-Hanson's China-shock design, the top five industries carrying over half the identifying weight (electronic computers, games and toys, household audio/video, telephone apparatus, computer equipment) are not the low-skill, labor-intensive industries the paper's own narrative emphasizes — and several of them show pre-2000 pre-trends, the same concentration pattern this graph's own acemoglu-2020-robots-and-jobs node finds when it runs the identical diagnostic on its robots instrument (67% of variation from the automotive industry alone).
