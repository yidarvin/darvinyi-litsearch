Approximate Nearest Neighbor Negative Contrastive Learning for Dense Text Retrieval

Dense retrieval keeps losing to BM25 because its negatives are too easy — ANCE fixes that by mining hard negatives from an asynchronously-refreshed ANN index built on the retriever's own current checkpoint.

Lee Xiong, Chenyan Xiong, Ye Li, Kwok-Fung Tang, Jialin Liu, Paul Bennett, Junaid Ahmed, Arnold Overwijk
venue ICLR 2021 citations 1,630 topic Benchmarks & Evals source ↗

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

Dense retrieval (DR) is supposed to fix the vocabulary-mismatch problem that sparse, term-matching methods like BM25 can't: a question about "the bad guy in Lord of the Rings" should be able to find a passage about "the villain Sauron" even though the two share no words. Encode queries and documents into the same vector space, match by dot product, and the model should generalize past exact token overlap. In practice, end-to-end learned DR models had repeatedly underperformed BM25, especially on longer documents — the opposite of what the architecture promises.

The paper's diagnosis is specifically about how negatives are chosen during training, not the encoder architecture. First-stage retrieval must learn to separate a query's relevant documents from the entire corpus of irrelevant ones, but no training scheme can enumerate the whole corpus per step — negatives have to be sampled. The dominant recipes at the time were: random in-batch negatives, BM25 top-100 negatives (Lee et al., 2019; Karpukhin et al., 2020), or a 1:1 mix of the two (DPR's recipe). All three share the same flaw: they are drawn from a fixed, stale pool that has nothing to do with what the current model actually finds confusing.

The authors formalize why this matters with an importance-sampling argument. A negative instance's contribution to the training gradient is bounded by its loss: once a negative's loss is near zero — which happens quickly for negatives an untrained-enough model can already separate easily, like a random passage about an unrelated topic — its gradient vanishes too, and it stops teaching the model anything. Because the corpus is enormous and only a tiny fraction of it is genuinely confusable with the relevant documents, a random mini-batch has a vanishingly small probability of ever containing one of those truly hard negatives. Figure 1 makes the gap visible: a t-SNE plot shows the negatives dense retrieval actually has to reject at test time (DR Neg) sitting right next to the query and its relevant documents, while randomly sampled negatives and BM25-retrieved negatives sit far away in representation space — training on the wrong negatives entirely.

// how it works

The method

The paper first derives, via the standard variance-reduction framework for importance sampling, that the optimal negative-sampling distribution is proportional to the per-instance gradient norm: p*d− ∝ ||∇θl(d+, d−)||². Negatives with near-zero loss have near-zero gradients under common loss functions (BCE, hinge), so they contribute almost nothing to learning — this is the formal version of the "too-easy negatives" problem. The fix implied by the theory is to sample negatives that the current model still finds hard, i.e. negatives with large gradient norm, which is exactly what a fixed pool (random or BM25-derived) cannot provide as the model improves.

D−ANCE = ANNf(q,d) \ D+ // the hardest negatives currently confusable with the positives, per the being-optimized retriever f

Approximate nearest neighbor Negative Contrastive Estimation (ANCE) operationalizes this by sampling negatives from the top results the retriever itself currently returns from an ANN index built over the whole corpus — the model's own live mistakes become its training signal. By construction these are close to D−∗, the theoretically optimal hard-negative set.

Asynchronous index refresh

The catch is that keeping this ANN index current is expensive: it requires re-encoding every document in the corpus with the latest checkpoint, which is far too slow to do every mini-batch. ANCE solves this with an asynchronous training scheme (following Guu et al.'s REALM): a Trainer process continuously updates the retriever using negatives drawn from the most recently built index, while an Inferencer process runs in parallel, re-encoding the entire corpus with a newer checkpoint and rebuilding the ANN index once it finishes. When the refresh completes, the Trainer switches over to the fresh index. The two processes never block each other — the Trainer always has negatives to sample from, and the negatives just lag the model by however long one refresh cycle takes.

Concretely: BERT-Siamese (a single shared dual encoder) with dot-product similarity and an NLL loss, refreshing the ANN index every 10,000 training batches, with a 1:1 GPU split between Trainer and Inferencer, batch size 8 with gradient accumulation over 2 steps on 4 GPUs, and one negative uniformly sampled per positive from the ANN's top-200 retrieved candidates (Faiss IndexFlatIP). Long documents are handled two ways: FirstP truncates to the first 512 tokens; MaxP splits into up to four 512-token passages and max-pools their scores (a max-pooling operation Faiss supports natively). Web-search runs are fine-tuned from RoBERTa-base and warmed up with BM25 negatives first; OpenQA runs are warmed up from released DPR checkpoints. ANCE is architecture-agnostic — it is a training procedure for negatives, applicable to any BERT-Siamese dense retriever, not a new encoder design.

// what they found

The results

On the TREC 2019 Deep Learning Track, holding the encoder fixed (BERT-Siamese/RoBERTa-base) and varying only the negative-construction scheme, ANCE (FirstP) is the only negative-sampling method that lifts dense retrieval to robustly beat sparse retrieval on document ranking. On MS MARCO Dev passage ranking it reaches 0.330 MRR@10 / 0.959 Recall@1k, versus 0.311 / 0.952 for the DPR-style "BM25 + Rand Neg" baseline and 0.240 for plain BM25. On TREC DL passage retrieval NDCG@10 it hits 0.648 (vs. DPR-style 0.600, BM25 0.506); on TREC DL document retrieval NDCG@10, ANCE (FirstP) reaches 0.615 retrieval / 0.641 rerank, and ANCE (MaxP) reaches 0.628 retrieval / 0.671 rerank — closing in on the interaction-based BERT Reranker cascade's 0.646 rerank score, which no other dense-retrieval negative scheme comes close to.

In OpenQA retrieval, run under the exact setup from Karpukhin et al. (2020), ANCE beats DPR on Natural Questions: 81.9/87.5 Top-20/100 answer coverage vs DPR's 78.4/85.4 (BM25: 59.1/73.7). On TriviaQA the two are close — ANCE 80.3/85.3 vs DPR 79.4/85.0. Swapping ANCE in for DPR's retrieval while keeping the same readers (RAG-Token on NQ, DPR Reader on TQA) pushes end-to-end exact match to 46.0 on NQ and 57.5 on TQA — both the best of any system compared, ahead of RAG-Sequence (44.5/56.1) and DPR (41.5/56.8).

ANCE was also tested inside a commercial search engine's production first-stage retrieval, where switching that system's DR training to ANCE produced consistent relative gains of roughly +18.4% (250M-document corpus, 768-dim, exact KNN), +14.2% (8B corpus, 64-dim, exact KNN), and +15.5% (8B corpus, 64-dim, approximate ANN search) — "about 15% relative gains all around," in the authors' words, though no absolute metric or dataset detail is given for this internal system.

The empirical mechanism behind the gains is shown directly: the overlap between a negative-sampling method's training negatives and the true top-100 hardest test negatives is ~0% for both NCE Neg and Rand Neg, 15% for BM25 Neg, and starts at 63% for ANCE, converging to 100% by design as training proceeds. Correspondingly, the measured gradient norms on ANCE's negatives are orders of magnitude larger than on any local-negative baseline across the bottom, middle, and top BERT layers, and ANCE's training loss stays high (~0.4–0.7) throughout training rather than collapsing to near zero — direct empirical confirmation of the paper's theoretical claim that easy negatives stop teaching the model anything.

On efficiency, dense retrieval's total online latency is 11.6ms (2.6ms query encoding + 9ms ANN search over batched queries) versus the sparse cascade's 1.42s (37ms BM25 retrieval + 1.15s BERT rerank) — roughly the 100x speedup the abstract advertises, since document encodings are precomputed offline. That efficiency has an upstream training cost, though: encoding the full corpus during index refresh takes 10 hours (4.5ms/doc), the same cost whether it's ANCE's periodic refresh during training or a one-time inference-time index build.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

The internal ablation design is genuinely careful: every dense-retrieval baseline (Rand Neg, NCE Neg, BM25 Neg, DPR-style BM25+Rand, and their BM25-warm-up variants) shares the identical BERT-Siamese/RoBERTa-base architecture and loss, so the paper isolates negative construction as the variable, not a confound of a better encoder or more data. That is the paper's strongest methodological choice, and it is why the theory-to-experiment story (gradient norms, negative overlap, loss curves in Figure 4) is convincing on its own terms.

But the training-time cost the method trades away is understated relative to how central it is. Table 5 reports the "10h/4.5ms" corpus-encoding cost once, as an inference-time number, and never states it plainly as a recurring training tax: ANCE re-pays that same 10-hour corpus encoding every single refresh cycle (every 10k batches here), which is why the paper needs a whole second GPU pool (the 1:1 Trainer:Inferencer split) just to hide the latency — effectively doubling the training hardware footprint compared to a static-negative baseline. The appendix says a 1:1 split is "sufficient to minimize the influence of" the staleness gap between the index and the live model, but that claim is checked only at that one allocation; there's no reported curve of accuracy vs. GPU split or refresh interval, so a practitioner without Microsoft's compute budget has no evidence for how ANCE degrades with a less generous split.

The generalization story is also narrower than the headline numbers suggest, and this graph already contains the paper that shows it directly: BEIR (Thakur et al., 2021) evaluates ANCE zero-shot across 18 heterogeneous IR datasets with no in-task fine-tuning, and finds it needs a re-annotated TREC-COVID to climb from below BM25 to 6.7 nDCG points above it — i.e., on the original judgments, this MS MARCO-trained retriever's headline advantage over BM25 evaporates the moment the domain shifts. Every result in this paper — TREC DL, NQ, TQA, the commercial system — is in-domain: either MS MARCO-trained-and-tested, or (for OpenQA) warmed up from a DPR checkpoint trained on the same Wikipedia-QA family it's evaluated on. Nothing here tests whether the ANN-refresh recipe transfers, and BEIR is the direct answer that it transfers less well than the in-domain gap implies.

baselines

Unusually rigorous for a negative-sampling paper: every DR baseline (random, NCE, BM25, DPR-style, and their BM25-warm-up variants) shares the same encoder and loss, isolating negative construction as the sole variable. The sparse/cascade baselines (BM25, DeepCT, BERT Reranker) are the standard TREC DL 2019 field, not strawmen.

contamination

Not discussed. The OpenQA setup is inherited wholesale from Karpukhin et al. (2020), including its known BM25-selected "gold" positives and the later-documented train/test answer overlap in NQ/TriviaQA — a confound this paper doesn't re-examine before reporting EM gains on top of it.

what's missed

The recurring cost of periodic full-corpus re-encoding (10h per refresh) is reported only as an inference-time efficiency number, not framed as a training overhead requiring 2x the GPUs; the commercial-system gains (Table 3) are relative percentages with no absolute metric, baseline, or dataset detail; and every evaluation is in-domain — no zero-shot generalization test is attempted here, though BEIR later supplied one and found it wanting.

would raise confidence

An accuracy-vs-refresh-interval and accuracy-vs-GPU-split ablation (only 1:1 is reported); absolute metrics for the commercial-system evaluation; and — since it's now available — reporting ANCE's own BEIR zero-shot numbers alongside its in-domain ones rather than leaving that comparison to a later paper.

// bottom line

The diagnosis holds up well: negatives sampled from a fixed pool (random or BM25) really do go stale and stop contributing gradient signal, and the paper proves it both theoretically and empirically (the 15%→63%→100% overlap progression, the orders-of-magnitude gradient-norm gap). As a training recipe, ANCE earns its in-domain numbers — MRR@10, Recall@1k, and OpenQA coverage all move in the right direction versus DPR-style negatives, at real efficiency gains at inference time. The catch is that "hard negatives mined from your own domain's ANN index" is exactly the kind of fix that can look domain-specific in disguise, and the training cost (a second GPU pool, permanently) is real. BEIR — already in this graph — is the natural companion read: it's the zero-shot check this paper never ran on itself.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. Dense retrieval's underperformance vs. BM25 traces to a specific, provable cause: negatives sampled from a fixed pool (random or BM25 top-100) quickly become "too easy," their loss and gradient norm collapse toward zero, and they stop teaching the model — a formal consequence of importance-sampling theory, not just an empirical observation.
  2. ANCE fixes this by sampling negatives from an asynchronously-refreshed ANN index built on the retriever's own current checkpoint — a Trainer keeps training on the last-refreshed index while an Inferencer re-encodes the whole corpus and rebuilds the index in parallel, so training negatives track the model instead of going stale.
  3. The gains are concrete and consistent: MS MARCO MRR@10 0.330 vs. 0.311 for DPR-style negatives, Natural Questions Top-20 answer coverage 81.9% vs. DPR's 78.4%, and end-to-end reader EM of 46.0/57.5 (NQ/TQA) — the best of any system compared — while ANCE (FirstP/MaxP) is the only negative scheme that lets dense document retrieval close in on an interaction-based BERT-reranker cascade, at roughly 100x lower online latency.
  4. The mechanism has a real, recurring cost — re-encoding the whole corpus every refresh cycle, mitigated only by dedicating a second GPU pool to it — and every evaluation here is in-domain; BEIR, already a node in this atlas, later ran the zero-shot check this paper skipped and found ANCE's advantage over BM25 much shakier out of domain.
