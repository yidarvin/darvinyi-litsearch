Breaking the Exploration Bottleneck: Rubric-Scaffolded Reinforcement Learning for General LLM Reasoning

RLVR only learns from rollouts the model can already stumble into — so on open-ended tasks with no verifiable answer, exploration stalls before learning can start. RuscaRL injects checklist-style rubrics into the prompt itself as decaying "scaffolding" that widens what the model dares to try, then reuses those same rubrics as the reward that trains on what it found.

Yang Zhou, Sunzhu Li, Shunyu Liu, Wenkai Fang, Kongcheng Zhang, Jiale Zhao, Jingwen Yang, Yihe Zhou, Jianwei Lv, Tongya Zheng, Hengtong Lu, Wei Chen, Yan Xie, Mingli Song

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

Reinforcement Learning with Verifiable Rewards (RLVR) — the recipe behind DeepSeek-R1-style reasoning gains — works cleanly when correctness has a checkable answer: a math proof either verifies, a unit test either passes. But most real tasks aren't like that. Medical consultation, creative writing, and general instruction-following are open-ended: there's no single ground-truth string to check a rollout against, so conventional RLVR has nothing to attach a reward to.

A recent line of work addresses the reward side of this with rubric-based evaluation: decompose a good response into checklist criteria (factuality, completeness, tone), score each with an LLM-as-Judge grader, and aggregate into a scalar reward. That fixes exploitation — RL now has something to optimize — but the paper identifies a second, less-discussed problem underneath it: exploration. RL policy-gradient methods only ever learn from the rollouts they happen to sample. If the base model's capability boundary means it rarely or never produces a high-quality response to a given open-ended prompt in the first place, no reward function — however well-designed — can teach it what a high-quality response looks like, because the model never generates one to be rewarded. The paper calls this the exploration bottleneck: "what cannot be explored cannot be learned." Worse, RL training itself actively narrows exploration over time — policy entropy collapses as training proceeds, converging the model onto an ever-smaller set of reasoning trajectories, which is the opposite of what's needed to discover the good responses that were out of reach at initialization.

Existing fixes for RL exploration collapse — prolonging training, entropy bonuses, hybrid-policy methods — the paper argues only reshuffle probability mass within the base model's existing output distribution. They bias sampling toward already-reachable high-reward responses; none of them give the model access to reasoning patterns it couldn't already produce unprompted. The gap RuscaRL targets is genuinely expanding what a model can discover during rollout generation, not just re-weighting what it already could.

// how it works

The method

RuscaRL (Rubric-Scaffolded Reinforcement Learning) trains with Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO) — the value-model-free RL algorithm that estimates advantage by normalizing a group of G sampled rewards against their own mean and standard deviation — and uses the same rubric in two deliberately separate roles within one training loop.

Rubrics as a data structure. A rubric R = {c₁, …, c_N} is a set of verifiable criteria, each with signed points p_i (positive for desirable properties, negative for undesirable ones — e.g. "+6: explain agent, environment, reward" alongside "−7: confuse environment and reward roles"). A Grader LLM makes a binary true/false call per criterion; the paper's reward is the sum of triggered criterion points normalized by the total possible positive score, keeping the final reward mostly within [0, 1] with occasional negative values for badly wrong responses:

S = Σᵢ sᵢS_total // s_i = b_i · p_i (criterion satisfied × its points); S_total = sum of positive-point criteria

That reward function alone — call it "Rubric-based RL" — is the paper's own ablation baseline and is not new; several 2025 papers (HealthBench, Rubrics as Rewards, Reinforcement Learning with Rubric Anchors) already use rubric scores as RL rewards this way. RuscaRL's actual contribution sits on top of it: reusing the rubric a second time, as scaffolding injected directly into the rollout-generation prompt.

Rubrics as scaffolding. Borrowing the term from Vygotsky's Zone of Proximal Development in educational psychology — give a learner just enough structured help to bridge what they can't yet do alone, then withdraw it as competence grows — RuscaRL prepends a randomly-sampled subset of the rubric's criteria directly into the instruction during rollout generation, so the policy is effectively sampling from π_θ(o | q, R_S) instead of π_θ(o | q). Two mechanisms control how much scaffolding each rollout gets:

  • Intra-group scaffolding differentiation — within one GRPO sampling group of G responses, the i-th sample gets scaffolding ratio λᵢ = (G−i)/(G−1), so some samples in the group see heavy guidance and others see almost none, directly manufacturing the response diversity GRPO's group-relative advantage needs to avoid collapsing into homogeneous samples.
  • Inter-step scaffolding decay — across training, the overall scaffolding intensity follows a sigmoid schedule λ_step(t) = 1 / (1 + e^(α(t−t₀))), starting near full scaffolding and decaying to none, so the model is pushed to internalize the reasoning patterns it initially needed rubric hints to find, rather than staying dependent on them.

The two combine multiplicatively into a single per-sample scaffolding ratio λ_S,i = λ_step(t) · λᵢ. Critically, the policy's log-probability computation during the gradient update is based on π_θ(o | q) — without the scaffolding tokens — even though the rollout that produced o was sampled with scaffolding present, which is what allows the improvement to transfer to unscaffolded inference-time behavior rather than making the model permanently dependent on rubric hints in its prompt.

// what they found

The results

RuscaRL is trained on backbones from the Qwen2.5, Qwen3, and Llama-3 families and evaluated on medical (HealthBench-500, LLMEval-Med, MedQA, MedMCQA), writing (WritingBench, Creative Writing v3), and instruction-following (IFEVAL, IFBench) benchmarks. The headline result: on HealthBench-500, RuscaRL takes Qwen3-30B-A3B-Instruct from 46.9 → 61.1 (+14.2), which surpasses OpenAI-o3's 59.8 and every other closed- or open-source model the paper reports, despite Qwen3-30B-A3B being a much smaller, non-reasoning-tuned base model. Gains are largest on weaker base models: Qwen2.5-7B goes from 8.5 → 46.3 (+37.8) on HealthBench-500 and 32.0 → 56.2 (+24.2) on IFEVAL; Llama-3.1-8B (base, not instruct) goes from 0.0 → 25.8 on HealthBench-500. The paper notes gains on the closed-ended multiple-choice benchmarks (MedQA, MedMCQA) are comparatively marginal — expected, since RuscaRL's open-ended rubric training doesn't specifically target that format.

Against the direct ablations that isolate what scaffolding adds beyond the reward alone, RuscaRL beats plain Rubric-based RL (reward only, no scaffolding) consistently — e.g. 56.4 vs. 52.0 on HealthBench-500 with Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct — and also outperforms two prior exploration-focused RL methods it compares against directly, RL-Plus (hybrid-policy importance sampling) and Entropy-Augmented RL (entropy bonus shaping), on most of the eight benchmarks tested.

The ablations narrow down which pieces of the scaffolding design actually matter. For intra-group control, the proposed linear differentiation (56.4) beats binary all-or-nothing splits (52.0 for no-scaffolding-in-group, down to 44.7 for full-scaffolding-in-group) — matching partial guidance to each sample individually beats giving some samples everything and others nothing. For inter-step decay, sigmoid decay (56.4) clearly beats constant scaffolding (44.0), linear decay (43.0), and every power-decay variant tested (41.1–43.2) — the paper attributes this to sigmoid's smooth transition avoiding both under-scaffolded early training and prolonged late-training overfitting to the scaffolding tokens themselves. The best sigmoid hyperparameters found are α = 125 (decay steepness) and t₀ = 0.2 (decay midpoint), though the paper reports RuscaRL stays ahead of Rubric-based RL across a fairly wide hyperparameter range, degrading only at extreme settings.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

The core empirical claim — scaffolding-then-decay beats reward-only rubric RL — is tested with a real ablation ladder (Rubric-based RL, MeRF, RL-Plus, EA-RL, SFT, SFT+Rubric-based RL) rather than just comparing against the raw initial model, which is the right comparison to isolate the scaffolding contribution specifically. But several parts of the evaluation design deserve scrutiny before taking the headline "beats OpenAI-o3 on HealthBench-500" claim at face value.

baselines

The closed-source comparisons (OpenAI-o3, GPT-4.1, Gemini-2.5-Pro, DeepSeek-R1-0528) in Figure 1 are pulled in as reference points, not retrained or re-evaluated under the same protocol the paper controls — they're presumably taken from public reports or the paper's own zero-shot runs, which the text doesn't fully specify. The genuinely controlled comparison — RuscaRL vs. Rubric-based RL vs. MeRF vs. RL-Plus vs. EA-RL, all trained and evaluated by the same team under the same setup — is the trustworthy part of the results, and there RuscaRL's advantage is real and consistent. The "beats o3" framing in the abstract figure leans on the less-controlled comparison.

contamination

Not discussed in the paper. The training data is described only as drawn from HealthBench, WritingBench, and IFEVAL-family task distributions; there's no explicit decontamination check against the eval benchmarks' own held-out splits, though HealthBench-500 is described as "a randomly selected subset of 500 samples from HealthBench" used as a held-out evaluation set. Whether the RL training rubrics/prompts and the HealthBench-500 eval subset are drawn from genuinely disjoint pools isn't verified in text — this matters given how central HealthBench-500 is to the headline claim.

what's missed

The entire reward signal — during both training and Best-of-N evaluation — runs through an LLM-as-Judge grader (Qwen3-32B for the Best-of-N cost-controlled runs). That makes the whole pipeline's validity conditional on grader reliability, which the paper doesn't report calibrating against human judgment on this specific rubric set — it cites the grader-reliability literature (Zheng et al., Gu et al.) rather than measuring it here. A model that learns to satisfy a checklist as scored by another LLM is optimizing for "passes the grader's binary calls," which can diverge from "produces an actually better response" in ways that are exactly the reward-hacking failure mode rubric-RL is known to be susceptible to (a concern later explicitly investigated by a follow-up paper, "Reward Hacking in Rubric-Based Reinforcement Learning," that cites this one). The paper's own generalization claim is also tempered by its data: gains on the two closed-ended multiple-choice benchmarks (MedQA, MedMCQA) are consistently much smaller than on the open-ended benchmarks the method was designed and tuned for — sensible, but it caps how "general" the improvement really is beyond open-ended, rubric-gradeable tasks.

would raise confidence

A head-to-head under one grader and one prompt format for every reported baseline — including the closed-source frontier models in Figure 1 — would remove the asymmetry between the controlled ablation table and the headline chart. A human-rated subset comparing RuscaRL responses against Rubric-based RL responses blind would test whether the Best-of-N and validation-score gains reflect genuinely better answers or grader-specific reward hacking. And an explicit train/eval decontamination statement for the HealthBench-derived training data versus the HealthBench-500 held-out set would close the one gap the paper leaves implicit.

// bottom line

The controlled part of this paper is solid: against every ablation the authors run themselves under matched conditions, scaffolding-then-decay beats reward-only rubric RL, and the mechanism story (entropy rises then falls, Best-of-N ceiling rises) is internally consistent across three independent lines of evidence. The part to be more skeptical of is the "beats o3" framing that leans on less-controlled external comparisons and an LLM-graded reward whose own reliability isn't independently validated in this paper.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. RuscaRL separates a rubric's two possible jobs in RL — reward signal and exploration scaffold — and uses the same rubric for both, rather than treating rubric-as-reward (already established by prior work) as the whole story.
  2. The scaffolding is deliberately withdrawn over training (sigmoid decay) and never seen by the policy's own gradient computation, so the model is pushed to internalize reasoning patterns rather than becoming dependent on rubric hints at inference time.
  3. Gains are largest on the weakest base models (Qwen2.5-7B base: +37.8 on HealthBench-500) — consistent with the paper's exploration-bottleneck framing, since a weaker model's rollout distribution has the most room the scaffolding can open up.
  4. The ablations (linear > binary differentiation; sigmoid > constant/linear/power decay) do real work isolating that the scaffolding schedule matters, not just the presence of any external guidance.
  5. Every result runs through an LLM-as-Judge grader for both training reward and evaluation — read the headline numbers as "performance under this specific grader," not an independently human-validated ceiling.
