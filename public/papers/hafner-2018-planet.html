Learning Latent Dynamics for Planning from Pixels

PlaNet learns a Recurrent State-Space Model (RSSM) — a latent dynamics model with both a deterministic and a stochastic transition path — from raw pixel observations alone, then chooses actions by planning directly inside that latent space with the Cross Entropy Method. On six image-based continuous-control tasks it reaches performance close to (and on one task, above) the strongest model-free algorithm, D4PG, while collecting on average about 200x fewer episodes.

Danijar Hafner, Timothy Lillicrap, Ian Fischer, Ruben Villegas, David Ha, Honglak Lee, James Davidson — Google Brain, DeepMind, Google Research, University of Michigan, University of Toronto

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

Planning is a powerful way to make decisions when the dynamics of the world are known — it lets an agent search over futures rather than merely react to the present, and its performance scales with the compute you're willing to spend searching. The catch is that almost nothing interesting has known dynamics: to plan in a real or simulated environment, the agent first has to learn a model of that environment from experience. Learning a dynamics model that is accurate enough to plan with — as opposed to merely accurate enough to look plausible — has been a long-standing obstacle, especially when the only observations are raw pixels rather than a physics engine's internal state.

Prior latent-space planning methods such as Embed to Control and Robust Locally-Linear Controllable Embedding had shown the idea works, but only on toy problems: balancing a simulated cartpole or steering a two-link arm, with dense rewards and a Markov (fully-observed) assumption baked into the model. Meanwhile, model-free algorithms like A3C and D4PG could solve much harder continuous-control tasks from pixels, but needed enormous amounts of environment interaction — on the order of 100,000 episodes — to get there, since they must propagate reward signal backward through many steps of Bellman backups rather than reasoning forward from a model. The gap PlaNet targets is squarely in the middle: can a learned latent dynamics model be made accurate enough to plan with directly, on tasks that actually have contact dynamics, partial observability, and sparse rewards — while using dramatically less data than the model-free alternative?

// how it works

The method

PlaNet (the Deep Planning Network) is a purely model-based agent: it has no policy network and no value network. Instead it learns three things from its own past episodes — a transition model, an observation (image) model, and a reward model, all operating in a compact latent space — and then, at every real environment step, re-plans from scratch by searching over thousands of candidate action sequences evaluated entirely inside that latent space (model-predictive control). Because the reward model predicts rewards directly from the latent state, the planner never has to render a single pixel during search, which is what makes evaluating thousands of rollouts per control step computationally feasible.

The Recurrent State-Space Model (RSSM)

The paper's central design finding is about what kind of latent transition function is actually good enough to plan with. A purely deterministic model (an RNN, Figure 2a) predicts one future crisply, but that same crispness lets a planner exploit its inevitable inaccuracies — and it can't represent that a given action might lead to more than one plausible future. A purely stochastic state-space model (Figure 2b) can represent multiple futures, but a chain of sampled stochastic variables struggles to reliably carry information — like "the cart is currently off-screen to the left" — across many time steps. The RSSM (Figure 2c) splits the latent state into both parts at once: a deterministic sequence hₜ (implemented as a GRU with 200 units) that carries history forward exactly, and a 30-dimensional diagonal-Gaussian stochastic state sₜ conditioned on hₜ that captures uncertainty over the future:

deterministic:  ht = f(ht−1, st−1, at−1)     stochastic:  st ∼ p(st | ht)     observation:  ot ∼ p(ot | ht, st)     reward:  rt ∼ p(rt | ht, st) // the RSSM recurrence (Eq. 4): every step advances a deterministic GRU state hₜ and draws a fresh stochastic latent sₜ conditioned on it

The whole model is trained end-to-end from encoded image sequences using a variational bound (a reconstruction term for images/rewards plus a KL term regularizing the approximate posterior toward the transition prior) — essentially a sequential VAE. The paper additionally proposes latent overshooting: the standard bound only trains the model to make good one-step predictions, but a planner needs to trust predictions many steps into the future. Latent overshooting generalizes the training objective to include KL terms between the multi-step-ahead prior and the informed posterior at every distance up to the planning horizon, entirely in latent space (no extra image decoding required) — though the paper notes their final RSSM configuration does not strictly require it to reach its reported results (Appendix D).

To choose actions, PlaNet runs the Cross Entropy Method (CEM), a population-based, gradient-free search: starting from a diagonal Gaussian belief over an H = 12-step action sequence, it samples J = 1,000 candidate sequences, evaluates each by unrolling the learned model and summing predicted rewards, refits the belief to the best K = 100 sequences, repeats for I = 10 iterations, and executes only the first action of the winning mean sequence before replanning from the new observation. Data is collected iteratively: starting from S = 5 random seed episodes, the partially-trained model plans one more real episode every C = 100 gradient updates, with small Gaussian exploration noise added to each chosen action.

// what they found

The results

PlaNet is evaluated on six image-based continuous-control tasks from the DeepMind Control Suite — cartpole swing-up, reacher, cheetah running, finger spinning, cup catching, and a walker that must first stand up — each observed only as 64×64×3 pixel images, with challenges ranging from a sparse catch-only reward (cup) to contact dynamics (finger, walker) to a camera that can lose the agent entirely (cartpole). Against the model-free baselines reported by Tassa et al. for the same suite, PlaNet reaches final performance close to D4PG (100,000 episodes, from pixels) and clearly ahead of A3C (100,000 episodes, from proprioceptive/non-pixel state) — using only 1,000 episodes of its own:

On Cheetah Run, PlaNet's final score of 662 actually beats D4PG's 524 outright, despite using 100x fewer episodes. On the other five tasks it lands close to D4PG's final performance (and close to the estimated upper bound of planning with the true simulator instead of a learned model) while needing anywhere from 40x to 500+x fewer episodes to get there; averaged across tasks the paper reports roughly 200x less environment interaction than D4PG for comparable final performance, at similar or lower wall-clock training time (10–20 hours on a single Nvidia V100 GPU).

A second ablation (Figure 5) isolates how much the CEM search itself matters: replacing PlaNet's iterative CEM refitting with "random shooting" (evaluate 1,000 candidate sequences once, no iterative refit) or with a fixed random data-collection policy both learn substantially slower, confirming that both a good model and iterative planning contribute to the final sample-efficiency numbers — not just the model.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

The headline "200x fewer episodes" number is real and the ablations backing it (Figures 4 and 5) are honest about what's driving it — but the comparison hides a cost that shows up at decision time rather than training time. PlaNet has no policy network: every single environment step triggers a fresh CEM search that samples J = 1,000 candidate 12-step action sequences and iteratively refits them over I = 10 rounds — 10,000 latent rollouts of horizon 12, from scratch, at every control step, for the entire episode's duration. The paper's "similar or less computation time" claim is about total training wall-clock (10–20 hours on one V100), which is a fair like-for-like number against A3C/D4PG's training time, but it obscures that a large share of that budget is spent on repeated online search rather than amortized into a cheap forward pass the way a policy network would be. That distinction is exactly what the Dreamer line of successors (built by the same lead author and already covered in this atlas) later fixes, by training an explicit actor-critic on trajectories imagined inside the same kind of RSSM instead of replanning from scratch every step.

baselines

A3C and D4PG were the strongest published model-free results on the DeepMind Control Suite at the time and D4PG in particular is a fair, current, pixel-based comparison — but neither is model-based, so the comparison is "planning vs. model-free," not "this planner vs. the best possible model-based competitor." Concurrent model-based methods (e.g. PETS, published the same year) aren't compared head-to-head here.

contamination

Not applicable in the usual sense — these are simulated continuous-control environments (DeepMind Control Suite) rather than a static text/eval corpus, so there's no train/test leakage risk, though results are necessarily specific to that simulator and its reward functions.

what's missed

Per-step planning cost (not just total training time) isn't reported in a directly comparable "actions per second" or "FLOPs per decision" number, so it's hard to judge how this would scale to real-time robotics. The action repeat R is also hand-tuned per task (from R=2 for walker/finger up to R=8 for cartpole) — a per-task hyperparameter that undercuts a "one recipe for many domains" story, which is precisely the property DreamerV3 later delivers with one fixed configuration across 150+ tasks.

would raise confidence

Reacher and Cup Catch show wide 5th–95th percentile bands in Figure 4, meaning PlaNet itself sometimes fails badly on individual seeds even though the median looks strong — success rates or seed-by-seed breakdowns (rather than only medians) on the sparse-reward tasks would make the efficiency claim easier to trust. A wall-clock or FLOPs comparison of planning-time compute against a trained policy's inference cost would also directly substantiate (or temper) the "similar computation time" claim.

// bottom line

The RSSM's core empirical claim — that a latent model needs both a deterministic and a stochastic path to be accurate enough to plan with — is well-supported by controlled ablations, and the sample-efficiency numbers against D4PG are real and specific. But this is a snapshot of one point on a trade-off curve: PlaNet buys sample efficiency by moving cost from training-time environment interaction to decision-time search, and that specific trade-off is what its own direct descendants (Dreamer → DreamerV3) were built to eliminate by learning a policy instead of re-planning forever.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. The Recurrent State-Space Model (RSSM) combines a deterministic path (an RNN state that reliably carries information across time) with a stochastic path (a latent variable that represents multiple possible futures) — the paper's ablations show a model with only one or the other is dramatically worse for planning, not just slightly worse.
  2. Because rewards are predicted directly from the latent state, planning never has to generate images — this is what makes it tractable to evaluate 10,000 candidate rollouts per real environment step using the Cross Entropy Method, entirely in latent space.
  3. PlaNet matches or beats D4PG's final performance on continuous control from pixels using roughly 200x fewer episodes on average — but it pays for that with a compute-heavy online search at every single decision, a cost its architectural descendant Dreamer removes by training an explicit policy on imagined rollouts instead.
