Constructing A Multi-hop QA Dataset for Comprehensive Evaluation of Reasoning Steps

2WikiMultiHopQA pairs Wikipedia article summaries with Wikidata knowledge-graph triples so every one of its 192,606 multi-hop questions carries an explicit evidence chain — a set of (subject, relation, object) triples tracing the reasoning path — not just the sentence-level supporting facts HotpotQA offered.

Xanh Ho, Anh-Khoa Duong Nguyen, Saku Sugawara, Akiko Aizawa

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

By 2020, four datasets required a model to read multiple documents to answer a question: ComplexWebQuestions and QAngaroo were built by pairing web/Wikipedia text with a knowledge base, but neither dataset records why the answer is correct — there is no annotation explaining the reasoning path from question to answer. HotpotQA closed part of that gap with sentence-level supporting facts: a justification (which sentences the model needs) but not an introspective explanation of how those sentences combine into an answer. R4C added both kinds of explanation in semi-structured natural language, but at only 4,588 questions it is too small to train an end-to-end multi-hop model.

The deeper problem is that HotpotQA's supporting-fact supervision does not stop models from answering via shortcuts rather than genuine multi-hop reasoning. Min et al. (2019) showed many of HotpotQA's comparison questions are solvable single-hop, once you exploit type-consistent surface patterns in the distractor paragraphs. Sugawara et al. (2018) — co-authored by this paper's senior author — had already shown that many reading-comprehension datasets contain a large fraction of "easy" questions answerable from just the first few words. And Jia and Liang (2017) demonstrated that appending one adversarial distracting sentence collapses strong SQuAD readers' F1 from 75.4% to 36.4%, evidence that models were substantially exploiting lexical overlap rather than comprehension. Put together: existing multi-hop datasets under-specify the reasoning chain, and the ones with sentence-level justification alone can't verify a model actually composed multiple facts rather than pattern-matched its way to the answer.

// how it works

The method

2WikiMultiHopQA is built from the intersection of two sources: a paragraph — the summary section of a Wikipedia article describing an entity — and Wikidata, a structured knowledge base of (subject, relation, object) statements about that same entity. Every question in the dataset is generated from one or two Wikidata triples whose subject/object entities are required to actually appear as text spans inside the corresponding Wikipedia paragraphs — a grounding constraint that keeps the KB-derived facts checkable against real prose, not just KB lookups. The dataset's headline addition is evidence: the specific triples used to derive the answer, given alongside the question, context, and sentence-level supporting facts (SFs). Figure 1 shows this directly — the same reasoning-path fact that a HotpotQA-style annotation would leave as two supporting sentences is instead recorded as two explicit triples chained through a shared bridge entity.

The dataset has four question types. Comparison questions compare two same-category entities on a shared property (e.g., which of two people was born first). Inference questions combine two triples (e, r₁, e₁) and (e₁, r₂, e₂) via a hand-authored logical rule into an unseen relation — the paper's example is father(a,b) ∧ father(b,c) ⇒ paternal-grandfather(a,c) — so the surface question reads naturally ("Who is the paternal grandfather of X?") while still requiring the two-hop composition. Compositional questions chain two triples through a bridge entity without a named logical relation, and bridge-comparison questions combine both bridge-finding and comparison, needing up to four gold paragraphs. Bridge-type (compositional/inference) generation follows the pipeline in Figure 2: from an entity e, filter Wikidata statements by a first relation to get 1-hop candidates H₁, then filter again to get 2-hop tuples (e, r₁, e₁, r₂, e₂) in H₂ — subject to the constraint that paragraph p (describing e) must mention e₁, and p₁ (describing e₁) must mention e₂, while neither paragraph leaks the other hop's answer entity.

Templates were built manually and semi-automatically: 799 compositional templates from 13 first-relation × 22 second-relation property combinations across 15 entity types, 62 bridge-comparison templates, and a small set of comparison/inference templates pruned of any pattern Min et al. (2019) had flagged as producible single-hop. Distractor paragraphs reuse HotpotQA's recipe — bigram tf-idf retrieval of the top-50 similar paragraphs, then the top-8 (or top-6 for bridge-comparison) by entity-type match, shuffled together with the gold paragraphs into a 10-paragraph context. The result is 192,606 questions (154,878 train-medium / 12,576 train-hard / 12,576 dev / 12,576 test, split by a single-hop model's per-question difficulty score), covering 708 distinct answer types drawn from Wikidata's instance of property — dominated by yes/no (31.2%), date (16.9%), film (13.5%), human (11.7%), and big city (4.7%).

The baseline model extends Yang et al. (2018)'s HotpotQA bi-attention reader with a new evidence-generation branch (the orange block in Figure 3): it reuses the existing bi-attention output to first predict the two triples' relation, then their subject and object spans, on top of the shared question/supporting-fact encoder.

// what they found

The results

Running the exact same (unmodified) HotpotQA baseline model on both datasets side by side, 2WikiMultiHopQA comes out harder on the metrics that matter: answer EM/F1 of 34.14 / 40.95 versus HotpotQA's 44.48 / 58.54, and joint EM/F1 of 9.22 / 26.76 versus HotpotQA's 10.97 / 40.52 — even though the supporting-fact scores are actually a bit higher on 2WikiMultiHopQA (26.47 EM / 66.94 F1 vs. 20.68 / 65.66). Since human performance is comparable across both datasets, the authors read the lower joint score as evidence their dataset packs in proportionally more difficult questions. A second, more targeted probe follows Min et al. (2019): a single-hop BERT reader (which cannot combine two paragraphs) scores 64.6 F1 on HotpotQA but only 55.9 F1 on 2WikiMultiHopQA — an 8.7-point drop the authors take as confirmation that a larger share of their questions actually require chaining two paragraphs rather than being answerable from either one alone.

The 100-sample human study is the sharpest result. The human answer F1 upper bound of 91.79 is a "relatively small gap" against the 98.8 HotpotQA borrows from its own paper — but the evidence-generation task shows the widest human/model split of any sub-task: 78.81 human-UB F1 versus just 16.74 F1 for the model, and 0.00% exact match for the model across every question type in the breakdown by type (Table 6), including compositional questions where the model still reaches only 2.57% evidence EM. Sentence-level SF prediction, by contrast, is nearly solved-looking (65–69 F1) precisely because — the authors note — it is "incapable of evaluating the reasoning and inference skills of the model": a binary per-sentence classifier can score well without composing anything. The authors attribute part of the evidence task's low score to Wikidata name ambiguity (a person can have several valid aliases; the ground truth uses only one), rather than model reasoning failure alone.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

The evidence-triple annotation is a real methodological advance: it turns "did the model use the right sentences" into a formally checkable "did the model produce the right (subject, relation, object) chain," which is exactly the granularity Min et al. (2019) and Sugawara et al. (2018) argued sentence-level supporting facts couldn't provide. But it only partially closes the reasoning-shortcut gap it's aimed at. The evidence field verifies which triples a model surfaces, not that it composed them rather than pattern-matched a template — a model can still, say, spot that a "date of birth" slot wants a date-shaped span in one of two paragraphs without ever traversing the bridge entity, exactly the kind of comparison-question shortcut HotpotQA had. And the evidence metric itself is brittle: exact-match on Wikidata's canonical entity strings means alias variation (the paper's own example: "Lord William Beauclerk" vs. other valid names) silently converts correct reasoning into a scored failure, with no relaxed/alias-aware variant offered to separate the two failure modes.

The template-generation process is also the dataset's biggest latent risk. Every question is assembled from a closed, hand-authored vocabulary — 799 compositional templates, 62 bridge-comparison templates, entity types capped at the top-50 most popular on Wikipedia — rather than paraphrased by crowdworkers the way HotpotQA's questions were. That means a model (particularly one pretrained on web-scale text that has since ingested this dataset, its GitHub repo, or papers describing its templates) has a fixed, learnable question surface to key on, and the paper never tests a template-holdout split (train on some templates, evaluate on unseen ones) to check whether reported scores would survive that. Its own baseline is also dated relative to its November 2020 publication: the headline joint-metric numbers (Table 5) come from Yang et al. (2018)'s pre-BERT bi-attention reader, with BERT used only narrowly, as in Min et al. (2019), for the single-hop shortcut probe rather than as the main answer/SF/evidence baseline — so the reported 66.69-point human-vs-model joint-F1 gap likely overstates how far from "solved" the task would look under a contemporaneous transformer reader.

baselines

A single pre-BERT bi-attention/RNN reader (extended from Yang et al. 2018) is the only full baseline reported; BERT appears only as a narrow single-hop shortcut probe, not as a competing full-task baseline, despite BERT-class readers already being standard by late 2020.

contamination

Questions are generated from a closed template vocabulary (799 + 62 + smaller comparison/inference sets) over a fixed Jan 2020 Wikipedia / Dec 2019 Wikidata snapshot — a learnable surface form with no template-holdout evaluation to rule out pattern memorization.

what's missed

The evidence metric is strict exact-match against Wikidata's canonical strings, so alias/naming variance (acknowledged by the authors) can misclassify correct reasoning as wrong; the joint metric multiplies three sub-scores, so tiny per-task shortfalls compound into a near-zero scalar (0.35% test joint EM) that undersells the more informative per-task numbers.

would raise confidence

An alias-aware or relaxed evidence-match metric; a template-holdout split to test generalization beyond the training template distribution; and a contemporaneous BERT/RoBERTa-based full baseline (not just a narrow shortcut probe) to give a fairer floor-to-ceiling read.

// bottom line

2WikiMultiHopQA's evidence-triple idea is a genuine step past HotpotQA's sentence-level justification, and the same-baseline HotpotQA-vs-2Wiki comparison is a clean piece of evidence that the new dataset resists shortcutting somewhat better. But "resists better" isn't "resists" — the template-closed construction and brittle exact-match evidence metric mean the dataset's real difficulty, and how much of its human/model gap survives a modern reader, is still an open question the paper's own experiments don't settle.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. The headline contribution is evidence: a chain of Wikidata (subject, relation, object) triples that formalizes the reasoning path from question to answer, going beyond HotpotQA's sentence-level supporting facts toward something closer to a checkable proof trace.
  2. At 192,606 questions across four templated types (comparison, inference, compositional, bridge-comparison), it's substantially larger than HotpotQA (~113K) and two orders of magnitude bigger than the small, evidence-annotated R4C (4,588 questions) it's most directly benchmarked against.
  3. The same HotpotQA-era reader scores lower on 2WikiMultiHopQA's joint metric (9.22 EM / 26.76 F1) than on HotpotQA itself (10.97 / 40.52) despite comparable human ceilings — real evidence the dataset demands more genuine multi-hop reasoning, though its closed-template construction and pre-BERT-only baseline leave open how much of that gap a modern reader, evaluated on held-out templates, would actually close.
