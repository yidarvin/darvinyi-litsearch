2018 · AAAI · Benchmarks & Evals

Deep Reinforcement Learning that Matters

Ten controlled experiments on TRPO, DDPG, PPO, and ACKTR show that random seed alone, or swapping in "the same" algorithm's other public codebase, or a network of (400,300) instead of (64,64), can move continuous-control results as much as — or more than — the algorithmic novelty a paper is claiming credit for.

Peter Henderson, Riashat Islam, Philip Bachman, Joelle Pineau, Doina Precup, David Meger · McGill University / Microsoft Maluuba

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

By 2017, deep RL papers routinely reported that a new method beat TRPO, DDPG, or PPO by some margin — but the literature also reported a wide range of numbers for the same baseline algorithms across different papers (a companion workshop paper by the same first two authors, Islam et al. 2017, had already flagged this). The standard reporting practice was a plot or table of average return from a handful of trials — the paper's own literature survey (Table 7) finds contemporaneous work using as few as 3 trials (Schulman et al.'s PPO), or reporting only the best of several runs rather than the average (Mnih et al.'s A3C used the top-5 of several trials; Wu et al.'s ACKTR used the top-2 or top-3) — with no significance test attached to any of it. Nobody had measured how much of a claimed improvement could be explained away by non-determinism the reader can't see: which random seed was drawn, which of several "reference" codebases implemented the baseline, what network architecture was quietly chosen, or how the reward signal happened to be scaled. Without that accounting, a reader has no way to tell whether a new algorithm's reported gain is real or just noise the authors didn't have to disclose.

// how it works

The method

The paper doesn't propose a new algorithm; it runs a controlled variance decomposition on four existing continuous-control policy-gradient methods — TRPO (Schulman et al. 2015), DDPG (Lillicrap et al. 2015), PPO (Schulman et al. 2017), and ACKTR (Wu et al. 2017) — mostly via the OpenAI Baselines implementations, on MuJoCo continuous-control tasks from OpenAI Gym (chiefly Hopper-v1 and HalfCheetah-v1, chosen to contrast stable vs. unstable dynamics, plus Walker2d-v1 and Swimmer-v1 for the cross-environment comparison). Every configuration is run for 5 trials with a shared preset set of random seeds and reported as mean ± standard error over 2M environment timesteps, unless the experiment is itself testing seed variance (in which case 10 trials are run and split into two groups of 5).

Six factors are varied one at a time while holding the rest fixed: network architecture and activation (three MLP sizes — (64,64), (100,50,25), (400,300) — crossed with tanh, ReLU, and Leaky ReLU); reward scale (multiplying the environment reward by a factor from 1e-4 to 100, with and without layer normalization in DDPG); random seed alone with every hyperparameter held fixed; choice of evaluation environment; codebase/implementation of the nominally same algorithm (three separate TRPO releases, three separate DDPG releases); and finally whether standard significance-testing machinery — a two-sample t-test, a Kolmogorov-Smirnov test, and a bootstrap percent-difference with 95% confidence intervals (10,000 bootstrap iterations, pivotal method) — can actually separate a real algorithmic gain from this noise floor.

// what they found

The results

Architecture and activation: Tables 1–2 report final average return ± standard error across the (64,64) / (100,50,25) / (400,300) × {tanh, ReLU, Leaky ReLU} grid. For PPO on HalfCheetah-v1, the (400,300) policy network scores −1180 ± 444 — a losing policy — while (64,64) scores 2201 ± 323 and swapping the activation to ReLU with (64,64) reaches 2971 ± 364: architecture choice alone spans a >4,000-point range and flips the sign. A footnote quantifies why: the KL divergence of updates under the (400,300) network averages 33.52× higher than under (64,64) — a "bigger network" silently changes how much the trust-region step actually moves the policy each update.

Reward scale: Figure 3 rescales DDPG's reward by σ̂ ∈ {1e-4, …, 100}. Below σ̂ = 0.01, HalfCheetah-v1 learning fails to progress at all, and layer normalization changes which scales fail — meaning this single scalar, rarely reported as a tuned hyperparameter, can be the difference between learning at all and not (the paper notes some Gym tasks, e.g. HumanoidStandup-v1, have native rewards in the millions, making the "right" scale environment-dependent).

Random seed alone: the paper's sharpest result. Ten TRPO-on-HalfCheetah-v1 trials, identical hyperparameters, split into two groups of five and averaged — nothing differs but which five seeds landed in which group. The resulting learning-curve distributions are different enough that a two-sample t-test across the full training distribution gives t = −9.0916, p = 0.0016: two runs of "the same" experiment are statistically distinguishable from each other. Table 7's survey of how many trials the field actually averages (3–9 for PPO, 5 for TRPO/DDPG, top-N cherry-picks for A3C and ACKTR) suggests most published comparisons never had the statistical power to rule this out.

Codebase: holding the algorithm and hyperparameters fixed and only swapping which public implementation runs it, three TRPO codebases (the original Schulman et al. 2015 release, the OpenAI Baselines version used in the 2017 PPO paper, and rllab's TensorFlow port) produce visibly different HalfCheetah-v1 learning curves — the original code trending toward ~2,000 average return by 2M steps while the other two plateau far lower and noisier. The same pattern holds for three DDPG codebases (rllab, rllab++, OpenAI Baselines). Nothing about "TRPO" or "DDPG" as algorithms changed; only which engineering team's reimplementation ran them.

DDPG wins HalfCheetah-v1 by a wide margin, then collapses to worst-or-near-worst on Hopper-v1 and Walker2d-v1 — its off-policy exploration noise causes early failures in unstable dynamics, and the paper shows DDPG on Hopper effectively learns the degenerate optimum of surviving to the trajectory limit rather than actually hopping. TRPO dominates Swimmer-v1 (214 vs. 107/50/31) largely by finding a "curl up and flail" local optimum worth ~130 return rather than genuine swimming. TRPO and PPO have the tightest, most stable bootstrap intervals across all four environments; DDPG and ACKTR the widest. As a worked example of why this matters: ACKTR nominally beats DDPG on Walker2d-v1, but the actual test gives t = 1.03, p = 0.334; KS = 0.40, p = 0.697; bootstrapped percent difference 44.47% (−80.62%, 111.72%) — not significant, despite the higher mean.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

This paper is itself a meta-evaluation-methodology contribution, so the honest move is to turn its own lens on its own eval choices — and on whether continuous-control RL actually changed its habits afterward.

First, the sample sizes: the paper's own headline result (Figure 5) shows that ten trials, split 5-and-5, can already yield a statistically significant t-test between two runs of the literally identical configuration. Yet every other sweep in the paper — the entire architecture/activation grid in Tables 1–2, the reward-scale sweep in Figure 3, the codebase comparison in Figure 6 — uses only 5 trials per cell, the same order of magnitude the paper just showed can itself produce spurious-looking divergence. The paper reports mean ± standard error for these, not the bootstrap/significance apparatus it built for the seed experiment, so a reader can't tell how much of, say, the 33.52× KL-divergence architecture effect is itself noise at N=5 versus a real, reproducible effect. The paper acknowledges the open problem directly ("there can be no specific number of trials specified as a recommendation... more investigation is needed") but doesn't retrofit its own biggest tables with the statistical rigor it's arguing the field needs — a plausible product of 2017-era compute budgets (each MuJoCo run is a non-trivial number of GPU/CPU-hours multiplied across a large grid), but a limitation nonetheless.

Second, scope: the main sweeps run on just two tasks (HalfCheetah-v1, Hopper-v1), and even the cross-environment section (Figure 4, Table 3) covers only four low-dimensional MuJoCo tasks — none of the higher-dimensional ones (Ant, Humanoid) where the paper's own logic (bigger networks, different reward scales, more unstable dynamics) would plausibly bite harder. Third, the significance toolkit itself is exploratory: the paper proposes the t-test, KS-test, and bootstrap CI as an "initial set of metrics based on results from ML" while explicitly flagging that "the assumptions pertaining to the underlying data with corrected metrics do not necessarily apply in RL" — RL learning curves are autocorrelated time series across training, not the i.i.d. samples classical significance tests assume, and the paper doesn't validate that its proposed tests have correct error rates under that violation.

On adoption: this is a paper whose entire thesis is a call to change field-wide reporting norms, so it's fair to check whether the norms actually moved, using this atlas's own neighboring nodes. Fujimoto et al.'s TD3 (2018), which cites this paper directly, visibly absorbed the lesson — it re-runs every baseline (including a re-tuned "Our DDPG") under one common harness across seven environments with 10 random seeds each, exactly matching the trial count this paper's Figure 5 showed was necessary to even attempt statistical separation. Haarnoja et al.'s SAC (2018) also cites this paper directly as related work on "hyperparameter and implementation sensitivity" — yet SAC's own reported experiments still use only five random seeds per condition with min/max shading, not the confidence intervals or significance tests this paper spent its whole back half building. Schulman et al.'s PPO (July 2017) predates this paper by two months and couldn't have responded — it reports 3–9 seeds with a single mean curve and no variance shown at all, exactly the practice Table 7 catalogs. The net picture: a hugely cited (2,400+) call for reform that some direct descendants (TD3) adopted in full and others (SAC) cited approvingly while only partially implementing — evidence that citing a reproducibility critique and actually practicing it are two different things.

baselines

Covers the four dominant 2017-era continuous-control policy-gradient methods (TRPO, DDPG, PPO, ACKTR) via OpenAI Baselines plus spot comparisons against original/rllab codebases — a reasonable sample for its moment, though it necessarily predates SAC, TD3, and other post-2017 continuous-control algorithms that the field would go on to scrutinize the same way.

contamination

N/A in the leakage sense (no training-data benchmark to contaminate); the closest analogue is presentation risk — Table 7 documents the field's own top-N-of-M cherry-picking (A3C's top-5, ACKTR's top-2/3), and the paper's chosen default hyperparameters/environments, while plausible, aren't shown to be free of similar selection.

what's missed

Only 5 trials for every sweep except the dedicated seed experiment (which used 10 and still needed a significance test to separate); only two tasks for the main grid and four total for the cross-environment comparison, all low-dimensional; and no validation that the proposed significance tests (t-test, KS-test, bootstrap) hold their nominal error rates against RL's autocorrelated learning curves.

would raise confidence

Bootstrap/significance-annotated versions of the big architecture and codebase tables (not just mean ± SE); at least one higher-dimensional environment (Humanoid, Ant) to test whether the sensitivity findings generalize past low-dimensional MuJoCo; and a synthetic-data check that the proposed tests have correct type-I error under known autocorrelation before recommending them field-wide.

// bottom line

The diagnosis is airtight and its influence is real — 2,400+ citations and a visible shift in how some direct successors (TD3) report results. But the paper's own fixes were only partially load-bearing: most of its own experiments use the same modest trial counts it flags as risky, its significance-testing toolkit is offered as a first pass rather than a validated instrument, and citation by later work (SAC) didn't guarantee adoption of the very practices being cited. A landmark reproducibility critique still had to compete with compute budgets and inertia — including, in places, its own.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. Ten trials of literally the same TRPO/HalfCheetah-v1 configuration, split 5-and-5 by random seed, produce statistically distinguishable learning curves (t = −9.0916, p = 0.0016) — before any hyperparameter, codebase, or algorithmic change enters the picture at all.
  2. Network architecture ((64,64) vs (400,300)), activation function, reward scale, and which public codebase implements "the same" algorithm can each swing final average return by thousands of points or flip its sign — variance the field's typical 3–9-trial, no-significance-test reporting (Table 7) can't distinguish from a genuine algorithmic gain.
  3. No single policy-gradient algorithm wins across all four tested MuJoCo environments (DDPG tops HalfCheetah-v1, TRPO tops Swimmer-v1 via a local optimum), so a paper's chosen benchmark suite alone can manufacture a "clear winner" narrative.
  4. The paper's own reform — more trials, real significance tests, full disclosure of hyperparameters/codebases — was adopted unevenly by the very papers that cite it: TD3 (2018) matched its 10-seed standard in full, while SAC (2018) cited the critique but kept 5 seeds and no significance test, showing that influence and adoption aren't the same thing.
