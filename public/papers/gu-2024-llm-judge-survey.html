A Survey on LLM-as-a-Judge

By late 2024 "LLM-as-a-judge" had become the field's default grader — GPT-4 scoring essays, ranking chatbots, and reward-modeling RLHF runs — under a dozen different names (grader, critic, verifier, examiner, reward model) with no shared definition. This survey gives the paradigm a formal notation, a four-question taxonomy of 226 references, a catalogued bias/adversarial-robustness bestiary, and a hands-on meta-evaluation experiment — then keeps revising itself, six times over, as reasoning-model judges arrive.

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

Before LLMs, evaluation forced a trade-off: expert human judgment was comprehensive but slow, expensive, and inconsistent; automatic metrics like BLEU and ROUGE were scalable and consistent but blind to anything beyond lexical overlap, failing badly on open-ended generation. "LLM-as-a-Judge" — prompting a strong model to grade another model's output — promised to merge the two: expert-level, context-sensitive reasoning at automatic-metric scale. By late 2024 it had become the field's default grader (MT-Bench, Chatbot Arena, RLHF reward pipelines, rubric-based evals all lean on it), but the authors identify two problems that had gone unaddressed while the paradigm scaled. First, there was no systematic review: the same underlying mechanism was studied and named independently as a Grader, Evaluator/Assessor, Critic, Verifier, Examiner, or Reward/Ranking Model, with no shared formal definition tying the literature together. Second — and more consequential — reliability was mostly asserted rather than measured: papers reported that a judge "correlates with human preference" without a standardized way to isolate whether that judge is actually trustworthy, biased, or brittle to adversarial inputs.

The gap the authors target, then, is not a missing benchmark but a missing map plus a missing yardstick: a formal definition of what LLM-as-a-Judge is, a taxonomy of how it is built and used, and — the paper's stated central question — a principled answer to "how to build reliable LLM-as-a-Judge systems?"

// how it works

The method

As a survey, the "method" is the taxonomy itself — 226 references read and reorganized around four foundational questions: what is it, how to use it, how to improve it, how to evaluate it (plus applications, challenges, and future work). The organizing device is a formal definition that treats any LLM-as-a-Judge system as an auto-regressive scoring function over an input and a context:

E ← PLLM(x ⊕ C) // E = the evaluation (score / choice / label / sentence); x = the item being judged; C = the prompt/context; ⊕ = however x and C are combined

Because the basic pipeline alone says nothing about whether the output is trustworthy, the authors layer a second, reliability-explicit definition on top — R ← fR(PLLM, x, C), where fR bundles the consistency, bias-mitigation, and robustness checks that turn a raw judge into a validated one. Every technique catalogued in the rest of the paper is classified as an instance of one of the basic pipeline's four stages: In-Context Learning (how the prompt frames the task — generate a score on a 1–5 or 1–10 scale, answer yes/no, run a pairwise comparison, or pick from multiple choices), Model Selection (a general closed- or open-source LLM used zero-shot, versus a model specifically fine-tuned as a judge, e.g. on instruction/evaluation/critique triples), and Post-processing (how the raw output — special tokens, token-level logits, or extracted sentences — is converted into a final numeric or categorical verdict).

Section 3 ("how to improve it") then catalogues technique families for making judges more reliable — prompt design (few-shot exemplars, decomposing the evaluation into steps or criteria, shuffling response order to fight position bias, converting the task into a more tractable format), capability enhancement (fine-tuning a dedicated evaluator like PandaLM or JudgeLM, or iterative self-refinement), and final-output optimization (multi-round self-consistency, voting across multiple judge LLMs, or hierarchical multi-agent debate frameworks such as ChatEval). Section 4 turns the lens onto the judges themselves, organizing evaluation-of-the-evaluator into three axes — agreement with human judgments, bias, and adversarial robustness — each with its own metrics and benchmark catalogue.

// what they found

The results

The breadth is the headline: 226 references reorganized into a taxonomy spanning definitions, usage patterns, improvement strategies, evaluation methodology, applications (core ML tasks plus finance, law, and AI4Science), open challenges, and a ten-item future-work agenda. Section 4.2's bias catalogue alone distinguishes task-agnostic biases (diversity/demographic bias, cultural bias, self-enhancement bias — a judge favoring its own family's outputs) from judgment-specific biases unique to comparison tasks (position bias, "compassion-fade" / model-name bias, style and sentiment bias, length/verbosity bias, and concreteness/authority bias, where a judge rewards citations and numbers regardless of whether they're correct). Table 1 catalogues twelve existing meta-evaluation benchmarks for judging judges, from MT-Bench (80 pairwise samples, 3 bias types measured) and Chatbot Arena Conversations (~30k crowdsourced pairs) up to CALM, which purpose-built an automated perturbation mechanism covering 12 distinct bias types across 4,356 samples — the widest single instrument in the table.

Beyond cataloguing, Section 4.4 runs the survey's own meta-evaluation experiment rather than just summarizing others'. Rather than collecting new labeled data, the authors combine two existing benchmarks — LLMEval2 (2,553 pairwise samples with human preference labels) for human-alignment, and EvalBiasBench (80 samples covering 6 bias types: length, concreteness, empty-reference, content-continuation, nested-instruction, familiar-knowledge) for bias, plus a position-swap procedure over both — and score six judge LLMs (GPT-4-turbo, GPT-3.5-turbo, Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct, LLaMA3-8B-Instruct, Mistral-7B-Instruct-v0.3, Mixtral-8×7B-Instruct-v0.1) at temperature 0. A later revision (the paper is up to arXiv v6) folds in four reasoning models — gemini-2.0-thinking, o1-mini, o3-mini, and deepseek-r1 — extending the same table forward as the field moved to reasoning-centric judges.

The table's most consequential number is one the survey doesn't headline: on the LLMEval2 samples where the human label is a genuine tie, every tested judge does badly (Table 3). GPT-4-turbo aligns with the human tie only 29.73% of the time; gemini-2.0-thinking, despite topping several other columns, collapses to 13.20%; even the best performer, o3-mini, tops out at 42.31% — meaning the field's strongest judge still misreads a majority of true ties as a decisive win for one side. Reasoning models improve raw human-agreement and bias-robustness over the 2024 open models on the model1-vs-model2 comparisons, but none of them fix this older, better-known failure mode: LLM judges default to picking a winner even when there shouldn't be one.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

Judge this as a survey, not a benchmark paper — and on that basis it does something rare: it keeps updating. The arXiv history shows six revisions between the original November 2024 posting and October 2025 (v6), and the most recent one visibly extends the Section 4.4 meta-evaluation table to include gemini-2.0-thinking, o1-mini, o3-mini, and deepseek-r1 — models that didn't exist at first submission. That is a genuinely uncommon thing for a survey to do (compare Chang et al.'s "A Survey on Evaluation of Large Language Models," frozen since its ninth 2023 revision, or "Judging the Judges," a one-shot empirical snapshot from mid-2024); it means the taxonomy's bones (the four-question structure, the bias catalogue) have stayed a live reference rather than decaying into a historical artifact within a year, which is the realistic shelf life for anything in this subfield.

That said, the paper's own empirical contribution is smaller than its framing suggests. The abstract and conclusion both describe "a novel benchmark designed for this purpose," but Section 4.4 is explicit that the "meta-evaluation tool" is a recombination of two pre-existing benchmarks — LLMEval2 and EvalBiasBench — plus a standard position-swap procedure, run once across six (later ten) off-the-shelf judges. No new human-labeled ground truth is collected, and the six-model comparison uses a single run at temperature 0 with no repeated-sampling variance estimate, so small gaps between adjacent judges (e.g. GPT-4-turbo's 61.54% vs o3-mini's 61.66% human agreement) shouldn't be read as meaningfully different. The taxonomy sections, by contrast, are a genuine synthesis contribution and hold up well as a map of the literature through late 2025.

The more striking gap is editorial, not empirical: the survey's own Table 3 shows every judge — including the newly added reasoning models — badly misclassifying genuine ties (13–42% accuracy, worse than several other columns in the same table), yet this doesn't surface as a takeaway anywhere in the paper's own challenges or future-work sections. A reader skimming Section 4.4's prose walks away with "GPT-4 outperformed other LLMs with a large margin," which is true on the columns the authors chose to narrate but buries the specific failure mode (tie-blindness) that's most actionable for anyone building a judge-based leaderboard today.

baselines

The taxonomy's source coverage (226 references) is broad and current for a Nov 2024–Oct 2025 window. The empirical Section 4.4 comparison is much thinner: six original judges plus four reasoning models added later, each run once, with no repeated-sampling error bars — adjacent scores (61.54 vs 61.66) shouldn't be over-read.

contamination

Not directly addressed for the survey's own experiment: LLMEval2 and EvalBiasBench are both pre-existing, publicly released benchmarks the judge LLMs may have partially seen in training or fine-tuning data, and the paper doesn't audit for this.

what's missed

The survey's own headline framing undersells its most useful empirical finding — that tie-detection (13.2%–42.3% accuracy) is the worst-performing axis in its entire meta-evaluation table, including for reasoning models — instead leading with the less surprising "GPT-4-turbo wins on average."

would raise confidence

A versioned or living companion benchmark (rather than a point-in-time PDF table) so "current best judge" claims can be checked against fresh model releases without waiting for the next arXiv revision; repeated-sampling variance on the Section 4.4 comparison; and an explicit tie-detection metric promoted alongside human-agreement and bias-robustness rather than left inside a secondary table.

// bottom line

The right paper to cite for "what is LLM-as-a-Judge and what biases does it have" — the four-question frame and the bias/adversarial-robustness taxonomy are comprehensive and, unusually for a survey, have been kept current through six revisions rather than frozen at first release. Treat the "novel benchmark" claim skeptically — it's a recombination of existing data, not new reliability ground truth — and don't assume its own headline numbers surface every finding worth knowing: the tie-detection failure buried in Table 3 is arguably more useful to a practitioner than the "GPT-4 wins" framing the prose leads with.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. The first systematic review of LLM-as-a-Judge: a formal definition (E ← PLLM(x⊕C), extended to a reliability-explicit R ← fR(PLLM,x,C)), 226 references organized around four questions — what is it, how to use it, how to improve it, how to evaluate it — and a bias taxonomy splitting task-agnostic biases (diversity, cultural, self-enhancement) from judgment-specific ones (position, length/verbosity, concreteness/authority, style, compassion-fade).
  2. Its own meta-evaluation experiment (combining LLMEval2 + EvalBiasBench, not new data) found GPT-4-turbo and reasoning-model judges (o3-mini, gemini-2.0-thinking) clustering around 61% human-agreement and 75–80% position-bias consistency, well ahead of open 7–8B judges like LLaMA3-8B-Instruct (50.72% / 38.85%) — but every judge tested, reasoning models included, badly misclassifies genuine ties (13.2%–42.3% accuracy), a failure the survey's own prose doesn't headline.
  3. Unusually for a survey, it's a living document: six arXiv revisions from November 2024 to October 2025, the latest of which extends its own benchmark table to reasoning-model judges — evidence that a taxonomy in this subfield needs active upkeep to stay current, and a rare case of a survey actually doing so rather than freezing at first release.
