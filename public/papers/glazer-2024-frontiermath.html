FrontierMath: A Benchmark for Evaluating Advanced Mathematical Reasoning in AI

Hundreds of brand-new, unpublished mathematics problems written and vetted by more than 60 research mathematicians, graded entirely by code instead of a human proof-reader — and every 2024 frontier model still failed more than 98% of them.

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

By late 2024 the standard mathematics benchmarks for language models had a saturation problem. GSM8K and the MATH dataset — the two most-cited testbeds for mathematical reasoning — are built from elementary and competition-style problems, and frontier models were closing in on the ceiling: by the authors' own accounting, o1 was hitting 94.8% on MATH, Claude 3.5 Sonnet was hitting 96.4% on GSM8K, and the college-mathematics subset of MMLU was at 98.1% for o1. A benchmark that nearly every leading model already aces can no longer discriminate between them, and it certainly can't track future progress — everyone converges on the same near-100% score and the benchmark goes quiet.

Efforts to write harder problems by hand existed already, and they show why a hand-curated fix doesn't scale either. The Advanced Reasoning Benchmark (ARB) combines university-level mathematics with contest problems spanning undergraduate to early-graduate topics; the GHOSTS dataset assembles 709 problems at the graduate level with an emphasis on natural-language interaction and human-expert grading rather than automated checking. Both raise the difficulty bar, but both still lean on either existing course material (which a large enough model may have seen in some form) or on human graders to assess free-form answers, which is exactly the bottleneck that keeps a benchmark from being run cheaply and repeatedly against every new model release.

The obvious fix — write harder and new problems — runs into a second problem: contamination. Olympiad-style benchmarks solve the ceiling issue by drawing on harder source material, but that material is old and public. OlympiadBench compiles 8,476 problems from mathematics and physics olympiads; PutnamBench draws its problems from the decades-running, publicly archived Putnam Competition; Omni-MATH pulls 4,428 problems from international competitions (including the IMO) and Art of Problem Solving forums. All of this is exactly the kind of text a large pretraining corpus is likely to contain, in whole or in close paraphrase, so a high score can reflect memorization as much as reasoning. Putnam-AXIOM tried to route around this by programmatically renaming variables and constants in existing Putnam problems — and the resulting drop is itself evidence that contamination was doing real work in the original numbers: o1-preview's accuracy falls from 50% on the original problems to 34% on the renamed variants. But renaming variables doesn't manufacture a harder problem, only a differently-labeled old one. The AI Mathematical Olympiad (AIMO) commissions genuinely novel olympiad-level problems, but restricts official evaluation to open-weight models, which rules out testing the closed frontier systems that define the state of the art. And the formal-proof route — MiniF2F's 488 problems, hand-formalized in Lean, Isabelle, and Metamath — trades contamination risk for a narrower one: it measures competition mathematics rather than research-level material, and it demands models write in specialized proof languages most were never trained on (the authors note that Lean's mathlib library doesn't even fully cover the undergraduate curriculum, let alone research mathematics).

What was missing was a benchmark that hit all three requirements at once: genuinely new problems that eliminate contamination by construction rather than by relabeling; genuinely hard problems that reach into research-level mathematics rather than competition tricks; and a grading process that is automated and scalable, so any model — including a closed system reachable only through an API — can be evaluated without a human reading a proof. FrontierMath is Epoch AI's attempt to hit all three at once, and the comparison below is why the first attempt looks convincing: on every other benchmark in the chart, the best-performing model already solves at least 60% of the problems (and often 74–98%); on FrontierMath, more than 98% go unsolved.

// how it works

The method

FrontierMath's problems come from a genuinely large expert-sourcing effort: more than 60 mathematicians across a dozen-plus countries, spanning graduate students to faculty, collectively holding 14 IMO gold medals, with one contributor holding a Fields Medal. Their combined specialties — algebraic geometry, number theory, algebraic topology, combinatorics, category theory, representation theory, PDEs, probability, differential geometry, logic, theoretical computer science — are what let the benchmark range from "hard competition problem" to "would occupy a subject-matter expert for days." The five publicly released sample problems make this concrete: a density computation about Artin's primitive-root conjecture conditional on GRH, a construction of a specific degree-19 polynomial via monodromy and the Riemann existence theorem, a p-adic continuation problem solved via the Skolem–Mahler–Lech theorem, a representation-theory orbit count via Coxeter relations, and a point count on a curve over 𝔽₅¹⁸ via the Weil conjectures.

Every submitted problem had to satisfy four requirements, and two of them are the interesting engineering decisions. Originality meant a problem had to be transformed or combined non-obviously enough that solving it required genuine insight rather than pattern-matching a known problem. Guessproofness is the sharper constraint: the authors target no more than a 1% chance of guessing the correct answer without doing most of the work a real solution requires — which is exactly why FrontierMath answers tend to be large, structureless integers or exact symbolic expressions rather than small round numbers a model could shotgun. Automated verifiability and computational tractability are what make grading possible at scale: an answer must be expressible as a Python/SymPy object (an integer, a symbolic expression, a matrix, a set), and any reference solution script must run in under a minute cumulatively on ordinary hardware. Where a problem has a unique answer, verification is an exact integer match or a SymPy zero-simplification check; where the answer isn't unique — a Diophantine tuple, a Hamiltonian path — a bespoke verification script checks the submitted object against the problem's real constraints, as in the Pell's-equation example below.

This pipeline is also what makes the evaluation harness tractable for closed, API-only models: no human ever reads a proof, so any model that can execute Python and dump an answer object can in principle be graded. Quality control layers on top of it. Every problem gets at least one blind peer review checking correctness, ambiguity, guessproofness, and the difficulty tags, and the authors additionally ran a second-review audit on a 35-problem subsample (30 chosen at random, plus the 5 problems pulled out entirely to serve as the public release). That audit found 2 of 35 problems had an author-provided answer that was simply wrong and had slipped past the first reviewer. Treating "a problem is wrong" as a Bernoulli trial and applying a Jeffreys prior gives a posterior error estimate of:

2 + 0.535 + 1 ≈ 6.9% // posterior error-rate estimate from the 35-problem second-review audit (Section 2.3); the authors round this up to ~10% once undetected second-review errors are accounted for

They note this isn't wildly out of line with error rates reported elsewhere — Northcutt et al. estimate over 6% label error in the ImageNet validation set, and Gema et al. find over 9% of MMLU questions contain errors — but on a benchmark where models solve well under 2% of ~300 problems, a non-trivial slice of the "unsolved" pile could in principle be wrong-as-stated rather than genuinely too hard. The second review also caught 6 problems with missing hypotheses (technically under-specified, though experts could likely infer intent) and 2 that failed guessproofness on closer inspection; all were revised before inclusion rather than dropped.

Difficulty is tracked along three author-assigned axes: Background (1–5, from high-school to research level), Creativity (estimated expert-hours to find the key idea, with no ceiling), and Execution (estimated expert-hours to grind out the computation once the idea is found, also uncapped). The five public sample problems span this range end to end, from Olli Järviniemi's research-level, 15-execution-hour number theory problem down to Alex Gunning's high-school/undergraduate Hasse–Weil problem — reproduced in the table below with the paper's own difficulty tags. The authors are candid that these ratings are soft: first- and second-reviewer difficulty ratings "rarely matched and often showed substantial differences," so a specific tier label is best read as a rough author impression rather than a calibrated measure.

On contamination prevention: contributors were asked to work over encrypted channels and keep written materials in password-protected archives, and the finished dataset was screened with the plagiarism tools Quetext and Copyscape — chosen over the more common Turnitin specifically because Turnitin would store submitted text in a database that could later be used to train a model — turning up no significant matches. In aggregate, the dataset leans heavily on number theory and combinatorics: by MSC2020 tag share, number theory is 17.8% of all classification tags and combinatorics 15.8%, together roughly a third of the total, with algebraic geometry and group theory next (about 14% combined), then algebraic topology, linear algebra, and special functions each in the 3–5% range. Counted by whole problems rather than tags, number theory touches 44% of all problems, combinatorics 39%, and group theory 22% — each of those subjects typically overlapping with a dozen-plus others (number theory and combinatorics co-occur in 13% of problems, the single most common pairing). Underneath that subject-level breakdown, the dataset draws on over 200 distinct solution techniques, and even the most common co-occurring pair of techniques still shows up together in at most 3 problems — a deliberately long-tailed, hard-to-template dataset by the authors' own analysis, built specifically so that no small set of "problem types" could be pattern-matched against.

// what they found

The results

Evaluation ran through an experiment-enabled harness rather than one-shot prompting: a model receives the problem, can write and execute Python in a loop, sees stdout/stderr fed back into its context, and keeps iterating up to a 10,000-token budget, after which a final prompt forces a best-guess submission via a specific pickle-dump convention. Six contemporaneous frontier models were evaluated this way on the full problem set: o1-preview, o1-mini, GPT-4o (2024-08-06), Claude 3.5 Sonnet (2024-10-22), Grok 2 Beta, and Gemini 1.5 Pro 002.

The headline number: on a single pass over the full benchmark, no model reached even 2% accuracy. That is the number behind the cross-benchmark comparison above — versus o1's 74% on AIME, 94.8% on MATH, and 98.1% on MMLU's college-math subset; Claude 3.5 Sonnet's 96.4% on GSM8K; o1-mini's 60.54% on Omni-MATH; and o1's 73.9% on MathVista.

Everything else is noisy — Grok 2 Beta solved problem 1 sixty percent of the time despite scoring 0% on problems 2–4, and Gemini 1.5 Pro solved problem 3 sixty percent of the time despite 0% everywhere else. Across these repeated trials o1-preview came out strongest overall, and it got there in a distinctive way: it averages just 1.29 responses per problem, largely committing to an answer immediately rather than experimenting, while Grok 2 Beta averages 3.81 responses. Token usage tells the same story — Gemini 1.5 Pro hits the 10,000-token ceiling on only 16.8% of questions and uses the fewest tokens overall (around 6,000/question), while Claude 3.5 Sonnet, GPT-4o, and Grok 2 Beta blow past the limit on more than 45% of their attempts, averaging 12,000–17,000 tokens.

One qualifier the authors add themselves: even a correct answer doesn't guarantee correct reasoning. On at least one solved problem, running a few simple simulations was enough to guess the right answer without real mathematical understanding — a reminder that on an automatically-graded benchmark, "correct answer" and "correct reasoning" aren't the same thing, even one engineered to be guessproof. Given that overall accuracy sits under 2%, though, the authors conclude such shortcuts aren't generalizing across the dataset.

What the mathematicians said

Beyond the quantitative evaluation, the authors interviewed four prominent mathematicians for their qualitative read on the benchmark: Terence Tao (2006 Fields Medal), Timothy Gowers (1998 Fields Medal), Richard Borcherds (1998 Fields Medal), and Evan Chen — an IMO coach who is also a co-author of the paper and contributed problems himself, alongside Tao. All four called the research-level problems exceptionally challenging. Tao's assessment of a sample set was blunt: "These are extremely challenging. I think that in the near term basically the only way to solve them, short of having a real domain expert in the area, is by a combination of a semi-expert like a graduate student in a related field, maybe paired with some combination of a modern AI and lots of other algebra packages." He separately estimated that, right now, guiding an AI system to a correct solution on a graduate-level FrontierMath question takes "about five times as much effort" as just solving it directly himself — though he expects that ratio to fall below one within a few years as tooling improves. Borcherds pushed back on the format itself, noting the benchmark's problems "aren't quite the same as coming up with original proofs" and that the requirement for a single numerical answer "feels somewhat contrived" relative to how mathematical research actually proceeds.

On the question of what an AI system that could solve these problems would be good for, Gowers offered a modest framing: such a system could serve as an assistant for "slightly boring bits of doing research where you, for example, make some conjecture that would be useful, but you're not quite sure if it's true... it could be a very, very nice time-saving device." Tao raised a practical caveat about compute cost that cuts against pure accuracy numbers: "if your amazing tool takes three days of compute off of all of Google to solve each problem... then that's less of a useful tool." All four generally agreed that human-AI collaboration would arrive well before full automation, and Tao specifically ventured that the benchmark would "resist AIs for several years at least."

// does it hold up?

The eval read

The engineering here is genuinely careful. Guessproofness as a design constraint, a verification pipeline that needs no human grader, an explicit error-rate audit with a Bayesian estimate rather than a hand-waved "we checked it" — this is a benchmark built by people who have thought hard about how math benchmarks fail. But three things temper how much weight the headline <2% number should carry, and one of them the paper itself doesn't disclose at all.

Start with what's disclosed. The 35-problem second-review audit is honest about its own limits: it covers roughly a tenth of the benchmark's few hundred problems, found a 2/35 undetected-error rate, and the authors round that up to an estimated 10% critical error rate once you account for errors even a second reviewer might miss. On a benchmark where the strongest model solves under 2% of problems, a 10% baseline error rate is not a footnote — it means a meaningful share of what gets scored as "the model failed to reason correctly" could instead be "the reference answer is wrong," and there is no way for an outside reader to tell which from the released aggregate numbers. The difficulty ratings (Background/Creativity/Execution) compound this: they're self-reported by the problem's own author, and the paper's own second-review pass found first- and second-reviewer ratings "rarely matched and often showed substantial differences" — so any claim along the lines of "this is a research-level, 15-hour problem" should be read as one mathematician's impression, not a calibrated difficulty score.

Then there's the part the paper does not disclose. The only place OpenAI appears in this paper is a single acknowledgments-footnote line: "We gratefully acknowledge OpenAI for their support in creating the benchmark." What that sentence doesn't say — and what only came out in a separate Epoch AI blog post published after the fact — is that OpenAI commissioned and funded 300 of the benchmark's problems and had early, exclusive access to both the problem statements and their solutions, with the single exception of a 50-question holdout set where OpenAI received only the statements. Epoch AI's own later admission is unusually blunt: they needed OpenAI's permission before publicly disclosing the relationship at all, and "we didn't clarify the data access and ownership agreement with OpenAI" even when they did disclose the sponsorship, around the time of the o3 announcement. That announcement is the crux of why this matters: on December 20, 2024 — about six weeks after this paper posted its <2% headline number — OpenAI announced that o3 scored 25.2% on FrontierMath, an order-of-magnitude jump that stunned working mathematicians. Whether that jump reflects a genuine reasoning leap, exposure (direct or indirect) to problems and solutions the model's own creator had funded and held in advance, or some mix of the two is precisely the question a reader cannot answer from the paper alone, because the paper never tells you the funding and access relationship existed.

Even setting the OpenAI question aside, the six-model comparison has a narrower issue: it's a single pass@1 run over the full benchmark for five of the six models, with 5-run repeats reserved only for the four problems that were already known to be solvable. That's a defensible way to save compute against a benchmark this expensive to run, but it means the paper's own "no model beats 2%" claim rests on a sample too small and too noisy to distinguish "model A is better than model B" from chance — a point the authors themselves flag, urging readers to treat the single-pass ranking "with significant caution."

To their credit, the authors are also unusually candid about what the benchmark structurally cannot measure, and this is worth separating from the OpenAI omission because it's a limitation they name outright rather than one a reader has to discover elsewhere. Because every problem must reduce to an automatically-checkable object, FrontierMath cannot include proof-writing or open-ended exploration — arguably the bulk of what research mathematicians actually spend their time on. And because problems are scoped to take an expert "hours" rather than the "weeks, months, or even years of sustained investigation" real research problems demand, the benchmark is, by the authors' own description, a test of concentrated technical problem-solving under a tight time box, not a test of the open-ended, exploratory, often-dead-ends-for-months character of research mathematics. Both caveats mean a high FrontierMath score — if and when one arrives — would demonstrate a specific, valuable, but narrower capability than "can do research mathematics."

baselines

Fair on paper — all six models are contemporaneous late-2024 frontier systems (o1-preview/o1-mini, GPT-4o 2024-08-06, Claude 3.5 Sonnet 2024-10-22, Grok 2 Beta, Gemini 1.5 Pro 002) evaluated through the same iterative code-execution harness. The weakness isn't the choice of models, it's that five of six were run only once each over the full set.

contamination

The novel-problems design genuinely defeats corpus-level contamination. But the paper's own contamination-prevention story has two cracks: a footnote admits standard (non-encrypted) email was used for a subset of problems outside the immediate team, and — undisclosed here — OpenAI had funded, early, and in most cases solution-level access to a third of the problem set months before this paper and the December 2024 o3 announcement.

what's missed

An estimated ~10% baseline error rate against a <2% solve rate; self-reported, poorly-inter-rater-reliable difficulty tags; a benchmark that by construction excludes proof-based and open-ended problems, so it measures a narrow slice of mathematical work (closed-form/numeric-answer construction and computation) rather than research mathematics broadly.

would raise confidence

Full second review of every problem, not 35 of a few hundred; explicit, paper-level disclosure of exactly which lab had which subset and when; multiple full-benchmark runs per model to put real confidence intervals on a number this close to zero; independent evaluation by a party with no funding relationship to any tested lab.

// bottom line

FrontierMath's verification pipeline and guessproofness rule are a real methodological advance over Olympiad-scraping benchmarks, and the <2% headline was a legitimately useful signal in November 2024 — right up until it wasn't a signal you could interpret on its own, because the paper doesn't tell you that its single largest funder had already seen a third of the questions and their answers. Treat any FrontierMath score you encounter as incomplete unless the source specifies whether it was run against the public sample, the OpenAI-funded 300, or the fully independent holdout.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. FrontierMath pairs hundreds of genuinely new, never-published problems spanning most of modern mathematics with a fully automated, no-proof-required verification pipeline — sidestepping both the contamination problem of Olympiad-scraping benchmarks and the human-grading bottleneck of formal-proof benchmarks like MiniF2F.
  2. The headline result — every 2024 frontier model under 2% accuracy, o1-preview topping out around 6% pass@8 — was a real, useful gap at publication, but it moved fast: OpenAI's o3 scored 25.2% about six weeks later, and the field only learned afterward that OpenAI had funded and had early access to a third of the problem set.
  3. Treat the 10%-ish estimated error rate, the self-reported difficulty tags, and the undisclosed OpenAI funding/access relationship as three separate reasons to read any specific FrontierMath score as a directional signal, not a precise, independently-verified measurement — and always check which problem subset a claimed score was run against.
