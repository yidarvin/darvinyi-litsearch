SimpleRL-Zoo: Investigating and Taming Zero Reinforcement Learning for Open Base Models in the Wild

DeepSeek-R1 claimed that long chain-of-thought reasoning emerges spontaneously from pure RL on a base model. But almost every open reproduction ran on Qwen2.5 — which already reflects before any training. This paper runs that recipe across 10 diverse base models, and finds the "aha moment" is real, model-dependent, and that response length alone proves nothing.

Weihao Zeng, Yuzhen Huang, Qian Liu, Wei Liu, Keqing He, Zejun Ma, Junxian He · HKUST · TikTok · Meituan

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

DeepSeek-R1 delivered a striking claim: start from a pretrained base model, apply reinforcement learning with a simple rule-based reward (right answer = +1), and long chain-of-thought reasoning together with self-reflection — the famous "aha moment" — emerge on their own, with no supervised fine-tuning in between. This paradigm became known as zero RL training. But the headline demonstration ran on DeepSeek-V3, a 671B-parameter model, and it was unclear whether the same magic survives on the generally smaller, weaker open base models everyone actually trains.

The open-source reproduction wave that followed had a blind spot: it converged almost entirely on the Qwen2.5 family. The trouble is that Qwen2.5 base models, thanks to heavy synthetic-data pretraining, already follow instructions and already exhibit reflective behaviors like backtracking and verification before any RL touches them. So a Qwen-based "zero RL" run cannot tell you whether RL is creating reasoning behavior or merely amplifying what was baked in at pretraining. Worse, these studies mostly tracked two superficial metrics — accuracy and response length — and a longer response can just as easily be repetitive gibberish as genuine deliberation.

This paper asks the questions the Qwen-only literature could not answer: (1) Does zero RL actually work on a representative zoo of base models, including weak instruction-followers? (2) Does a true "aha moment" emerge for models that don't start with self-verification? (3) What design choices decide whether zero RL succeeds or collapses?

// how it works

The method

The recipe is deliberately bare. Take 10 base models spanning families and sizes — Llama-3.1-8B, Mistral-v0.1-7B, Mistral-Small-24B, DeepSeek-Math-7B, Qwen2.5-0.5B/1.5B/7B/14B/32B, and Qwen2.5-Math-7B — and run GRPO directly on each, with no SFT cold start. Training data is only the GSM8K and MATH train sets (~8K problems), and the reward is pure answer correctness: +1 if right, 0 if wrong. Crucially, there is no format reward — no penalty for failing to wrap the answer in \boxed{} — a choice the paper argues is decisive (see §what they found). The same hyperparameters train all 10 models; weak instruction-followers get a simpler "reason step by step" prompt to avoid early instability.

GRPO (Group Relative Policy Optimization) is critic-free: instead of a learned value model, it samples a group of G responses per query and normalizes each reward against the group's mean and standard deviation to get an advantage. The authors use a length-rectified objective — they drop GRPO's original length-normalization term (which biases toward longer outputs), following concurrent work (DAPO; Dr.GRPO) and the default of their verl training codebase:

Âi = ri − mean(r1 … rG)std(r1 … rG) // group-normalized advantage — no value network; reward r ∈ {0, 1}. The policy is then updated with a clipped surrogate plus a KL penalty to a frozen reference.

The methodological contribution is really the instrumentation. Accuracy and length are kept, but the paper adds three deeper probes. A Reasoning Behavior Ratio uses GPT-4o (following the cognitive-behavior framework of Gandhi et al.) to classify whether each response actually performs Backtracking, Verification, Subgoal Setting, or Enumeration — measuring reasoning, not word count. A Clip Ratio tracks the fraction of responses truncated at the context limit (a tell-tale sign of repetitive collapse), and Average Stopped Length measures the length only of responses that terminated normally. Together these separate healthy reasoning growth from length inflation.

// what they found

The results

With only ~8K training problems, zero RL lifts every one of the 10 base models, and the gains reach competition-level benchmarks (AIME 2024, AMC 2023), not just the training distribution. The 6-benchmark averages below tell the story: the biggest absolute jumps come from Qwen2.5-Math-7B (37.2 → 59.5) and Mistral-Small-24B (27.6 → 49.6), while a weak base like DeepSeek-Math-7B more than doubles (11.3 → 29.2) — the paper notes its accuracy rises more than threefold after just 80 iterations as its responses grow from ~300 to over 1,200 tokens.

The "aha moment" is real — but only where it wasn't already there

This is the paper's headline finding, and it cuts against the prevailing narrative. By measuring actual cognitive behaviors (not length), the authors show the emergence story is model-specific. For Qwen2.5-7B and 32B, the frequency of verification and backtracking is high from the very first step and barely moves during training — these models already reflect; RL just buys them a little more thinking time. Their response length grows, but no new reasoning behavior emerges. Length, in other words, is a red herring.

The genuine "aha moment" — reflection appearing from scratch — shows up in the models that didn't start with it. In Mistral-Small-24B, verification and backtracking climb from nearly 0% to roughly 50% over training. The paper reports observing this emergence for the first time in small models outside the Qwen family, notably Llama-3.1-8B and DeepSeek-Math-7B. And the cautionary case: Mistral-7B-v0.1 inflates its response length too — but inspection reveals incoherent, mixed-language gibberish and a high clip ratio. Its length grows while its reasoning does not, exactly the failure mode that pure length-tracking would have mislabeled as success.

Not just reranking: pass@k genuinely improves

A standing skepticism about RL-for-reasoning (raised by DeepSeekMath) is that it only reranks answers the base model could already produce — it concentrates probability on good outputs without expanding the underlying capability. The paper pushes back with pass@k. On Mistral-Small-24B, a substantial gap between the base and the RL-trained model persists even at large k: from 13 to 30 absolute points as k ranges up to 128. By iteration 100, the model's pass@1 (26.4) already exceeds the base model's pass@8 (19.4), and the pass@1–pass@8 gap widens rather than closes during training. The authors read this as RL enhancing fundamental reasoning ability, not merely reordering a fixed candidate set.

Three things that break zero RL

The paper's most practically useful contribution is a clean catalog of what sabotages zero RL on weaker base models:

  • Strict format rewards penalize exploration. Forcing answers into \boxed{} and penalizing format failures (−1) wrecks training. Llama-3.1-8B's length explodes early as it struggles to obey the format, never learns to answer, and collapses; even the format-compliant Qwen2.5-7B degrades in later training. Reward verifiability, not rigid formatting.
  • Data difficulty must match the model. Mistral-7B-v0.1 collapses on hard data (MATH lv.3–5) — it rarely earns a positive reward, so length balloons with no accuracy gain. Qwen2.5-7B is the mirror image: on easy data its length and accuracy even shrink; it needs harder problems to keep reasoning deeper. Difficulty has to align with the base model's intrinsic exploration ability.
  • A traditional SFT cold start hurts. The pre-R1 default — SFT on short-CoT data before RL — boosts early performance but caps the ceiling. Using NuminaMath short-CoT SFT on Mistral-24B, more SFT steps mean lower final accuracy and length; at 500 SFT steps, verification and enumeration behaviors actively decline later in RL. Imitation suppresses the free exploration RL needs.

Generalization holds up too: trained on only 8K math examples, the models improve on out-of-domain benchmarks — IFEval, MMLU, and GPQA-Diamond. Mistral-Small-24B's average across those three rises from 25.0 to 55.3.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

The qualitative findings are the durable part of this paper, and they are well-instrumented. But the headline numbers carry the usual small-benchmark fragility, and a couple of the strong claims are more contested than the framing suggests.

The AIME noise problem is baked into the headline average. AIME24 is only 30 problems, so a single question is worth 3.3 points and Pass@1 is wildly noisy. The paper knows this — it reports both Pass@1 and Avg@32 — and the gap is enormous: Qwen2.5-Math-7B scores 40.0 Pass@1 but 24.0 Avg@32 on AIME (a 16-point swing), and Qwen2.5-32B scores 36.7 vs 27.2. Yet the headline "Avg." column in Table 1 is built from the noisier Pass@1, which mildly inflates the averages exactly on the most-cited competition benchmark.

The "aha moment" rests on an LLM judge. The central qualitative claim — verification rising from ~0% to ~50% — is GPT-4o classifying behaviors, not a human-audited reasoning trace. The authors do validate GPT-4o against human annotation in the appendix, which is the right move, but the headline emergence curves inherit whatever biases the judge has, and "50% of responses verify" is a classifier output rather than a guarantee of correct verification.

"Not just reranking" is a live debate, not a settled result. The pass@k argument is measured only to k=128. The very paper that later uses SimpleRL-Zoo's models as subjects — Yue et al.'s "Does Reinforcement Learning Really Incentivize Reasoning Capacity Beyond the Base Model?" — argues that at large enough k the base model catches up, i.e. RLVR sharpens the sampling distribution but does not expand the reasoning boundary. SimpleRL-Zoo and that critique are pointed in opposite directions; treat the reranking question as open.

baselines

Strong internal design — 10 base models, fixed hyperparameters, honest ablations. But base models use greedy decoding while trained models sample at temperature 1.0 / top-p 0.95, so Pass@1 is not a strictly controlled comparison; and there is no distillation-from-a-strong-teacher baseline, the regime where a long-CoT SFT cold start can actually win.

contamination

GSM8K test and MATH500 are held out but in-distribution with the GSM8K/MATH training sets, so part of those gains is in-domain. The clean out-of-distribution evidence is AIME24 / AMC23 / OlympiadBench / Minerva — and those genuinely improve, which is the load-bearing result.

what's missed

The headline average uses noisy AIME Pass@1 (up to 16 pts above Avg@32); the emergence story is a GPT-4o-judge label; the pass@k "real capability gain" is contested by a later citing paper at larger k. Math-only training, so the MMLU/GPQA generalization could partly be improved instruction-following rather than new knowledge.

would raise confidence

Report Avg@k everywhere (not Pass@1) on the small competition sets, human-audit a sample of the GPT-4o behavior labels, push pass@k well past 128, and add a distillation baseline to scope the "zero RL beats SFT" claim to the no-teacher regime where it actually holds.

// bottom line

The lasting value here isn't a leaderboard number — it's the negative result that the Qwen-only reproduction wave was studying an unrepresentative best case, plus a careful, honest map of what breaks zero RL: format rewards, mismatched difficulty, and SFT cold starts. The headline gains inherit small-benchmark noise and one genuinely contested capability claim, but the core qualitative lesson — reflection can emerge from scratch in non-Qwen models, and response length alone proves nothing — is the part that survives scrutiny.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. Zero RL works far beyond Qwen. GRPO with a pure correctness reward and only ~8K GSM8K/MATH problems lifts all 10 base models — Mistral-Small-24B's 6-benchmark math average goes 27.6 → 49.6, DeepSeek-Math-7B 11.3 → 29.2.
  2. Response length is a misleading signal; the "aha moment" is model-dependent. Qwen2.5 lengths grow with no rise in verification (they already reflected); Mistral-Small-24B grows verification/backtracking from ~0% to ~50% from scratch; Mistral-7B inflates length with multilingual gibberish.
  3. Three failure modes break zero RL on weak base models: strict format rewards (penalize exploration → collapse), training data whose difficulty doesn't match the model's exploration ability, and a traditional short-CoT SFT cold start (caps the ceiling, suppresses reflection).
  4. pass@k rises 13–30 points up to k=128 — the paper's evidence that RL improves reasoning rather than just reranking — but whether this survives at much larger k is contested by later work.
