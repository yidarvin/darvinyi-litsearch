Stochastic Backpropagation and Approximate Inference in Deep Generative Models

Rewrites a sample from a Gaussian latent variable as a deterministic function of its mean and covariance plus an independent noise draw, turning an otherwise non-differentiable sampling step into an ordinary node in a backprop graph — one of the two papers, alongside Kingma & Welling's contemporaneous "Auto-Encoding Variational Bayes," that independently invented the reparameterization trick underlying the VAE.

Danilo J. Rezende, Shakir Mohamed, Daan Wierstra — Google DeepMind, London

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

By early 2014, deep directed generative models with continuous latent variables — hierarchical models that generate data by sampling a latent code and pushing it through a non-linear decoder — were an obvious idea but a stubborn engineering problem. Training such a model by maximum likelihood requires integrating out the latent variables, which is intractable for anything beyond the simplest models, so you fall back to a variational lower bound and optimize an approximate posterior q(ξ|v) jointly with the generative model. The obstacle is that this objective is an expectation over a distribution whose own parameters you're trying to differentiate with respect to — and sampling is not a differentiable operation, so the ordinary backpropagation that trains every other layer of a deep network has nowhere to attach.

The existing toolkit for this problem was a patchwork of compromises. The wake-sleep algorithm alternates two separate, inconsistent objectives rather than descending a single free energy. Mean-field variational EM and INLA-style Laplace approximations scale poorly past a handful of hyperparameters. REINFORCE-style score-function estimators are unbiased and general but have variance that grows with the dimensionality of the latent space — expensive precisely where deep models need many latent units. None of these were both scalable to hundreds or thousands of latent dimensions and compatible with the ordinary SGD/backprop machinery already used to train the rest of the network. The paper sets out three explicit desiderata for a fix: the model has to be deep (to capture hierarchical structure), allow fast ancestral sampling, and be computationally tractable at scale.

// how it works

The method

The paper introduces Deep Latent Gaussian Models (DLGMs): a stack of L layers of Gaussian latent variables, where the topmost layer is drawn from a standard Gaussian and each lower layer's activation is a non-linear transformation of the layer above (an MLP) plus its own Gaussian noise, bottoming out in an observation likelihood over the visible data v. This single family already generalizes factor analysis, non-linear factor analysis, and non-linear Gaussian belief networks as special cases depending on the choice of transformation.

The real contribution is a method for computing gradients through the sampling step. The authors first derive two general "Gaussian gradient identities" (due to Bonnet's and Price's theorems) that let you push a gradient operator inside a Gaussian expectation. Then they show a cheaper, more practical route to the same result: any Gaussian sample ξ ~ N(μ, C) can be rewritten via a change of variables as a deterministic function of a parameter-free noise source,

ξ = μ + Rε,  ε ~ N(0, I),  C = RR⊤ // the reparameterization: sampling becomes an ordinary differentiable function of (μ, R) plus independent noise ε

Because ε is drawn independently of the parameters, the whole expression is now a differentiable computation graph node — gradients with respect to μ and R flow through it by ordinary backprop, exactly like any deterministic layer. The paper calls the resulting family of gradient rules "stochastic backpropagation."

On top of this, they specify an amortized recognition model q(ξ|v) — a second neural network that maps each data point directly to the mean and (rank-one-corrected) covariance of its approximate posterior, playing the role of a stochastic encoder. Both the generative model (the "decoder," parameters θg) and the recognition model (the "encoder," parameters θr) are trained jointly by descending a single free-energy objective — a KL-divergence-to-prior regularizer plus a reconstruction term — using one forward pass (bottom-up recognition, top-down generation) and one backward pass through both networks per minibatch. This is, structurally, the encoder/decoder training loop that the field would soon standardize as the Variational Autoencoder.

A practical wrinkle the paper spends real effort on: a full covariance matrix costs O(K³) per layer for a K-dimensional latent space. They introduce a rank-one-plus-diagonal parameterization of the precision matrix (C⁻¹ = D + uu⊤) that, via the Woodbury identity, lets both the KL term and the reparameterization factor R be computed and sampled in O(K) time — the difference between a toy demonstration and a model that scales to hundreds of latent units.

// what they found

The results

On binarized MNIST, the headline comparison is test-set negative log-likelihood (lower is better; Table 1 in the paper, numbers reproduced exactly below). A three-layer DLGM (two 200-unit deterministic layers, one 200-dimensional stochastic layer) with the rank-one covariance parameterization reaches 86.60 nats, versus 87.30 for the plain diagonal-covariance version — both comfortably ahead of factor analysis (106.00), the non-linear Gaussian belief network (95.80), and the wake-sleep algorithm (91.3), and competitive with strong 2014-era autoregressive density estimators such as NADE and EoNADE (85.10–90.69) and RBM/DBN baselines (86.34 / 84.55).

Beyond the headline number, the paper shows the rank-one covariance is doing real work, not just adding parameters: visualizing the true vs. approximate posterior for two-latent-dimension MNIST models shows the diagonal recognition model producing axis-aligned approximations that miss posterior correlation, while the rank-one model captures the correlation directly — exactly the gap that separates 87.30 from 86.60 nats.

The model also generates convincing samples well beyond MNIST: the 2D latent embedding of MNIST separates the ten digit classes into distinct regions, and the same architecture (with dataset-appropriate layer sizes) produces qualitatively good samples on NORB (24,300 96×96 object images), CIFAR-10 (8×8 patches from the 50,000-image RGB set), and the Frey faces dataset (~2,000 28×20 facial-expression images) — all trained with the identical stochastic-backpropagation recipe.

Finally, the paper demonstrates missing-data imputation and denoising on SVHN, Frey faces, and MNIST, under both Missing-at-Random (60% and 80% of pixels dropped) and Not-Missing-at-Random (a masked square region) conditions, by running a Markov chain that alternates resampling missing pixels and re-encoding through the recognition model — shown to converge to the true marginal over the missing pixels given the observed ones, with the residual uncertainty visible as digit identity occasionally flipping between plausible completions (e.g. a 9 resolving toward a 7) across resampling iterations.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

This paper's most consequential fact isn't in its results tables — it's a single sentence buried in section 3.2: "Concurrently with this paper, Kingma & Welling (2014) present an alternative discussion of stochastic backpropagation. Our approaches were developed simultaneously and provide complementary perspectives." That's the authors themselves flagging, in the paper, one of the cleanest cases of simultaneous independent discovery in modern ML: Kingma & Welling's "Auto-Encoding Variational Bayes" (arXiv:1312.6114) posted in December 2013, this paper (arXiv:1401.4082) posted six weeks later in January 2014, arriving at essentially the same mechanism — a reparameterized Gaussian latent variable, an amortized recognition network, and a joint encoder/decoder training objective — from largely independent starting points (Rezende et al. via Gaussian gradient identities rooted in Bonnet's and Price's theorems and prior work by Opper & Archambeau and Graves; Kingma & Welling via a more direct variational-Bayes derivation).

History has since assigned the two papers very different weight: Kingma & Welling's "VAE" framing became the field's canonical name and textbook citation (16,818 citations at the time this page was written), while this paper — despite being at least as general (the multi-layer DLGM stack, the explicit rank-one covariance trick for O(K) scaling, and gradient identities that extend beyond the Gaussian case) — sits at 5,702. That's not a small gap for two papers usually credited jointly as "the reparameterization trick," and it's worth remembering when a method gets attributed to a single name.

On the evaluation itself, judged by 2014 conventions rather than today's, it's solid but narrow. The only quantitative benchmark is binarized MNIST log-likelihood, and even there the comparison table splices together numbers computed by different groups under (presumably, but not verifiably here) matched preprocessing — the bottom half of Table 1 is explicitly sourced from Uria et al. (2014) rather than re-run by the authors, and MNIST binarization/log-likelihood estimation is known to be sensitive enough to preprocessing choices that cross-paper number-splicing of this kind should be read with a discount. NORB, CIFAR-10, and Frey results are qualitative-only — sample grids, no log-likelihood, no FID-style metric (none existed yet), so "looks good" is the entire evidentiary standard for three of the four datasets tested. The posterior-visualization comparison (figure 2) that motivates the rank-one covariance is a single two-latent-dimension toy setup, not systematically repeated across the higher-dimensional benchmark models.

baselines

Reasonable for 2014 — wake-sleep, factor analysis, RBM/DBN, and the strong NADE/EoNADE family — but the bottom half of the comparison table is borrowed from a different paper's numbers rather than re-run in-house under an identical protocol.

contamination

Not applicable in the modern sense (pre-web-scale-pretraining), but MNIST binarization itself is a preprocessing choice known to shift log-likelihood by several nats across implementations, undermining strict cross-paper comparability.

what's missed

No quantitative results for NORB/CIFAR/Frey (samples only); no multi-seed variance reporting beyond the single error-bar plot in figure 2(c); no discussion of posterior collapse, an issue that would dominate later VAE literature; only shallow (≤3-layer) architectures tested.

would raise confidence

Quantitative log-likelihood/ELBO numbers on all four datasets under one shared protocol; a systematic diagonal-vs-rank-one ablation across every benchmark rather than just the MNIST toy visualization; and — since it wasn't possible at the time — a head-to-head with Kingma & Welling's concurrent numbers on identical splits.

// bottom line

This is the paper that, together with Kingma & Welling's contemporaneous work, supplied the mathematical scaffolding — reparameterize the latent sample, amortize inference with a recognition network, train both jointly by ordinary backprop — that essentially every subsequent deep generative model builds on, from VAE variants to the Gaussian reparameterization tricks reused in Bayes-by-Backprop-style weight-uncertainty methods already in this graph. Its ideas won decisively; its name mostly didn't. A useful case study in how citation credit and technical priority don't always move together.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. The reparameterization trick — write a sample as ξ = μ + Rε with ε ~ N(0, I) drawn independently of the parameters — turns a non-differentiable sampling step into an ordinary node in a backprop graph, letting gradients flow jointly through a generative decoder and a recognition encoder.
  2. This paper and Kingma & Welling's "Auto-Encoding Variational Bayes" independently arrived at the same core mechanism within about six weeks of each other in late 2013/early 2014 — acknowledged by the authors themselves in the text — yet the two now sit at very different citation counts (5,702 vs. 16,818), a stark example of asymmetric credit for a tied discovery.
  3. On binarized MNIST, the rank-one-covariance DLGM reaches 86.60 nats test negative log-likelihood, beating wake-sleep (91.3) and factor analysis (106.00) and landing competitively alongside contemporary RBM (86.34) and DBN (84.55) baselines.
  4. The amortized "recognition model" introduced here — a single encoder network trained to map any data point directly to its approximate posterior, rather than re-solving inference per example — is exactly the encoder architecture that every subsequent VAE-family model still uses.
