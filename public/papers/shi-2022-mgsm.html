Language Models are Multilingual Chain-of-Thought Reasoners

The first benchmark for multilingual mathematical reasoning — 250 grade-school word problems hand-translated into ten typologically diverse languages — and the finding that a 540B model can chain-of-thought its way through them even in languages that make up under 0.01% of its pretraining.

Freda Shi, Mirac Suzgun, Markus Freitag, Xuezhi Wang, Suraj Srivats, Soroush Vosoughi, Hyung Won Chung, Yi Tay, Sebastian Ruder, Denny Zhou, Dipanjan Das, Jason Wei · Google Research

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

By late 2022, two research threads had matured in parallel but never met. One showed that prompting a large model with explicit chain-of-thought exemplars — worked-out reasoning steps before the answer — unlocked multi-step arithmetic and commonsense reasoning in English (Wei et al., 2022b). The other showed that multilingual pretrained models could do respectable NLP across typologically distant languages. But the multilingual benchmarks of the day — cross-lingual QA, natural-language inference, lexicon induction — asked for recognition, not reasoning: a single retrieval or classification step, not a chain of dependent arithmetic operations.

So a basic question had no answer: can a language model that reasons step-by-step in English do the same in Swahili, Bengali, or Telugu — languages that barely appear in its training data? Or is "reasoning" really just an English-language skill dressed up as a general one? There was no dataset to even ask the question. The authors build one. MGSM (Multilingual Grade School Math) is, to the authors' knowledge, the first multilingual benchmark for the multi-step arithmetic reasoning of language models, and the vehicle for a surprising empirical claim: that reasoning ability, once it emerges with scale, transfers to languages the model has almost never seen.

// how it works

The method

MGSM is built by manual translation, not machine translation. The authors took the first 250 problems from the GSM8K (Cobbe et al., 2021) test set — English grade-school word problems that each need 2 to 8 reasoning steps in the official solution — and hired paid professional translators (native speakers with at least two years of experience) to render every problem into ten typologically diverse languages: German, French, Spanish, Russian, Chinese, Japanese, Thai, Telugu, Bengali, and Swahili. Translators signed a machine-translation non-usage declaration; a separate translator and an n-gram overlap check against popular MT toolkits verified that nobody quietly ran the problems through Google Translate. The numeric answers were kept as Arabic numerals across all languages, so the target output is identical regardless of the question's language.

On top of the data sit four ways to prompt the model, which together separate "can it reason in this language?" from "can it route the problem through English?":

// the four solution formats

DIRECT — predict the answer with no intermediate steps (the no-reasoning floor). NATIVE-COT — reason step-by-step in the question's own language. EN-COT — keep the question in its native language but write the reasoning in English. TRANSLATE-EN — machine-translate the problem into English with the Google Translate API, then solve it with an English chain of thought.

These are crossed with three choices of few-shot exemplar — all-native questions, all-English questions, or a generic multilingual mix — and everything is run few-shot (6 exemplars where the context budget allows) with greedy decoding (temperature 0). The headline evaluation uses two of the strongest few-shot models of the era, GPT-3 (text-davinci-002) and PaLM (8B / 62B / 540B); a later section adds Codex (code-davinci-002). Crucially, the four formats let you read off where any multilingual ability actually comes from: NATIVE-COT measures genuinely in-language reasoning, EN-COT measures reasoning that "thinks in English" about a foreign question, and TRANSLATE-EN measures what you get by simply offloading the language problem to a translation system first.

// what they found

The results

The first finding is that reasoning steps matter enormously and scale gates them. With DIRECT prompting both models solve under 20% of problems; with chain-of-thought, PaLM-540B jumps to a 48.1% average (NATIVE-COT) and a 55.0% average in its best setting (TRANSLATE-EN). In those stronger settings it clears 40% in every language tested (TRANSLATE-EN bottoms out at 49.6% on Telugu); only the harder NATIVE-COT format dips below, to 35.2% on Swahili. Smaller models get almost nothing from CoT: neither GPT-3 nor PaLM shows a substantial solve rate until a certain size (text-davinci-001 for GPT-3, PaLM-62B for PaLM), so the authors frame multilingual reasoning as an emergent ability that appears only past a scale threshold.

The second — and the paper's signature result — is that performance is strikingly flat across language frequency. For PaLM-540B with NATIVE-COT, the four underrepresented languages (Swahili, Bengali, Telugu, Thai) average 44.9%, only about three points below the six high-resource languages at 47.9% — despite Swahili and Bengali each accounting for less than 0.01% of PaLM's training tokens. Thai, Telugu, and Bengali reasoning lands roughly on par with French, Japanese, and Chinese. The authors read this as evidence that, past the emergence threshold, reasoning is not primarily bottlenecked by how much data the model saw in a given language; the model transfers reasoning competence across the language barrier.

Two more patterns shape how the field would later use MGSM. Reasoning in English beats reasoning in-language: EN-COT consistently matches or beats NATIVE-COT (51.3 vs 48.1 average for PaLM-540B), so the paper recommends English intermediate steps as a strong default baseline for multilingual reasoning work. And the abilities generalize beyond math: with just four chain-of-thought examples, PaLM-540B sets a new state of the art of 89.9% on the XCOPA causal-commonsense benchmark — about 14 points over the prior best (a RoBERTa-Large translate-test system) — while Codex trails PaLM by 9 points. The transfer is not universal, though: on the XL-WiC word-sense benchmark, chain-of-thought gives no improvement over direct prediction, which the authors attribute to the task's rationales being short and example-specific rather than genuinely multi-step.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

As an artifact, MGSM has aged extremely well — it is still the canonical multilingual math benchmark, reused everywhere from DeepSeekMath and DeepSeek-V3 to newer multilingual suites. But the empirical claims rest on a thinner foundation than the clean plots suggest, and a careful reader should keep three things in view.

First, the headline numbers ride on a 250-example test set with no confidence intervals. The signature claim — "underrepresented languages are only 3% behind high-resource ones" (44.9% vs 47.9%) — is a difference of a few problems out of 250 per language, reported from single greedy decodes with no significance test. A three-point gap on n=250 is well inside the noise band, so "no strong correlation with language frequency" is a reasonable reading of the data but is asserted more firmly than the sample size licenses. Second, the best setting is partly a translation result, not a reasoning one: TRANSLATE-EN (the 55.0% average) Google-Translates the problem into English and then reasons in English, so it measures Google Translate + English CoT, not the model's native multilingual reasoning. The genuinely multilingual settings sit lower (NATIVE-COT 48.1, EN-COT 51.3), and the fact that EN-COT beats NATIVE-COT hints the model is leaning on English internally even when the surface language is Bengali. Third, only two closed model families are tested, and the flagship — PaLM-540B — was never public, so the central "emergence" curve could not be independently reproduced at the time.

Contamination cuts in an interesting, asymmetric way. The English anchor is suspect: GSM8K's test set is public, and MGSM deliberately uses its first 250 problems, which are easy to scrape and may sit in pretraining. But the non-English splits are fresh human translations created for this paper, so the multilingual numbers are less likely to be contaminated than the English ones — which, if anything, makes the cross-lingual transfer claim more credible than a naive leakage worry would suggest, while leaving the English baseline as the shakiest single column.

baselines

Strong for their moment (GPT-3 davinci-002, PaLM, Codex), but both proprietary and the headline PaLM-540B is unreleased — no independent replication. GPT-3 scale is only qualitative since the sizes (A–D2) aren't public.

contamination

No explicit contamination audit. The English split reuses GSM8K's public first-250 problems (leakage-prone); the ten translated splits are new gold human translations, so they're comparatively clean — the more load-bearing multilingual claim is the better-protected one.

what's missed

No confidence intervals on a 250-problem-per-language set; single greedy decode, no variance. The best 55.0% average folds in Google Translate quality rather than isolating in-language reasoning, and the "flat across frequency" claim is asserted on differences inside the noise.

would raise confidence

Error bars / significance tests on the per-language splits; open-model replications of the emergence curve; a contamination check of GSM8K and its translations against pretraining; and decoupling MT quality from reasoning (e.g. human-translated vs MT inputs in TRANSLATE-EN).

// bottom line

A landmark for the right reasons: MGSM is a small, carefully human-translated benchmark that asked a question nobody had operationalized, and the answer — that step-by-step reasoning, once it emerges with scale, largely survives the jump into very low-resource languages — has held up across hundreds of follow-on papers. Just treat the precise figures as directional: a 250-example, single-decode, two-closed-model study where the strongest number is half a Google-Translate result.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. MGSM is GSM8K, ten times over. 250 grade-school math problems, each 2–8 steps, hand-translated by professional translators into ten typologically diverse languages (including Swahili, Bengali, Telugu) — the first multilingual benchmark for multi-step arithmetic reasoning.
  2. Reasoning transfers across the data cliff. PaLM-540B with chain-of-thought solves >40% in every language and stays within ~3 points (44.9% vs 47.9%) between languages under 0.01% of pretraining and high-resource ones — and only after scale crosses an emergence threshold.
  3. English is a crutch and a baseline. Reasoning in English (EN-COT, 51.3%) beats reasoning in-language (NATIVE-COT, 48.1%), and translating to English first (TRANSLATE-EN, 55.0%) is best of all — useful as a strong default, but a reminder that the highest numbers measure translation as much as multilingual reasoning.
