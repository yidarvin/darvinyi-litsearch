CLIPScore: A Reference-free Evaluation Metric for Image Captioning

Take a frozen, off-the-shelf CLIP, embed the image and the candidate caption, take their cosine similarity — no reference captions, no fine-tuning — and you get a metric that correlates with human judgment better than CIDEr or SPICE ever did with four or five ground-truth references in hand.

Jack Hessel, Ari Holtzman, Maxwell Forbes, Ronan Le Bras, Yejin Choi — Allen Institute for AI & University of Washington

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

Image captioning has always been graded reference-based: collect four or five human-written captions per image, then score a machine caption by how closely it overlaps with them — BLEU-4 and ROUGE-L measure n-gram precision/recall, METEOR aligns words, CIDEr does tf-idf-weighted n-gram consensus, and SPICE parses references into a scene graph and matches predicted objects/attributes/relations against it. That is not how humans judge a caption. A human looks at the image and reads the candidate — no references required.

This mismatch has concrete costs. References are expensive to collect, and even four or five of them can't anticipate every correct phrasing: Figure 1's own examples show a caption describing dogs "on a marshy area" scoring badly on BLEU-1 (33.3), SPICE (0.0), METEOR (3.3), and CIDEr-D (0.5) purely because "marshy" doesn't appear in any reference, even though humans rate the caption correct in 4 of 4 judgments; conversely, a wrong caption that happens to reuse familiar reference words ("A grey dog walks on top of a fallen tree in the woods") can score well on the same n-gram metrics (BLEU-1 46.2, METEOR 18.1, CIDEr-D 10.3) despite only 1 of 4 humans agreeing it's correct. Reference-based scoring rewards word overlap, not caption quality.

There's a second, structural gap: plenty of real captioning domains simply don't have usable references at all — alt-text for accessibility on Twitter, news photo captions, social-media "personality" captions, clip-art scenes. For those, reference-based metrics either can't be applied or have to fake references from unreliable surrounding text. Machine translation had already found an answer to an analogous problem — reference-free "quality estimation" using cross-lingual pretrained representations (LASER, M-BERT) instead of more monolingual references — and this paper asks whether the same move works for images: can a pretrained vision-language model's cross-modal similarity substitute for human references in caption evaluation?

// how it works

The method

CLIPScore does almost nothing clever — that's the point. It takes CLIP (Radford et al., 2021), a cross-modal retrieval model contrastively trained on 400M (image, caption) pairs scraped from the web, and applies it completely off-the-shelf, with zero fine-tuning, as an evaluation metric. The paper uses CLIP's ViT-B/32 configuration: images pass through a Vision Transformer over a 7×7 grid of patches from a 224×224 input (12 transformer layers, 86M parameters), and text passes through a 12-layer transformer over a 49K-token BPE vocabulary. Both towers output a single 512-D vector, trained via InfoNCE to pull matching (image, caption) pairs together and push mismatched pairs apart.

To score a candidate caption against an image: embed the image (vector v), embed the candidate text (vector c) — prefixed with the prompt "A photo depicts", which the authors found to correlate slightly better than CLIP's own recommended "A photo of" — and take the cosine similarity. Because that raw similarity empirically clusters in a narrow band (roughly 0 to 0.4, never negative in their experiments), they rescale it by a constant w = 2.5, following the same rescale-for-readability move BERTScore made, so the distribution stretches toward [0, 1]:

CLIP-S(c, v) = w · max(cos(c, v), 0) // w = 2.5; corpus-level score is just the mean CLIP-S over (candidate, image) pairs — no references anywhere in this formula

That's the entire reference-free metric. They also define RefCLIPScore, which folds references back in when they happen to be available: embed every reference caption with CLIP's text tower too, then take the harmonic mean of CLIP-S and the highest cosine similarity between the candidate and any reference:

RefCLIP-S(c, R, v) = H-Mean( CLIP-S(c, v), maxr∈R max(cos(c, r), 0) ) // R = the set of CLIP text-embedded references — RefCLIP-S degrades gracefully to CLIP-S when R is empty

Nothing here is trained on captioning data at all — it's a direct, frozen application of a general-purpose vision-language model, which is exactly why it generalizes to domains that have no references to speak of (alt-text, news photos, clip-art). It's also fast: roughly 4K image-candidate pairs per minute on a single consumer GPU, since both towers are small and the forward pass is a single embedding lookup per side.

// what they found

The results

On every literal-description benchmark tested, the reference-free CLIP-S beats every reference-based metric, and RefCLIP-S (which adds references back in) beats CLIP-S again. On Flickr8K-Expert (17K expert 1–4 Likert judgments over 5,664 images), CLIP-S reaches Kendall τc = 51.2 and RefCLIP-S reaches 53.0, ahead of ViLBERTScore-F (50.1), TIGEr (49.3), SPICE (44.9), CIDEr (43.9), METEOR (41.8), and BLEU-1 (32.3). On Flickr8K-CF (145K binary CrowdFlower judgments, τb), CLIP-S scores 34.4 and RefCLIP-S 36.4, against CIDEr's 24.6 and SPICE's 24.4. On Composite (12K judgments over MSCOCO/Flickr8k/Flickr30k images), CLIP-S reaches 53.8 and RefCLIP-S 55.4, ahead of ViLBERTScore-F (52.4), TIGEr (45.4), and CIDEr (37.7). At the system level on the 2015 MSCOCO Challenge submissions, CLIP-S correlates with the "beats a human caption" / "passes the Turing test" metrics at Spearman ρ = .59/.63, and RefCLIP-S at .69/.74 (all p < .05).

On Pascal-50S pairwise preference (4K sentence pairs, 48 human judgments each, four categories from two-human-captions to two-machine-captions), CLIP-S averages 80.7% accuracy and RefCLIP-S 83.1%, both ahead of CIDEr (79.6%), SPICE (78.7%), and BLEU-4 (72.6%) — see the table below. On the FOIL hallucination test (captions with one noun-phrase adversarially swapped, e.g. "motorcycle"→"bicycle"), CLIP-S hits 87.2% accuracy using zero references — outperforming every reference-based metric except BERT-S (RoBERTa-F), and matching its own 1-reference and 4-reference performance identically, since it never touches references at all; RefCLIP-S reaches 91.0%/92.6% (1-ref/4-ref).

A forward-selection information-gain analysis (Figure 2) asks whether CLIP-S is redundant with existing metrics or captures something new: across 10 bootstrap resamples on both Composite and Flickr8K-Expert, CLIP-S or RefCLIP-S is always the first metric a greedy regression selects to explain human judgment variance — evidence it isn't just re-deriving what SPICE or ViLBERTScore-F already capture.

Four case studies then map out where the reference-free promise holds and where it doesn't. On Twitter alt-text ratings (2.8K judgments), CLIP-S reaches 48.4 τc while the best reference-based metric, BERT-S (RoBERTa-F), manages only 15 τc — actually worse than a naive 25-τc length baseline, because tweet text makes an unreliable stand-in for a real reference. On Abstract-50S clip-art pairwise judgments, CLIP-S reaches 68% accuracy (vs. a 53% baseline) but trails CIDEr/METEOR (79% each) and BERT-S (73%). On Personality Captions, CLIP-S prefers literal descriptions over more "engaging" personality captions 80% of the time even though humans rate the personality captions more engaging 65% of the time — and when asked to rank two engaging captions against each other, CLIP-S manages only 57% agreement, identical to a length-only baseline (reference-based metrics do no better: 52–53%). On news image captioning (NYT photos requiring named-entity and contextual knowledge), reference-based metrics dominate outright — METEOR hits 93% accuracy and BLEU-4 91%, while CLIP-S manages only 65%, "only slightly above random." Finally, a targeted memorization check on 250 personal photos never posted online shows CLIP-S recovering majority human caption preference 86% of the time (vs. 50% random and a 72% length baseline; human agreement itself is 93%) — evidence, though not proof, that CLIP-S isn't purely regurgitating memorized training images.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

The most interesting risk with CLIPScore isn't in the numbers it reports — it's structural. CLIP itself is trained on 400M (image, caption) pairs harvested by running 500K search-engine queries and keeping whatever text the web attached to the resulting images, with no quality control over what makes a "good" caption in that corpus. Using that same model as the judge of caption quality means the standard a caption is held to is implicitly "does this look like what the open web says about images," not any principled notion of descriptive accuracy. The paper is admirably upfront about a version of this: it cites Agarwal et al. (2021)'s finding that CLIP "disproportionately" misclassifies people — "male images were misclassified into classes related to crime" — and flags that CLIP-S therefore "reflects the biases of the pre-training data," the same caveat BERTScore carries. But the paper stops at citing that risk; it never audits CLIP-S's own scores for demographic skew (e.g., does CLIP-S systematically rate certain people/scenes lower for reasons unrelated to caption accuracy?). That matters more than it looks, because the paper's own "promising future work" section proposes using CLIP-S as an RL reward for caption generation — at which point a biased judge stops being a measurement problem and becomes a training-time amplifier of whatever bias it carries.

A related gap: nothing in the paper tests CLIP-S under optimization pressure. Every experiment scores captions that were written by humans or by captioning systems trained on ordinary MLE/CIDEr objectives — never a system trained explicitly to maximize CLIP-S. The FOIL hallucination test (87.2% accuracy catching a single swapped noun-phrase) shows CLIP-S notices at least one class of error, but detecting a naturally-occurring hallucination is a much weaker bar than surviving adversarial optimization: a generator directly rewarded by CLIP-S might learn keyword-dense, CLIP-flattering phrasing that isn't actually a better caption by any human standard — the classic reward-hacking failure mode for any model-based metric used as a training signal. The paper's own discussion of "contemporaneous work" notes that UMIC (Lee et al., 2021) addresses a version of this by training against synthetic hard-negative captions; CLIP-S, applied frozen and off-the-shelf, has no such adversarial exposure baked in.

To its credit, the paper doesn't oversell the "reference-free" pitch — its own case studies are the sharpest critique of CLIP-S available, and they're self-administered. News captioning (65% accuracy vs. METEOR's 93% and BLEU-4's 91%) and Personality Captions engagingness (57%, tied with a length-only baseline) are both domains where CLIP-S is shown to lose outright, because the task requires context CLIP was never trained to supply (named-entity knowledge, non-literal engagement). That intellectual honesty is a genuine strength — but it also means CLIP-S's headline wins are real only within "literal, visually-verifiable description," a narrower claim than the paper's title suggests. One more loose thread: the rescaling constant w = 2.5 is fit for readability against the observed cosine-similarity range, not derived from any principle, and Appendix B's own distribution plots (Figure 4) show that raw cos(c,v) values shift shape and location noticeably across corpora — a single global constant is a convenient approximation, not a calibrated one, and it means CLIP-S values aren't obviously comparable in absolute terms across domains even though within-corpus rankings are preserved.

baselines

Strong and current for 2021 — five classic n-gram metrics (BLEU-1/4, ROUGE-L, METEOR, CIDEr) plus SPICE and four contemporary learned metrics (BERT-S++, TIGEr, ViLBERTScore-F, NUBIA). The authors also re-derive several numbers themselves after finding replication discrepancies in prior reporting (Appendix A documents exact aggregation-method choices), rather than just quoting old published figures.

contamination

CLIP's 400M pretraining pairs have unknown overlap with Flickr8K/Composite/Pascal-50S/MSCOCO. The authors lean on Radford et al.'s train-test overlap analysis (built for zero-shot classification, not captioning) plus their own small, author-graded, non-blinded 250-image "never posted online" spot check — a good instinct, but thin evidence at the scale CLIP was actually trained on.

what's missed

No adversarial/optimization-pressure test of CLIP-S as a training reward (which the paper itself proposes as future work); no demographic bias audit of CLIP-S's own output scores, only a citation to a related classification-bias finding; no confidence intervals on the correlation numbers, several of which (Personality Captions engagingness, Abstract-50S) are already close to baseline.

would raise confidence

Train a captioner against CLIP-S as an RL reward and check for degenerate, CLIP-flattering outputs; run a demographic bias breakdown of CLIP-S scores by depicted-person attributes; report confidence intervals alongside the correlation coefficients; replicate the memorization check at a larger scale with blinded raters.

// bottom line

CLIPScore is a rigorously scoped empirical finding, not an overclaimed one: an off-the-shelf, frozen CLIP beats every 2021-era reference-based caption metric on literal image description, and the paper's own case studies honestly draw the boundary where that stops being true (news captions, non-literal engagement). What remains untested is the harder question anyone reusing CLIP-S as a training signal will eventually hit — whether a judge built from noisy web image-text pairs, with no adversarial exposure and no bias audit of its own scores, stays trustworthy once a model is optimized directly against it rather than merely measured by it.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. CLIP-S(c, v) = 2.5 · max(cos(c, v), 0) — a completely reference-free caption score built from a frozen, off-the-shelf CLIP ViT-B/32, with zero fine-tuning and no training data of its own; RefCLIPScore folds references back in as a harmonic mean when they're available.
  2. On literal image-description benchmarks (Flickr8K-Expert/CF, Composite, Pascal-50S, FOIL hallucination), CLIP-S alone beats every reference-based metric tested — CIDEr, SPICE, METEOR, BLEU, ROUGE-L — and RefCLIP-S beats CLIP-S again; a forward-selection analysis shows CLIP-S is consistently the single most informative metric, not a redundant one.
  3. The paper's own case studies are its best critique: CLIP-S loses badly on news captioning (65% vs. METEOR's 93%) and non-literal "engagingness" judgments (57%, no better than a length heuristic) — CLIP-S's real domain is literal, visually-verifiable description, and neither its own bias risk (inherited from CLIP's noisy web pretraining) nor its behavior under direct optimization pressure has been stress-tested.
