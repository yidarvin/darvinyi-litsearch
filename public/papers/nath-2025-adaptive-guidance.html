Adaptive Guidance Accelerates Reinforcement Learning of Reasoning Models

RL on verifiable rewards mostly sharpens what a model can already do — it converts pass@16-latent solutions into pass@1, not genuinely new capability. Guide injects a reference-derived hint only on prompts where every rollout fails, then uses an off-policy importance weight to train the hint-free policy — turning dead, zero-gradient prompts into learning signal and beating vanilla GRPO by 1–4 points on math.

Vaskar Nath, Elaine Lau, Anisha Gunjal, Manasi Sharma, Nikhil Baharte, Sean Hendryx · Scale AI

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

Reinforcement learning from verifiable rewards (RLVR) — the recipe behind DeepSeek-R1 and o1 — reliably lifts a reasoning model's benchmark scores. But how it does so was contested. Does RL teach the model to solve problems it genuinely could not before, or does it just bias the output distribution toward solutions the base model already had latent? The distinction is not academic: a concurrent result the authors cite (Yue et al., "Does RL Really Incentivize Reasoning Capacity Beyond the Base Model?") found that at very high sampling budgets (pass@k, k=256) RLVR actually shrinks a model's reasoning boundary — it sharpens, it doesn't expand. If RL only re-allocates probability mass, then squeezing more capability out of it needs a different lever.

There is also a concrete sample-efficiency wall baked into GRPO. The advantage is computed by z-normalizing rewards within a group of rollouts for one prompt. When every rollout for a hard prompt is wrong, the rewards are identical, the normalized advantage is zero, and that prompt contributes no gradient at all. State-of-the-art recipes like DAPO simply discard these all-incorrect prompts to stabilize training — which means the hardest, most informative problems are thrown away precisely when the model most needs to learn from them. This paper asks: can you instead rescue those dead prompts and convert them into capability gain?

// how it works

The method

Step 1 — decompose what RLVR is actually doing

The authors first split every improvement RLVR makes into two measurable buckets, defined relative to the base (untrained) policy:

  • Self-distillation — problems the base model could already solve with multiple attempts (pass@16, temperature 1.0) that RL converts into a reliable single-shot answer (pass@1, temperature 0). The capability was latent; RL makes it dependable.
  • Capability gain — problems the base model could not solve even at pass@16, which the RL-trained model solves at pass@1. This is genuinely new problem-solving ability.

Running this decomposition across Qwen-2.5 at five scales (0.5B, 3B, 7B, 32B, 72B) on >500,000 problems spanning math, STEM, and code, the verdict is lopsided: self-distillation dominates at every scale. Capability gain exists — the 0.5B model gains the most in relative terms — but it is always the minority contributor. The available headroom (the unsolved set |U|) also contracts sharply with scale: 3,195 unsolved items at 0.5B, 2,150 at 3B, 1,913 at 7B, 1,617 at 32B, and just 1,532 at 72B. RLVR, in their words, "primarily re-allocates probability mass rather than discover truly novel solutions."

Step 2 — guidance buys capability gain

If the bottleneck is that hard prompts never produce a correct rollout, the fix is to make correct rollouts appear. The authors append a prompt-specific hint h to the context — high-level conceptual direction and a solving framework distilled from the human reference solution, deliberately not the answer — and require the model to still derive the full chain itself: πθ(oi,t | q, h, oi,<t). As a proof of concept (Figure 2), adding hints to Qwen-2.5-Math-7B raises unbiased pass@k across the board and, critically, solves previously-unsolvable questions (capability gain) with the gap growing in k.

Step 3 — Guide: harvest guided rollouts, but train the hint-free policy

The catch: you want a model that performs well without hints at test time, so guided rollouts are off-policy with respect to the deployment policy. Guide handles this with two moves. First, hints are injected only on prompts where all k standard rollouts fail — the dead, zero-advantage prompts — so guidance is a targeted rescue, not a crutch. Second, the guided trajectories enter the GRPO objective through an off-policy importance weight whose numerator conditions on the plain prompt while the denominator conditions on the prompt actually used to sample the rollout (plain or guided). This projects the off-policy guided gradient onto the on-policy, hint-free direction.

ρt = πθ( rt | xq, r<t )πθold( rt | sq, r<t )   ,   sq ∈ { xq , x̃q } // importance weight: numerator scores under the PLAIN prompt xq; denominator under the prompt sq actually sampled from (plain xq or guided x̃q). Guided rollouts r ∼ πθold(·|x̃q) are drawn only when all k plain rollouts fail.

Two stability details matter. The importance weights must be taken relative to the guided sampling distribution (conditioned on prompt + hint), not just the prompt — otherwise the ratios misrepresent the gradient and training destabilizes. And the PPO min-clip must be removed for guided trajectories: because guided rollouts carry systematically smaller probability ratios, clipping artificially inflates most token ratios and reliably diverges training at ~50 steps. The authors give variants for both GRPO (Guide-GRPO) and PPO (Guide-PPO), and prove a first-order result: whenever the expected guided advantage on unsolved prompts is positive, Guide-GRPO's one-step expected improvement exceeds vanilla GRPO's — with the margin growing on hard prompts (large failure probability (1−pq)k) as long as the unguided success probability pq is non-zero so credit can propagate.

// what they found

The results

On Qwen-2.5-Math-7B (4K context), Guide-GRPO posts the best aggregate scores: 51.03 macro-average pass@1 vs 49.34 for vanilla GRPO, 47.94 for GRPO-with-filtering (the DAPO-style discard baseline), 40.42 for SFT on reference solutions, and 38.43 for the base model. Tellingly, it also lifts macro pass@16 to 70.15 (vs GRPO 67.06) — a metric that reflects breadth of solvable problems, not just single-shot sharpening, which is the signature of genuine capability gain rather than pure distillation. The gains concentrate on the hardest benchmarks: AIME 2024 pass@1 jumps to 30.67 (vs GRPO 26.67, Filter-GRPO 18.67, base 6.67), OlympiadBench to 43.11 (vs 39.56), MATH500 to 82.68 (vs 79.00). On easier or saturated sets it merely ties (GSM8K 91.43 vs GRPO 91.71; Minerva 32.35 vs 32.72).

The advantage holds when scaling resources. At 8K context, Guide-GRPO improves macro pass@1 by +3.39 points at 32B (56.26 vs GRPO-32B 52.87) and micro pass@1 by +1.89 (76.36 vs 74.47), with consistent 1–4 point gains across configurations — though the 7B-at-8K case is essentially flat (49.58 vs 49.29). A threshold ablation confirms the gating is load-bearing: applying guidance only on all-incorrect prompts (51.03 macro pass@1) ties applying it below 25% accuracy (50.48) but far outperforms unconditional guidance (40.97) — too much hand-holding stops the model from learning to reason on its own.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

The diagnostic half is the strong part: decomposing RLVR into self-distillation vs capability gain is a clean, reproducible lens, and the finding (sharpening dominates) lands squarely in an active debate. The algorithm is a sensible, well-motivated fix for a real GRPO failure mode. But the empirical case for Guide is narrower than the framing suggests, and rests on choices worth poking at.

Two things load the dice on the capability-gain story. First, "capability gain" is defined relative to a pass@16 ceiling — but the very paper they corroborate measures the RL-vs-base frontier at k=256, where they concede the boundary can shrink. Raise k and much of what's labeled capability gain reclassifies as latent, so the headline is sensitive to a hand-picked budget (and to the asymmetric temperatures: pass@1 at temp 0, pass@16 at temp 1). Second — and more pointed — Guide's hints are derived from the human reference solutions. On the hardest prompts the model is effectively trained on information distilled from the gold answer; "capability gain" here is partly reference leakage dressed as exploration. The authors' defense (hints give framework not answer, the model derives the chain, hints vanish at test time) is fair, but this is closer to a reference-conditioned curriculum than to RL discovering new reasoning unaided.

And the lift itself is modest and non-uniform. 1–4 points of macro pass@1 on Qwen-Math benchmarks that are widely contaminated sits close to the noise floor; GRPO ties or beats Guide on GSM8K and Minerva pass@1 (and on AIME24 pass@16), the 7B-8K configuration is flat, and AIME's 30-problem sets make those deltas high-variance (AIME 2025 pass@1 is an identical 13.33 — 4/30 — for all three RL methods, i.e. at the resolution floor). Everything is one model family (Qwen) and, for the Guide experiments, math only — even though the self-distillation diagnosis spanned math, science, and code.

baselines

Current and fair for mid-2025 — GRPO, DAPO-style filtering, SFT-on-references, base. But confined to one lineage (Qwen-2.5-Math), a model already heavily math-tuned and a known contamination risk on exactly these benchmarks; no Llama/other family to support the "generalizes" claim.

contamination

Two exposures. The math benchmarks (MATH500, GSM8K, AMC, AIME, Olympiad) are heavily leaked for Qwen-Math, and 1–3 point gains live in that noise. Separately, hints are distilled from reference solutions — a controlled but real information path into training on the hardest prompts.

what's missed

The capability-gain claim is anchored to pass@16; the cited counter-result uses k=256. Gains are small, non-uniform (ties on easy sets, flat at 7B-8K), and AIME deltas are at the resolution floor. Removing PPO ratio-clipping to stabilize is an untested departure from standard PPO.

would raise confidence

A non-Qwen base; Guide evaluated on the science/code benchmarks used in the diagnosis; hints generated without reference-solution access (e.g. from a weaker model) to separate "better exploration" from "reference leakage"; and pass@k at large k to test whether Guide raises the ceiling or just sharpens.

// bottom line

A genuinely useful mechanistic result — RLVR ≈ mostly self-distillation, not new capability — paired with a clean fix that rescues GRPO's dead, all-incorrect prompts by injecting reference-derived hints only there and correcting off-policy so the hint-free policy is what improves. The empirical lift is real, scales with model size and context, and unusually moves pass@16 (not just pass@1) — but it's modest, Qwen-and-math-only, and the capability-gain narrative leans on a fixed pass@16 ceiling and reference-derived hints. Promising mechanism and method; not yet a decisive demonstration that RL can manufacture capability.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. RLVR's gains are mostly self-distillation — turning pass@16-latent solutions into reliable pass@1 — not the discovery of new capability. Capability gain exists but is a minority contributor at every scale from 0.5B to 72B, and the headroom shrinks as models grow.
  2. The bottleneck for capability gain is the all-incorrect prompt: when no rollout succeeds, the group-relative advantage is zero and the prompt teaches nothing. DAPO discards these; Guide rescues them by injecting a reference-derived hint only on those prompts, harvesting the now-correct guided rollouts, and using an off-policy importance weight to optimize the hint-free policy.
  3. Guide-GRPO beats vanilla GRPO by 1–4 points macro pass@1 on math (51.03 vs 49.34 at 7B; 56.26 vs 52.87 at 32B-8K) and — unusually — also lifts pass@16 while keeping training entropy higher (less mode collapse). Gains scale with context and model size but remain Qwen- and math-only, and the hints carry reference-solution signal.
