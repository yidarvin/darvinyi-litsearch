BLEURT: Learning Robust Metrics for Text Generation

A BERT-based metric for grading generated text, pre-trained on roughly 6.5 million synthetic sentence perturbations before it is ever fine-tuned on a few thousand human ratings — buying both the expressivity of a fully-learned metric and the robustness to distribution shift that earlier learned metrics lacked.

Thibault Sellam, Dipanjan Das, Ankur P. Parikh · Google Research

What problem is this solving?

For two decades the default way to score generated text was to measure n-gram overlap with a human reference — BLEU for translation, ROUGE for summarization. They are cheap and perfectly reproducible, but they are only sensitive to surface form. They cannot reward a correct paraphrase that happens to share few n-grams with the reference, and they correlate poorly with human judgment precisely in the regime that matters most: when the systems being compared are all roughly as good as each other and the metric has to make fine distinctions.

The field's answer was to inject learned components into metrics, and by 2019 the WMT Metrics shared task was dominated by neural approaches. But these split into two camps with a frustrating trade-off. Fully-learned metrics (RUSE, BEER, ESIM) are trained end-to-end on human ratings and can fit the ratings distribution tightly — but they assume training and test data are identically distributed. Hybrid metrics (YiSi, BERTScore) bolt handwritten logic onto contextual embeddings; they need little or no rating data and are more robust, but they are less expressive.

The IID assumption is exactly what breaks in NLG evaluation. There is domain drift — a metric tuned on news may later be asked to score restaurant reviews. Worse, there is quality drift: NLG systems improve year over year, so a metric trained on 2015 ratings may be unable to separate the top systems of 2019. An ideal metric would both exploit whatever rating data exists and extrapolate past it. BLEURT's bet is that you can have both — if you first teach the model what text degradation looks like, before you ever show it a single human rating.

// how it works

The method

At inference BLEURT is almost trivially simple: concatenate the reference x and the candidate x̃, push them through BERT, and read a single regression head off the [CLS] vector to predict the human rating. The whole stack — BERT included — is fine-tuned on the supervised ratings with an ℓ₂ loss. With only a few thousand WMT ratings this already reaches state-of-the-art correlation. The catch is that fine-tuning BERT directly still needs a sizable IID rating set, which is the brittleness BLEURT set out to avoid.

ŷ = f(x, x̃) = W·ṽ[CLS] + b     ℓsupervised = 1N Σ ‖yi − ŷ‖² // a linear head on BERT's [CLS] embedding, fine-tuned on human ratings (typically a few thousand examples)

The actual contribution is a pre-training stage inserted between BERT's own pre-training and the rating fine-tune. The authors take 1.8 million Wikipedia segments and corrupt them into roughly 6.5 million synthetic reference–candidate pairs using three perturbations: mask-filling with BERT (fluent lexical swaps), backtranslation through French and German (meaning-preserving paraphrases, plus the translation model's own mistakes as realistic errors), and random word dropping (to mimic truncations and empty predictions). None of these pairs carries a human label. Instead, each pair is tagged with nine automatically-computed signals, and BLEURT is pre-trained to predict all nine at once with a weighted multi-task loss whose weights are set by grid search.

BLEURT comes in two sizes — BLEURT (BERT-Large; 24 layers, 1024 hidden, 16 heads) and BLEURTbase (BERT-Base; 12 layers, 768 hidden) — and is trained in three steps: ordinary BERT pre-training, 800,000 steps of synthetic pre-training, then 40,000 steps of fine-tuning on task ratings. The payoff of the middle step is that BLEURT arrives at the rating fine-tune already knowing what fluency, paraphrase, entailment, and degradation look like, so a few thousand ratings suffice and it degrades gracefully when those ratings are scarce or skewed.

// what they found

The results

On the WMT Metrics shared task — the field's standard for grading metrics by how well they imitate human assessments — a BLEURT model tops every to-English language pair for 2017 and 2018. Averaged across the seven 2017 pairs, BLEURT reaches Kendall's τ / Pearson r of 62.5 / 81.8, ahead of BERTScore-with-RoBERTa (58.0 / 76.8), MoverScore (52.3 / 72.4), and sentence-BLEU at just 32.4 / 47.5. On 2019 the picture is more measured: BLEURT wins every pair on Kendall's τ but comes first on only 3 of 7 pairs under the shared task's robust DARR metric.

Pre-training is the load-bearing piece. It adds up to 7.4 Kendall-τ points (BLEURTbase, zh-en, 2017) and is most valuable exactly where rating data is smallest — its effect is milder on the larger 2018/2019 sets (up to 2.1 points, tr-en 2018), and BLEURTbase with pre-training is often better than the larger BLEURT without it.

The robustness claim is tested head-on with a quality-drift stress test (Figure 2): the training set is sub-sampled to keep low-rated translations while the test set keeps high-rated ones, controlled by a skew factor α that also shrinks the data — down to 11.9% of 5,344 records at α=3.0. Under training skew, BLEURT without pre-training collapses: it drops below BERTScore at α=1.0 and below sentence-BLEU at α≥1.5. With pre-training it stays on top until α=3.0, the most extreme drift the authors construct.

Adapting to a brand-new task tells the same story on the WebNLG data-to-text challenge (Figure 3). The more pre-trained BLEURT is, the faster it adapts: a plain BERT regressor needs about a third of the WebNLG ratings to beat the BLEU/TER/Meteor/BERTScore baselines and still trails on semantics, while pre-trained BLEURT is competitive with as few as 836 records — and the fully pre-trained model matches BERTScore with no WebNLG fine-tuning at all.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

BLEURT's evaluation is unusually honest for a metrics paper, and the baseline set is genuinely strong: it compares against the actual top WMT contestants (chrF++, BEER, Meteor++, RUSE, YiSi, ESIM, YiSi-SRL) plus MoverScore and BERTScore, and deliberately runs BERTScore on BLEURT's own BERT-large uncased backbone "for fairness" (while also reporting the stronger recommended RoBERTa version for completeness). The numbers come from the authors' own reimplementation of the benchmark — which they justify, noting the official WMT scripts are poorly documented and the 2019 edition's own README discourages using them. They flag the divergences: a few 2018-table entries are more than 0.2 (up to 0.4) points off the official results, the 2019 entries all fall within 0.2, and they report results as "globally consistent" with the official numbers. Still, it means the headline ranking rests partly on a non-official harness.

Three caveats temper the "robust" in the title. Scope: every result is to-English machine translation plus one data-to-text dataset (WebNLG). The metric is English-only and reference-based, and the very tasks where BLEU is most notorious — summarization and dialogue — are not evaluated here. What "out-of-distribution" means: the robustness shown is robustness to skew within WMT/WebNLG; the synthetic pre-training draws on Wikipedia, which shares a domain with WMT's news references, so some of the generalization is across nearer distributions than the framing implies. Inherited blind spots: BLEURT is pre-trained to imitate BERTScore and an MNLI-trained entailment classifier, so any bias in those models is baked in — and the 2019 results, where it wins on τ but only 3/7 on DARR, hint the dominance is partly metric-dependent.

The deepest open question is one the paper doesn't address — and, in fairness, one that largely post-dates it: by the standards of later reward-hacking work, a learned metric is a black-box scalar with no absolute scale, and the moment it becomes a selection or training target it is exposed to Goodharting. The authors even show that pre-training on BLEU and ROUGE hurts — proof the metric is sensitive to which signals it imitates — yet they never stress-test BLEURT against an adversary optimizing it directly.

baselines

Strong and contemporary — the real WMT shared-task winners plus MoverScore and BERTScore, with BERTScore matched to BLEURT's own BERT-large "for fairness" (RoBERTa also reported). Caveat: scored on the authors' own benchmark reimplementation — a few 2018 entries up to 0.4 pts off official (2019 within 0.2), though they report it as "globally consistent."

contamination

No test-set leakage in the usual sense (held-out WMT years), but BERT and the synthetic pre-training both draw on Wikipedia, which overlaps the news domain of the WMT references; and the metric imitates BERTScore + an MNLI classifier, inheriting their biases.

what's missed

English-only, reference-based, MT-centric (plus WebNLG). Summarization and dialogue — where n-gram metrics fail worst — go untested. The output is a single uninterpretable scalar with no absolute scale, never stress-tested as an optimization target.

would raise confidence

Multilingual and reference-free settings; held-out NLG tasks beyond MT/WebNLG; an adversarial / reward-hacking probe of the metric; and a domain-overlap ablation separating Wikipedia pre-training from WMT news.

// bottom line

BLEURT convincingly solves the problem it poses — a fully-learned metric that survives quality and domain drift — and became a default text-generation metric for good reason. But "robust" is demonstrated within to-English MT and one data-to-text task, on a private reimplementation of the benchmark, and the harder robustness question — what happens when BLEURT itself is the thing being optimized — is left for future work.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. BLEURT's one trick is mid-training: insert a synthetic pre-training stage between BERT and the rating fine-tune, teaching the model what paraphrase, entailment, and text degradation look like from ~6.5M unlabeled Wikipedia perturbations before it ever sees a human score.
  2. That pre-training is what buys robustness — without it the metric collapses below sentence-BLEU under quality drift (α≥1.5); with it, BLEURT tops every to-English WMT 2017/18 pair (avg τ 62.5) and matches BERTScore on WebNLG with zero in-task fine-tuning.
  3. The honest limits: English-only, reference-based, MT-centric; scored on the authors' own benchmark reimplementation; and a black-box learned scalar never tested against an adversary optimizing it — the failure mode that matters most once a metric becomes a training target.
