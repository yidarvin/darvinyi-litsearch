Video-MME: The First-Ever Comprehensive Evaluation Benchmark of Multi-modal LLMs in Video Analysis

900 hand-curated YouTube videos, 11 seconds to an hour, spanning 6 domains and 30 subfields, with subtitles and audio treated as first-class inputs alongside frames. Gemini 1.5 Pro tops out at 75% overall accuracy; the best open-source model manages 59%.

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

By mid-2024, multimodal LLM evaluation was overwhelmingly built around static images — MMBench, MMMU, and similar suites test perception and reasoning on a single frame at a time. Video understanding, which requires tracking objects and events across time rather than reading one still image, had comparatively thin coverage, and the video benchmarks that did exist had real gaps: MSRVTT-QA and MSVD-QA average clips of roughly 15 and 10 seconds, and even EgoSchema, one of the longer existing sets in the paper's own comparison, averages only 180 seconds. None of the widely-used prior benchmarks combined a long duration range, broad open-domain content, and multiple input modalities (frames, subtitles, and audio) in one place — see the paper's own Table 1 comparison, where every prior dataset in the table scores "✗" on at least one of "Multi-level" (duration), "Open-domain," or "Sub.&Aud." (subtitle-plus-audio) coverage.

That matters because a benchmark whose videos are all short and narrow in domain can't distinguish a model that genuinely tracks long-range temporal structure from one that's just pattern-matching a few frames. Video-MME's stated goal is to close that gap directly: cover video lengths from 11 seconds to a full hour, span a genuinely broad set of real-world domains, and evaluate models under frames-only, frames+subtitles, and frames+audio settings so that the contribution of each modality can be measured rather than assumed.

// how it works

The method

Video-MME is built in three stages: video collection, QA annotation, and quality review. For collection, the authors first define a domain hierarchy of 6 top-level categories — Knowledge, Film & Television, Sports Competition, Artistic Performance, Life Record, and Multilingual — subdivided into 30 fine-grained subfields (technology, documentary, news report, esports, magic show, fashion, and so on), sourced from YouTube. Within each category they deliberately collect videos across three duration bands: short (<2 minutes), medium (4–15 minutes), and long (30–60 minutes). The result is 900 videos total — 300 per duration band — of which 744 ship with subtitles and all 900 ship with an audio track.

Annotation is fully manual: annotators with research experience in vision-language learning watch each video in full and write three multiple-choice questions per video (four options each), yielding 2,700 QA pairs across 12 task types spanning perception, reasoning, and information synthesis. Quality review has two passes: a second annotator independently checks clarity, answerability, and logical consistency of each option set; then, to guard against questions answerable from text alone, every question (without the video) is fed to Gemini 1.5 Pro, and any question it can answer from the question text alone is sent back for revision. The authors report that in this text-only setting Gemini 1.5 Pro scores below 15% accuracy — evidence that the surviving questions genuinely require the video. The benchmark also borrows the "certificate length" metric from EgoSchema (the minimum span of sub-clips a human needs to verify an answer) and reports median certificate lengths of 26s / 164.7s / 890.7s for short/medium/long videos — all longer than EgoSchema's own ~100s median, which the authors use to argue Video-MME is the more temporally demanding set.

// what they found

The results

The headline comparison is frames-only accuracy: Gemini 1.5 Pro leads among commercial models at 75.0% overall, ahead of GPT-4o at 71.9% and GPT-4V at 59.9%. Among open-source video models, VILA-1.5 (34B) leads at 59.0% overall, with VITA-1.5 (7B) the strongest smaller model at 56.1%. Notably, image-only MLLMs extended to multi-frame input — Qwen-VL-Max and InternVL-Chat-V1.5 — reach 51.3% and 50.7% respectively, roughly matching dedicated video models like LLaVA-NeXT-Video (52.0%), which the authors read as evidence that image understanding is a prerequisite for video understanding rather than a fully separate skill. All models decline as video length increases: Gemini 1.5 Pro's frames-only accuracy falls from 81.7% (short) to 74.3% (medium) to 67.4% (long).

Adding modality information helps substantially and unevenly. Subtitles lift Gemini 1.5 Pro's overall accuracy from 75.0% to 81.3% (+6.2 points, with the Knowledge category alone gaining +9.2 points), and audio lifts it to 79.4% overall (+4.3 points), with especially large gains in the Multilingual category (+16.7% on long videos with subtitles, +12.5% with audio) and Sports Competition (+14.3% with audio on long videos). The benefit grows with video length — exactly where models otherwise struggle most — suggesting subtitles/audio partly compensate for weak long-range visual tracking rather than being redundant with it. Most open-source models tested don't support audio input at all, so that comparison is closed-source-only; subtitles alone still help open-source models across the board (e.g., VITA-1.0 gains 3.4 points overall from subtitles, 55.8%→59.2%).

// does it hold up?

The eval read

Video-MME's central engineering strength is genuine: 900 manually-watched, manually-annotated videos across a real duration spread (11s to 1hr) and a real domain spread (6 domains, 30 subfields) is a meaningfully larger and more diverse claim than prior video QA sets, and the text-only filter (rejecting any question Gemini 1.5 Pro can answer below 15% from the question alone) is a sensible, verifiable contamination-adjacent check that most contemporaneous video benchmarks skipped. The certificate-length comparison against EgoSchema is also a genuine attempt at quantifying difficulty rather than asserting it.

That said, a few things temper how far the headline numbers should be trusted. First, the questions were sourced from YouTube videos, which almost certainly appear (in whole or clipped form) in the pretraining corpora of the very models being evaluated via their captions, subtitles, or web mentions — the paper's contamination check only screens whether the question text alone leaks the answer, not whether the underlying video's content was memorized by an LLM's text-side knowledge (e.g., a well-known documentary or news event). Second, "Gemini 1.5 Pro filters what counts as a valid question" is a review step performed by one of the very models later benchmarked — a design choice that's practical but introduces a mild circularity (Gemini's blind spots could systematically shape which questions survive). Third, all evaluation is multiple-choice with four options; a 25% guessing floor and no free-response component means the benchmark measures discrimination among options more than open-ended video description or generation, and the paper reports no calibration or option-order-bias check (e.g., a "how much of the score is from eliminating implausible options via world knowledge alone" ablation, as NOLIMA runs for text). Finally, the reported "with subtitles" condition times subtitles to the sampled frames rather than the full transcript — reasonable for consistency, but it means the subtitle-boost numbers may understate what a model with a full-transcript context window could extract.

baselines

A broad, fair snapshot for its window — 4 commercial models (GPT-4V, GPT-4o, Gemini 1.5 Flash/Pro), 9 open-source video MLLMs, and 3 image MLLMs (Qwen-VL-Chat/Max, InternVL-Chat-V1.5) extended to multi-frame input — but frozen at mid-2024/early-2025 model versions, already superseded by 2025–2026 video-native frontier models.

contamination

Only checks that the question text alone doesn't leak the answer (Gemini 1.5 Pro scores <15% text-only); it does not check whether the underlying YouTube video's content (a documentary, viral clip, or news event) was memorized separately through captions or web text during pretraining.

what's missed

Multiple-choice-only format (25% guessing floor, no free-response or generation evaluation); the same model used to filter questions (Gemini 1.5 Pro) is later benchmarked, a mild circularity; subtitle timing is capped to sampled frames rather than the full transcript.

would raise confidence

A video-level contamination audit (not just question-text), an option-order/guessing-floor calibration check, a free-response variant of a question subset, and re-running the same 900 videos against 2026-era video-native models to see whether the long-video gap has actually closed.

// bottom line

Video-MME earns its "comprehensive" framing on scale and duration/domain diversity more than any prior open video benchmark at the time, and the subtitle/audio ablations are a genuinely useful multimodal-contribution signal. But the multiple-choice format, the single-model contamination filter, and the now-dated model roster mean its absolute numbers (Gemini 1.5 Pro's 75%) are best read as a 2024-era snapshot of a real, still-open gap — long-video performance decay — rather than a settled measurement of current video-MLLM capability.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. Video-MME assembles 900 manually-annotated YouTube videos (11 seconds to 1 hour) across 6 domains and 30 subfields into 2,700 multiple-choice QA pairs — explicitly designed to cover the duration and domain breadth that prior video QA benchmarks (MSRVTT-QA, ActivityNet-QA, EgoSchema, and others) lacked in combination.
  2. Gemini 1.5 Pro leads commercial models at 75.0% overall accuracy (frames only), ahead of GPT-4o (71.9%); the best open-source model, VILA-1.5 (34B), reaches only 59.0% — and every model's accuracy declines as video length grows (Gemini 1.5 Pro: 81.7% short → 67.4% long).
  3. Subtitles and audio are not redundant with frames: adding subtitles lifts Gemini 1.5 Pro to 81.3% overall (+6.2 points) and audio to 79.4% (+4.3 points), with the largest gains concentrated in exactly the long-video, multilingual, and sports-competition cases where frame-only performance is weakest.
