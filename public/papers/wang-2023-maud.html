MAUD: An Expert-Annotated Legal NLP Dataset for Merger Agreement Understanding

The first large-scale, expert-annotated reading-comprehension benchmark for contract review — 47,457 lawyer-verified annotations over 152 public merger agreements, recasting the American Bar Association's 2021 deal-point study into 144 multiple-choice questions where fine-tuned encoders still top out near 58% AUPR.

Steven H. Wang, Antoine Scardigli, Leonard Tang, Wei Chen, Dimitry Levkin, Anya Chen, Spencer Ball, Thomas Woodside, Oliver Zhang, Dan Hendrycks

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

Pretrained Transformers had already matched or beaten humans on general-domain reading comprehension — SQuAD 2.0 and SuperGLUE were effectively solved. But those benchmarks are short, everyday text. Whether a model can read and interpret real, specialized legal prose was largely untested, and legal text is exactly where length and ambiguity bite hardest.

Lawyers reviewing a contract do two distinct things. First they extract the relevant clauses out of a long document (a span-extraction task); then they interpret what those clauses mean (a reading-comprehension task). The same lab's earlier benchmark, CUAD (Hendrycks et al., 2021), supplied a large expert-annotated dataset for the extraction half. But for the interpretation half there was, the authors note, no large-scale, expert-annotated reading-comprehension dataset for contract review — or for any English-language legal task at all.

MAUD fills that gap for one of the highest-stakes contract types on earth: merger agreements, the legal documents behind public-company acquisitions valued at hundreds of billions of dollars a year. Their pivotal clauses are called "deal points" — definitions like what counts as a "Material Adverse Effect," or whether a deal's consideration is a fixed ratio versus a fixed value — and reading them correctly is precisely the interpretive judgment that highly trained M&A attorneys are paid to make.

// how it works

The method

MAUD is built directly on the American Bar Association's 2021 Public Target Deal Points Study. For that study, an ABA-appointed M&A attorney designed 130 standardized deal-point questions reflecting current deal trends; 92 of them are represented in MAUD. The task: given a deal-point question and the extracted deal-point text, a model predicts the correct answer(s) from a predefined, ABA-standardized list of choices (Figure 1). Several questions are multilabel, so the authors recast every multilabel question as a set of binary multiple-choice questions — raising the effective question count from 92 to 144.

The corpus. 152 English-language public merger agreements pulled from the SEC's EDGAR filing system, annotated into 47,457 total annotations — comprising 8,226 unique deal-point text annotations and 39,231 question–answer pairs (the "examples" a model trains on). Each text carries one of 22 deal-point types, grouped into 7 scoring categories: General Information, Conditions to Closing, Material Adverse Effect, Knowledge, Deal Protection & Related Provisions, Operating & Efforts Covenants, and Remedies.

The expensive part — annotation. MAUD took over 10,000 hours of work by 36 annotators (law students and experienced lawyers). Before labeling, each law student went through 70–100 hours of training, including lectures and workshops from practicing M&A lawyers. Each annotation was produced by a team of three students by consensus, then reviewed by an experienced M&A lawyer; disagreements escalated to a panel of 3–5 expert lawyers. The authors estimate the dataset's pecuniary value at over $5 million, using a prevailing $500/hour M&A rate.

Three sub-datasets, one careful split

MAUD ships as three datasets reflecting how examples were generated:

  • Main (20,623 examples) — original deal-point text extracted verbatim from the 152 agreements.
  • Abridged (14,928 examples) — joined substrings of the main texts, with an <omitted> delimiter marking skipped passages, to teach a model to focus on the pertinent text.
  • Rare Answers (3,680 examples) — main-dataset texts that legal experts minimally edited to manufacture rare answer classes that otherwise appeared in too few contracts to allow a train/dev/test split at all.

The split reserves a random 20% of the combined main + abridged data as the test set; the remainder plus all rare-answer examples is split 80/20 into train/dev, stratified by question–answer pair. To prevent leakage, main examples are split first, and abridged examples from the same contract follow into the same split. One consequence worth holding onto: the rare-answer examples appear only in train/dev (0 in test).

Metric. Because most questions have heavily imbalanced answer distributions, the headline metric is minority-class AUPR (area under the precision–recall curve), computed per answer, averaged into a per-question score, then averaged over all questions and over three runs.

Models. The baselines are fine-tuned encoders. In the single-task setting one model is fine-tuned per question: BERT-base (110M), RoBERTa-base (125M), LegalBERT-base (110M), DeBERTa-v3-base (184M), and BigBird-base (127M). In the multi-task setting a single encoder gets 144 classification heads; they run RoBERTa, LegalBERT, and DeBERTa (BERT omitted as dominated, BigBird omitted for compute). Crucially, 50.0% of texts across examples (27.6% of unique texts) exceed RoBERTa's 512-token limit — which is why BigBird (4,096-token sparse attention) is included; every other model truncates inputs to 512 tokens.

// what they found

The results

Models clear random comfortably but plateau well short of "solved." In the single-task setting the overall AUPR runs BERT 52.6% → RoBERTa 55.5% → LegalBERT 55.9% → DeBERTa 57.1% → BigBird 57.8% (best), against a random baseline of 16.8%. In the multi-task setting: RoBERTa 51.4%, LegalBERT 55.8%, DeBERTa 53.0%. Single-task models beat their multi-task counterparts by about 4 points for RoBERTa and DeBERTa, while LegalBERT is roughly a tie.

The category structure is the real story. The easy categories are near-saturated — Remedies (single-task LegalBERT 100%), General Information (DeBERTa 87.2%), Operating & Efforts (LegalBERT 89.0%). But the three hardest sit near a coin-flip-adjusted floor: Deal Protections (best single-task 58.6%), Material Adverse Effect (50.9%), and Conditions to Closing (48.2%). These are exactly the categories with the longest texts — Material Adverse Effect has 99.0% of its texts over 512 tokens, Conditions to Closing 43.9%, Deal Protections 21.7% — and the ones attorneys themselves report finding the hardest to review.

Legal pretraining helps. LegalBERT beats vanilla BERT and slightly edges RoBERTa single-task, and beats DeBERTa multi-task; it posts the top micro-F1 in every category except Knowledge, and the top macro-F1 in all but three. Scale helps too — the +23.7 pp gain from 5%→100% of the data is the paper's argument for why a dataset this large was worth building. And the companion extraction task (released in the appendix) is harder still: a RoBERTa-base baseline scores just 19.7% AUPR, far below the 42.6% RoBERTa managed on CUAD's extraction task — a reminder that finding the clauses is even harder than interpreting them.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

MAUD's data is excellent; its baselines are where the 2023 framing shows its age. Every model evaluated is a fine-tuned encoder in the 110–184M-parameter range. There is no zero- or few-shot LLM baseline — no GPT-3, GPT-3.5, or GPT-4 — even though the paper cites GPT-3 and flags "prompt-friendly" question descriptions as future work. So the headline "substantial room for improvement" is measured against small encoders, not the in-context-learning regime that, by the time the paper landed at EMNLP, was the obvious comparison for reading comprehension. We simply don't learn whether a prompted frontier model would crush these clauses or trip over them.

Two structural quirks make the numbers slightly rosier than they look. The abridged texts are constructed — joined substrings with an <omitted> marker — and abridged subscores are systematically higher than main subscores (e.g. multi-task LegalBERT 73.3% abridged vs 70.0% main). That gap is partly the abridging itself leaking a relevance signal: the dataset has already done some of the "find the pertinent text" work, so a test set mixing abridged examples is optimistic relative to raw, un-pre-segmented contracts. And run-to-run variability is 1–2% AUPR, which means several of the model "rankings" — single-task BigBird 57.8% vs DeBERTa 57.1% vs RoBERTa 55.5% — sit inside the noise band. The trends (legal pretraining wins; bigger/newer is better) are real but thin.

On annotation quality the authors are refreshingly candid: they did not retain the records needed to compute inter-annotator agreement. The only agreement signal is that reviewing lawyers "agreed about 80% of the time" with student annotations, plus a tiny post-hoc audit of the hardest category (an independent attorney disagreed with the gold label on just 3 of 440 Material Adverse Effect labels — and on review, all three were resolved in favor of the gold label). Reassuring, but small, and there is no IAA number characterizing label noise where it would matter most.

baselines

Fine-tuned encoders only (BERT / RoBERTa / LegalBERT / DeBERTa / BigBird, 110–184M). No zero- or few-shot LLM baseline despite citing GPT-3 — the in-context regime that defined 2023 is left untested.

contamination

Contracts are public EDGAR filings and the data is CC-BY on GitHub, so any future LLM eval risks train/test leakage. Internally, leakage between main/abridged is handled (same-contract examples kept in one split), but the constructed "abridged" texts arguably leak a relevance cue, which likely contributes to their higher subscores vs. main.

what's missed

512-token truncation (every model but BigBird) might be expected to handicap the longest categories (Material Adverse Effect: 99% long texts) — but BigBird's full 4,096-token context still scores only 50.9% AUPR on Material Adverse Effect, barely above the truncated models, so context length alone does not explain why these categories are hard. Model gaps often fall inside the 1–2% run-to-run noise. No inter-annotator-agreement figure.

would raise confidence

Add prompted frontier-LLM baselines; report IAA on the hard categories; test generalization beyond the 2021 / >$200M / US-public snapshot; and evaluate end-to-end on raw contracts (extraction → interpretation) rather than pre-extracted, partly-abridged spans.

Coverage caveats, in the paper's own words. The 152 agreements cover most — but not all — US public-target acquisitions above $200M that closed in 2021: a single-year, large-cap, US-only snapshot, and only 92 of the ABA's 130 questions are represented. The authors explicitly warn that models trained on MAUD may be weaker on private, sub-$200M, or other-year deals, and that MAUD "should not be used as the sole source" for an M&A review tool. The synthetic Rare Answers set lives only in train/dev, so rare-answer performance is never measured on held-out test.

// bottom line

MAUD is a genuinely hard, genuinely expert benchmark — $5M of M&A lawyer time compressed into 144 multiple-choice questions about real merger clauses. Its 2023 encoder baselines are already a generation behind, but the annotations are the asset: the ~58% ceiling on Deal Protections, Material Adverse Effect, and Conditions to Closing marks exactly where contract-reading models still fail, and MAUD now lives on as a task suite inside LegalBench for measuring modern LLMs against expert legal judgment.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. MAUD is the first large-scale, expert-annotated reading-comprehension benchmark for contract review — 152 merger agreements → 47,457 lawyer-verified annotations → 144 ABA-standardized multiple-choice questions — the interpretation counterpart to CUAD's extraction benchmark.
  2. Fine-tuned encoders clear random (16.8% AUPR) easily but plateau around 56–58% overall, collapsing toward ~50% on the three categories with the longest clauses — Material Adverse Effect, Deal Protections, and Conditions to Closing — the same ones human attorneys find hardest.
  3. The lasting contribution is the annotation, not the model: legal-domain pretraining (LegalBERT) and raw dataset scale both measurably help (+23.7 pp going from 5%→100% of the data), and MAUD now serves as a task suite inside LegalBench for evaluating frontier LLMs.
