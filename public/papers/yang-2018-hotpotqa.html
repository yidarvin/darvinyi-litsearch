HotpotQA: A Dataset for Diverse, Explainable Multi-hop Question Answering

The Wikipedia benchmark that made multi-hop reasoning and explanation first-class in QA — 112,779 crowd-written questions that each force a model to chain facts across two documents, shipped with the sentence-level supporting facts that justify the answer, and a distractor-vs-open-web design that became the substrate of the agentic-retrieval era.

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

By 2018 question answering had a depth problem. The datasets that drove the field's progress mostly rewarded matching a question against a single nearby span of text. In SQuAD (Rajpurkar et al., 2016) a question is answerable from one paragraph — often one sentence — so it never tests reasoning across a larger context. TriviaQA and SearchQA raise the bar by attaching several retrieved documents to each question, but because those documents are gathered after the fact by information retrieval, the answer still usually sits within a few adjacent sentences of one paragraph; the extra context is noise to filter, not a second hop to take.

The datasets that did target multi-hop reasoning — QAngaroo and ComplexWebQuestions — were generated from knowledge-base schemas, so their questions inherit a fixed relation set and the diversity of what can be asked is capped by the KB. And none of them tell you why an answer is right: they provide only the final answer (distant supervision), so a model can be correct for the wrong reasons and the dataset gives you no way to tell, nor to supervise the reasoning itself.

HotpotQA goes after four things at once: (1) questions that genuinely require finding and reasoning over multiple supporting documents; (2) free-text diversity not bound to any KB schema; (3) sentence-level supporting facts that double as strong supervision and as an explainability target; and (4) a new comparison question type that forces a system to extract and compare properties of two entities. The name, per the authors' footnote, comes from the first three authors arriving at the idea over a hot pot dinner.

// how it works

The method

The hard part is eliciting genuine multi-hop questions: hand a crowd worker two arbitrary paragraphs and they can rarely write a meaningful one. HotpotQA's pipeline solves this with Wikipedia's own link structure. The authors build a directed graph G over English Wikipedia where an edge (a, b) means article a's first paragraph hyperlinks to article b. A pair along such an edge shares a natural bridge entity b — to ask "when was the singer-songwriter of Radiohead born?" you hop through the bridge entity Thom Yorke. Bridge entities are restricted to a manually curated set (drawn from 591 categories of popular pages) so the pairs actually support an interesting question and don't bottom out on over-linked, generic entities like countries.

A second track produces the new comparison questions: sample two entities from one of 42 lists of similar entities (e.g. two rock bands) and ask the worker to compare them — including yes/no comparisons ("Are Iron Maiden and AC/DC from the same country?") that can't be shortcut from a single article. For every question the worker also highlights the sentences they used: the supporting facts. Collected on Amazon Mechanical Turk through the ParlAI interface, the pipeline yields 112,779 examples (≈113k).

To keep the test honest, the authors isolate the easy questions. They train a QA model and use its confidence to triage: questions answerable from a single paragraph become train-easy (18,089), confidently-answered multi-hop questions become train-medium (56,814), and the residue of genuinely hard multi-hop questions is reserved — train-hard (15,661) plus 7,405 each for dev, test-distractor, and test-fullwiki — so the dev and test sets are hard by construction.

Two settings, three pairs of metrics

In the distractor setting the model sees the 2 gold paragraphs plus 8 bigram-tf-idf-retrieved distractors, shuffled — this isolates reasoning from retrieval. In the fullwiki (open-domain) setting the gold paragraphs are withheld and the model must find them among 5,000,000+ Wikipedia first-paragraphs (tf-idf narrows to a pool, then the top-10 are kept) — QA in the wild. Scoring uses exact-match and F1 on the answer, EM/F1 on the predicted supporting-fact set, and joint metrics that multiply the two, so a system is credited only when it gets both the answer and its justification right:

P(joint) = P(ans)·P(sup)    R(joint) = R(ans)·R(sup)    Joint F1 = 2·P(joint)·R(joint)P(joint) + R(joint) // answer and supporting facts must both be right — the metric penalizes getting one without the other

The baseline reader reimplements Clark and Gardner (2017): character- and word-level embeddings, bi-attention, and self-attention — a configuration that subsumed the SQuAD state of the art of the day — with a 3-way yes/no/span classifier and an added per-sentence binary classifier that predicts supporting facts, trained jointly with the answer objective in a multi-task setup ("strong supervision").

// what they found

The results

Even with the gold paragraphs mixed into the context, the task is hard. In the distractor setting the baseline reaches 45.46 EM / 58.99 F1 on the answer (test), but the joint metric — answer and supporting facts both correct — is only 12.04 EM / 41.37 F1. Open-domain is far harder: fullwiki answer F1 falls to 34.40 and joint F1 to 17.85 (test).

The collapse is mostly retrieval. In fullwiki, the tf-idf retriever lands both gold paragraphs in the top-10 only 56.06% of the time (Hits@10, dev) at a mean rank around 315. The question-type breakdown confirms the diagnosis: bridge questions — which need both hops retrieved — crater from 59.09 → 30.42 F1 between distractor and fullwiki, while comparison questions, where both named entities are easy to find, barely move (55.05 → 50.70).

The supporting facts earn their keep. Feeding only the gold supporting sentences as context lifts the model to 51.95 EM / 66.98 F1 — roughly 10 F1 over the gold-paragraph baseline (63.58) — so the right two sentences really are most of the task. Training with supporting-fact supervision adds a more modest ~2 F1 (58.28 vs 56.19 without it), which the authors candidly call likely suboptimal and leave as future work.

The gap to people is wide on every axis: on a 1,000-example sample, humans answer at 91.40 F1 / 83.60 EM and reach 82.55 joint F1 — roughly 30+ points above the model on both the answer and its justification — with the annotator upper bound near 96–99 F1. And the pipeline does deliver on diversity: question forms fan out across what / which / who / how-many / comparison (Figure 3), and answers span people (30%), groups/orgs (13%), locations, dates, numbers, and yes/no.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

HotpotQA's central guarantee is weaker than the "multi-hop" label suggests. The pipeline ensures a question was authored about two paragraphs — not that answering it requires both. Within a year, several analyses showed a large fraction of HotpotQA is solvable single-hop: a reader given only one paragraph, or a single-hop model, recovers much of the answer accuracy — especially on comparison and "locate-by-properties" (Type II) questions, where entity-type matching ("which team…?" → the only NBA team in the context) hands over the answer. The distractor setting amplifies this: with only 8 tf-idf distractors, the 2 gold paragraphs are often the only on-topic text in the window, so a model can find the answer by topical salience without ever traversing the bridge. Later adversarial-distractor work (Jiang & Bansal, 2019) drives scores down sharply precisely because it removes that shortcut.

The fullwiki number has a different problem: it conflates retrieval and reasoning. With Hits@10 at 56%, the headline open-domain F1 is largely a verdict on a 2018 bigram-tf-idf retriever; swap in a dense retriever and the score moves for reasons that have nothing to do with multi-hop reasoning. The benchmark also fixes reasoning depth: ~98% of questions are exactly two hops (bridge or comparison), with "Other" (>2 supporting facts) at just 2% — so once a model learns that one depth, the ceiling arrives quickly. Finally, the authors themselves note lower inter-annotator agreement on supporting facts than on answers, so the explainability supervision is noisier than it looks, and crowd-written questions carry stylistic regularities a model can exploit.

baselines

A single 2018 BiDAF-style reader (char+word, bi-/self-attention, Clark & Gardner). No pretrained-LM baseline — BERT landed only weeks later — so the reported 58.28 distractor F1 reads as a floor, and undersells how fast the benchmark would fall.

contamination

Built entirely on public English Wikipedia, now in every LLM's pretraining; the distractor leaderboard climbed into the high-70s/80s joint-F1 within ~2 years. Effectively solved and contaminated for frontier models — its live value is as a lineage baseline, not a fresh test.

what's missed

"Written about two paragraphs" ≠ "requires two hops." Follow-ups showed a large share is answerable single-hop; the fullwiki score mostly grades a weak tf-idf retriever (Hits@10 56%), not reasoning; and depth is pinned at 2 hops (>2 = 2%).

would raise confidence

Adversarial (not tf-idf) distractors by default; per-question necessity checks that both hops are actually needed; ≥3-hop items; entity-disjoint train/test splits to block memorization; and reporting retrieval and reasoning as separate axes rather than one fullwiki number.

// bottom line

HotpotQA reframed QA around multi-hop reasoning and made the justification a graded object — a template the field still uses. But its guarantee was that questions were authored over two documents, not that they require two hops, and weak tf-idf distractors plus a fixed two-hop depth left enough shortcuts that "multi-hop" was partly aspirational. Read it as the benchmark that defined the problem and seeded the agentic-retrieval era — and whose leakiness taught the field how hard honest multi-hop evaluation really is.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. Multi-hop and explainable. 112,779 Wikipedia questions that chain two documents, each shipped with the sentence-level supporting facts that justify the answer — making explanation a measurable target via joint EM/F1, not an afterthought.
  2. The pipeline is the contribution. A Wikipedia hyperlink graph + curated bridge entities + comparison lists turns crowd workers into reliable authors of genuinely two-hop questions, and the distractor (reasoning) vs fullwiki (retrieval+reasoning) split became the standard template for open-domain multi-hop QA.
  3. The numbers are humbling and honest. Distractor joint F1 41.37, fullwiki 17.85, against human 82.55 — and the fullwiki collapse traces to retrieval (Hits@10 56%), while oracle supporting-fact context (66.98 F1) shows the right two sentences are most of the task.
  4. It became the field's workhorse — and a cautionary tale. HotpotQA trained and tested the tool-use / agentic-retrieval lineage (ReAct, Reflexion) and is the "too-easy-to-find" baseline that browsing benchmarks like BrowseComp and DeepResearch Bench explicitly move past; the reasoning-shortcut critiques it provoked are themselves a foundational lesson in benchmark design.
