Training Language Models to Follow Instructions with Human Feedback

Making a language model bigger does not make it follow your intent. OpenAI fine-tunes GPT-3 against human judgments of output quality — and the resulting 1.3B InstructGPT is preferred to the 175 billion-parameter original, despite 100× fewer parameters.

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

A pretrained language model is optimized for exactly one thing: predicting the next token on a webpage. That objective is misaligned with what a user actually wants. Asked to "explain the moon landing to a six-year-old," GPT-3 is just as likely to continue the prompt with more instructions, drift off topic, fabricate a fact, or emit something toxic — because none of those failures are penalized by next-token likelihood. Scaling the model up makes it a better web-text predictor, not a better instruction-follower.

Prompting and few-shot examples paper over the gap but don't close it: you are still coaxing a misaligned objective into cooperating, one prompt at a time. The paper attacks the objective itself. Its predecessor, OpenAI's Learn-to-Summarize work, had shown you could optimize a single narrow task (summarization) against human preferences instead of a likelihood or ROUGE target. InstructGPT asks the harder question: can you do the same across the open-ended distribution of real API requests — brainstorming, rewriting, extraction, code, Q&A — and get a model that is broadly aligned to user intent: helpful, more truthful, and less toxic, all at once?

// how it works

The method

Reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF), in three stages, following Stiennon et al. (2020) but generalized from summarization to arbitrary instructions. OpenAI first hired a team of ~40 contractors (screened on Upwork and Scale AI) to produce the human data.

1 — Supervised fine-tuning (SFT). Labelers write demonstrations of the desired behavior on a mix of API-submitted and labeler-written prompts (~13k training prompts). GPT-3 is fine-tuned on these for 16 epochs (cosine LR decay, residual dropout 0.2). This gives a decent instruction-follower — and a starting policy for RL.

2 — Reward model (RM). For each prompt, the SFT model samples K = 4 to 9 outputs; a labeler ranks them best-to-worst. A 6B reward model is trained to score outputs so that the human-preferred completion scores higher, using a pairwise cross-entropy (Bradley–Terry) loss over all (K choose 2) comparisons from a prompt — crucially treated as a single batch element, since scattering correlated comparisons across batches overfit the RM in a single epoch. The RM dataset has ~33k prompts. (A 175B RM was unstable as the RL value function, so they used 6B.)

loss(θ) = −E(x, yw, yl) log σ( rθ(x, yw) − rθ(x, yl) ) // reward model: yw is the labeler-preferred output, yl the rejected one — the score gap is the log-odds of the human preference

3 — Reinforcement learning (PPO). The RM score becomes the reward, and the SFT policy is optimized against it with PPO. A per-token KL penalty (coefficient β = 0.02) pins the policy to the SFT model so it can't wander into high-reward gibberish that games the frozen RM. To stop the model from regressing on standard NLP benchmarks (the "alignment tax", below), they also mix in the original pretraining gradients — using 8× as many pretraining examples as RL episodes — yielding the PPO-ptx variant. The combined objective:

objective(φ) = E(x, y)∼πRL [ rθ(x, y) − β log πRLφ(y|x)πSFT(y|x) ] + γ Ex∼Dpretrain [ log πRLφ(x) ] // reward − KL-leash-to-SFT + pretraining-mix (γ>0 ⇒ PPO-ptx). Models come in 1.3B, 6B, 175B (GPT-3 architecture).
// what they found

The results

The headline is a scale inversion: on OpenAI's API prompt distribution, outputs from the 1.3B InstructGPT (PPO-ptx) are preferred to those from the 175B GPT-3, a model with over 100× more parameters. Measured directly head-to-head, 175B InstructGPT is preferred to 175B GPT-3 85 ± 3% of the time, and to few-shot 175B GPT-3 71 ± 4% of the time (95% confidence intervals throughout). Win-rates are computed against the 175B SFT model as a mid-pack anchor; PPO and PPO-ptx clear the 0.5 line at every model size, while the GPT-3 baselines sit far below.

The gains are not just "labelers like it more." On per-output metadata judgments, InstructGPT attempts the correct instruction far more often, follows explicit constraints roughly twice as often as GPT-3 (~0.43 vs ~0.22), and roughly halves closed-domain hallucinations — from ~0.41 for GPT-3 down to ~0.17 for PPO-ptx.

On TruthfulQA, InstructGPT gives truthful-and-informative answers about twice as often as GPT-3 (and the gain holds on the subset of questions not adversarially selected against GPT-3). On RealToxicityPrompts, it generates ~25% fewer toxic outputs than GPT-3 when prompted to be respectful — but it does not improve on bias benchmarks. The "alignment tax" is real: plain PPO regresses versus GPT-3 on SQuADv2, DROP, HellaSwag, and WMT'15 Fr→En translation; PPO-ptx mitigates these (it even surpasses GPT-3 on HellaSwag) though it still lags on DROP, SQuADv2, and translation. Encouragingly, the gains generalize to held-out labelers who produced no training data (inter-annotator agreement was 72.6 ± 1.5% for training labelers, 77.3 ± 1.3% for held-out).

// does it hold up?

The eval read

"A 1.3B model beats 175B" is the line everyone quotes — but read the y-axis. It is a preference win-rate on OpenAI's own API prompt distribution, judged by ~40 contractors trained on OpenAI's labeling instructions. That measures alignment to a specific preference spec (the researchers' instructions, proxied through screened labelers who agree with each other only ~73% of the time), not raw capability. The paper is admirably explicit about the tilt: its training prompts "are designed to be used with InstructGPT models," so the evaluation distribution is friendly to instruction-tuned models by construction, and GPT-3 is a deliberately handicapped baseline — never optimized for this instruction format, then graded on it.

The deeper risk is one this paper names but cannot fully chart: reward-model overoptimization. PPO against a frozen 6B RM is the textbook Goodhart setting — push hard enough and the policy games the proxy. The KL leash (β = 0.02) is the only guard, and there's no sweep here of how far the reward can be exploited before it diverges from true quality (a gap a later OpenAI paper, Scaling Laws for Reward Model Overoptimization, filled). "Truthfulness," meanwhile, is narrow — TruthfulQA plus a closed-domain hallucination rate — and the authors footnote that the automatic TruthfulQA metric overstated the PPO gains. Bias is unchanged. So "aligned" here really means "more helpful and a bit less toxic when asked," not honest or fair in any deep sense.

baselines

GPT-3 (zero/few-shot) was never tuned for the instruction format — fair as "vs the base model," weak as "vs a strong instruction-tuned model." FLAN and T0 are compared separately and also beaten, which helps.

contamination

Not a classic train/test leakage story (open-ended generation, human-judged), but the eval prompt distribution is curated by OpenAI and skewed toward instruction-following — structurally favorable to InstructGPT.

what's missed

Capability and preference are conflated in the headline. Reward-model overoptimization, honesty beyond TruthfulQA, bias, and any non-OpenAI value system are out of scope.

would raise confidence

A reward-vs-KL overoptimization sweep; evaluation on an independent prompt set and labeler pool outside OpenAI's instructions; and head-to-heads against strong instruction-tuned baselines at matched scale.

// bottom line

This is the paper that turned RLHF into the default alignment recipe and made "1.3B beats 175B" a rallying cry — but the claim is about preference alignment on OpenAI's own distribution, not capability. The template it set (SFT → reward model → PPO with a KL leash, plus a pretraining-mix patch for the alignment tax) is what nearly every later post-training paper builds on; its honest limitations — proxy-reward gaming, narrow truthfulness, a small unrepresentative annotator pool — are precisely what the rubric-reward and RLVR line spent the next four years attacking.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. RLHF in three stages became the default post-training recipe. Demonstrations → SFT, rankings → a reward model, PPO against that reward with a KL leash to the SFT policy. The entire rubric-reward / RLVR cluster descends from this pipeline.
  2. Alignment can beat scale — on the right metric. 1.3B InstructGPT is preferred to 175B GPT-3, and 175B InstructGPT wins 85 ± 3% head-to-head. But this is a human-preference win-rate on OpenAI's API distribution, not a capability claim — keep the two separate.
  3. Alignment has a tax and a price. Helpfulness and lower toxicity come with regressions on SQuAD/DROP/translation (patched, not erased, by mixing pretraining gradients), unchanged bias, and "honesty" only as far as TruthfulQA — the exact limits later work was built to address.
