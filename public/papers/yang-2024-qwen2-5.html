Qwen2.5 Technical Report

Alibaba's open-weight LLM family — seven dense sizes (0.5B–72B) plus two MoE API models — pre-trained on 18 trillion tokens and aligned with a staged SFT → DPO → GRPO pipeline. The flagship 72B-Instruct trades blows with Llama-3-405B-Instruct at roughly a fifth of the parameters.

Qwen Team — An Yang, Baosong Yang, Beichen Zhang, Binyuan Hui, Bo Zheng, Bowen Yu, Dayiheng Liu, Fei Huang, Jingren Zhou, Junyang Lin, et al. (Alibaba)

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

By late 2024 the open-weight LLM race had a clear shape: Meta's Llama and Mistral led the West, and the question was whether an open model could close the gap to the best proprietary systems (GPT-4o, Claude 3.5) and the largest open one (Llama-3-405B) without a 400-billion-parameter budget. Qwen2.5 is Alibaba's answer — and its predecessor Qwen2 left obvious headroom.

The report frames three concrete shortfalls of Qwen2. Data: Qwen2 saw only 7T pre-training tokens, leaving knowledge, math, and code under-fed. Coverage: Qwen2 skipped the 3B / 14B / 32B sizes that are the sweet spot for resource-limited deployment and are under-represented across open models. Usability: generations capped at ~2K tokens, weak structured I/O (tables, JSON), clumsy tool use, and short context windows.

The deeper agenda is a recipe, not a single model: scale and re-balance the pre-training corpus, then layer a much heavier post-training stack (a million-plus SFT examples and two-stage reinforcement learning) so the same base can be specialized into Qwen2.5-Math, Qwen2.5-Coder, QwQ, and multimodal variants. That recipe is what made Qwen2.5 the de-facto open base for the post-DeepSeek-R1 wave of RL-with-verifiable-rewards work.

// how it works

The method

The architecture is deliberately conventional — a Transformer decoder with Grouped-Query Attention (cheaper KV cache), SwiGLU activations, Rotary Position Embeddings, QKV bias, and pre-norm RMSNorm. Dense models span 0.5B / 1.5B / 3B / 7B / 14B / 32B / 72B; the two API-only models, Qwen2.5-Turbo and Qwen2.5-Plus, swap the feed-forward layers for fine-grained Mixture-of-Experts with shared-expert routing. The tokenizer is byte-level BPE with a 151,643-token vocabulary, and the control-token set was expanded from 3 to 22 (including two new tokens for tool use).

Pre-training: scale × mixture

The corpus grew from 7T to 18 trillion tokens, but the report's thesis is that mixture matters as much as size. Four levers: (1) Qwen2-Instruct models act as multi-dimensional quality scorers to filter samples; (2) math and code data are seeded directly from Qwen2.5-Math and Qwen2.5-Coder; (3) synthetic data is generated by Qwen2-72B-Instruct and Qwen2-Math-72B-Instruct, then re-filtered by a reward model; and (4) the mixture is re-balanced — down-sampling over-represented web domains (e-commerce, social media, entertainment) and up-sampling technology, science, and academic text. Hyper-parameters (batch size, learning rate) are set by scaling laws fit across 44M–14B dense and 44M–1B-active MoE models on 0.8B–600B-token runs.

Long context

Pre-training runs in two phases: a 4,096-token phase, then an extension to 32,768 tokens for every model except Turbo, with the RoPE base frequency raised from 10,000 to 1,000,000 (the ABF technique). Qwen2.5-Turbo instead climbs progressively — 32K → 64K → 128K → 256K — at a RoPE base of 10,000,000, training on 40% max-length and 60% shorter sequences at each stage. At inference, YARN + Dual Chunk Attention quadruple the usable length, giving Turbo a 1M-token window and the others 128K.

Post-training: SFT, then two-stage RL

Supervised fine-tuning uses over 1 million examples targeting eight weak spots — long-form generation (up to 8,192 output tokens), math chain-of-thought, ~40-language coding with sandboxed unit-test validation, code-checked instruction following, structured-data reasoning, 70,000 new logical-reasoning queries, cross-lingual transfer, and robust system prompts — fine-tuned for two epochs at 32,768-token sequence length. Reinforcement learning is split: Offline RL uses DPO on ~150,000 verifiable preference pairs (one epoch, Online Merging Optimizer) for tasks with checkable answers; Online RL uses GRPO against a reward model scored on six axes (truthfulness, helpfulness, conciseness, relevance, harmlessness, debiasing), sampling 8 responses per query and prioritizing high-reward-variance queries.

To keep the benchmark numbers honest, the report applies an explicit decontamination filter: a training sequence st is dropped if it overlaps any test sequence se too much, by longest-common-subsequence:

remove st  if  |LCS(st, se)| ≥ 13  and  |LCS(st, se)| ≥ 0.6 × min(|st|, |se|) // n-gram / LCS decontamination rule, applied to both pre- and post-training data
// what they found

The results

The flagship claim: Qwen2.5-72B-Instruct matches or beats Llama-3.1-405B-Instruct on several headline benchmarks while being ~5× smaller. On the 70B-tier instruct table it surpasses the 405B model on MMLU-redux (86.8 vs 86.2), MATH (83.1 vs 73.8), MBPP (88.2 vs 84.5), MultiPL-E (75.1 vs 73.5), LiveCodeBench (55.5 vs 41.6), Arena-Hard (81.2 vs 69.3), and MT-Bench (9.35 vs 9.08) — trading the others (GSM8K 95.8 vs 96.8, HumanEval 86.6 vs 89.0, IFEval 84.1 vs 86.0) narrowly.

The base 72B model tells the same story: 86.1 MMLU, 86.3 BBH, 62.1 MATH, 91.5 GSM8K, 84.7 MBPP — comparable to the Llama-3-405B base "while utilizing only one-fifth of the parameters." Smaller sizes punch up too: Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct hits 75.5 on MATH and 84.8 on HumanEval, beating Gemma2-9B-IT and Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct on every benchmark except IFEval.

Long context holds at 1M tokens

On RULER, Qwen2.5-72B-Instruct posts the best average (95.1), ahead of GPT-4 (91.6) and GPT-4o-mini (87.3); at 128K it scores 88.4 with DCA+YARN versus 67.0 without — the extrapolation trick is doing real work at the long end. Qwen2.5-Turbo reaches 100% accuracy on 1M-token passkey retrieval (Figure 2), and a Minference-based sparse-attention scheme cuts attention compute ~12.5× at 1M tokens, giving a 3.2–4.3× time-to-first-token speedup (Figure 3).

// does it hold up?

The eval read

As model reports go, this one is unusually candid in places and unusually opaque in others. The strongest, most reproducible numbers are on contamination-resistant public benchmarks — LiveBench and LiveCodeBench (windowed 2305–2409), both designed to resist leakage — and Qwen2.5 leads there, which is the right place to win. But the headline "matches Llama-3-405B" deserves three asterisks.

First, the comparison target moves: the base-model tables (§5.1, Table 2) compare against Llama-3-405B, while the instruct tables use Llama-3.1-405B, and the conclusion says "six times smaller" where the abstract says "around 5 times." Both are roughly true (405/72 ≈ 5.6×), but the framing is loose. Second, parameter count is the wrong axis for the efficiency story: Qwen2.5-72B is not a smaller model doing more with less compute — it was trained on 18T tokens of heavily curated and synthetic data. It's a data-and-recipe win, and "5× smaller" quietly credits architecture for what is largely corpus engineering.

Third, a large share of the instruct evaluation runs on in-house, unreleased benchmarks (the English, Chinese, and multilingual tables), so the claim that 72B "matches or exceeds Llama-3.1-405B in all metrics except instruction following" partly rests on numbers no one else can reproduce. To the report's credit, the reward-model section openly warns that RM-benchmark scores do not predict downstream RL performance and invokes Goodhart's law — a rare negative result to find in a flagship release.

baselines

Current and reasonable (Llama-3/3.1, Gemma2, Mistral/Mixtral, GPT-4o-mini), but the "405B" reference flips between Llama-3 and Llama-3.1 across base vs. instruct claims, and several 405B-base cells are simply missing ("-").

contamination

A real LCS decontamination rule is disclosed and applied — better than most reports — but it is self-administered on private training data, with no external audit. Large base→instruct math jumps (7B MATH 49.8→75.5) sit beside heavy synthetic-math seeding.

what's missed

MoE total/active parameter counts for Turbo and Plus are undisclosed, so the "more cost-effective than GPT-4o" claim can't be independently checked. The "5× smaller" framing credits scale-efficiency for what is mostly data quality.

would raise confidence

More weight on frozen, third-party leaderboards instead of in-house tables; disclosed MoE sizes; and released decontamination details so the math/code gains can be audited against the test sets.

// bottom line

A genuinely strong, broadly capable open family with an honest streak (decontamination disclosed, reward-model caveat stated). The capability gains are real on leakage-resistant benchmarks; the "matches a 5×-larger model" headline is best read as a triumph of data curation and post-training, not of parameter efficiency per se.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. 18T tokens + staged post-training beat raw scale. A 72B model matches a 405B one on MATH (83.1 vs 73.8), Arena-Hard (81.2 vs 69.3), and LiveCodeBench (55.5 vs 41.6) — the win is the corpus and the SFT→DPO→GRPO stack, not the parameter count.
  2. The full size ladder is the strategic move. Seven dense sizes (0.5B–72B) plus a 1M-token Turbo made Qwen2.5 the default open base for the post-R1 RLVR wave — DAPO, General-Reasoner, and RLVR-limit all train on Qwen2.5 bases.
  3. A candid reward-model caveat. The report states that reward-model benchmark scores do not predict the RL models they guide, and warns of Goodhart's law — a useful corrective baked into an otherwise promotional document.
