METEOR: An Automatic Metric for MT Evaluation with Improved Correlation with Human Judgments

BLEU counts n-gram precision and stops there. METEOR builds an explicit word-to-word alignment — matching on surface form, stem, and WordNet synonym — then scores it with recall weighted nine times over precision plus a fragmentation penalty for word order. The payoff: a metric that tracks human judgment one sentence at a time, where BLEU's geometric average collapses to zero.

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

By 2005, BLEU (and its close relative NIST) had made automatic MT evaluation cheap, fast, and ubiquitous — the DARPA TIDES program ran on it. But the authors, who had been studying these metrics empirically, argued that BLEU has structural blind spots that hurt it most exactly where you most want a metric to work: scoring a single sentence. Four weaknesses motivate METEOR:

  • No recall. BLEU's core is n-gram precision — matched n-grams over candidate n-grams. It never measures recall (did the translation cover the reference's content?), because recall is awkward to define against multiple references. Instead it bolts on a brevity penalty as a crude proxy. The authors believed — and their experiments confirm — that this under-compensates, and that recall is the more important half.
  • Higher-order n-grams as a stand-in for word order. BLEU uses 2-, 3-, 4-gram precision as an indirect signal of grammaticality. The authors argue an explicit measure of how well-ordered the matched words are would capture word order more faithfully.
  • No explicit word-to-word matching. Counting n-grams doesn't pin each candidate word to a specific reference word, so it can credit spurious matches — especially of common function words.
  • Geometric averaging zeroes out. BLEU combines its n-gram precisions with a geometric mean, so if any single order has zero matches the whole score is zero. Over a corpus that's rare; on one sentence it's common, which makes sentence-level BLEU largely meaningless — and BLEU was only ever intended for corpus-level aggregate scoring.

That last point is the crux. A metric you can trust per sentence is what lets you detect small differences between systems, or between versions of one system during development. The authors note that segment-level correlations with humans were "still rather low" across the board — leaving real room to improve.

// how it works

The method

The name spells out the idea: Metric for Evaluation of Translation with Explicit ORdering. METEOR scores a candidate translation against a reference by first building an explicit alignment between their words, then scoring that alignment with a recall-heavy F-measure discounted by a word-order penalty.

1 · Build a word-to-word alignment in stages

An alignment maps every unigram in each string to at most one unigram in the other. METEOR builds it incrementally through a series of stages, each running two phases:

  • Phase 1 — propose. A mapping module lists all possible unigram matches between the two strings. Three modules, applied in order: exact (identical surface forms), Porter stem (identical after Porter stemming, so "computers" matches "computer"), and WN synonymy (the two words share a WordNet synset — a "poor-man's" synonymy with no sense disambiguation).
  • Phase 2 — select. Choose the largest subset of proposed matches that still forms a valid alignment. If several subsets tie on size, keep the one with the fewest crossing matches. Two matches (ti→rj) and (tk→rl) cross when:
( pos(ti) − pos(tk) ) · ( pos(rj) − pos(rl) ) < 0 // a negative product means the lines drawn between matched words cross — penalised in phase 2 (a variant of Turian et al. 2003)

Each stage only maps words left unmapped by earlier stages, so the stage order imposes a priority: by default match exactly first, then by stem, then by synonym.

2 · Score the alignment: recall-weighted F, then a penalty

From the final alignment, precision P is matched unigrams over the candidate length, and recall R is matched unigrams over the reference length. METEOR combines them with a harmonic mean that puts nine times the weight on recall:

Fmean = 10 · P · RR + 9 · P // harmonic mean of P and R, weighted toward recall (van Rijsbergen, 1979)

Fmean only sees unigrams, so it ignores word order entirely. To put order back in, METEOR groups the matched words into the fewest possible chunks — runs of words that are adjacent in both strings — and penalises fragmentation. For "the president spoke to the audience" vs. the reference "the president then spoke to the audience" there are two chunks ("the president" / "spoke to the audience"). The more chunks relative to matched words, the bigger the penalty:

Penalty = 0.5 · # chunks# matched unigrams3 // one chunk (perfect order) → penalty near 0; many chunks → up to a maximum of 0.5
Score = Fmean · ( 1 − Penalty ) // no bigram-or-longer matches ⇒ penalty hits 0.5 ⇒ Fmean is halved

With multiple references, METEOR scores the candidate against each one and keeps the best. A whole-system score is computed the same way, on precision, recall, and penalty aggregated over the test set — mirroring how BLEU aggregates. The 9:1 recall weight and the penalty's 0.5 coefficient and cube exponent were hand-set on development data; the paper flags learning them as future work.

// what they found

The results

METEOR is evaluated on the DARPA/TIDES 2003 MT data released for the ACL 2005 workshop: Arabic→English (664 sentences, 6 systems) and Chinese→English (920 sentences, 7 systems), each sentence with four reference translations and assessed by two human judges for adequacy and fluency (1–5). A translation's human score is the average of those; the metric's quality is the Pearson correlation between metric scores and human scores.

System level: recall alone already beats BLEU

Aggregating to one score per system on the Chinese data, METEOR and its sub-components correlate with human system rankings far better than BLEU or NIST. The striking result: plain recall — the thing BLEU refuses to measure — already lifts correlation from BLEU's 0.817 to 0.941.

The paper's real focus is per-sentence correlation. Here each piece of the scoring function contributes — recall >> precision, Fmean beats either alone, and the fragmentation penalty adds a final nudge — but the absolute numbers are modest (≈0.33–0.35), which the authors openly call "still rather low."

One last diagnostic: when the human labels are denoised (normalized by a group at MITRE), METEOR's correlation jumps from 0.347 → 0.403 on Arabic and 0.331 → 0.365 on Chinese — evidence that a big chunk of the residual "error" is noise in the human judgments, not in the metric.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

METEOR's core diagnosis of BLEU is correct and has aged well — recall matters, explicit alignment beats blind n-gram counting, and sentence-level scoring is where it counts. But read the tables closely and the win is more measured than the legend.

The headline contrast — METEOR 0.964 vs. BLEU 0.817 — is the system-level table, and it carries two caveats. First, those correlations are computed over just seven systems; a Pearson R on seven points is high-variance, and the gaps between F1 (0.948), Fmean (0.952) and METEOR (0.964) are almost certainly within noise, yet are reported to three decimals as if separable. Second, BLEU was explicitly designed for system-level aggregate scoring — the paper itself concedes that scoring sentences with BLEU "is unfair to those algorithms." So the place METEOR is built to win (the segment level, where BLEU's geometric averaging zeroes out) is also the place BLEU is handicapped by construction. That's a fair motivation, but the framing tilts the field toward METEOR's home turf.

At the segment level — where the comparison is fair — METEOR's edge over its own Fmean is small (0.347 vs. 0.340 on Arabic, 0.331 vs. 0.327 on Chinese): the chunk penalty barely moves the needle, and the WordNet-synonymy stage adds only ~0.02 on top of exact+stemming. Most of the lift is recall, then stemming. And the parameters that do the work — the 9:1 recall weight, the 0.5 penalty coefficient, the cube exponent — were hand-set on a separate development set rather than learned, and, in the authors' own words, "have not yet been trained to be optimal" — so how well they generalize beyond this setup is an open question.

Finally, METEOR never escapes surface lexical matching. Stemming and WordNet synonyms widen the net, but a correct paraphrase outside WordNet earns nothing, a fluent-but-wrong translation that reuses reference words can still score well, and the synonymy module is English-only — so it can't help when the target language isn't English. This is the ceiling that learned, embedding-based metrics (BERTScore, BLEURT, COMET) and later LLM-as-judge evaluators (G-Eval) were built to break through.

baselines

The right contrasts — BLEU, NIST, and a clean component ablation (Precision / Recall / F1 / Fmean / +penalty). But the system-level comparison runs on only 6–7 systems with no significance tests on the deltas, and it pits BLEU on the segment level where its own geometric averaging cripples it.

contamination

No learned model, so no train/test leakage in the usual sense. The one caveat: the recall weight and penalty constants were hand-set on a separate development set and, the authors note, "have not yet been trained to be optimal" — a hand-tuning / generalization concern they flag for future work, not contamination.

what's missed

Still surface matching — no semantics beyond WordNet synonyms, no paraphrase, English-only synonymy, and absolute segment-level correlation only ≈0.33. The chunk penalty and synonymy stage each contribute little; recall and stemming do most of the work.

would raise confidence

Many more MT systems per language (n ≫ 7) plus significance tests for the system-level claim; parameters learned with held-out validation rather than hand-tuned; and evaluation across more domains and into non-English target languages.

// bottom line

METEOR correctly named BLEU's blind spots — no recall, no explicit alignment, meaningless sentence-level scores — and fixed them with a recall-weighted, alignment-based score that tracks humans better, especially per sentence. The absolute correlations are modest and the parameters are hand-tuned, but the diagnosis was right and durable. It's the pivotal middle node of the automatic-metric lineage: the bridge from pure n-gram counting to the learned and LLM-based evaluators that followed, and still the standard lexical companion reported alongside BLEU two decades later.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. Recall > precision for MT eval. METEOR's biggest single lesson: plain recall correlated 0.941 with human system rankings vs. BLEU's 0.817, so METEOR's Fmean weights recall nine times over precision.
  2. Explicit alignment + matching beyond exact strings + an order penalty makes a metric work per sentence. Stem and synonym matching over a crossing-minimised word alignment, scored by a recall-heavy F-mean discounted for fragmentation, succeeds at the segment level where BLEU's geometric average collapses to zero.
  3. It's hand-tuned surface matching with a low absolute ceiling (~0.33 segment-level R). That recognised ceiling is exactly what motivated learned (BERTScore / BLEURT / COMET) and LLM-as-judge (G-Eval) metrics — yet METEOR remains the lexical metric reported next to BLEU to this day.
