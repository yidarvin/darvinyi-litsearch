Solving math word problems with process- and outcome-based feedback

DeepMind's first head-to-head of process- vs. outcome-based supervision on a natural-language reasoning task — and the surprise finding that an outcome-only reward model quietly learns to check reasoning steps anyway.

Jonathan Uesato, Nate Kushman, Ramana Kumar, Francis Song, Noah Siegel, Lisa Wang, Antonia Creswell, Geoffrey Irving, Irina Higgins · DeepMind
venue arXiv citations 814 topic Post-training & RL source ↗

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

By late 2022, chain-of-thought prompting had already shown that asking a language model to write out its reasoning steps improves accuracy on multi-step problems. But prompting is not the same as training, and once you fine-tune or reinforcement-learn a model to reason, you have to pick a supervision target. You can grade only the final result — outcome-based supervision — or you can grade every step of the reasoning trace — process-based supervision. The two are not obviously interchangeable: a model chasing final-answer reward alone can find a shortcut that lands on the right number for the wrong reasons, and that failure mode is exactly the one an outcome-only metric cannot see.

The prior empirical evidence for this dichotomy came almost entirely from algorithmic tasks — sorting lists, executing programs — where "correct reasoning" can be checked automatically against a ground-truth execution trace. Nobody had run a controlled comparison on a natural-language reasoning task, where "was this step correct" has no programmatic check and has to be judged by a human, and where the stakes for silent reasoning errors are highest: the paper explicitly flags education as a domain where an answer with faulty reasoning "may often confuse more than it explains." The authors pick GSM8K — grade-school math word problems with human-written reasoning traces — as a task hard enough to require genuine multi-step reasoning, but constrained enough (integer final answers, traceable arithmetic) to make a rigorous process-vs-outcome comparison tractable, including collecting fresh human step-correctness annotations rather than relying on synthetic traces.

// how it works

The method

Every variant in the paper starts from the same backbone — a single Chinchilla-optimal pretrained LM ("Base-70B", from Hoffmann et al., 2022) — and the comparison lives entirely in how that backbone gets turned into a GSM8K solver. Three ways to obtain a policy: 5-shot prompting (no fine-tuning), supervised fine-tuning (SFT) on GSM8K's own human-written reasoning traces (this is the process-based imitation baseline), or reinforcement learning via expert iteration — alternating rounds of generating K=96 samples per problem, filtering/scoring them, then distilling the filtered set back into the policy.

The RL policy-improvement step is where process vs. outcome supervision actually gets varied, in three flavors (Fig. 2 of the paper): Final-Answer RL (the STaR recipe — keep whole solutions whose final answer matches, discard the rest, using zero information about intermediate steps); ORM-RL (train an outcome-supervised reward model — a binary "will this land on the correct final answer" classifier trained on final-answer-correctness labels — then keep the single highest-scoring full solution per problem); and PRM-RL, which scores every individual step with a process-supervised reward model trained on human step-correctness labels, and greedily keeps the best-scoring next step at each of up to 15 steps. The PRM's training signal is the paper's one genuinely new data collection effort: annotators are shown a model-generated solution and asked to mark the first step containing a "major mistake" — defined in the instructions as "a step where the information expressed is incorrect, or it would no longer be possible to reach the correct solution without undoing that step" — after filtering low-agreement raters and ambiguous problems (about 20% of the raw data), this yields 9,856 step-level binary labels across 1,560 annotated solutions.

At test time, K=96 samples are decoded and reduced to one answer either by majority voting (self-consistency over the sampled final answers) or, when a reward model is available, RM-weighted decoding — sum each candidate's RM probability into a bucket keyed by its final answer, pick the highest-weighted final answer, then return the highest-scoring sample that produced it:

f* = argmaxf Σyᵢ : final_ans(yᵢ)=f rm_prob(yᵢ) // RM-weighted ("verifier-voting") decoding: aggregate reward-model mass by final answer, then pick the best sample behind the winning answer

Throughout, two metrics are tracked separately rather than folded into one number: final-answer error (exact-match on GSM8K's integer answers — cheap, automatic) and trace error (the fraction of final-answer-correct solutions that contain at least one incorrect reasoning step, per human raters). Splitting these apart is the paper's central methodological move — it is designed specifically to expose the gap between "looks right" and "reasoned correctly," which final-answer accuracy alone cannot distinguish.

// what they found

The results

The best combined recipe — SFT initialization, then ORM-RL, decoded with ORM reranking — improves the state of the art (16.8% final-answer error from Li et al., 2022; 14.0% trace error from Wang et al., 2022 / Wei et al., 2022) to 12.7% final-answer error and 3.4% trace error. Selective prediction pushes final-answer error lower still: for the SFT model with PRM reranking (14.1% baseline), abstaining on 30% of the hardest-to-judge questions (thresholding on RM score) cuts final-answer error to 2.7%, and to 1.5% at a 50% abstain rate — the SFT+ORM reranking curve tracks almost identically.

Three findings structure the paper's argument. First, outcome-based and process-based supervision reach nearly identical final-answer error on their own: without any reward model, SFT (process-based, imitating full human traces) gets 22.3% final-answer error versus 23.5% for Few-shot+Final-Answer RL (outcome-based, using only 1–4 tokens of label per question); with reward-model reranking, 14.8% versus 16.6%. Outcome supervision is, in this sense, a label-efficient way to hit a target final-answer accuracy.

Second — the paper's most cited result — reward models trained purely on outcome labels end up behaving like process-based judges. The ORM is trained only to predict whether a full solution's final answer is correct, yet its per-step predictions agree with the human PRM step-correctness labels 85% of the time, versus only 77% agreement with the ORM's own outcome training labels (Fig. 4). The authors' reading: it is apparently easier for the model to learn "is this step locally sound" than to internally compute and check the final numeric answer, so an outcome-only training signal ends up teaching something closer to step-level judgment.

Third, low trace error is not a free byproduct of low final-answer error — despite similar final-answer error, the outcome-based Few-shot+Final-Answer RL model has much higher trace error than the process-based SFT model (19.8% vs. 11.4%, no RM). With ORM reranking the gap persists (12.4% vs. 4.4%/3.5% for ORM/PRM reranking on top of SFT), and it is closed only by training the RL policy against a reward model rather than raw final-answer correctness — Few-shot+ORM-RL cuts trace error from 12.4% to 5.5% relative to Few-shot+Final-Answer RL, consistent with the ORM having implicitly learned a process-like signal.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

The central empirical claims are internally consistent and the authors are unusually candid about their own limits, but the headline "beats SOTA" framing and the trace-error numbers both deserve a discount before citing them as settled.

The comparisons to prior published results mix supervision method with base-model scale: the "16.8% → 12.7%" and "14.0% → 3.4%" headline deltas are measured against Li et al. (2022)'s Codex-175B and Wang/Wei et al. (2022)'s PaLM-540B, while this paper's own models all run on a single Chinchilla-optimal 70B backbone. The paper is explicit about this in one place — noting its STaR reproduction improves from Zelikman et al.'s reported 89% error to 23.5% partly "through the use of a better base model" — but the abstract's headline numbers don't carry that caveat forward, so a reader skimming the abstract could easily attribute the full gain to the supervision recipe rather than partly to model quality and scale.

The trace-error numbers, which are the paper's most novel contribution, rest on a genuinely small annotation budget: 9,856 step-level labels over 1,560 solutions for PRM training, and evaluation trace-error is measured on only 200 correct-final-answer solutions per model (10 models), double-rated. Table 1's own min–max ranges are wide — the paper's best trace error is reported as "3.4% (0.0–6.8%)," meaning the true rate is anywhere from indistinguishable-from-zero to nearly double the point estimate depending on whether one or both raters have to agree on an error. No inter-rater reliability statistic (e.g., Cohen's kappa) is reported beyond the qualitative note that low-agreement annotators were filtered out. That's a legitimate scientific hedge on the authors' part, but it means the precise "14.0% → 3.4%" framing in the abstract carries more false precision than the underlying data supports.

On generalizability, the authors themselves flag the sharpest limitation (Section 4.2): GSM8K is close to a best case for the paper's most surprising finding — that an outcome-only reward model implicitly tracks process correctness — precisely because in arithmetic word problems, an incorrect reasoning step rarely happens to still land on the correct final number. In domains where wrong reasoning routinely produces plausible-looking correct outputs (persuasion, long-form summarization, tool-use agents papering over a broken intermediate step), the ORM-approximates-PRM result may simply not hold, and the paper does not test any domain where it would be stress-tested. Contamination of the 70B pretraining corpus against GSM8K (published about a year earlier, in October 2021) is not discussed at all — a live concern for any "improves SOTA" claim, though since every supervision variant shares the same pretrained backbone, contamination would inflate all conditions roughly equally and mostly threatens the comparison to external baselines rather than the internal process-vs-outcome comparison.

baselines

Prior-SOTA comparisons (Li et al. 2022 Codex-175B; Wang/Wei et al. 2022 PaLM-540B) use different base models and scales than this paper's uniform 70B backbone, so "beats SOTA" partly reflects base-model strength, not just the supervision recipe — acknowledged once in passing, not carried into the headline framing.

contamination

Not discussed. GSM8K (Oct. 2021) predates the 70B model's pretraining cutoff; a shared pretraining corpus would inflate every supervision variant similarly, so it threatens the external "beats SOTA" claim more than the internal process-vs-outcome comparison.

what's missed

Trace-error human annotation is small (9,856 step labels; 200 eval solutions/model) with wide, unquantified inter-rater uncertainty (min–max ranges up to 2× the point estimate); no domain is tested where wrong reasoning plausibly yields a correct-looking answer, which the authors themselves flag as the case where their central finding might not generalize.

would raise confidence

Report inter-rater reliability (kappa) for trace-error judgments; replicate on a domain with more "spurious correctness" than arithmetic (agentic tool use, multi-hop QA); scale the PRM label budget by an order of magnitude or two, as the contemporaneous PRM800K effort (800K step labels vs. this paper's 9,856) did six months later on MATH.

// bottom line

This is the paper that turned "process vs. outcome supervision" from a philosophical framing into a measured, natural-language empirical question, and its most durable finding — that a reward model trained only on final-answer correctness quietly ends up agreeing with human step-correctness judgments more than with its own training labels — is a real, useful surprise. But the "improves SOTA" numbers blend base-model gains with supervision gains, the trace-error estimates rest on an annotation budget two orders of magnitude smaller than what the field settled on soon after (PRM800K), and the authors' own caveat that GSM8K is close to a best case for their central result deserves more weight than the confident abstract gives it.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. The first comprehensive process- vs. outcome-based supervision comparison on a natural-language reasoning task (GSM8K): the best combined recipe (SFT + ORM-RL, ORM reranking) cuts final-answer error 16.8%→12.7% and trace error 14.0%→3.4% versus prior best, and to 2.7% final-answer error when allowed to abstain on 30% of questions.
  2. Outcome-only supervision is nearly as accurate as process supervision on final-answer error alone, at a fraction of the label cost — but only process-based feedback, or a reward model that has learned to emulate it, reliably suppresses "right answer, wrong reasoning" trace errors (19.8% vs. 11.4% without a reward model).
  3. The surprise result: an outcome-only reward model's predictions agree with human process-correctness labels (85%) more than with its own outcome training labels (77%) — an early empirical clue for why process reward models (PRM800K / "Let's Verify Step by Step," six months later) became the field's default verifier, though the authors caution this may be specific to math's low rate of spuriously-correct wrong reasoning.
