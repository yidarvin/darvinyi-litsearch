COMET: A Neural Framework for MT Evaluation

A learned, source-aware machine-translation metric built on a cross-lingual pretrained encoder — trained directly on human judgments rather than counting n-grams, and the framework that went on to displace BLEU on WMT metrics leaderboards.

Rei, Stewart, Farinha, Lavie
venue EMNLP 2020 citations 1,631 topic Benchmarks & Evals source ↗

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

For nearly two decades, machine translation quality was judged almost entirely by BLEU (Papineni et al., 2002) and its lexical-overlap relatives — METEOR, NIST, chrF, TER — all of which score a hypothesis by counting matched n-grams (or edit operations) against a reference translation, without ever looking at the source sentence. That approximation held up while MT output was itself lexically constrained, but neural MT now produces fluent, semantically faithful translations that deviate from monotonic lexical transfer — exactly the paraphrase-heavy behavior n-gram metrics are blind to. The WMT19 Metrics Shared Task (Ma et al., 2019) made the failure concrete: segment-level correlation with human judgment was frustratingly low across every submitted metric, and every metric's ability to rank the best MT systems against each other collapsed once the field narrowed to the top performers — precisely the regime where a metric matters most for deciding which system is actually better. The field had stalled organizationally too: WMT19 drew 153 MT system submissions but only 24 metrics submissions, nearly half of which were Quality Estimation entrants repurposed as metrics — MT evaluation was falling behind MT itself.

COMET's authors set out to fix both failures — segment-level fidelity and top-system discrimination — with a framework that learns a scoring function directly from human judgments instead of hand-designing lexical-overlap rules, and that is among the first mainstream MT metrics to feed the source sentence into the score, not just the hypothesis and reference — reasoning that a translation can be unfaithful to the source in ways no reference-only comparison can ever catch.

// how it works

The method

COMET is a framework, not a single model. All three trained variants share a cross-lingual pretrained encoder — the paper uses XLM-RoBERTa (base), noting multilingual BERT or XLM would plug in equally well — that independently encodes the source, the hypothesis, and the reference into token embeddings, which a pooling layer compresses into one sentence embedding per segment. On top of that shared backbone, the paper builds two distinct architectures with different training objectives.

The Estimator model concatenates the hypothesis and reference embeddings with four interaction features — the element-wise products h⊙s and h⊙r, and the absolute differences |h−s| and |h−r| — into a single vector fed to a feed-forward regressor trained to predict a quality score by minimizing mean-squared error against a human judgment. Notably, the raw source embedding s is deliberately left out of that concatenation: an ablation that added it back gained only +0.0009 average Kendall's tau, so the source only enters through the interaction terms. The Translation Ranking model instead takes a 4-tuple — source, reference, a "better" hypothesis h⁺, and a "worse" hypothesis h⁻ — and optimizes a triplet margin loss (the same loss FaceNet used for face embeddings) that pulls h⁺ closer than h⁻ to both anchors, the source and the reference, in embedding space.

At inference, the Translation Ranking model has only one hypothesis ĥ to score, so it computes the harmonic mean of ĥ's Euclidean distance to the source and to the reference, then converts that distance into a bounded similarity score:

f(s,ĥ,r) = 2 · d(r,ĥ) · d(s,ĥ)d(r,ĥ) + d(s,ĥ)     f̂(s,ĥ,r) = 11 + f(s,ĥ,r) // harmonic mean of the hypothesis's distance to reference and to source, then squashed to a [0,1] similarity

Because the two architectures accept different flavors of human judgment, the paper trains three separate models to demonstrate the framework generalizes across judgment types. COMET-HTER (Estimator) regresses on Human-mediated Translation Edit Rate — TER between the hypothesis and a human post-edit — using the 173K-tuple QT21 corpus (IT and life-sciences domain, four language pairs: en-de, en-lt, en-cs, de-en). COMET-MQM (Estimator) regresses on a proprietary Multidimensional Quality Metrics score, MQM = 100 − (IMinor + 5·IMajor + 10·ICrit) / sentence-length × 100, from a 12K-segment internal Unbabel corpus spanning 12 language pairs, always English-to-X. COMET-RANK (Translation Ranking) trains on the WMT Direct Assessment relative-ranking (DARR) data from the 2017 and 2018 Metrics shared tasks.

// what they found

The results

Evaluated on the WMT19 Metrics DARR corpus — the same benchmark whose failures motivated the paper — all three COMET variants beat every baseline tested (BLEU, chrF, the 2019 shared-task-winning YiSi-1, and the contemporaneous BERTScore and BLEURT) on the great majority of language pairs, often by wide margins. For English-into-X, COMET-RANK's Kendall's tau roughly doubles BLEU's on several pairs — en-kk: 0.693 vs. BLEU's 0.363; en-fi: 0.664 vs. 0.395; en-lt: 0.665 vs. 0.333 — and beats YiSi-1 and both BERTScore variants on seven of the eight pairs — the exception is en-ru, where COMET-RANK's 0.580 trails BERTScore(xlmr-base)'s 0.599, though the MQM Estimator's 0.615 leads there instead. For X-into-English, COMET-RANK outperforms the English-specific BLEURT metric in five of seven pairs (zh-en: 0.445 vs. BLEURT-large's 0.436; de-en: 0.202 vs. 0.174), even though BLEURT was purpose-built around English-BERT for exactly this direction. The MQM Estimator — trained on only 12K annotated segments, an order of magnitude less data than the other two variants — stays competitive throughout and wins outright on en-ru (0.615, ahead of every other metric).

A targeted ablation (Table 4) isolates the paper's central design choice — feeding in the source — by training a reference-only Translation Ranking model on WMT17 and comparing it against the full model on WMT18: adding the source improves Kendall's tau on every one of the eight tested pairs, and the gain is far larger for X→English pairs (Δτ up to +0.155 on de-en, +0.132 on tr-en) than for English→X pairs (Δτ +0.024 to +0.051) — direct evidence that source-conditioning helps most exactly where the reference alone is least informative.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

COMET's core idea — replace a hand-designed formula with a model trained to imitate human judgments — is also its central vulnerability: a metric that learns to mimic annotators inherits whatever those annotators got systematically wrong, and because the model is a black-box neural regressor rather than a transparent scoring rule, that inherited bias is far harder to spot than a BLEU miscount. This is the meta-evaluation problem that later "LLM-as-judge" systems would run into in a more acute form, and COMET is an early instance of it.

Concretely: COMET-MQM is trained on a proprietary, unreleased 12K-segment corpus annotated under Unbabel's own internal MQM typology, covering only English-as-source language pairs — nobody outside Unbabel can audit the annotators, their agreement rates, or which error types they systematically over- or under-penalized, yet this is the variant the paper highlights as surprisingly strong and even "zero-shot" competitive on X→English pairs it never saw in training (Section 5.2). COMET-HTER's QT21 training data is public but narrow — 173K tuples confined to IT and life-sciences text across just four language pairs — so its regression target (post-editing effort) may not transfer as cleanly to other domains as the paper's cross-lingual framing implies. And because the paper evaluates all three variants exclusively against the WMT DARR relative-ranking protocol (training on 2017–2018, testing on 2019 — a later year, but the same annotation methodology and largely the same crowd-sourcing pipeline), a strong showing partly demonstrates that COMET learned to reproduce that specific pipeline's quirks — its 25-point DA exclusion threshold, its relative-ranking conversion, whatever fluency-over-adequacy or anglocentric tendencies its annotators share — rather than "translation quality" as a construct independent of how WMT happens to measure it. There's also a real interpretability regression versus what COMET replaces: a BLEU or TER score decomposes into exactly which n-grams matched or which edits were needed, letting a practitioner see why a system scored low; COMET's score is the output of an XLM-R regressor or triplet-embedding distance with no per-segment explanation, making it much harder to debug a low score or contest a leaderboard ranking that hinges on it.

baselines

Fair and current for the time — BLEU, chrF, 2019's own shared-task winner YiSi-1, and the concurrently published BERTScore and BLEURT are all included, with BLEURT specifically compared on its home turf (X→English). No comparison against human judgments collected outside the WMT DARR pipeline, though.

contamination

No literal segment leakage — COMET-RANK trains on WMT 2017–2018 DARR and evaluates on WMT19 DARR. But train and test share the same annotation protocol and largely the same annotator pool, so the metric may have learned that pipeline's specific idiosyncrasies rather than a protocol-independent notion of quality — a softer overfitting risk the paper doesn't test for.

what's missed

Whether COMET's judgments hold up against a differently-collected human evaluation (e.g., independent expert MQM, not WMT's crowd DARR); whether COMET-HTER and COMET-MQM generalize outside their narrow training domains and language pairs; and any per-segment explanation for why a translation received the score it did.

would raise confidence

Evaluation against human judgments from a different pipeline/annotator pool than the one COMET trained on; release of the MQM training corpus and its annotator guidelines/inter-annotator agreement so outside researchers can audit for systematic bias; and some form of per-segment attribution to close the interpretability gap with the n-gram metrics it replaces.

// bottom line

COMET's central bet — that a learned, source-aware metric correlates with human judgment far better than n-gram overlap — is well supported by its own WMT19 numbers, and it went on to become the dominant MT metric in exactly the way this paper hoped. But "correlates well with the human judgments we trained it on" and "measures translation quality" are not the same claim, and the paper's evaluation can't fully separate them — a gap worth remembering every time a leaderboard cites a COMET score as ground truth rather than a very good, but not fully transparent, model of one annotation pipeline's opinions.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. COMET was among the first widely-adopted MT metrics to feed the source sentence into the score alongside the hypothesis and reference — a departure from BLEU, METEOR, NIST, and TER, which are all reference-only — and the paper's own ablation shows this helps most exactly where the reference is least informative (X→English pairs, Δτ up to +0.155).
  2. One framework, two architectures, three models: the Estimator (feed-forward regression + MSE) trained on HTER and proprietary MQM judgments, and the Translation Ranking model (triplet margin loss over a cross-lingual embedding space) trained on WMT's relative-ranking DARR data — all sharing an XLM-RoBERTa backbone.
  3. COMET-RANK beat every 2020-era baseline (BLEU, chrF, YiSi-1, BERTScore, BLEURT) on nearly every WMT19 language pair and, crucially, kept discriminating between top MT systems even as BLEU's correlation collapsed toward zero — directly answering the WMT19 Metrics Shared Task's two headline complaints.
  4. Being trained on human judgments is COMET's strength and its open risk: it can only be as unbiased as the (partly proprietary, WMT-DARR-shaped) annotators it learned from, and unlike BLEU's transparent n-gram count, there's no way to inspect why a given segment got the score it did.
