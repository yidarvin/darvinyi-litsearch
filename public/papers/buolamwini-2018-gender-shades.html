Gender Shades: Intersectional Accuracy Disparities in Commercial Gender Classification

Three shipping commercial gender classifiers, audited on a skin-type-balanced benchmark. Aggregate accuracy looked fine — 87.9–93.7%. Disaggregated, the same systems misclassified darker-skinned women up to 34.7% of the time and lighter-skinned men at most 0.8%. The number that hid the failure was the average.

Joy Buolamwini, Timnit Gebru

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

Commercial facial-analysis APIs shipped with a single headline accuracy number — and that number was load-bearing in procurement, policy, and press. But an aggregate metric averages over whatever population the test set happens to contain, and the two then-standard face benchmarks contained mostly light-skinned faces: by the authors' own audit, 79.6% of IJB-A and 86.2% of Adience subjects are lighter-skinned. Neither benchmark carried skin-type annotations, so no one could even ask how a model performed on darker-skinned subjects.

That combination is dangerous. A classifier can post 90%+ overall accuracy while failing catastrophically on a subgroup that barely appears in the test set, and the headline number will never reveal it — the few errors on a tiny subgroup are swamped by the many correct calls on the over-represented majority. Compounding this, none of the commercial vendors reported performance broken down by demographic subgroup, and none reported numbers on existing gender-estimation benchmarks at all. The gap was twofold: there was no facial-analysis benchmark balanced by skin type and gender, and there was no norm of reporting error rates disaggregated by phenotypic subgroup. Systematic, intersectional failures could hide in plain sight behind one average.

// how it works

The method

The audit makes two moves: build a benchmark that isn't skewed, then measure the systems through the lens of intersectional subgroups instead of a single average.

1 · A benchmark balanced by skin type and gender

The authors assembled the Pilot Parliaments Benchmark (PPB): 1,270 official portraits of members of national parliament from three African countries (Rwanda, Senegal, South Africa) and three Nordic European countries (Iceland, Finland, Sweden). Parliaments were chosen deliberately — they publish high-quality, consistent frontal headshots, they span the skin-tone range when you pick countries across that range, and several have meaningful gender balance. The resulting set is close to balanced on both axes: 44.6% female / 55.4% male and 46.4% darker / 53.6% lighter, with the smallest subgroup (darker female) at 21.3% rather than the single-digit share it holds in prior benchmarks.

2 · Phenotype labels, not race

Rather than label race — socially constructed and unstable across geographies — each face is annotated on the dermatologist-developed Fitzpatrick six-point skin-type scale (I–VI), with a board-certified surgical dermatologist providing the definitive labels for PPB. The six types are collapsed into darker (Fitzpatrick IV–VI) and lighter (I–III). Crossing skin type with the binary gender labels the commercial APIs return yields four intersectional subgroups: darker female (DF), darker male (DM), lighter female (LF), lighter male (LM).

3 · Audit three commercial APIs as black boxes

In April–May 2017 the authors evaluated three shipping commercial gender-classification services — Microsoft (Cognitive Services Face), Face++ (a China-based computer-vision company), and IBM (Watson Visual Recognition) — each returning a binary male/female label. Following the gender-classification evaluation precedent set by NIST, they report, per subgroup, the true positive rate (TPR), the error rate, the positive predictive value (PPV), and the false positive rate (FPR). The single decisive idea is that the error rate is computed within each intersectional cell rather than pooled:

Errorg = 1 − TPRg,   g ∈ { DF, DM, LF, LM } // the same prediction stream, scored inside each skin-type × gender cell — never averaged into one headline number
// what they found

The results

Every classifier was best on lighter-skinned men and worst on darker-skinned women — and the spread between those two cells was enormous. The maximum error rate for lighter-skinned males was 0.8% (Microsoft 0.0%, IBM 0.3%, Face++ 0.8%). For darker-skinned females the error rates were 20.8% (Microsoft), 34.5% (Face++), and 34.7% (IBM) — so the intersectional gap from best to worst cell runs from 20.8 points (Microsoft) up to 34.4 points (IBM).

The marginal axes both showed it too: every classifier was better on male than female faces (an 8.1–20.6 point error-rate gap) and better on lighter than darker faces (an 11.8–19.2 point gap). Yet the overall accuracy looked respectable — Microsoft 93.7%, Face++ 90.0%, IBM 87.9% — which is exactly the point: the aggregate number certified systems that misclassify as many as one in three darker-skinned women. The disparity also survived a within-country control: on the South-African subset alone, darker-female error rates were 23.8% (Microsoft), 36.0% (Face++), and 33.1% (IBM), so this is not a nationality confound.

The damage even showed up in the models' own confidence: IBM, the only vendor returning confidence scores, was markedly less confident and more erratic on darker-female faces than on the other three subgroups — the cell it got most wrong was also the cell it was least sure about.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

This is an audit, not a model, so the read is about the audit's own rigor — and about what its numbers can and can't claim. On its central claim it is bulletproof: black-box evaluation of shipping products, with disparities so large (20+ points) that no measurement noise explains them, replicated within a single country. The honest caveats are about scope and durability, several of which the authors flag themselves.

baselines

The "baselines" are the commercial systems themselves, audited as black boxes — fair, high-stakes, and exactly the right targets. But it is a spring-2017 snapshot, and the specific error rates are perishable: vendors patched within months once named (the 2019 follow-up, Actionable Auditing, documented exactly that). The enduring contribution is the method, not the three numbers.

construct validity

Not train/test leakage (nothing was trained), but proxy risk. "Gender" is operationalized as a binary perceived label inferred from parliamentary records and photos — which the authors call reductive and unable to capture gender identity or non-binary people. "Darker/lighter" collapses a six-point dermatological scale into two bins, with skin type standing in (imperfectly) for race. The audit measures disparity across these proxies, not across gender or race as lived.

what's missed

One task (binary gender classification), one benchmark of 1,270 well-lit, frontal, public-figure headshots — i.e. the easy imaging case. It says nothing about face detection, verification, or identification, nor about in-the-wild image quality, and PPB's parliamentarians are not a random sample of any population. Skin type isn't the sole driver either: lighting, camera processing, and labeling all confound it, as the paper notes.

would raise confidence

More subjects and countries (n=1,270 is thin once split four ways — Iceland's darker cells are literally zero); multiple annotators with reported inter-rater reliability on Fitzpatrick labels (one author labeled the existing benchmarks); extending the audit to detection/verification/identification; and re-running over time to verify vendors actually close the gap rather than the named three.

// bottom line

The lasting importance of Gender Shades isn't the three error rates — it's the demonstration that a single aggregate accuracy number can certify a system as "90% accurate" while it fails as many as one in three darker-skinned women. It turned disaggregated, intersectional evaluation from a nice-to-have into table stakes, and became the empirical backbone for datasheets, model cards, and subgroup reporting. The numbers aged; the question it forced — what's the accuracy on the worst-off subgroup? — did not.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. Aggregate accuracy hides intersectional failure. The same classifiers that scored 87.9–93.7% overall misclassified darker-skinned women up to 34.7% of the time and lighter-skinned men at most 0.8% — a gap the headline number erases.
  2. The fix was infrastructural, not algorithmic. A skin-type-and-gender-balanced benchmark (PPB) plus a dermatologist-grounded phenotype annotation (Fitzpatrick I–VI) is what made the disparity measurable at all; you can't audit a subgroup your test set barely contains.
  3. Evaluate the worst-off subgroup, and report the breakdown. Gender Shades is the reason disaggregated, intersectional metrics — and dataset/model documentation like datasheets and model cards — became expected practice in responsible AI.
