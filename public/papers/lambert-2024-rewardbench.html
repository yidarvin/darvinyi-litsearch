RewardBench: Evaluating Reward Models for Language Modeling

Every RLHF pipeline hinges on a reward model nobody benchmarks. RewardBench is the first common yardstick and public leaderboard for scoring reward models — classifiers and DPO checkpoints alike — on whether they can tell a genuinely better answer from a worse one.

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

By early 2024, reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF) had become the default way to turn a raw pretrained model into a usable assistant — ChatGPT, Claude, and Llama 2 Chat all lean on a reward model (RM) somewhere in the pipeline to tell a policy which of two candidate answers a human would prefer. Yet, as the authors put it, "while the post-RLHF model (known as the policy) and even the pretrained model are extensively documented and evaluated, the basic properties of the RLHF process like the RMs receive far less attention." Nearly every new chat model ships MMLU, HellaSwag, and instruction-following numbers; almost none ship anything that says whether the reward model that shaped it could actually tell correct from incorrect, safe from unsafe, or well-reasoned from subtly wrong.

The evaluations reward-model builders did lean on inherited the ceilings baked into the RLHF datasets they were carved from. Anthropic's Helpful-and-Harmless data and OpenAI's Learning to Summarize test sets are "known to have ceilings on accuracy between 60 and 70% due to inter-annotator disagreement" — so an RM that "solves" them isn't being tested past the point where two human labelers would already disagree with each other. Meanwhile the newer preference datasets people had started training reward models on — UltraFeedback, UltraInteract, Nectar — don't ship held-out test sets at all, so there was no standard way to check whether an RM trained on them actually generalizes.

There was a second, more structural gap: reward models don't all compute a "reward" the same way. Some are trained as explicit classifiers on chosen/rejected pairs. Others are never trained as a reward model in the traditional sense at all — Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) reparameterizes the RLHF objective so the policy model itself implies a reward, computed from log-probability ratios against a reference model. Comparing a DPO checkpoint against a classifier RM, on the same evaluation, with the same inference stack, simply hadn't been done at scale before this paper.

The introduction frames the paper's answer as four concrete deliverables, and it's worth naming them because each becomes a section of the results below: (1) a common evaluation framework and inference stack that works across RM architectures, released alongside every text-score pair used to produce the leaderboard, so others can re-analyze the raw scores without re-running inference; (2) a head-to-head illustrating that DPO models, "while more plentiful due to the method's simplicity, fail to generalize to popular preference data test sets and present a higher variance in performance" than classifiers; (3) a charted landscape of state-of-the-art RMs — scaling behavior, refusal propensity, reasoning competence; and (4) a demonstration of "the limitations of existing preference data test sets," via subsets purpose-built around "subtle instruction pairs (e.g. intentionally modified rejected responses), which superficially look high quality but answer the wrong prompt."

The stakes go beyond leaderboard bragging rights. A reward model is the artifact where an organization's judgment calls about what counts as "helpful" or "safe" actually get baked into a policy's weights — RewardBench is pitched explicitly as a tool for asking "whose and which values are embedded" in a given RM. A miscalibrated reward model doesn't just make a chatbot a little worse; it quietly sets the ceiling on how correct, careful, or safe the RLHF'd model that inherits its judgments can ever become — and until this paper, that ceiling was almost never measured directly.

// how it works

The method

RewardBench's evaluation primitive is deliberately simple: a prompt paired with a chosen and a rejected completion, where the chosen completion has been manually verified to actually be the better one — not merely the one a crowd-worker vote happened to favor. Both completions are scored independently by the reward model under test, and the prompt counts as a win if the chosen completion's score is higher than the rejected completion's. Accuracy — the fraction of prompts won — is the entire scoring mechanism (Figure 1). That single design choice is what lets RewardBench compare architectures that would otherwise be incomparable: a classifier RM's raw logit and a DPO checkpoint's implicit reward are on totally different numeric scales, but "did the chosen response score higher" is well-defined for both.

For classifier-style reward models, training itself follows the Bradley-Terry pairwise-comparison model: the probability that completion y₁ is preferred to y₂ given prompt x is a softmax over the two scalar rewards, and the model is fit by maximizing the log-likelihood of the observed chosen-over-rejected preferences.

p*(y₁ ≻ y₂ | x) = exp(r*(x,y₁))exp(r*(x,y₁)) + exp(r*(x,y₂)) // the Bradley-Terry model reward models are trained to fit (Eq. 1)

DPO models never see this loss. Instead, DPO reparameterizes the RLHF objective so the reward is implicit in the trained policy's log-probabilities relative to a frozen reference model, scaled by the KL-regularization constant β:

r(x,y) = β log π(y|x)πref(y|x) + β log Z(x) // DPO's implicit reward — no separately-trained RM required (Eq. 2)

To score a pair under this formulation, RewardBench compares the log-ratio of the policy's probability to the reference model's probability for each completion, and calls it a win when that ratio is larger for the chosen completion than the rejected one — meaning every DPO evaluation needs both the policy and its declared reference model loaded at inference time.

The dataset itself is organized into five sections, each targeting a different reward-modeling failure mode, totaling 2,958 prompts in the primary suite (excluding Prior Sets):

  • Chat (358 prompts) — basic instruction-following pulled from AlpacaEval (Easy: GPT-4-Turbo vs. Alpaca 7B; Length: same-length completions from Llama 2 Chat 70B vs. Guanaco 13B; Hard: Tülu 2 70B DPO vs. Davinci003) and MT-Bench (Easy/Medium).
  • Chat Hard (456 prompts) — trick questions and adversarially similar responses, built from MT-Bench Hard and LLMBar's natural and four adversarial subsets (Neighbor, GPTInst, GPTOut, Manual), where the rejected answer is a subtly-modified variant of a prompt that superficially looks just as good.
  • Safety (740 prompts) — Refusals Dangerous/Offensive (should the RM prefer a refusal over compliance with a harmful request), plus XSTest Should-Refuse and Should-Respond and Do-Not-Answer subsets that specifically probe over-refusal on benign-but-scary-sounding prompts.
  • Reasoning (1,431 prompts) — HumanEvalPack reformatted across six languages (correct vs. buggy code, 164 prompts each) plus 447 PRM Math prompts pairing a correct human answer against an incorrect model generation from PRM800k.
  • Prior Sets (~17.2k prompts) — legacy test splits from Anthropic Helpful (6,192), Anthropic HHH (221), a filtered Stanford Human Preferences subset (1,741), and OpenAI's Learning to Summarize (9,000), kept for continuity with older RM work but down-weighted 0.5× in the final composite score "due to noise, lack of clearly defined tasks."

Every subset except Reasoning and Prior Sets is scored with a per-prompt-weighted average across its constituent subsets so unevenly-sized subsets don't distort a section's score; Reasoning up-weights PRM Math to balance it against the six evenly-split code languages. By construction, the chosen response in nearly every subset is the same length or shorter than the rejected one, specifically to keep a length-biased RM from winning "for free" (Figure 9, discussed below). The chosen/rejected split is also designed around verifiable, not merely preferred, correctness — Figure 11 shows one of the retained MT-Bench Medium examples, where both completions attempt the same word-count instruction but the rejected completion undercounts one of the target words, giving the reward model an objectively wrong answer to catch rather than a stylistic judgment call.

Operationally, the leaderboard evaluates "over 80 models, such as those trained as classifiers, including UltraRM, Starling, PairRM… models from Reward rAnked FineTuning, and others," plus popular DPO-trained chat models (Zephyr, Qwen-Chat, StableLM, Tülu 2), and — as a separate comparison — closed frontier LLMs used as generative judges (GPT-4, Gemini 1.5, Claude 3). Reward-model sizes span "400 million (PairRM) to 70 billion (Tülu 2)" parameters. Per Appendix C, the core suite of 75 open models was run on NVIDIA A100 GPUs hosted by Cirrascale, at roughly 12 A100-hours per model in 16-bit precision — about 1,000 A100-hours to reproduce the entire leaderboard from scratch. All of it — code, inference stack, and the text-score pairs behind every leaderboard row — ships publicly on GitHub and Hugging Face, which is arguably as important a contribution as the numbers themselves: before RewardBench there wasn't even a shared codebase for running a classifier RM and a DPO checkpoint through the same evaluation loop.

A third scoring paradigm sits alongside classifiers and DPO: what the authors term generative reward modeling, "when the judgements are used to curate a reward signal for training" but the underlying mechanism is an LLM-as-judge — the model is prompted to compare the two completions directly (sometimes explaining its reasoning before deciding) rather than emitting a scalar from its final layer. This traces back to the older discriminative-vs-generative-classifier framing of Ng and Jordan (2001), and RewardBench applies it to score both open instruction-tuned chat models (Llama 3 70B Instruct, prompted as its own judge) and closed frontier APIs (GPT-4, Gemini 1.5, Claude 3) that have no accessible logits to read a reward from at all. It's a heavier-compute path per sample — a judge call plus reasoning tokens, versus one forward pass through a classifier head — but it's the only way to put closed models on the same leaderboard as open ones.

// what they found

The results

The headline leaderboard result is a wide, previously-invisible spread. The top model, RLHFlow/ArmoRM-Llama3-8B-v0.1, a custom classifier built on Llama 3 8B, scores 89.0 overall (Chat 96.9, Chat Hard 76.8, Safety 92.2, Reasoning 97.3, Prior Sets 74.3) — comfortably ahead of the next-best classifiers, RLHFlow/pair-preference-model-LLaMA3-8B (85.7) and sfairX/FsfairX-LLaMA3-RM-v0.1 (83.6, which itself hits a near-perfect 99.4 on Chat). Among DPO-trained models, the best is allenai/tulu-2-dpo-70b at 76.1 — a full 12.9 points behind the top classifier, despite Tülu 2 70B being nearly nine times larger than ArmoRM's 8B backbone.

Scale helps, but inconsistently. Within the Tülu 2 DPO family, score climbs monotonically with size — 71.7 (7B) → 73.4 (13B) → 76.1 (70B) — with the largest gains on Chat Hard and Reasoning. Qwen1.5-Chat, also evaluated via DPO-style scoring, does not scale monotonically: Qwen1.5-7B-Chat scores 68.7, Qwen1.5-14B-Chat 69.8, but Qwen1.5-72B-Chat only 68.2 — the paper attributes this to "out of distribution generalization challenges" rather than a straightforward capacity story. At matched ~7B scale (Table 4), the four best classifiers (ArmoRM 89.0, pair-preference-model 85.7, FsfairX-LLaMA3 83.6, Eurus-RM-7b 81.6) all clear 81, while the best Zephyr-style DPO models cluster in the low-to-mid 70s (zephyr-7b-alpha 73.4, zephyr-7b-beta 71.8, tulu-2-dpo-7b 71.7) — the same classifier-beats-DPO gap holds at fixed parameter count, not just at the top of the leaderboard.

The Chat Hard and Safety breakdowns surface behavior a single score hides. On Chat Hard, ArmoRM's 76.8 average masks real unevenness across its six sub-splits: it scores 93.0 on LLMBar Natural but only 66.0 on LLMBar Adversarial GPTOut, while Qwen1.5-14B-Chat — 70.2 overall on Chat Hard, well behind ArmoRM — actually beats it on LLMBar Adversarial Neighbor (83.6 vs. 67.9). On Safety, models split into three failure modes: well-calibrated RMs like ArmoRM score high on both refusing dangerous prompts (93.0/97.0) and answering benign-but-scary-sounding XSTest prompts (87.2 "should respond"); "helpfulness-only" models like UltraRM-13b score a strong 90.4 on XSTest Should-Respond but a weak 21.0 on XSTest Should-Refuse — the mirror-image failure of an over-cautious model that refuses too much.

The Reasoning section has "the widest, smooth variation in performance — e.g. models populate many levels, from 35% accuracy (well below random) all the way to 97% accuracy," which the authors attribute to the format itself: the HumanEvalPack pairs differ from their buggy counterpart by "just one or two tokens," so a reward model has to notice a near-invisible edit rather than a stylistic difference, and plenty of RMs simply can't. That range — a 62-point spread on prompts a single-character diff separates — is itself informative: it means Reasoning is currently the sharpest single lever for telling a mediocre RM from a good one, more so than Chat, where most models cluster near ceiling.

Closed frontier models evaluated as generative judges (LLM-as-a-judge, prompted to compare the two completions rather than scored via logits) are surprisingly competitive: google/gemini-1.5-pro-0514 tops even the best classifier at 88.1, ahead of openai/gpt-4-0125-preview (84.3), gpt-4-turbo-2024-04-09 (83.9), and gpt-4o-2024-05-13 (83.3); among open-weight generative judges, meta-llama/Meta-Llama-3-70B-Instruct (75.4) and prometheus-eval/prometheus-8x7b-v2.0 (75.3) lead but still trail the best purpose-built classifiers. Finally, the Prior Sets column is its own cautionary tale: models actually trained on those splits (UltraRM-13b, PairRM-hf) score well there, as expected, but "the very low average score of DPO models on these test sets indicates substantial research is needed" to understand whether the older HH/SHP/Summarize data is still a meaningful check on a modern RM at all.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

RewardBench's central contribution — a common scoring loop that works for both classifier and DPO-style reward models — is real and was genuinely missing before this paper. But its own appendix is candid about exactly where the headline 89.0-vs-random spread stops being trustworthy, and it's worth taking that candor at face value rather than reading only the leaderboard.

The sharpest gap is one the authors name themselves in the conclusion: "a crucial next step is needed to correlate performance in RewardBench to RLHF usefulness," with "initial experiments with ranking RMs with best-of-N sampling and downstream training with PPO... underway" but not reported in this paper. That means the entire leaderboard measures whether a reward model can classify a curated prompt-chosen-rejected trio correctly — it does not, in this paper, show that a higher RewardBench score produces a better policy after actual RLHF. Given that the whole point of a reward model is to shape a downstream policy, that's the one correlation the benchmark's name implies it settles and doesn't.

The DPO-side numbers are also more fragile than a single leaderboard row suggests. Appendix Table 7 shows that computing DPO's implicit reward without its declared reference model — a real ambiguity, since "some of the released DPO models do not clearly document which reference model is used in training" — costs mistralai/Mixtral-8x7B-Instruct-v0.1 an 18.0-point overall drop (82.2 → 64.2), with Chat Hard falling 28.5 points and Safety falling 35.3 points. The authors separately report testing DPO scoring with a similar-but-wrong reference model and finding it "reduced the DPO trained RM performance to similar levels as random baseline." A metric that swings by double digits based on an undocumented modeling choice is measuring something real, but it's measuring it on a knife's edge.

Contamination is a named, not merely hypothetical, risk: the limitations section states plainly that "there might be a chance of possible data contamination, in cases where models are (wrongly) directly trained on alpacaeval or MTBench data" — and RewardBench's own Chat and Chat Hard sections are built almost entirely from AlpacaEval and MT-Bench completions, both public and widely used in SFT/DPO training mixes. No decontamination audit against evaluated models' training data is reported.

The single composite score also papers over two judgment calls that are easy to miss reading only the leaderboard. First, the weighting scheme is admittedly ad hoc — Prior Sets counts for "0.5 weight of the others due to multiple factors: noise, lack of clearly defined tasks," a reasonable-sounding but ultimately hand-picked discount factor with no sensitivity analysis showing how much the final ranking would shift under a different weight. Second, per the checklist, the paper reports no error bars across evaluation runs — it notes only that "the temperature should be set to 0 and have substantially lower variance than training experiments," an assumption rather than a measurement, so a 1-2 point gap between two adjacent leaderboard rows (there are several) is not clearly distinguishable from noise in either the sampling or the underlying human-verification process that built the chosen/rejected labels.

There's also a fairness question the authors raise about themselves in Appendix B under "Safety In or After RLHF": some systems route safety through a small dedicated classifier bolted on after generation rather than through the core RM, and "if some LLMs or RMs are designed to be used with additional safety classifiers after the fact, evaluating them on RewardBench may not be a fair comparison" — a caveat that applies directly to the Safety-section rankings discussed above, since a model's raw RM score there says nothing about whatever downstream safety filter it may or may not ship with in production.

baselines

Thorough among open weights — "over 80" reward models from 400M to 70B, classifiers and DPO alike, plus closed frontier LLMs run as generative judges. But the paper explicitly excludes actual closed-lab reward models: "scores of RMs from private LM providers are on the public leaderboard, but not in the paper because they are not reproducible" — the RMs behind GPT-4 or Claude's own RLHF are absent by design, only their judge-mode stand-ins are present.

contamination

Named as a real risk by the authors, not addressed with an audit: Chat/Chat Hard prompts are drawn from public AlpacaEval and MT-Bench data that some evaluated models may have trained on.

what's missed

Whether a RewardBench win rate predicts a better post-RLHF policy — the paper's own stated "crucial next step," left to future work. Also glossed: DPO's implicit reward is only as well-defined as its reference model, and Table 7 shows that ambiguity alone is worth up to 35 points.

would raise confidence

A published correlation between RewardBench score and downstream best-of-N or PPO policy quality on a fixed base model; a decontamination check against each evaluated model's known training mixture; repeated-run variance bars so a 1-2 point leaderboard gap can be told apart from noise.

// bottom line

RewardBench is a genuine methodological unlock — the first shared yardstick and codebase that lets a Bradley-Terry classifier and a DPO checkpoint be scored the same way — and its subset design (verifiable-not-just-preferred errors, length-matched chosen/rejected pairs, explicit over-refusal probes) is thoughtfully built to resist the easiest ways an RM eval could be gamed. But treat a high RewardBench score as evidence the RM can discriminate correct-from-incorrect on curated trios, not as proof it will produce a better RLHF'd model — the authors say that link is still unproven, and the DPO numbers specifically are sensitive to modeling choices the benchmark can't fully control for.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. RewardBench is the first standardized way to score any reward model — classifier or DPO's implicit reward — on the same evaluation loop, across 2,958 curated chat/chat-hard/safety/reasoning prompts plus ~17.2k legacy Prior Sets prompts, replacing ceiling-limited reuse of Anthropic HH (60-70% inter-annotator agreement) or datasets with no test split at all (UltraFeedback, Nectar).
  2. Classifier-trained RMs beat DPO-derived implicit rewards by a wide, scale-independent margin: the best classifier (ArmoRM-Llama3-8B-v0.1, 89.0) leads the best DPO model (tulu-2-dpo-70b, 76.1) by 12.9 points despite being nine times smaller, and the same gap holds when comparing similarly-sized 7B models. DPO models specifically underperform on the legacy Prior Sets data even where they hold their own elsewhere.
  3. The paper's own candor is the real finding: it explicitly leaves "correlate performance in RewardBench to RLHF usefulness" as unresolved future work, and shows in its own appendix that DPO's implicit reward can swing by up to 35 points depending on an often-undocumented reference-model choice. A high RewardBench score is necessary evidence a reward model can discriminate good from bad — it is not, by the authors' own admission, sufficient proof it will make RLHF work better.
