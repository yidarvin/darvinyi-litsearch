Language Models are Unsupervised Multitask Learners

GPT-2: a 1.5-billion-parameter Transformer trained on nothing but next-word prediction over 40 GB of web text — yet it answers questions, translates, summarizes and reads comprehension zero-shot, setting state of the art on 7 of 8 language-modeling benchmarks without ever touching their training data.

Alec Radford*, Jeffrey Wu*, Rewon Child, David Luan, Dario Amodei**, Ilya Sutskever**

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

By 2018 the NLP playbook was the same for every task: collect a labeled dataset for question answering, translation, summarization or reading comprehension, train (or fine-tune) a model on it, and test on independent, identically-distributed held-out examples. Even the freshly-minted "pretrain then fine-tune" recipe — ELMo, GPT-1, BERT — still demanded task-specific supervised fine-tuning, and often architecture or objective changes, for each new task. The result, the authors argue, is a field of narrow experts: systems that look strong on a benchmark yet are brittle to small shifts in the data distribution or the task specification, "competent" on the leaderboard and fragile off it.

Multitask learning was the proposed cure, but in NLP it was barely off the ground. The two most ambitious efforts to date had trained on a total of just 10 and 17 (dataset, objective) pairs. From a meta-learning view each pair is a single training example sampled from the space of tasks — and current ML needs hundreds to thousands of examples to induce a function that generalizes. Hand-curating that many datasets and objectives is not going to scale.

So the paper poses a sharper question: the demonstrations of these tasks already exist, scattered through ordinary web text — naturally-occurring translation pairs, question/answer exchanges, "TL;DR" summaries. Could a single model learn to perform many tasks with zero task-specific supervision, simply by getting good enough at predicting the next word across a large, diverse enough corpus?

// how it works

The method

The core claim is that language modeling is implicit multitask learning. A general system should model not just p(output | input) but p(output | input, task) — and crucially, the task can be specified in the input itself, as natural language or format, rather than baked into the architecture. "Translate to french, <english text> =>", "<document> TL;DR:", or "<passage> Q: … A:" all turn a downstream task into ordinary conditional next-token prediction. A model that predicts the continuation of such contexts well is, in effect, doing the task. So the objective is just the standard autoregressive factorization:

p(x) = ∏i=1n p(si | s1, …, si−1) // maximize the likelihood of each token given its predecessors — no task labels anywhere

The data is the real lever. To get scale, diversity and quality without manual curation, the authors scraped every outbound link from Reddit that had earned at least 3 karma — a cheap human-interest filter. After de-duplication and heuristic cleaning, this WebText corpus holds slightly over 8 million documents / 40 GB of text. All Wikipedia documents were deliberately removed, since Wikipedia is a common source for evaluation datasets and would muddy the analysis.

Input representation. GPT-2 uses a byte-level Byte-Pair Encoding with a vocabulary of 50,257 tokens. Operating over bytes means any Unicode string is representable — no out-of-vocabulary tokens, no lossy lower-casing or tokenization preprocessing that would otherwise restrict the space of strings the model can score.

Architecture. It is the decoder-only Transformer of GPT-1, with tweaks: layer normalization is moved to the input of each sub-block (pre-norm) with an extra layer norm after the final self-attention; residual-layer weights are initialized scaled by 1/√N (N = number of residual layers); the context window grows from 512 to 1024 tokens and the batch size to 512. Four sizes were trained — 117M (equivalent to the original GPT), 345M (equivalent to BERT-large), 762M, and the headline 1542M (1.5B) "GPT-2." Every task below is evaluated with no fine-tuning and no training examples; perplexity comparisons use invertible de-tokenizers so GPT-2 can be scored fairly against datasets with aggressive preprocessing.

// what they found

The results

The signature finding is a trend, not a single number: zero-shot performance rises log-linearly with model size across every task tried, and the largest model still hasn't plateaued. Capacity, the authors conclude, "is essential to the success of zero-shot task transfer."

On language modeling itself, GPT-2 sets zero-shot state of the art on 7 of 8 datasets — with no training on any of them. The biggest gains come on small corpora and long-range benchmarks: Penn Treebank perplexity falls 46.54 → 35.76, WikiText-2 39.14 → 18.34, and LAMBADA from 99.8 → 8.63 perplexity with accuracy rising 19% → 52.66% (63.24% once a stop-word filter approximates the "must be a final word" constraint). Children's Book Test common-noun accuracy reaches 93.30% and named-entity 89.05%; enwik8 drops to 0.93 BPB and text8 to 0.98 BPC. The lone exception is the One Billion Word Benchmark (42.16 vs 21.8 SOTA) — its sentence-level shuffling destroys exactly the long-range structure GPT-2 exploits.

The downstream task results are more modest in absolute terms but striking for a system with zero supervision. On the CoQA reading-comprehension dataset GPT-2 reaches 55 F1, matching or exceeding 3 of 4 baseline systems without using the 127,000+ training examples — though still well short of the ~89 F1 of supervised systems and humans. It improves the Winograd Schema Challenge SOTA by 7% to 70.70% (on just 273 examples), and answers 4.1% of Natural Questions exactly while being well-calibrated — 63.1% correct on the 1% of questions it is most confident about. Generation (summarization, translation) is weaker:

And the finding that aged best: GPT-2 still underfits WebText. Train and test perplexity both keep falling with scale and stay close together (Figure 3), so the model is nowhere near the limits of its data — more capacity would keep helping. That observation — a 1.5B model that still hasn't saturated its own training set — is, in hindsight, the seed of the scaling push that produced GPT-3.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

The zero-shot framing is genuinely new and the LM-benchmark sweep is clean — but read the downstream numbers carefully, because the comparisons are mostly against weak or unsupervised baselines, not supervised state of the art. "55 F1 on CoQA, matching 3 of 4 baselines" is impressive given zero training data, yet it sits far below the ~89 F1 ceiling (the human level, which the supervised BERT-based SOTA was only nearing), and the paper notes GPT-2 frequently leans on a crude heuristic (answer a "who" question with a name pulled from the passage). Summarization lands below Lead-3, a baseline that just copies the first three sentences; En→Fr translation (5 BLEU) loses to a word-by-word bilingual lexicon. The honest reading of the downstream results is "far above chance, far below supervised," not "the model can do these tasks."

Where the paper is unusually rigorous — and where its most durable contribution lies — is contamination. GPT-2 trained on 40 GB of uncurated web text, so any benchmark whose text leaked onto the web could be partly recalled rather than solved. Instead of waving this away, the authors built Bloom filters of WebText 8-grams (false-positive rate bounded by 1e-8) and measured overlap (Table 6): the LM test sets overlap WebText train by 0.9–6.3% (mean ≈ 3.2%) — comparable to or below their overlap with their own training sets (0.7–13.2%, mean ≈ 5.9%). They also found one Children's Book Test book (The Jungle Book) sitting in WebText and reported CBT on the contamination-free split instead. A separate memorization check in the appendix points the same way: GPT-2's own generated samples overlap the training set less than the genuine held-out test set does — most samples have under 1% 8-gram overlap (over 30% have none) against a 2.6% median for the real test set — so the high benchmark scores aren't simple regurgitation.

The caveat: this analysis bounds the contamination effect, it doesn't remove it. The authors can't say precisely how much of the 7-of-8 SOTA is genuine generalization versus recall, and they explicitly recommend n-gram-overlap de-duplication as a standard sanity check for future datasets — an admission that the field (themselves included) had not been doing this. Add the proxy-metric problem (ROUGE/BLEU for generation, judged largely qualitatively for free-form samples), the 273-example Winograd set, and the easily-over-read "63.1% on its top 1%" framing for Natural Questions, and the picture is a powerful demonstration accompanied by appropriately hedged measurement.

baselines

Mostly unsupervised/weak comparators. Even where "competitive," GPT-2 trails supervised SOTA badly — CoQA 55 vs ≈89 F1, summarization below Lead-3, En→Fr 5 BLEU under a word-by-word lexicon.

contamination

Self-audited with 8-gram Bloom filters (Table 6): test sets overlap WebText 0.9–6.3% (mean ≈3.2%), ≈ their own internal train/test overlap; one CBT book was in WebText. Bounded, not eliminated.

what's missed

Perplexity SOTA partly rewards a bigger, cleaner corpus; the more surprising downstream task numbers are weak absolutely and sometimes heuristic; sample quality assessed qualitatively, not with human eval.

would raise confidence

Decontaminated re-evaluation to separate recall from generalization, supervised baselines reported side-by-side, larger small-benchmark sets (Winograd), and human evaluation of generation over ROUGE/BLEU.

// bottom line

GPT-2's lasting result isn't any leaderboard cell — it's the demonstration that scaling an unsupervised next-token predictor on diverse text makes task ability emerge without any task-specific supervision — slowly, and gated by capacity — while the model still underfits its data. The evaluation is refreshingly honest about its own contamination (a template later work adopted), but the downstream numbers are proof-of-concept, not parity with supervised systems. The headline is the trend line, not the leaderboard.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. Language modeling is implicit multitask learning. A single model trained only to predict the next token on a large, diverse corpus (40 GB WebText) performs QA, translation, summarization and reading comprehension zero-shot — by conditioning on a task framed in natural language, with no task-specific data, architecture or fine-tuning.
  2. Capacity is the lever, and the tank isn't full. Zero-shot ability climbs log-linearly with model size; GPT-2 (1.5B) takes zero-shot SOTA on 7 of 8 LM benchmarks yet still underfits WebText — pointing straight at the scaling thesis GPT-3 would cash in a year later.
  3. It made contamination a first-class question. An 8-gram Bloom-filter audit showed benchmark test sets leak into web-scale corpora about as much as into their own train sets, and produced a standing recommendation — dedup by n-gram overlap — that outlived the model and reshaped how evals are built.
