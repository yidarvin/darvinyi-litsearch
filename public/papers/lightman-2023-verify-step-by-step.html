Let's Verify Step by Step

Grade a reward model on every reasoning step instead of only the final answer, and it becomes a far better judge of hard math: OpenAI's process-supervised reward model solves 78.2% of a representative MATH subset under best-of-N search, versus 72.4% for the outcome-only model and 69.6% for majority voting. The paper also releases PRM800K, 800K human step-level labels.

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

Large language models solve multi-step problems by writing out a chain of thought — but a single wrong step is enough to derail an otherwise long, correct solution, and models "exhibit a tendency to invent facts in moments of uncertainty." To make a model more reliable you train a reward model to tell good outputs from bad, then use it either to drive reinforcement learning or to rerank samples (best-of-N search). The catch: the resulting system "is only as reliable as the reward model itself," so the real question is how to train a reliable reward model.

There are two ways to supervise one. Outcome supervision labels only the final answer — cheap and fully automatic on a dataset like MATH, whose answers are auto-checkable. Process supervision labels every intermediate step — which needs humans, but is appealing for several reasons: it pinpoints exactly where a solution went wrong, it is easier for a person to interpret, and it directly rewards a human-endorsed chain of thought rather than treating the final answer as a proxy. Outcome-only models are known to "regularly use incorrect reasoning to reach the correct final answer," a misalignment process supervision should suppress.

So is the human-labeling cost of process supervision actually worth it? The closest prior study, Uesato et al. (2022), compared the two on grade-school math (GSM8K) and found they led to similar final-answer performance, with process supervision merely more data-efficient — a result that, taken at face value, says don't bother. This paper revisits the comparison with three deliberate changes: a much more capable base model, far more human feedback, and the harder MATH dataset — and releases the public dataset the field was missing.

// how it works

The method

A single fixed generator produces all solutions in a newline-delimited, step-by-step format. Crucially, the generator is never RL-trained here — that is explicitly out of scope. Every reward model is judged the same way: sample N solutions uniformly from the generator, pick the one the reward model scores highest, auto-grade its final answer, and report the fraction correct. A better reward model picks the correct solution more often. Experiments run in two regimes: large-scale, fine-tuned from the base GPT-4 model (next-token pretraining only, no RLHF), to push the state of the art; and small-scale (≈200× less pretraining compute), used for clean ablations. Both first get a lightweight math-pretraining boost on MathMix, a filtered 1.5B-token corpus of math problems and discussion.

The outcome-supervised reward model (ORM) follows Cobbe et al. (2021): train on uniformly sampled solutions to predict whether each one is correct or incorrect, determined by automatically checking the final answer; at test time the score is the model's prediction at the final token. The large-scale ORM is trained on 100 uniform samples per problem.

The process-supervised reward model (PRM) instead predicts the correctness of each step — a single token emitted after each step — and is trained as an ordinary language model. To turn per-step predictions into one comparable number, the PRM score for a solution is defined as the probability that every step is correct, implemented as the product of the per-step correctness probabilities:

score(solution) = ∏ i = 1..K pθ( stepi is correct ) // product over the K steps of a solution; neutral steps are counted as correct (best of four scoring variants, Table 4)

Process supervision is provided only up to the first incorrect step. This keeps the comparison clean — for a correct solution both methods say "every step is fine"; for an incorrect one both reveal a mistake exists, and process supervision additionally reveals where — and keeps human labeling cost similar, since finding the first mistake is roughly the work of judging the whole solution.

Collecting PRM800K — and "convincing wrong-answer" active learning

Human labelers mark each step positive, negative, or neutral through the interface below. Rather than show labelers uniform samples — most of whose errors are obvious and uninformative — the team surfaces convincing wrong-answer solutions: those rated highly by the current best PRM but which reach a wrong final answer, so the PRM is guaranteed to be wrong about at least one step. They re-train the PRM across ten generations of data collection, an active-learning loop. The released dataset, PRM800K, contains about 800,000 step-level labels across 75,000 solutions to 12,000 problems (filtered down from 1,085,590 raw labels over 101,599 solutions).

To run controlled ablations without paying for more human labels, the team uses the large-scale PRM (PRMlarge) as a synthetic oracle to supervise smaller models — marking a step incorrect when PRMlarge assigns it >20% probability of being negative. This trick simulates large-scale data collection cheaply and is what makes the apples-to-apples small-scale comparison possible.

// what they found

The results

At large scale, the PRM is the decisive winner. Evaluated by best-of-N search over up to 1,860 samples per problem, the process-supervised model solves 78.2% of the representative MATH subset, versus 72.4% for the outcome-supervised model and 69.6% for majority voting (a strong baseline in its own right). The PRM leads at every value of N, and — tellingly — the gap widens as N grows, meaning it is better at searching over a large pool of candidates, not just at picking among a few.

But the large-scale ORM and PRM training sets are not directly comparable (more on that in the critique), so the paper runs a clean small-scale synthetic comparison, holding the dataset identical and varying only the form of supervision (process from PRMlarge, outcome from PRMlarge, or outcome from final-answer checking). Here process supervision significantly outperforms both forms of outcome supervision at every data-collection scale. And outcome supervision from PRMlarge beats plain final-answer checking — because final-answer grading hands positive labels to solutions that reach the right answer through wrong reasoning.

Two further findings. Active learning — preferentially labeling convincing wrong-answer solutions — is estimated to be roughly 2.6× more data-efficient than uniform labeling. And the result is not just MATH-test memorization: on a held-out set of 224 fresh STEM problems from recent AP and AMC exams (released after pretraining), the PRM again leads with 72.9% aggregate best-of-100 accuracy versus 63.8% for the ORM and 61.3% for majority voting — winning on AP Calculus (86.7 vs 68.9), AP Chemistry (80.0 vs 68.9), AP Physics (86.7 vs 77.8), and AMC 10/12 (53.2 vs 49.1). A scoring ablation (Table 4) shows the headline number is robust to the exact reduction: all four step-aggregation strategies land between 77.4% and 78.2%.

// the alignment angle

Process supervision is the safer signal — it rewards an interpretable, human-endorsed chain of thought instead of using the outcome as a gameable proxy. Usually safer methods cost performance (an "alignment tax"). Here the safer method is also the more capable one, so the paper argues process supervision carries a negative alignment tax — a rare case where the aligned choice is also the performant one.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

This is an unusually self-aware paper — it flags most of its own confounds — so the job here is to weight them, not unearth them. The sharpest reads:

The headline isn't apples-to-apples, and the authors say so. PRM800K was built with active learning, is biased toward wrong-answer solutions, and is "an order of magnitude smaller" than the ORM's training set of 100 uniform samples per problem. So the 78.2-vs-72.4 large-scale comparison confounds supervision type with data distribution and scale. The honest head-to-head is the small-scale synthetic ablation (Figure 4), where process supervision still wins at every scale. One caveat: the paper's preferred outcome baseline is itself produced by PRMlarge, a process-supervised model, so that particular referee shares the home team's training signal — though process supervision also beats a PRMlarge-independent final-answer baseline, which blunts the concern. Either way, the cleanest comparison is not the one in the abstract.

It's a verifier result, not an RL result. The generator is held fixed by design — no policy is ever RL-fine-tuned. So the paper demonstrates that PRMs are better rerankers under best-of-N search, not that process supervision yields better models. And the headline gap is measured at best-of-1860, a very large test-time-compute budget; the PRM leads at every N, but its margin over the ORM is much smaller at small N and only widens as the sample budget grows (Figure 3). Read 78.2 vs 72.4 as "better search signal at high N," not "uniformly better reward."

Contamination on a known-leaky benchmark, with a tiny eval set. MATH problems circulate in online venues and likely sit in the pretraining corpus; the team string-matches MATH out of MathMix but "can make no strong guarantees" — that pretraining leakage is the genuine contamination risk. A separate point is the eval's size: to avoid overfitting the 7,500 training problems, the team moved 4,500 of the 5,000 MATH test problems into PRM800K's training set and report on only the remaining 500 — which keeps those 500 out of training, but leaves a small held-out set. To their credit, the fresh AP/AMC results (PRM 72.9 vs ORM 63.8) and the generator's low single-digit solve rates on surfaced problems are real evidence the result isn't memorization — that out-of-distribution check is the strongest part of the rigor story.

Reproducibility. The large-scale numbers ride on the closed base GPT-4; only PRM800K is released, not the generator or the reward models. The field inherited the dataset, not the headline system — and the 2.6× active-learning figure is a slope estimate from lines of best fit, with the largest active-learning point itself underperforming the trend.

baselines

Strong on paper — majority voting plus a maximally-resourced ORM (10× more data) — but the large-scale ORM/PRM training sets are deliberately not comparable. The clean comparison lives in the small-scale ablation, judged by a process-trained oracle (PRMlarge).

contamination

MATH is a known-leaky benchmark (problems likely sit in pretraining); the eval is just the 500 problems left after 4,500 test problems were moved into training to curb overfitting. Mitigated by fresh post-cutoff AP/AMC exams (PRM 72.9 vs ORM 63.8, best-of-100) and low generator solve-rates, not by a clean held-out guarantee.

what's missed

Best-of-N reranking only — no RL on the generator; the 78.2-vs-72.4 gap is the best-of-1860 extreme and shrinks toward parity at small N; everything is math-only, with cross-domain generalization left open.

would raise confidence

RL-fine-tune the generator with each reward model and compare end-to-end policies; report the gap as a function of N rather than at 1860; train an ORM on the same active-learning distribution; replicate on an open base model.

// bottom line

A landmark, carefully-hedged demonstration that step-level human feedback makes a far better verifier than answer-only feedback on hard math — and that the safer, more interpretable signal is also the more capable one. Because the headline is a best-of-N search result on a leaky benchmark with non-comparable training sets, the durable contributions are the direction (process > outcome for verification), the recipe (convincing-wrong-answer active learning), and PRM800K — the public dataset that seeded a generation of process-reward-model research.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. Supervising every reasoning step (process supervision) trains a far more reliable verifier than supervising only the final answer (outcome supervision): 78.2% vs 72.4% on a MATH subset under best-of-1860, with the gap widening as you search over more samples.
  2. The result is about verification under best-of-N search, not RL-trained policies — the generator is fixed by design — and the large-scale comparison is not apples-to-apples; the clean evidence is the small-scale synthetic ablation, where process still wins at every data scale.
  3. PRM800K (800K human step-labels over 75K solutions) plus "convincing wrong-answer" active learning (≈2.6× more data-efficient) are the lasting artifacts — they seeded the process-reward-model lineage running through DeepSeekMath / GRPO, TÜLU 3, rubric-reward work, and the o1-style reasoning models.
