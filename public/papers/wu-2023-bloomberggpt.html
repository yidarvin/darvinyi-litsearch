BloombergGPT: A Large Language Model for Finance

Bloomberg trained a 50-billion-parameter LLM from scratch on the largest domain-specific corpus yet assembled — 363B tokens of financial data — mixed roughly half-and-half with general text. The bet: a model that dominates finance NLP without giving up general-purpose ability. The honest subtext: is building one from scratch even worth it?

Shijie Wu, Ozan İrsoy, Steven Lu, Vadim Dabravolski, Mark Dredze, Sebastian Gehrmann, Prabhanjan Kambadur, David Rosenberg, Gideon Mann (Bloomberg)

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

By early 2023, general-purpose LLMs — GPT-3, OPT, BLOOM, the about-to-land LLaMA — had shown that scale plus broad web text buys you a model that is competent almost everywhere. But finance is a domain where "almost" is expensive. A headline like "COMPANY to cut 10,000 jobs" reads as negative in ordinary sentiment but is often positive for the stock; tickers, filings, and earnings-call transcripts carry conventions that web-trained models rarely see cleanly. At the time of writing, the authors note, no LLM specialized for the financial domain had been reported in the literature.

The obvious objection is that you don't need a bespoke model: take an open LLM and prompt or fine-tune it. The paper's real question is sharper and more uncomfortable — if an organization happens to sit on a uniquely large, clean, proprietary financial corpus, does training a model from scratch on it actually beat the general models on the tasks that matter, and does it cost you anything on everything else? Bloomberg is one of the few places that can even ask this honestly: four decades of curated news, filings, press releases, and market data. The contribution is less "here is a finance chatbot" and more a controlled data point on domain-specific pretraining — does mixing in-house data with general data produce a model that is best-in-class on finance while staying comparable to its size-peers on standard benchmarks?

// the wager

Spend a 1.3M GPU-hour budget on a 50B model trained 51% on Bloomberg's own data and 49% on public text — then measure whether the finance specialization came for free on general tasks, or at a cost.

// how it works

The method

FinPile — the data is the moat

The centerpiece is FinPile: a 363-billion-token dataset drawn from Bloomberg's archives (51.27% of training) — Web (298B), News (38B), Filings (14B), Press releases (9B), and Bloomberg-authored content (5B). To that they add 345B tokens of public data (48.73%): The Pile (184B), C4 (138B), and Wikipedia (24B). The total training corpus is roughly 700B tokens, deliberately kept near a 50/50 finance-to-general split so the in-domain signal never drops below half. They also train their own Unigram tokenizer (vocabulary 131,072) on this mixture rather than reusing an off-the-shelf one — a choice they later credit as the second-largest driver of the model's quality.

A Chinchilla-sized model, then capped by data

Sizing follows the Chinchilla compute-optimal recipe. Starting from a 1.3M A100-hour budget (and discounting for activation-checkpointing overhead), their fits imply an optimal ~53B parameters and ~1.1 trillion tokens. They can grow general data freely, but FinPile is finite and they refuse to let it fall below half the corpus — so they are data-limited, not compute-limited. They pick the largest model they can fully train on the tokens they have while leaving ~30% of compute as a buffer for restarts: a 50.6B-parameter decoder-only model. Figure 1 shows where it lands relative to the scaling frontier and its contemporaries.

Architecturally it is a BLOOM-style decoder: 70 transformer layers, 40 heads (head dimension 192), hidden size 7,680, ALiBi positional biases (so it extrapolates past its 2,048-token training context), GELU feed-forward blocks, tied input/output embeddings, and an extra LayerNorm after the token embeddings for stability. The update per layer is the standard pre-norm residual:

h̄ℓ = hℓ−1 + SA(LN(hℓ−1))  ;  hℓ = h̄ℓ + FFN(LN(h̄ℓ)) // pre-norm self-attention + feed-forward residual blocks, 70 deep

The training run, warts and all

Training ran on AWS SageMaker across 512 A100 40GB GPUs (64 × p4d.24xlarge), using ZeRO stage-3 sharding, MiCS, activation checkpointing, BF16 mixed precision, and fused kernels — landing at 102 TFLOPs average and 32.5 s/step. They trained for 139,200 steps (~53 days), completing only ~80% of a single epoch (569B of the 709B available tokens) before the held-out loss stopped improving. Unusually, the paper publishes its Training Chronicles: the run required hands-on intervention late, with three learning-rate cuts (and added dropout) after step 115,500 to fight a flattening validation loss (Figure 2). It is one of the more candid public accounts of what babysitting a large run actually looks like.

// what they found

The results

The comparison cohort is three open models the team could run themselves under identical conditions — GPT-NeoX (20B), OPT 66B, and BLOOM 176B — plus GPT-3 numbers quoted from prior papers (no API access). Every task is plain few-shot prompting on the base model: no fine-tuning, no chain-of-thought, no per-model prompt tuning. The first sanity check is language-modeling fit on a temporally held-out, deduplicated slice of FinPile (Figure 3): BloombergGPT has the lowest bits-per-byte in every category, and the gap is largest on Filings — documents that are public but usually locked in PDFs and therefore absent from web corpora.

On the external financial benchmarks (four FLUE tasks + ConvFinQA), BloombergGPT wins four of five and posts the best average and win rate. The standout is ConvFinQA, which demands numerical reasoning over earnings-report tables across conversational turns:

The margins blow open on Bloomberg's internal aspect-sentiment tasks, where BloombergGPT averages 62.47 against 29.23 / 35.76 / 33.39 and wins every task (WR 1.00). On Equity News sentiment it scores 79.63 versus 14.17 / 20.98 / 19.96 — a 25-to-60+ point gulf on every task except social media, where the others are competitive. On the joint NER+NED task (resolve a company mention to its stock ticker, e.g. "Intel" → INTC), it averages 64.83 with a 0.95 win rate, far ahead of the like-sized models. On plain internal NER the much larger BLOOM 176B edges it out, and BloombergGPT is merely best of the smaller models — a reminder that generative LLMs are an awkward fit for span-extraction.

The pattern is steady: BloombergGPT beats GPT-NeoX and OPT 66B almost everywhere, trades blows with the 3.5×-larger BLOOM 176B (on MMLU it wins by all of 0.05 points, 39.18 vs 39.13), and sits a notch below GPT-3 and the 10×-larger PaLM 540B. The headline claim is supported in its careful form: specializing on finance did not visibly degrade general ability for a model of this size. The authors close by attributing the model's edge, in decreasing order, to (1) the curated internal dataset, (2) the custom tokenizer, and (3) an up-to-date architecture. The flashier capabilities — turning natural language into valid Bloomberg Query Language, suggesting news headlines, answering "who is the CEO of X" — appear only as qualitative samples (Figure 4 in the paper), not as scored benchmarks.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

The evaluation is unusually disciplined in one respect — every open baseline was run in-house under an identical few-shot harness, with no per-model prompt tuning, so the financial-task comparison is genuinely apples-to-apples among GPT-NeoX, OPT 66B, BLOOM 176B, and BloombergGPT. That fairness is real and rare. But it is bounded on every side, and the most-quoted numbers rest on data nobody outside Bloomberg can see.

Start with the contamination the paper itself flags. For the internal sentiment and NER datasets — the tasks with the most spectacular margins — the authors write that "it is likely that the unlabeled versions of the data used in our internal datasets occur in FinPile and are therefore seen by BloombergGPT during training." Only the labels are held out; the input text was, by their own account, probably in pretraining. They argue web copies may have leaked into the other models too, but BloombergGPT saw the clean, Bloomberg-curated versions of exactly these documents. A 79.63-vs-14.17 gap on Equity News sentiment is therefore measuring in-domain familiarity as much as reasoning, on a benchmark that can never be independently audited. (The held-out language-modeling test in Figure 3, by contrast, is clean — temporally future and deduplicated — and is the more trustworthy in-domain result.)

Next, the baselines are fair but neither current nor complete. By the paper's March 2023 release the relevant frontier was GPT-3.5 / ChatGPT — with GPT-4 already launched two weeks earlier — yet none appear; the strongest comparison is GPT-3 numbers lifted second-hand from 2020–21 papers. And the paper explicitly omits task-specific non-LLM baselines ("due to differences in the evaluation setup"), so we never learn whether a fine-tuned 110M-parameter FinBERT beats a 50B-parameter BloombergGPT on FPB or FiQA. Given that all evals are zero-fine-tuning few-shot, the absolute numbers are modest — FPB at 51 F1, MMLU at 39 — so the claim is strictly relative ("best among LLMs of its class"), not state-of-the-art on the tasks themselves. On the external financial sets they also make their own train/test splits and report on dev sets, which makes cross-paper comparison fragile.

Finally, what the headline elides. The "no degradation" story is true but narrow: the general-purpose wins over BLOOM 176B are often razor-thin (0.05 points on MMLU), the model trails GPT-3 across the board, and it never finished even one epoch. The economically interesting capabilities — BQL generation, headline writing, financial QA — are shown only as cherry-picked qualitative examples, with no quantitative eval, no failure analysis, and no measure of hallucination on a domain where a wrong number is a real liability. The "win rate" metric compounds this: a 0.1-point win counts the same as a 50-point one. In hindsight the paper is best read as an honest, well-documented negative-leaning data point — within a year, open-weight finance fine-tunes (FinBen, PIXIU) and far stronger general models made "train a domain LLM from scratch" the expensive path not taken.

baselines

Open models (GPT-NeoX 20B, OPT 66B, BLOOM 176B) all run in-house under one identical few-shot harness — genuinely fair. But no GPT-3.5/4 (available at writing), GPT-3 only quoted second-hand, and fine-tuned task-specific baselines deliberately excluded, so absolute competitiveness is untested.

contamination

Self-disclosed: unlabeled text of the internal sentiment/NER sets is in FinPile, so the biggest margins (e.g. 79.63 vs 14.17) partly reflect in-domain exposure. The Figure-3 held-out LM loss is the clean exception (temporally future + deduplicated).

what's missed

Few-shot only, no fine-tuning → modest absolute scores; flagship use cases (BQL, headlines, CEO QA) shown only qualitatively; no hallucination or calibration measurement on a domain where wrong numbers carry liability; win-rate weights a 0.1-pt edge like a 50-pt one.

would raise confidence

A contamination-controlled public financial benchmark; comparison to fine-tuned small models and to contemporaneous GPT-3.5/4; variance across few-shot seeds; and a quantified, error-analyzed evaluation of the BQL/QA applications rather than hand-picked samples.

// bottom line

A rigorously-run, refreshingly candid study whose fairness on the open baselines is exemplary — but whose marquee results live on proprietary, partly-pretrained-on data, omit the strongest baselines available, and answer "is a from-scratch domain LLM worth it?" with a quiet "probably fine-tune instead."

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. Data is the moat, not scale. A 50B model on a 363B-token proprietary financial corpus (mixed ~50/50 with general text) beats its 20B–176B open peers on finance and stays even on general benchmarks — the authors credit the curated dataset and custom tokenizer above the architecture.
  2. Domain pretraining was nearly free on general ability — but the absolute bar was low and the margins thin. BloombergGPT leads its size class yet trails GPT-3 everywhere and beats BLOOM 176B on MMLU by 0.05 points, all in zero-fine-tuning few-shot. The relative win is solid; absolute SOTA on the tasks is not claimed.
  3. The most honest contribution is the candor. Published Training Chronicles, a temporally-clean held-out LM test, and an explicit contamination disclosure make this a model card for how to report a domain LLM — even as the closed weights, closed data, and missing frontier baselines make the headline finance numbers impossible to reproduce.
