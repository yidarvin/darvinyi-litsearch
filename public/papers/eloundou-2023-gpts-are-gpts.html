GPTs are GPTs: An Early Look at the Labor Market Impact Potential of Large Language Models

A title that is a thesis: Generative Pre-trained Transformers are General-Purpose Technologies. To prove it, the authors score an LLM-exposure rubric over every one of O*NET's 1,016 occupations — using both human annotators and GPT-4 — and turn "which jobs will LLMs touch?" into a measurable quantity. The headline: ~80% of U.S. workers have at least 10% of their tasks exposed, and, unusually, exposure rises with wages.

Tyna Eloundou, Sam Manning, Pamela Mishkin, Daniel Rock

What problem is this solving?

Economists already had a toolkit for measuring how technology touches jobs. Frey & Osborne (2017) estimated which occupations were "automatable"; Brynjolfsson, Mitchell & Rock (2018) built a "suitability for machine learning" rubric; Felten et al. (2018) and Webb (2020) mapped AI patents and capabilities onto worker tasks. Every one of these was designed for a prior wave of technology — robotics, classification ML, computer vision — and most were built before ChatGPT existed.

Large language models break that mold. Their strengths — drafting text, summarizing, translating, writing and debugging code, answering open-ended questions — cut across white-collar work in a pattern that earlier automation indices were not built to capture. After the jump from GPT-3.5 to GPT-4, the live policy question became concrete: across the whole U.S. economy, which tasks can an LLM — or software built on top of one — actually accelerate, and who does that fall on?

This paper's contribution is to answer that with a task-level, LLM-specific exposure measure, grounded in a single concrete rubric (can the task be done in half the time at equal quality?), applied at economy scale, and — crucially — validated against two independent raters: experienced human annotators and GPT-4 itself. One framing caveat is load-bearing throughout: this measures "exposure," not automation or job loss. It is an estimate of technical potential to make labor more efficient, explicitly not a forecast of adoption, displacement, or wages.

// how it works

The method

The substrate is the U.S. Department of Labor's O*NET 27.2 database, which decomposes 1,016 occupations into 19,265 tasks and 2,087 Detailed Work Activities (DWAs). The question is asked at the task/DWA level and then aggregated up to occupations.

The core instrument is the exposure rubric, with a deliberately interpretable threshold — could the LLM cut the time to complete the task by at least 50% while holding quality equal ("equivalent quality" meaning a third party, typically the recipient, wouldn't notice or care that an LLM helped)?

  • E0 — No exposure: using the LLM yields no meaningful time saving, or degrades quality.
  • E1 — Direct exposure: the LLM accessed directly (ChatGPT or the OpenAI Playground) can cut task time by ≥50%.
  • E2 — LLM+ exposed: the LLM alone can't hit the bar, but additional software built on top of it could (image-generation systems are folded in here as well).

Two independent raters applied the rubric. Humans: the authors (who labeled physically demanding activities) plus experienced annotators from OpenAI's alignment work. GPT-4: given a slightly tuned version of the rubric as a prompt and run over all task/occupation pairs. The authors are explicit that neither is ground truth; human ratings are the primary results, with GPT-4 as a scalable second opinion.

From the E0/E1/E2 labels they build three exposure measures — a lower bound, a weighted middle, and an upper bound — by taking the (core-weighted) share of an occupation's tasks at each level:

α = share(E1)   |   β = share(E1) + ½·share(E2)   |   ζ = share(E1) + share(E2) // α = direct-access lower bound · β = preferred measure (E2 discounted for the co-invention it requires) · ζ = upper bound with full LLM-powered tooling. Core tasks weighted 2× supplemental; weights sum to 1.

Do the two raters agree? At the occupation level, broadly yes — and that agreement is the methodological linchpin. For the preferred β measure, human and GPT-4 ratings correlate at Pearson 0.591 (rising to 0.654 for ζ), with categorical agreement of 80.8% / 65.6% / 82.1% for α / β / ζ. The agreement is strongest at the low and high ends and noisiest in the middle — exactly where the speculative E2 judgment dominates.

// what they found

The results

The pervasiveness claim holds. Based on the β measure, ~80% of U.S. workers belong to an occupation with at least 10% of its tasks exposed to LLMs, and ~19% are in an occupation where more than half of its tasks are exposed. The average occupation has roughly 15% of tasks directly exposed (α), rising to over 30% under β and past 50% under ζ.

Reframed across all U.S. tasks: with direct LLM access, about 15% of all worker tasks could be completed significantly faster at equal quality — and once you count software and tooling built on top of LLMs, that share climbs to between 47% and 56% (the spread between human and GPT-4 ζ estimates). That gap between 15% and ~50% is the paper's thesis: the economic impact is scaled not by the model alone but by the applications layered on it.

Exposure rises with wages — the surprise

The signature, counterintuitive result is about who is exposed. Plotting occupation-level exposure against the log of median annual wage, the trend slopes up: higher-wage occupations are, on average, more exposed to LLMs. This directly inverts earlier machine-learning-exposure measures (Brynjolfsson et al., 2023), where lower-wage work was the more exposed. Exposure shows essentially no relationship with employment level — it is spread across the economy, not concentrated in a few large occupations.

The skill regressions sharpen the picture. Importance of science and critical-thinking skills is strongly negatively associated with exposure (the science coefficient is −0.230*** on β), while writing and programming are strongly positively associated (writing is +0.467*** on β). The technology is, in the authors' framing, most aligned with verbal and code-generation work and least with empirical scientific reasoning.

Barriers to entry: more preparation, more exposure

Grouping occupations by O*NET "Job Zones" (1 = least preparation, ~3 months; 5 = most, 4+ years of education/training) shows exposure rising with the difficulty of getting into the job — peaking at Job Zone 4 and leveling off or dipping at 5. The share of workers in occupations with >50% β-exposure runs 0.00% (JZ1), 6.11% (JZ2), 10.57% (JZ3), 34.5% (JZ4), 26.45% (JZ5) — even as median income climbs monotonically from $30,230 (JZ1) to $80,980 (JZ5).

Concretely, the occupations rated fully exposed (100% of tasks at ζ) by human raters include Mathematicians, Tax Preparers, Financial Quantitative Analysts, Writers and Authors, and Web & Digital Interface Designers; humans flagged 15 occupations as fully exposed and GPT-4 flagged 86. The least exposed are physically intensive, lower-wage manual jobs. Finally, the new measure isn't redundant with the old ones: prior exposure indices plus wage controls explain only 60–72% of its variance, leaving 28–40% genuinely new.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

This is an influential paper that is careful about its own limits — and the careful reader should hold onto those limits, because the headline numbers travel far more widely than the caveats. Three things deserve scrutiny.

The raters grade their own kind. The measurement rests on GPT-4 assessing what GPT-class models can do, scored against a rubric the authors iteratively tuned on a small validation set to match human labels — and the human labelers were OpenAI alignment annotators, "not occupationally diverse." The authors themselves flag that GPT-4's outputs are sensitive to rubric wording, prompt order, and the examples included. High agreement is reassuring, but note that the direct-exposure α correlation between raters is only Pearson 0.223; the strong β/ζ correlations (0.591 / 0.654) lean on the shared, far more speculative E2 "imagine software could be built for this" judgment.

"Exposure" is potential, not destiny. The rubric asks whether a task's time could be halved at equal quality — a statement about technical capability, not about adoption, diffusion, regulation, human-oversight norms, or labor demand. The paper makes no prediction about timelines and is upfront that real-world time reductions will likely be smaller than the rubric's threshold. The most-quoted figure — 47–56% of tasks — comes almost entirely from E2, i.e. from hypothetical LLM-powered software that did not exist when the paper was written. Strip that away and the directly measurable number (α) is ~15%.

The task-bag assumption is doing a lot of work. The whole pipeline treats an occupation as a weighted bag of independent O*NET tasks. The authors concede that if this decomposition is invalid — because of tacit skills, task interdependence, or precursor tasks that gate downstream ones — "our exposure analysis would largely be invalidated." Annotators were often unaware which occupation a given DWA even belonged to, which complicates aggregation. And there is no validation against observed productivity: exposure is a proxy, never an in-the-wild measurement of how much faster real workers actually go.

baselines

Benchmarked against six prior exposure indices (Frey–Osborne, Webb, Felten et al., Brynjolfsson et al., Acemoglu–Autor); those plus wage controls explain only 60–72% of the new measure's variance, so it carries genuinely new signal. But there is no benchmark of measured task speedups to validate the rubric against.

contamination

Not leakage in the usual sense — the analogous risk is self-assessment circularity: GPT-4 grading GPT-class capabilities, with a rubric tuned on a small held-out set to match human labels from a narrow annotator pool.

what's missed

Adoption and diffusion timelines, regulation, human-oversight requirements, labor-demand and wage effects, and task interdependence. "Exposure" ≠ automation or job loss; the 47–56% headline rides on hypothetical software (E2), not anything deployable in 2023.

would raise confidence

Occupation-expert raters; validation against observed task-time reductions; sensitivity analysis isolating the E2 contribution; replication on a non-O*NET task taxonomy; longitudinal tracking of exposure → adoption → productivity.

// bottom line

A landmark reframing: it converted "will AI take jobs?" into a concrete, task-level exposure quantity and surfaced the durable, counterintuitive finding that LLM exposure rises with wages and educational barriers to entry. But the famous 80% / 47–56% figures are upper-bound estimates of technical reach, not predictions of automation — and they lean on the most speculative part of the rubric and on the model grading its own kind. Read it as an exposure map of the economy, not a forecast of it.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. GPTs are GPTs. The claim is that LLMs are general-purpose technologies — pervasive across the economy — and the paper operationalizes it with a task-level exposure rubric scored over all 1,016 O*NET occupations rather than a single benchmark. ~80% of workers have ≥10% of tasks exposed; ~19% have over half.
  2. Exposure rises with wages and education. The signature result inverts prior ML/automation-exposure measures: writing- and programming-heavy white-collar work is the most exposed, science- and critical-thinking-heavy work the least — and higher Job Zones (more preparation) are more exposed, not less.
  3. Potential, not destiny. Directly measurable exposure (α) is ~15% of tasks; the 47–56% headline depends on the E2 "software could be built on the LLM" judgment and assumes away adoption and diffusion friction. It's an upper bound on technical reach, not a job-loss forecast — and the raters include GPT-4 grading its own kind.
