CIDEr: Consensus-based Image Description Evaluation

Score a generated caption by how closely it matches the consensus of many human descriptions. A TF-IDF-weighted n-gram cosine that matched human judgment better than BLEU, ROUGE, or METEOR — and, as CIDEr-D, became the default captioning metric inside the MS COCO evaluation server.

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

By late 2014, generating a sentence to describe an image had become a hot problem — riding advances in object detection, attribute and action classification, and crowdsourcing. But the field had no trustworthy way to measure progress. Human studies (rating captions 1–5 on grammaticality, saliency, correctness, or judging them pairwise) are expensive, slow, hard to reproduce, and — worse — there is no principled way to fold those separate axes into a single quality number.

So practitioners borrowed automatic metrics from neighboring fields: BLEU (n-gram precision, from machine translation) and ROUGE (n-gram recall, from summarization), with METEOR (an MT metric with stemming and synonym matching) doing somewhat better. The trouble is that all three correlate only weakly-to-moderately with human judgment at the level of an individual sentence — BLEU's effectiveness had been "repeatedly questioned" — and a third family of metrics could only rank a fixed pool of candidate captions, not score a novel generated one.

The paper's sharpest move is conceptual. The authors note a subtle trap: "what humans like often does not correspond to what is human-like." The caption a human rates "best" is frequently not the one most typical of how people actually describe that image. Rather than chase the slippery target of "quality," they redefine the goal as human-likeness: how well a candidate matches the consensus — the way the majority of people describe the image. That target is concrete and measurable. The remaining obstacle was data: existing caption datasets had at most five descriptions per image, far too few to estimate what a "majority" would say.

// how it works

The method

The work has three parts that fit together: a human protocol that produces a ground-truth notion of consensus, an automatic metric (CIDEr) that approximates it cheaply, and two new datasets with enough references to make consensus estimable.

A relative, forced-choice human protocol

To get ground truth, the authors avoid absolute 1–5 ratings — which are noisy and hard to calibrate — in favor of a triplet (two-alternative forced-choice) task. A worker sees three sentences A, B, and C, where A is one human reference and B, C are two candidate captions, and answers a single question: "Which of B or C is more similar to A?" Pairing each candidate pair with every reference sentence yields many triplets; three workers vote on each, and the majority winner across all of an image's references is the consensus-preferred caption. A metric's accuracy is then the fraction of candidate pairs on which the metric's higher-scored caption matches this human-chosen winner. The relative phrasing makes the judgment far more objective than an absolute score.

The CIDEr metric: TF-IDF n-gram cosine

CIDEr scores how well a candidate caption ci matches a set of reference sentences Si. Every word is first reduced to its stem ("fishes", "fishing", "fished" → "fish"), and each sentence is represented by the set of n-grams it contains, for n = 1 to 4. Each n-gram is weighted by TF-IDF: the term-frequency term rewards n-grams that recur in the references for this image, while the inverse-document-frequency term — the log of the number of images divided by the number of images whose references contain that n-gram — downweights n-grams that are common across all images. That IDF doubles as a saliency signal: generic phrases ("there is a", "in the") get discounted, so the metric leans on the words that actually distinguish this image.

For each n-gram length, CIDEr is the average cosine similarity between the candidate's TF-IDF n-gram vector and each reference's — which captures both precision (don't say things the references don't) and recall (do say what they do):

CIDErn(ci, Si) = 1m  Σj  gn(ci) · gn(sij) ‖gn(ci)‖ ‖gn(sij)‖ // mean cosine of TF-IDF n-gram vectors over the m reference sentences

The final score sums the four n-gram lengths with uniform weights (the authors found uniform weighting worked best):

CIDEr(ci, Si) = Σn=1N wn CIDErn(ci, Si) // wn = 1/N, with N = 4

Two 50-sentence datasets

To measure consensus you need many references, so the authors collected PASCAL-50S (50 human sentences for each of the 1,000 UIUC Pascal Sentence images) and ABSTRACT-50S (50 sentences for 500 clipart "Abstract Scenes" images). Sentences were gathered on Amazon Mechanical Turk (US workers, >95% approval, ≥500 prior HITs), each by a different person, averaging 8.8 words (PASCAL) and 10.59 words (ABSTRACT). These are testing sets, built specifically to make consensus-based evaluation possible.

CIDEr-D: hardening the metric against gaming

Because a metric becomes a target the moment people optimize against it, the authors ship a robust variant, CIDEr-D: it (1) removes stemming so word forms must be correct, (2) adds a Gaussian penalty (σ = 6) on the difference between candidate and reference lengths to stop long, repetitive captions from inflating the score, and (3) clips each n-gram's candidate count to its reference count, penalizing repeated "confident" phrases. A ×10 factor keeps its scale comparable to other metrics. CIDEr-D correlates with the original CIDEr at Spearman ρ = 0.94 while being harder to game — and it is the version that was integrated into the MS COCO caption evaluation server.

// what they found

The results

How many references do you need? Agreement with human consensus climbs steeply over the first ~10 reference sentences, keeps improving out to about 50, then saturates. For ROUGE1, going from 1 to 5 references lifts accuracy from 0.63 to 0.77. Tellingly, BLEU1 gets worse with more references — it takes a max over per-reference matches, so adding references only raises the odds of matching some low-quality one. This is the empirical case for collecting 50 sentences per image rather than the field's customary five.

Matching human consensus. At 48 references, CIDEr is the best metric on both datasets, followed by METEOR, reaching 84% agreement on PASCAL-50S and 84% on ABSTRACT-50S. For context, BLEU1/ROUGE1 at the standard 5 references score only 76%/74% on PASCAL-50S — so CIDEr's 84% closes much of the gap toward the human ceiling of 90%. On the Flickr8K dataset (correlating against 1–5 human ratings), CIDEr reaches Spearman ρ = 0.58, edging METEOR's 0.56.

Breaking accuracy down by the type of pair being judged tells the more interesting story. CIDEr is best in 5 of 6 columns, and its biggest margins come on the hardest, most fine-grained comparisons — machine–machine (MM, 72.2 vs METEOR's 67.7) and human–human-correct (HC, 71.8 vs 66.3) pairs — the discrimination you most need as captioners improve. The one column it trails on is human–machine (HM); the paper treats HI and HM as the easier pairs, where every metric already scores in the 90s.

Ranking real captioners. Used to rank five 2014-era systems (Midge, Babytalk, Story, and two video-description models), CIDEr agrees with humans almost perfectly: Midge is best and Story worst under both, and the Pearson correlation between human and CIDEr win-fractions is 0.98. In a head-to-head where humans and the metric see the same 24 references, the best machine (CIDEr) hits 82% on PASCAL-50S against humans' 90%, and 82% vs 83% on ABSTRACT-50S.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

CIDEr's defining strength is also its ceiling: consensus is popularity, by construction. The metric rewards a caption for matching the majority phrasing, and the authors are admirably explicit that "what humans like often does not correspond to what is human-like." CIDEr optimizes for human-like (typical), not for what a person would judge best — so a precise, unusual, or more informative caption can score lower simply for being atypical. As a tracker of "are captioners producing human-like sentences," that's the right target; as a proxy for caption quality or correctness, it measures conformity to the crowd, not truth.

Second, the headline "best metric" lives in a regime almost no benchmark uses. CIDEr pulls clearly ahead at 48 references, but the community — and the COCO server it shipped in — evaluates with about five. At five references on PASCAL-50S, METEOR is competitive with (and on that dataset, edges) CIDEr, precisely because its soft WordNet matching helps when references are sparse. A good chunk of CIDEr's advantage is bought with 10× more reference sentences than real datasets carry.

Third, it is still a surface n-gram metric. TF-IDF cosine over stemmed n-grams has no semantics; paraphrase and synonymy beyond stemming are invisible to it. That is exactly the blind spot that motivated later metrics (SPICE's scene graphs, then embedding- and model-based metrics) — and the well-documented failure mode where n-gram metrics, CIDEr included, saturate and stop discriminating among strong neural captioners. The paper validates on weak 2014-era template/retrieval systems and argues CIDEr "will continue to perform well as methods improve," but that was an untested promise that later neural captioners would strain.

baselines

Fair for 2014: BLEU1/BLEU4, ROUGE1/ROUGEL, and METEOR v1.5, each in its best-performing variant. But all are pre-neural surface metrics — there is no semantic or learned baseline to beat.

contamination

N/A — CIDEr is a metric, not a trained model, so there is no train/test leakage. The subtler issue is mild circularity: the gold standard is itself a "majority-similarity" triplet protocol that shares CIDEr's inductive bias, so some agreement is baked in.

what's missed

Semantics and paraphrase (n-gram only); absolute quality vs. mere typicality; discrimination among strong modern captioners. The advantage also shrinks toward the 5-reference counts real benchmarks actually use.

would raise confidence

Correlation with human ratings on a broad set of modern neural captioners; results reported at 5 references (the real regime), not just 48; and a quantified adversarial/gaming stress test rather than the heuristic CIDEr-D patches (σ=6, clipping).

// bottom line

CIDEr turned a sharp idea — score a caption by how much it looks like the consensus human description — into a cheap TF-IDF n-gram cosine that beat BLEU, ROUGE, and METEOR at matching crowd judgment, and as CIDEr-D became the field's default captioning metric through the COCO server. Its premise is also its limit: it rewards typicality over correctness and stays a surface metric, so it ages into the same n-gram blind spots once captioners get good. A landmark for how to validate a generation metric against human consensus; a metric to use with eyes open about what "consensus" does and doesn't mean.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. CIDEr reframes caption evaluation: instead of chasing "quality", it measures human-likeness — similarity to the consensus of how many people describe an image — and makes that measurable with a relative, forced-choice (2AFC) triplet protocol.
  2. The metric itself is simple: TF-IDF-weighted n-gram (1–4) cosine similarity, averaged over references. The IDF term doubles as a saliency weight, discounting words that are common across all images.
  3. Reference count is load-bearing. Agreement keeps rising to ~50 references and saturates; CIDEr reached 84% agreement on PASCAL-50S/ABSTRACT-50S against a 90% human ceiling — but at the five references real datasets carry, METEOR is competitive.
  4. CIDEr-D (de-stemmed, with a Gaussian length penalty and n-gram clipping to resist gaming) shipped in the MS COCO caption evaluation server and went on to become the field's standard captioning metric.
