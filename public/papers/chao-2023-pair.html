Jailbreaking Black Box Large Language Models in Twenty Queries

PAIR pits an attacker LLM against a target LLM in a fully automated back-and-forth loop, finding human-readable jailbreaks with only black-box query access — usually in under twenty queries, versus the hundreds of thousands a gradient-based attack needs.

Patrick Chao, Alexander Robey, Edgar Dobriban, Hamed Hassani, George J. Pappas, Eric Wong

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

By late 2023, jailbreaking LLMs — coaxing an aligned model into overriding its own safety training — had split into two families, and each had a disqualifying flaw for anyone trying to red-team a model at scale. Prompt-level jailbreaks (hand-written social-engineering prompts, like "pretend you're DAN" or role-play framings) are semantically meaningful and effective, but the paper is blunt about the cost: "this technique requires creativity, manual dataset curation, and customized human feedback, leading to considerable human time and resource investments." Token-level jailbreaks — exemplified by GCG (Zou et al., 2023), which optimizes an adversarial token suffix via gradients — are automated and effective, but require white-box access to the target model's weights and gradients, and the paper notes they "require hundreds of thousands of queries to the target model and are often uninterpretable to humans." Neither family works for the setting that actually matters for red-teaming production systems: you usually only have black-box API access to a closed model (GPT-4, Claude, Gemini), and you want something both scalable (no human sitting in the loop hand-crafting each prompt) and interpretable (a human can read the resulting jailbreak and understand what vulnerability it exploits, rather than staring at token soup). The paper frames its goal directly: "strike a balance between the labor-intensive and non-scalable prompt-level jailbreaks with the uninterpretable and query-inefficient token-level jailbreaks."

// how it works

The method: an attacker LLM interrogating a target LLM

PAIR — Prompt Automatic Iterative Refinement — is "inspired by social engineering attacks": it uses one LLM (the attacker A) to automatically generate and refine jailbreak prompts against a second LLM (the target T), with no human in the loop and no access to either model's weights or gradients — pure black-box query access to both. The attacker is seeded with a system prompt describing the objective O (e.g., "design a phishing email that can trick users into giving away their login credentials") and one of three persuasion-strategy templates — role-playing, logical appeal, or authority endorsement — following the taxonomy of Zeng et al. Each iteration runs four steps: (1) attack generation — the attacker A proposes a candidate prompt P; (2) target response — P is sent to the target T, producing a response R; (3) jailbreak scoring — a JUDGE function scores the pair (P, R) as jailbroken (S=1) or not (S=0); (4) iterative refinement — if S=0, the tuple (P, R, S) is appended to the attacker's conversation history, and the attacker generates a stronger candidate, explicitly reasoning about why the last attempt failed (a chain-of-thought "improvement" field the attacker must output in JSON alongside each new prompt).

Because each conversation stream is independent, PAIR is fully parallelizable: the paper runs N streams in parallel, each for up to K iterations, for a maximum query budget of N·K. The default configuration throughout the main experiments is N = 30 streams, K = 3 iterations — the authors found this "N ≫ K" regime (many shallow streams rather than few deep ones) more effective than the reverse, since an ablation (Figure 5 in the paper) shows most successful jailbreaks are found in the first one or two iterations of a stream, with diminishing returns after that and outright performance collapse past K > 50 as the attacker gets stuck in a generation loop.

Choosing the JUDGE function turns out to be its own small research problem: the authors built a 100-prompt/response dataset from AdvBench, had three expert human annotators label each as jailbroken or not (95% inter-annotator agreement), then benchmarked six candidate JUDGE functions against that majority-vote label (Table 1). GPT-4 scored the highest agreement at 88% (16% FPR, 7% FNR), but the authors ultimately chose Llama Guard instead — a 76% agreement, 7% FPR, 47% FNR classifier — reasoning that "minimizing the FPR is essential... it is more important to remain conservative to avoid classifying benign behavior as jailbroken," and that an open-source JUDGE keeps the whole pipeline reproducible, unlike GPT-4.

// what they found

The results

Evaluated on the 100-behavior JBB-Behaviors dataset (part of JailbreakBench, spanning 10 misuse categories) with Mixtral 8x7B as the attacker, PAIR's headline jailbreak percentages (JB%) and mean queries-per-success were: Vicuna-13B 88% (10.0 queries), Llama-2-7B-chat 4% (56.0 queries), GPT-3.5-Turbo 51% (33.0 queries), GPT-4 48% (23.7 queries), Claude-instant-1.2 3% (13.7 queries), Claude-2.1 0%, and Gemini-Pro 73% (23.5 queries) — the paper states "PAIR is the first automated jailbreak that has been shown to jailbreak Gemini-Pro." Against the same targets, GCG (which requires white-box access, so it's only evaluable on the two open-source models) gets 56% on Vicuna and 2% on Llama-2, but needs 256,000 queries per the paper's protocol — PAIR's efficiency advantage on Vicuna alone is roughly 25,600× fewer queries for a higher success rate. A baseline using 10 popular human-crafted templates from jailbreakchat.com (labeled JBC) scores lower than PAIR across nearly every model (Vicuna 56%, GPT-3.5 20%, Gemini 17%, GPT-4 3%, Llama-2/Claude-1/Claude-2 0%).

Transferability — reusing prompts found against one target on a different target — is strong for a purely semantic attack: jailbreaks discovered against GPT-4 transfer to Vicuna at 71%, GPT-3.5 at 65%, and Gemini at 44%; jailbreaks found against Vicuna transfer to GPT-3.5 at 52% and GPT-4 at 27%. The paper attributes this to PAIR's prompts being "semantic," so they "target similar vulnerabilities across LLMs... trained on similar datasets," unlike GCG's suffixes, which are comparatively brittle across models. Efficiency numbers are concrete: averaged across JBB-Behaviors with Mixtral attacking Vicuna, PAIR finds a successful jailbreak in 34 seconds of wall-clock time using 366 MB of CPU memory at a cost of $0.026 — all via API calls, no GPU required — versus GCG's reported 1.8 hours and 72 GB of GPU memory. (One inconsistency worth flagging: the paper's "Efficiency" bullet in the introduction states this result for "Vicuna-17B," which does not match any Vicuna release size used elsewhere in the paper — Table 4's efficiency analysis and every other table use Vicuna-13B, so this reads as a typo in the original text rather than a different configuration.)

Ablations (role-playing vs. logical appeal vs. authority endorsement as the attacker's persuasion strategy) show role-playing dominates: it alone accounts for 82 of Vicuna's 88 successful jailbreaks, with authority endorsement typically the least effective of the three. Swapping the attacker model matters too — Mixtral-8x7B as attacker (88% JB%, 10.0 queries/success) beats Vicuna-13B as attacker (78%, 20.0 queries) and, "somewhat surprisingly," beats GPT-3.5 as attacker (69%, 28.6 queries), which the authors attribute to GPT-3.5's own safety alignment making it a reluctant attacker.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

The comparison to GCG is honestly framed but structurally lopsided: GCG needs white-box access and hundreds of thousands of queries, so of course a black-box method that needs dozens of queries looks dramatically more efficient — that's the point of the paper, not a flaw, but it does mean the "250-fold improvement" headline is comparing two methods built for different threat models rather than a true apples-to-apples ablation. The paper is transparent about this asymmetry (GCG can only be evaluated on the two open-source targets), which is the right way to handle it, but readers should treat the efficiency comparison as "black-box vs. white-box tradeoff," not "PAIR is a strictly better GCG."

The bigger soft spot is the JUDGE function itself. The paper's own reliability study (Table 1) shows even the best classifier, GPT-4, agrees with human-majority labels only 88% of the time against a 95% human-human agreement baseline — and the JUDGE actually deployed for every headline number, Llama Guard, agrees only 76% of the time, with a 47% false-negative rate. That means the reported 88% JB% on Vicuna, for instance, is a rate as measured by an automatic classifier with a non-trivial disagreement rate against experts, not a directly human-verified success rate; the true human-judged number could plausibly sit several points in either direction. There's also a small internal inconsistency worth flagging: the paper justifies picking Llama Guard because it "exhibits the lowest FPR" among the six candidates, but Table 1's own numbers show BERT actually has a lower false-positive rate (4% vs. Llama Guard's 7%, tied with GPT-4-Turbo) — Llama Guard is the best FPR among the classifiers with usable agreement, not the literal lowest FPR of the six, since BERT's FNR of 74% makes it useless despite its low FPR. The reasoning holds up once you account for that, but the stated justification is imprecise relative to the paper's own table. Separately, the authors chose Llama Guard specifically to minimize false positives (inflated JB%) at the cost of missing real jailbreaks (FNR 47%), which is a defensible conservative choice, but it means the reported numbers are more likely to understate PAIR's true jailbreak rate than overstate it — worth knowing when comparing across papers that use different JUDGE functions, since JB% is not a stable, judge-independent quantity.

The headline JB% numbers are also undefended-model numbers, and the paper's own Table 5 shows they don't hold up well under even mild defenses: a SmoothLLM defense drops PAIR's Vicuna JB% from 88% to 39% (a 56% relative drop) and its GPT-3.5 JB% from 51% to 10% (an 88% relative drop); a simple perplexity filter is gentler but still cuts GPT-3.5's rate from 51% to 17%. PAIR does degrade less than GCG under the same defenses (GCG's Vicuna rate collapses from 56% to 5% under SmoothLLM), which is a genuine and well-evidenced claim — but it means "PAIR jailbreaks GPT-4 48% of the time" is really "PAIR jailbreaks an undefended GPT-4 48% of the time," a materially different claim than what a production deployment with input filtering would actually see. The authors' own Section 5 "Limitations" is candid about a further gap: PAIR "struggles against strongly fine-tuned models including Llama-2 and Claude-1/2" (4%, 3%, and 0% respectively) — exactly the models with the heaviest safety post-training — which somewhat undercuts the generality of the "competitive jailbreaking success rates... on open and closed-source LLMs" framing in the abstract; PAIR's strong numbers are concentrated on the more permissive targets (Vicuna, Gemini-Pro, GPT-3.5/4), not uniformly across the field.

baselines

GCG and a human-template baseline (JBC) are both included and are reasonable choices, but the GCG comparison spans different threat models (white-box vs. black-box) rather than a controlled ablation — flagged transparently by the authors, not hidden.

contamination

Not a benchmark-leakage concern in the usual sense, but a construct-validity one: JB% depends entirely on the JUDGE function's accuracy, and the paper's own study shows even the chosen JUDGE (Llama Guard) disagrees with human experts roughly a quarter of the time.

what's missed

All headline numbers are undefended-target numbers; Table 5's own defended-performance results (SmoothLLM, perplexity filtering) show PAIR's success rate drops sharply once basic input-side defenses are in place, and this context doesn't make it into the abstract.

would raise confidence

A human-adjudicated re-scoring of a sample of PAIR's "successful" jailbreaks (rather than trusting Llama Guard's 76%-agreement judgment), and headline numbers reported against a standard defended baseline alongside the undefended one, would make the reported success rates comparable across papers and deployment-relevant.

// bottom line

PAIR's core contribution — an automated, interpretable, black-box jailbreak that needs dozens rather than hundreds of thousands of queries — is real and the paper documents it carefully, including its own limitations (JUDGE unreliability implicitly via Table 1, defended-performance collapse via Table 5, and resistance from heavily safety-tuned models via its own Limitations section). The main thing a reader should not do is take the paper's own headline framing — "a jailbreak percentage of 50% for GPT-3.5/4, 88% for Vicuna-13B, and 73% for Gemini-Pro" — at face value without the caveat that these are undefended-model, judge-scored rates that the paper's own later tables show move substantially under either a stronger JUDGE, a real defense, or a more heavily safety-tuned target.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. PAIR automates prompt-level jailbreaking by pitting an attacker LLM against a target LLM in an iterative refinement loop — no gradients, no white-box access, and typically fewer than twenty queries to succeed, versus GCG's reported 256,000.
  2. Headline jailbreak rates (88% Vicuna-13B, 73% Gemini-Pro, 51%/48% GPT-3.5/4, single digits or 0% on Llama-2 and Claude) are all measured against undefended targets using an automatic JUDGE (Llama Guard) that itself only agrees with human experts about 76% of the time — treat exact percentages as approximate, directionally trustworthy signals, not precise ground truth.
  3. PAIR's jailbreaks are semantically meaningful and transfer well across models (up to 71% from GPT-4-discovered prompts reused on Vicuna) — the interpretability that token-level attacks like GCG lack is a genuine, load-bearing advantage, not just a side benefit.
  4. Simple defenses meaningfully blunt PAIR (SmoothLLM cuts its Vicuna success rate from 88% to 39%), and it struggles against heavily safety-tuned models like Llama-2 and Claude out of the box — PAIR demonstrates a real vulnerability class, but "jailbreaks GPT-4 48% of the time" is a claim about an undefended, moderately-aligned target, not a ceiling on what defenses can achieve.
