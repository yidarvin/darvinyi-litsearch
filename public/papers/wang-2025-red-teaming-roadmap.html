A Red Teaming Roadmap Towards System-Level Safety

A position paper from Scale AI's SEAL team. Its claim: the flood of LLM red-teaming research is, in aggregate, optimizing the wrong objective. It should stop chasing abstract ethical violations on bare models and instead test concrete product safety specifications under realistic threat models — then graduate from model-level robustness to system-level safety, where monitoring, rapid response, and banning malicious users do most of the work.

Zifan Wang, Christina Q. Knight, Jeremy Kritz, Willow E. Primack, Julian Michael · Scale AI (SEAL)

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

Safeguard red teaming — systematic, strategic attempts to make a model violate its developer's policy — has been a genuine success story. Automated attacks and human red teamers have repeatedly broken state-of-the-art refusal-trained LLMs. But the authors, who run red teaming at Scale AI, argue that the sheer volume of red-teaming submissions to NeurIPS, ICLR, and ICML does not, in aggregate, prioritize the right research problems. The field keeps inventing new abstract risk categories, new datasets, and new jailbreak algorithms while the questions that matter for real deployments go unanswered.

They name three specific misallocations:

  • Abstract principles over product specs. "Harmful" has no one-size-fits-all definition. A model helping novelists brainstorm should allow content a children's math tutor must refuse; an image of two people kissing is benign or malicious depending entirely on context. Frontier models already ship with materially different content policies, so red teaming against generic ethical frameworks measures something no product actually promises.
  • Unrealistic threat models. Borrowed from cybersecurity, a threat model specifies the attacker's access, resources, and goals. Much red-teaming work assumes setups that don't match real attackers — unlimited black-box queries against a model that, in deployment, would patch itself and ban the account long before.
  • Model-level robustness as the finish line. A safe model becomes dangerous once wired to tools, plug-ins, payment APIs, or even other safe AIs. A survey of 200+ safety evaluations found most remain model-centric and ignore deployment context, leaving entire risk categories unmeasured.

The stakes: AI capability is racing toward general — even superhuman — intelligence, yet total investment in safety remains minuscule next to capability research. Red teaming is a high-leverage slice of that safety budget, so it should aim at the feedback signals that most cheaply improve real safeguards.

// how it works

The argument: three nested layers of safety

This is a position paper, not an experiment — its "method" is a framework and a set of prescriptions. The load-bearing move is a vocabulary that separates three things people usually conflate:

  • A model is a neural network trained to perform some behavior.
  • A product is an application deployed via API and/or UI to realize a use case — a model plus its integrated tools, interface, and retrieval pipeline. Sometimes a product is nearly indistinguishable from its model (a video model called directly via API); often, as with a financial-analysis agent built on a reasoning model, they are very different.
  • A system is a product situated in its deployment context: monitoring infrastructure, engineering staff, users, and other environmental factors.

Safety considerations should flow inward-to-outward: a product safety specification derives from the model-level spec while accounting for integrated tools, the deployment context, target users, and local regulation. Red teaming, the authors argue, should be defined against that product spec — with a "specific, actionable goal, a clearly defined task and evaluation metric," so a researcher knows what counts as a breach and how to measure it. They note the field's own maturation here: from keyword/regex matching toward rubric-based harm classification, where success is rarely binary.

Pillar 2 — make the threat model realistic

Conventional adversarial ML works under crisp threat models. The canonical one is ℓp-bounded robustness: an attacker may add any perturbation δ to an input so long as it stays inside an ε-ball.

‖δ‖p ≤ ε // the classic adversarial-ML threat model. The authors' point: even this toy setup is still unsolved a decade on — a large benign-vs-adversarial accuracy gap persists on CIFAR-10 — and it isn't even realistic (real attackers aren't bounded by an ε-ball, and consumers usually have only black-box access). LLM threat models are far messier.

For LLMs the right questions multiply: Are users adversarial? What behavior do they want to induce, and what's their incentive? How many of them are there, with what compute? Do they know which model they're hitting? White-box, black-box, or in between? Can they query repeatedly? Two concrete corrections follow:

  • Realistic black-box attacks assume limited query access. A deployer can patch the model and ban users, so attacks relying on unlimited, undisguised queries don't reflect real deployments.
  • Realistic white-box attacks assume fine-tuning. An adversary with full weights can fine-tune for a few iterations — "generally sufficient to break model safeguards." So white-box robustness without considering fine-tuning is studying the wrong adversary.
// what they found

The prescriptions

Being a position paper, the "results" are recommendations, organized into product-specific threat models, good-practice guidance, and a system-level agenda.

Four products, four threat models

The paper works through four common AI product types to show that "realistic threat model" means something different in each. The throughline for chatbots: multi-turn attacks should be prioritized over single-turn, because models trained to resist single-turn jailbreaks "remain highly vulnerable to multi-turn jailbreaks," single-turn attacks are now easily patched by input/output filters, and conversational affordances like memory open attack surfaces invisible in the single-turn view.

Autonomous agentsVast input/output space, tool & MCP vulnerabilities, multi-agent miscoordinationInvest in sandboxes & real environments; cover cross-modal, compounding harms

Good practices for product red teaming

  • Test safeguards both in-stack and in isolation — one strong safeguard can mask a weak one; if model defenses and an external classifier catch (or miss) the same harms, the stack adds nothing.
  • Emulate reality — simulate the actual deployment (workspace, API environment, web interface); sandbox internet-facing products, and report the gap when the sandbox can't match the real internet's complexity.
  • Cover a variety of attacks — ensemble algorithm-based attacks, human red teamers, and LLM red teamers; the authors note LLM red teamers are the scalable option for academics, since most cost sits in API calls.
  • Assess the delta — acknowledge the distance between the red-team environment and the real threat space (attacker skill/motivation, environment fidelity, and the model possibly "playing along" when it infers it's in an exercise).
  • Don't over-trust scores. One brilliantly crafted jailbreak reported alone is a 100% failure rate; the same jailbreak plus 99 failed attempts is a 1% rate — identical vulnerability, different campaign. A single bioweapon-instruction leak can matter more than a hundred minor policy violations.

System-level safety is the next step

The paper's most forward-looking section argues safeguard research should stop trying to prevent every individual harmful response and instead build (and red team) systems that wrap the product in monitoring and control. Why: many realistic harms require the model's cooperation over many turns, and most harmful requests don't cause immediate catastrophe — which means the system level offers cheaper, more effective mitigations than perfect per-response refusal:

  • Environment modeling & simulation — an agent action like "save this password to autofill" is safe on a personal computer, unsafe on a public one; assessing harm requires tracking world state, so red teaming needs realistic and simulated environments.
  • Trajectory monitoring — classify harm over entire output/action trajectories, not single responses, since harm can accrue across a long interaction.
  • User monitoring — "if a small proportion of users represent a large portion of harmful requests, detecting and banning these users can mitigate a great deal of total harm." Invest in malicious-user detection, Sybil detection, and robust identity proofs.
  • Rapid response — closed- (and some open-) weight products aren't static; newly discovered jailbreaks can be patched with lightweight safeguards before they're exploited at scale, as long as asynchronous monitoring catches failures.
  • Red teaming the monitor — the red team should run sabotage experiments: complete an adversarial task without tripping the monitor, mapping the boundary of its effectiveness (e.g., the limits of monitoring only chain-of-thought at each step).
// does it hold up?

The eval read

This is a manifesto, not a measurement, so the right lens is the strength of the argument rather than the fairness of a baseline table. By that standard it's persuasive and well-sourced — its empirical claims (single-turn defenses don't generalize to multi-turn; fine-tuning breaks white-box safeguards in a few steps) are pinned to cited work rather than abstract assertion. But a position paper earns the right to redirect a field only if its prescriptions are actionable, and that's where the gaps show.

The central instruction — "red team against the product safety specification" — assumes such specs exist and are accessible. Frontier labs rarely publish detailed product specs, and the paper concedes academics may have to "define or infer a plausible policy context," which quietly reintroduces the very normative judgment calls it faults abstract-principle red teaming for. The system-level turn is the most exciting part and the least operationalized: "trajectory-level harm" is named but never given a metric, and user monitoring + banning is presented as a clean win without grappling with its false-positive cost, privacy implications, or the adversarial dynamics of Sybil/identity systems it leans on. The paper's own §6 ("Alternative Views") honestly flags the biggest tension: a product-spec frame, by design, undercounts diffuse societal harms (bias, misinformation at scale) that no single product spec captures — exactly the "abstract" risks it deprioritizes.

evidence base

No new experiments — it's argument by synthesis over 100+ cited works. Claims are well-attributed, but readers can't independently check, e.g., how much product-spec red teaming would surface that model-level red teaming misses.

actionability

"Test against the product spec" presumes specs academics rarely have; "infer a plausible policy context" smuggles back the normative judgment it criticizes. System-level proposals lack concrete metrics or protocols.

what's missed

No worked end-to-end example; no metric for trajectory-level harm; user-monitoring/banning ignores false positives, privacy, and Sybil-detection arms races; little on who bears the cost of building realistic sandboxes.

would raise confidence

One fully worked product (spec → threat model → red-team campaign → system-level mitigation), plus a small demonstration that product-spec, system-level red teaming catches vulnerabilities a model-only campaign provably misses.

// bottom line

A clear-eyed re-orientation of red-teaming priorities from people who do it at scale: stop fetishizing model-level robustness against abstract harms, and start testing products and systems against realistic adversaries. The diagnosis is sharp and likely right. The prescription is still a roadmap — it names the hard problems (defining specs, metricizing diffuse and trajectory-level harm, the cost of monitoring) far more crisply than it solves them.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. Red team the product, not the bare model. "Harmful" is contextual; frontier models already ship different content policies. Define red-teaming objectives against a concrete product safety specification — with an actual task and metric — not against abstract ethical principles.
  2. Match the threat model to real attackers. For chatbots, prioritize multi-turn over single-turn; assume black-box attackers face limited query budgets (deployers patch and ban) and that white-box attackers can fine-tune. And don't over-trust attack-success scores — one crafted jailbreak is a 100% rate or a 1% rate depending only on how many failures you also report.
  3. Move to system-level safety. Most real harm accrues over trajectories and many requests, so monitoring, rapid response, and detecting/banning malicious users can mitigate the bulk of it even when model safeguards fail — which makes red teaming the monitor (sabotage experiments), not just the model, the next frontier.
