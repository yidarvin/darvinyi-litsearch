Experimental Evidence on the Productivity Effects of Generative Artificial Intelligence

A preregistered randomized trial hands ChatGPT to half of 444 college-educated professionals doing real occupation-specific writing tasks. Time taken falls by roughly a third, grades rise, and — unusually for an automation technology — the biggest gains go to the initially weakest writers.

Shakked Noy, Whitney Zhang (MIT)

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

By early 2023, ChatGPT had gone from research curiosity to a tool tens of millions of people had tried, and it was already reshaping expectations about knowledge work. But almost everything anyone knew about its effect on productivity was anecdotal — screenshots of impressive outputs, or surveys asking people whether they felt more productive. Nobody had causal, task-level evidence that access to a generative-AI chatbot actually changes how much real work gets done, by whom, and at what quality, in a setting designed to resemble an actual job.

That absence mattered because the stakes of the answer are unusually large. Prior waves of computer automation — spreadsheets, industrial robots, routine-task software — mostly displaced predictable, codifiable tasks and hit middle-skill work hardest (Autor and Dorn's polarization story, Acemoglu and Restrepo's robots-and-jobs evidence). Writing, by contrast, is exactly the kind of creative, difficult-to-codify task that earlier automation waves left alone. If a generative model can now write a competent press release or grant proposal, does it substitute for the professionals who write those documents, complement their skills, or do something in between — and does it help everyone equally, or does it flatten (or widen) the gap between strong and weak performers? Noy and Zhang built a preregistered field-style experiment specifically to answer this with real occupation-specific tasks and paid, incentivized output, rather than a lab puzzle or a self-reported survey.

// how it works

The method

The authors recruited 444 experienced, college-educated professionals — marketers, grant writers, consultants, data analysts, HR professionals, and managers — through an online panel, and asked each to complete two occupation-specific, incentivized writing tasks: press releases, short reports, analysis plans, and "delicate" workplace emails, each a 20–30 minute assignment designed to resemble something the participant might actually be asked to do at work. Most participants reported having done similar tasks before and rated the assignments as realistic.

After the first (baseline) task, participants were randomly assigned: the treatment group was given access to ChatGPT for the second task, the control group was not. Each submission was graded blind by paid evaluators on a 1–7 scale across dimensions including overall quality, writing quality, content quality, and originality; a subset of tasks used a "convex" payment scheme that paid an extra $3 for a grade of 6 or 7, sharpening the incentive to actually produce high-quality work rather than just finish quickly. A supplementary arm let 30% of the treatment group revise or replace their first-task output with ChatGPT's help after the fact, and a subset of respondents were tracked with a two-week follow-up survey to see whether ChatGPT use persisted once the study ended.

Because assignment to treatment is random, any difference in outcomes between the two groups can be interpreted causally — this is the paper's central methodological advantage over the surveys and observational adoption studies that dominated the initial public discussion of ChatGPT's economic impact. The preregistration (task design, primary outcomes, and inequality analysis all specified in advance) further rules out post-hoc cherry-picking of which comparisons to report.

The results

Take-up was high: 92% of treated participants successfully signed up for ChatGPT and 81% used it on the second task, rating it 4.4 out of 5 for usefulness. (Some contamination existed even in the control group — 10–20% self-reported or objectively measured ChatGPT use — meaning the experiment's estimates are, if anything, a lower bound on ChatGPT's true effect, since there was still at least a 60-percentage-point gap in usage between arms.)

The headline productivity result: average time taken on the post-treatment task dropped by 10 minutes, a 37% reduction relative to the control group's 27-minute average (treatment effect −0.83 SDs, 95% CI [−0.63, −1.03], p = 0.000), while average evaluator grades rose by 0.45 SDs (95% CI [0.27, 0.63], p = 0.000) — roughly similar increases across overall, writing-quality, content-quality, and originality sub-scores. The peer-reviewed Science version of the paper, run on a slightly larger final sample of 453 participants, summarizes the same result as "time taken decreased by 40% and output quality rose by 18%."

The paper's second major finding is about who benefits. In the control group, first-task and second-task grades are strongly correlated (slope 0.491, SE 0.053) — professionals who write well once tend to write well again, a persistent skill gap. Under treatment, that slope falls to 0.248 (SE 0.065), a change of −0.243 (95% CI [−0.08, −0.41]): ChatGPT compresses the productivity distribution by lifting initially low-scoring writers more than high-scoring ones. High-ability workers mostly convert ChatGPT access into speed while holding quality roughly constant, whereas lower-scoring workers gain more on quality. Notably, when the paper tested for heterogeneity along a different axis — participants' self-rated writing skill relative to their other skills — it found no clear pattern (Figure 3b): the compression effect tracks initial task performance, not self-assessed relative writing ability.

ChatGPT also restructured how the work got done. Pre-treatment, participants split their time roughly 25% brainstorming / 50% rough-drafting / 25% editing; post-treatment, the share of time spent rough-drafting fell by more than half while the share spent editing more than doubled — consistent with ChatGPT substituting for the drafting stage rather than simply speeding up every stage proportionally. Strikingly, the paper finds no evidence that human editing improved on raw ChatGPT output: respondents who edited or replaced their first-task response with ChatGPT's output did not receive higher grades than the unedited ChatGPT text graded on its own — even in the convex-incentive group, where participants had a strong monetary reason to improve on it.

Subjective outcomes moved too: job satisfaction rose 0.5 SDs (95% CI [0.32, 0.68]) and self-efficacy rose a smaller, less precisely estimated 0.2 SDs (95% CI [−0.02, 0.42]). Concern and excitement about automation both rose simultaneously — treated participants were more worried about being replaced (diff 0.264 SDs, p = 0.006) and more excited about their own capabilities being enhanced (diff 0.389 SDs, p = 0.000), with overall optimism up 0.2 SDs (p = 0.037). In the two-week follow-up (82% response rate among 423 invited), 33% of former treatment-group participants reported using ChatGPT in their actual job in the past week, versus 18% of control — and restricting to people who had never used ChatGPT before the study, 26% of treated versus 9% of control were now using it on the job. The Science abstract reports this persistence as treated workers being "2 times as likely" to use ChatGPT at 2 weeks and "1.6 times as likely" at 2 months.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

As an identification strategy, this is about as clean as economics gets: real random assignment, a preregistered design, blind grading, and an incentive scheme engineered to reward genuine quality rather than just checking a box. The causal claim — that access to ChatGPT, not just self-selected use of it, raises measured output on these tasks — is well supported. The interesting questions are less about internal validity and more about how far the result travels.

The tasks are short (20–30 minutes), self-contained, and stripped of the messy context — client history, internal politics, proprietary data, iterative back-and-forth with a manager — that real professional writing usually involves. The authors say as much themselves in their own limitations discussion, flagging that this may inflate the measured usefulness of ChatGPT relative to a genuine job setting. It is also a single-exposure, single-session design: participants are trying a still-novel tool for the first time under experimenter observation, which is exactly the setting where a novelty/demand-effect boost is hardest to rule out. The two-week and two-month follow-up usage numbers (33%/18% and 2×/1.6× persistence) are reassuring evidence that people keep choosing to use it, but voluntary continued use is a different claim from continued 0.83-SD-sized time savings — the study doesn't re-measure the size of the effect at those follow-ups, only whether people logged back in.

There's also a task-selection question baked into the design: writing press releases, reports, and emails is precisely the genre where 2022-era ChatGPT was strongest, and the six occupations sampled are all fundamentally writing-centric knowledge jobs. The result says little about quantitative, highly technical, or physically-embedded work. And the "no clear heterogeneity by self-rated writing skill" finding (Figure 3b) is a useful check against over-reading the inequality result — the compression shows up against initial task performance, which conflates skill with effort, task familiarity, and plain variance in a single graded sample, not against an independent skill measure.

Perhaps the most consequential and under-discussed finding is the null result on human editing: participants who revised or replaced their draft with ChatGPT's output scored no better than ungraded raw ChatGPT text, even under strong monetary incentives to improve it. Read generously, this says ChatGPT output was already near the ceiling the task rubric could detect; read more critically, it's a warning sign that professionals may not add — or may not bother to add — real editorial value on top of AI drafts, which has downstream quality-control implications the study wasn't designed to test (e.g., factual errors or client-specific inappropriateness that a 1–7 rubric graded by a paid evaluator wouldn't catch).

baselines

Randomized control arm rather than a fixed benchmark — appropriate for a causal claim, but the control group's own ~10–20% ChatGPT usage introduces mild contamination that biases the estimated effect downward (a lower bound, per the authors), not upward.

contamination

Not a data-leakage concern (this is a human-subjects RCT, not a model eval) — the analogous risk is spillover/contamination of the control arm, which the authors document and treat as a conservative bias.

what's missed

Durability of the effect size beyond a single session; performance on non-writing, technical, or highly contextual tasks; and whether "no gain from human editing" reflects ChatGPT quality or evaluator/rubric insensitivity to the errors editing would normally catch.

would raise confidence

A field experiment embedded in an actual employer (as Brynjolfsson, Li & Raymond did a month later for customer-support agents), repeated measurement of the same workers over months rather than a two-task, two-week design, and task variants outside writing-centric occupations.

// bottom line

A well-identified, preregistered RCT that gives real causal weight to a claim that was, before it, mostly anecdotal: ChatGPT access measurably speeds up and improves realistic professional writing tasks, and does so more for weaker performers than stronger ones. The tradeoff for that clean identification is a short, one-shot, writing-only lab-style task battery drawn from an online panel rather than an embedded workplace — treat the magnitudes as an upper-bound-ish estimate of a real but probably smaller and more context-dependent effect on the job.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. One of the first randomized, preregistered causal estimates of generative AI's effect on white-collar work: giving 444 professionals ChatGPT access for real writing tasks cut time taken by ~0.83 SDs (37%, ~10 minutes off a 27-minute baseline) and raised grades by ~0.45 SDs; the peer-reviewed Science version (N = 453) reports the same story as "40% faster, 18% higher quality."
  2. The gains are concentrated among the initially weaker writers — the slope linking a worker's first- and second-task grades falls from 0.491 to 0.248 under treatment — compressing performance inequality rather than widening it, the opposite pattern from most historical automation, which hit middle-skill workers hardest.
  3. ChatGPT behaves more like a substitute for the drafting stage than a complement to the writer: rough-drafting time collapses, editing time doubles, and editing ChatGPT's output added no measurable quality even under strong pay incentives to do so — while treated workers were still roughly twice as likely as control to be using ChatGPT on their actual job two weeks later.
