SuperGLUE: A Stickier Benchmark for General-Purpose Language Understanding Systems

A year after GLUE launched, pretrained models had blown past its human baseline. SuperGLUE rebuilt the leaderboard with eight harder tasks and a verified human ceiling — then watched a strong BERT baseline trail humans by ~18 points, only for T5 to close almost all of it within a year. It's the benchmark that taught the field that static NLU leaderboards don't survive scaling.

// the gap

A benchmark that ran out of headroom

The GLUE benchmark had been the field's progress dashboard for transfer learning since 2018: nine English tasks recycled from public datasets, collapsed into one macro-averaged leaderboard number, with privately-held test labels and a hand-built linguistic diagnostic. It was deliberately designed so that strong scores required sharing knowledge across tasks rather than fitting each one — and for about a year it worked beautifully as a thermometer for the pretraining revolution.

Then the thermometer hit its ceiling. On GLUE, the strongest pre-pretraining baseline scored 63.7; an ELMo-based model reached 66.5, OpenAI GPT 72.8, BERT 80.2 — and by early July 2019 the state of the art (XLNet, Yang et al.) reached a GLUE score of 88.4, surpassing the non-expert human estimate of 87.1 by 1.3 points and exceeding it on four of the nine tasks. Once machines pass the human line, the single-number metric stops discriminating between models: the remaining gap is too small to track progress, even though the benchmark's high-level goal — general-purpose language understanding — plainly wasn't met.

The proof that it wasn't met lived in GLUE's own diagnostic suite. On the broad-coverage entailment diagnostic, the best models scored just 0.42 R3 against an average human 0.80 R3, performing near or below chance on phenomena like disjunction, downward monotonicity, and restrictivity. Models had become superb statistical summarizers of text while still missing details crucial to semantics. The authors' conclusion was blunt: GLUE was no longer a suitable metric for quantifying progress, so they would build a harder one.

// how it works

Same template, harder tasks, a real human ceiling

SuperGLUE keeps GLUE's high-level design — a public leaderboard built around a fixed set of English tasks drawn from existing data, summarized by a single macro-average and an evaluation server with held-out test labels — but improves it along five axes: harder tasks (it retains the two hardest GLUE tasks, RTE and the Winograd Schema Challenge, and selects the rest for difficulty); more diverse formats (beyond GLUE's sentence- and sentence-pair classification, it adds question answering, coreference resolution, and word-sense disambiguation); comprehensive human baselines for every task; an upgraded analysis toolkit (the jiant library); and refined usage rules — a cap of two submissions per day and six per month to limit test-set overfitting, plus mandatory citation of source datasets.

Choosing eight tasks

The tasks came from an open call for proposals to the NLP community (~30 submissions), filtered by explicit criteria: each must be solvable by college-educated English speakers without domain expertise; have an automatic metric that tracks human judgment (which excludes most generation tasks, where BLEU/ROUGE correlate poorly with quality); use public training data; have a simple input/output format so the benchmark doesn't reward elaborate task-specific architectures; and carry a permissive license. For each surviving candidate the authors ran both a BERT baseline and a human baseline, and kept only tasks that were hard for BERT yet comfortable for humans — operationalizing "headroom" as the criterion for inclusion.

The result is eight tasks, several deliberately small (nearly half have fewer than 1k training examples; all but one have fewer than 10k):

  • BoolQ — yes/no QA over a Wikipedia passage, from naturally-occurring Google search queries.
  • CB (CommitmentBank) — 3-way textual entailment on how committed a writer is to an embedded clause; only ~250 training examples.
  • COPA — causal reasoning: pick the more plausible cause or effect of a premise.
  • MultiRC — multi-sentence reading comprehension where each question can have several correct answers, scored independently.
  • ReCoRD — cloze-style commonsense QA over CNN/Daily Mail news, predicting a masked entity.
  • RTE — textual entailment merged from the PASCAL RTE competitions (carried over from GLUE).
  • WiC — word-sense disambiguation cast as binary classification: same sense of a polysemous word across two sentences?
  • WSC — the Winograd Schema Challenge as coreference, recast here as binary classification. Crucially, the authors remove the adversarial train/dev split that made GLUE's WNLI nearly impossible (premises reappearing with flipped labels).

One number, plus two diagnostics

As in GLUE, the headline score weights every task equally; for a task with multiple metrics (CB's accuracy/F1, MultiRC's F1a/EM, ReCoRD's F1/EM) the metrics are first averaged into a single task score, then the eight task scores are averaged.

SuperGLUE = 18 Σt=1..8 st,   st = mean(metrics of task t) // equal weight per task, regardless of test-set size; metrics averaged within a task first

Two diagnostics ride alongside the leaderboard but are excluded from the headline average: AXb, the broad-coverage linguistic diagnostic carried from GLUE (scored by Matthews correlation), and AXg, the Winogender gender-bias diagnostic (scored by accuracy and a gender-parity score). Human performance is estimated by hiring Mechanical Turk crowdworkers who first complete a short training phase, then re-annotate a sample of each test set — the same protocol GLUE used.

// what they found

A strong BERT baseline still trailed humans by ~18 points

The trivial baselines confirm the tasks aren't gameable by priors: predicting the most frequent class scores 47.1 and a bag-of-words GloVe model 44.3 — near chance on several tasks. Fine-tuning BERT-large per task lifts the average by ~25 points to 69.0. BERT++ — BERT with STILTs-style intermediate fine-tuning (MultiNLI before BoolQ/CB/RTE, SWAG before COPA) — adds a further ~2.5 points to 71.5. Against a human estimate of 89.8, that leaves a gap of nearly 20 points (precisely 18.3).

The gap is wildly uneven. It is largest on WSC — a 35.7-point chasm (64.3 vs 100.0), where BERT actually scored worse than the trivial baselines, likely because the dataset is tiny and the baseline used no data augmentation — and on COPA (73.8 vs a perfect 100.0). It is smallest, around 10 points, on BoolQ, CB, RTE, and WiC. On the broad-coverage linguistic diagnostic, BERT++ reaches only 38.0 MCC against a human 77.0. On Winogender, the baselines post near-perfect gender-parity scores — but that's an artifact of predicting near chance, which trivially yields parity, so the parity number is meaningless without accuracy to back it.

The authors expected these gaps to be hard to close: human performance is perfect on WSC and COPA and in the mid-to-high 90s on three more. Their conclusion was that closing them "should require substantive innovations" in sample-efficient, transfer, multi-task, and self-supervised learning.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

SuperGLUE is excellent benchmark engineering — diverse formats, a difficulty-screened task set, verified per-task headroom, and submission caps to slow overfitting. But its entire value proposition was that ~18-point gap, and the gap evaporated almost immediately. SuperGLUE launched in May 2019 with BERT++ at 71.5; within roughly a year T5-11B (Raffel et al., 2020) scored 88.9 — within 0.9 of the 89.8 human baseline — and models soon passed it outright. The "stickier" benchmark came unstuck about as fast as GLUE had. That isn't a fluke of one task; it's the structural flaw: a static suite with a fixed human ceiling measures this year's models, not the task's intrinsic difficulty, so the headroom you certify today is gone by next year's scaling step.

Two deeper issues compound it. First, the "human" number is a non-expert crowd estimate collected on a sample of each test set — a reasonable reference, but a floor-ish one, not an expert ceiling. When later models "surpassed humans," they surpassed this MTurk estimate; the superhuman framing that followed is partly an artifact of a weak human reference, a point Bowman's own group pressed afterward. Second, every task is recycled from public web-prevalent text — BoolQ from Wikipedia + Google queries, ReCoRD from CNN/Daily Mail, RTE from news and Wikipedia — and as pretraining corpora grew to web scale, that source text (and plausibly some examples) entered pretraining unchecked. SuperGLUE predates contamination auditing and reports none, so later leaderboard gains are confounded with leakage.

baselines

Right for 2019: BERT-large, BERT++ (STILTs intermediate fine-tuning), trivial most-frequent/CBoW floors, and a per-task "outside best." But there is no strong non-BERT contender, and the screening tuned difficulty to that baseline — so RoBERTa/T5 made the launch numbers look stale within months.

contamination

No analysis. Tasks reuse Wikipedia, CNN/Daily Mail news, and PASCAL RTE text that is heavily represented on the web; as LM pretraining went web-scale, source-text leakage into pretraining is a real, unaudited confound for any post-2019 submission.

what's missed

One equal-weighted average over tasks ranging from CB's 250 / WSC's 146 test examples to ReCoRD's 10k, with no confidence intervals — small tasks are high-variance and a 1–2 point leaderboard gap on Avg can be noise. And the headline omits the diagnostics, where models stayed far below humans (AXb 38 vs 77), so the average reads "almost solved" while the hard part says "not understood."

would raise confidence

Expert human ceilings with inter-annotator agreement instead of crowd estimates; per-task confidence intervals rather than one average; refreshed/living test sets and contamination audits against pretraining data; and folding the linguistic diagnostic into the headline so the part that stays hard can't be ignored.

// bottom line

SuperGLUE correctly diagnosed that GLUE was dead and built a sharper instrument — but it inherited GLUE's fatal feature: a fixed task suite, a non-expert human ceiling, and one saturating number. Designed to be sticky, it was ~90% closed by T5 within a year and surpassed soon after. Its lasting contribution is negative knowledge: it's the experiment that convinced the field that static NLU leaderboards can't keep pace with scaling, motivating living benchmarks (Dynabench), held-out frontier evals (MMLU, BIG-bench, HELM), and the move to LLM-judge and arena-style evaluation.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. GLUE saturated within ~12 months (SOTA 88.4 > human 87.1 by mid-2019). SuperGLUE answered with eight harder, difficulty-screened tasks spanning QA, coreference, and word-sense disambiguation — plus crowd-sourced human baselines on every task and tighter submission rules.
  2. At launch, a strong BERT++ baseline scored 71.5 against an 89.8 human estimate — a ~18-point gap (35.7 points on WSC alone) the authors expected to be hard to close. T5-11B closed it to within ~1 point inside a year, and models passed the human baseline soon after.
  3. The enduring lesson is methodological, not linguistic: a static suite with a fixed, non-expert human ceiling and a single averaged number cannot track scaling — which is exactly why evaluation moved toward living, contamination-resistant, and judge/arena-based benchmarks. SuperGLUE's own diagnostics (AXb 38 vs 77 MCC) showed "understanding" was never solved even as the headline number filled up.
