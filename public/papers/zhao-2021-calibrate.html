Calibrate Before Use: Improving Few-Shot Performance of Language Models

GPT-3's few-shot accuracy can swing from near-chance to near-state-of-the-art on nothing but the order of the same four training examples. This paper traces that swing to three concrete biases in the model's output distribution — and fixes it with a calibration step that needs zero labeled data.

Tony Z. Zhao, Eric Wallace, Shi Feng, Dan Klein, Sameer Singh
venue ICML 2021 citations 1,963 topic Benchmarks & Evals source ↗

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

Brown et al.'s GPT-3 (2020) had just shown that a large autoregressive LM can perform a task from nothing but a natural-language prompt — a task description plus a handful of labeled examples, no gradient updates. That's an attractive story for practitioners: rapid prototyping, a fully natural-language interface, one model serving every task. But treating the prompt as a fixed, minor hyperparameter turns out to hide a lot: this paper is the first to systematically measure just how unstable in-context learning's accuracy is with respect to the prompt itself, and to show that the instability is predictable rather than random noise.

The prompt has three independent knobs — the training-example selection, their permutation (order), and the format template — and the paper shows each one alone can swing accuracy wildly. On 4-shot SST-2 with GPT-3 2.7B, simply re-ordering the same four training examples moves accuracy from 54.3% (near chance, on a binary task) to 93.4% (near the state of the art at the time) — same examples, same format, only the order changed. Table 2 in the paper gives a stark two-example case: one ordering of two training examples reaches 88.5% test accuracy; reversing those same two examples drops it to 51.3%. Crucially, this variance doesn't wash out with scale: it persists at 16-shot and at GPT-3's largest 175B size (Figure 1 of the paper). Since practitioners typically hand-pick one prompt and report one number, and since prior in-context-learning papers (Radford et al., 2019; Brown et al., 2020) reported single per-task scores without probing this axis, the reported few-shot numbers for a model could just as easily be an unlucky ordering as a lucky one — undermining any claim about what a model "can" or "can't" do in the few-shot regime.

// how it works

The method

The paper first explains why the prompt matters this much: it identifies three biases that push GPT-3 toward predicting certain answers regardless of the actual test input, each a systematic shift in the model's output distribution rather than random noise.

Majority label bias. The model is pulled toward whichever answer appears most often among the training examples in the prompt — even on datasets the authors keep label-balanced. This explains a repeated oddity in the baseline numbers: accuracy often drops going from 0-shot to 1-shot, because adding a single training example just teaches the model to repeat that example's label. On 4-shot LAMA with GPT-3 2.7B, 50.2% of the model's predictions are a verbatim repeat of one of the four training answers, versus a 24.7% base rate at which that repeat would actually be correct.

Recency bias. The model over-weights training examples that sit near the end of the prompt — a direct consequence of left-to-right autoregressive processing. Figure 4 (below) demonstrates this cleanly on 4-shot SST-2: prompts ending in two Negative examples skew the model toward predicting Negative even when the overall label balance is even, and recency can outright overpower majority — a "PPPN" prompt (three Positive training examples, one Negative) still pushes the model to predict Negative on nearly 90% of test inputs, because that lone Negative example sits last. On 4-shot LAMA, the training answer in prompt position 1 through 4 is over-predicted by 8.5%, 8.3%, 14.3%, and 16.1% respectively — a monotonic increase toward the end of the prompt.

Common token bias. Independent of the prompt's contents, GPT-3 favors answer strings that are common in its pretraining corpus. On the balanced 14-way DBPedia topic-classification task, GPT-3 predicts the "book" class 11× more often than "artist," and the frequency of a label's name in Google Ngrams correlates with GPT-3's rate of predicting that class at r = 0.67. For LAMA fact retrieval, this shows up as the model guessing common entities (e.g., "America") over the correct but rarer ground-truth entity.

Since the net effect of all three biases is a shift in the model's output distribution (the paper's Figure 5 shows this as a whole-distribution shift toward high p(Positive) on a sentiment prompt, where the optimal decision threshold turns out to be 0.68, not the default 0.5, and using it would reach 94% validation accuracy on the same prompt), the fix follows a classic calibration recipe. Borrowing the affine reparameterization from Platt scaling (Platt, 1999) and the "vector scaling" variant of Guo et al.'s (2017) neural-network calibration — a diagonal weight matrix to keep the parameter count linear rather than quadratic in an output space of ≈50,000 tokens — they define:

q̂ = softmax(W p̂ + b) // W constrained diagonal ("vector scaling"); p̂ = the model's raw predicted-class probabilities for the current prompt

The obstacle in the zero-/few-shot setting is that there's no labeled validation data to fit W and b. Their fix: feed the model the exact same training examples, permutation, and format, but replace the real test input with a content-free placeholder — the string "N/A", the token "[MASK]", or the empty string — and read off the resulting prediction p̂cf. Since a content-free input carries no signal about the true label, any skew in p̂cf away from uniform is the bias to remove. In the paper's running sentiment example, "N/A" gets scored 61.8% Positive by the 2-shot prompt — that 61.8% (not 50%) is exactly the correction needed. They set W = diag(p̂cf)−1 and b = 0, which forces the content-free input to a uniform prediction, then apply the same W, b to real test inputs. In practice they average p̂cf across all three content-free strings (N/A, [MASK], empty string), which they found strongest empirically. The whole procedure needs no extra training data, adds negligible compute, and is "a few lines of code" (their phrasing) on top of a completed forward pass.

// what they found

The results

They test contextual calibration across three GPT-3 sizes (2.7B, 13B, 175B) plus GPT-2 1.5B, on 13 datasets across three task families: text classification (SST-2, AGNews, TREC, CB, RTE, DBPedia), fact retrieval (LAMA), and information extraction (ATIS airline/departure-date, MIT Movies genre/director). Across nearly every cell of that grid, calibration improves both mean and worst-case accuracy — by up to 30.0 points absolute — and it does so consistently enough that GPT-3 2.7B with calibration sometimes beats the uncalibrated 175B baseline by up to 19.3 points, despite being more than 50× smaller.

Beyond raw accuracy, calibration also reduces the standard deviation of accuracy across different random choices of training examples in a majority of task/shot combinations (their Figure 6), without meaningfully increasing it in the rest. The paper names four cases with a baseline accuracy drop from 0-shot to 1-shot — TREC, AGNews, DBpedia, and SST-2 — and reports that calibration removes the drop in three of the four, consistent with the majority-label-bias explanation. It transfers cleanly to prompt format variation too: across 15 hand-written SST-2 formats and 15 LAMA paraphrase templates, calibration again raises mean and worst-case accuracy and shrinks the spread. And it works "out of the box" on GPT-2 1.5B as well, suggesting the underlying biases are a general property of autoregressive LMs, not a GPT-3 quirk.

Two additional checks support the method's soundness. First, against an "oracle calibration" that is allowed to fit the same diagonal W using labeled validation data, contextual calibration (with zero labels) lands surprisingly close on AGNews — most of the fixable bias is captured without any supervision. Second, an ablation over which content-free string to use (Table 3) shows the choice matters somewhat — on SST-2, the uncalibrated baseline is 66.5%, a single "N/A" probe reaches 74.2%, and their final ensemble of "N/A" + "[MASK]" + empty string reaches 79.0% (66.5% on AGNews, up from 48.5% uncalibrated) — but the method is robust to the exact choice: even nonsense strings like "dasjhasjkdhjskdhds" recover most of the gain (79.3% on SST-2).

// does it hold up?

The eval read

The core empirical claim — few-shot in-context learning is far more prompt-sensitive than the single-number leaderboard entries of the time suggested — is careful, well-quantified, and has aged extremely well; every subsequent order/selection-sensitivity study in the in-context-learning literature builds on exactly this diagnosis. The fix is genuinely elegant: a training-free, single-forward-pass probe that needs no labeled data. But look closely at the paper's own Table 1 and the story is a mitigation, not a cure, which the paper is honest about but which is easy to skim past.

Does contextual calibration fix the instability, or just re-center it? Calibration corrects a systematic, static bias — the model's average tendency to over-favor certain answers for a given prompt configuration — estimated once via a content-free probe. It does not touch the underlying instability mechanism: the model's hidden representation of a given (examples, order, format) triple is untouched, so if two orderings genuinely lead the model to reason differently about the task (not just shift its output distribution), calibration can't recover that. The paper's own numbers show this: at 8-shot, several tasks converge to near-identical baseline and calibrated results (LAMA 175B: 63.8 ± 1.0 vs. 63.6 ± 1.3; MIT-Director 13B: 77.8 ± 0.5 vs. 77.8 ± 0.5) — the fix's marginal value shrinks as more context reduces the bias on its own. And it isn't uniformly a win: CB 2.7B 1-shot mean accuracy is slightly lower after calibration (33.8% → 33.0%), and ATIS-Airline 13B 4-shot variance actually increases (±10.4 → ±13.4). The paper's own Discussion section states this plainly — "contextual calibration does not eliminate the need to engineer prompts, however, it does mitigate it" — but that caveat is easy to miss under a headline "+30.0% absolute" number.

How this connects to modern in-context-learning and LLM-judge eval practice. The paper's framing — that "small but non-trivial decisions such as calibration can greatly influence results," making it "difficult to correctly develop and compare new methods" — reads today as an early, precise diagnosis of the reproducibility problems that later exploded across LLM benchmarking: different eval harnesses (lm-eval-harness, HELM, and others) have repeatedly been shown to report meaningfully different numbers for the same model on the same benchmark because of undocumented prompt-formatting and few-shot-ordering choices. The same underlying failure mode — an evaluator's judgment shifting based on presentation order rather than content — resurfaces in a different guise in koo-2023-cobbler's audit of LLM-as-judge biases: there, "Order" bias (which candidate answer is shown first) is one of six measurable biases baked into pairwise LLM judging, just as recency bias here is baked into few-shot classification. Both papers land on the same throughline — don't trust a single-order, single-run evaluation number — but neither the biases nor the content-free calibration fix here have been re-validated on instruction-tuned, chat-formatted, or RLHF'd models, where the raw next-token label-scoring this method depends on is often not even exposed via the API.

baselines

Clean same-model, same-prompt A/B (baseline vs. calibrated logits on the identical training-set draw) — no unfair baseline tuning. The one asymmetry: the "oracle calibration" upper bound (Figure 8) uses validation labels and is only benchmarked on one dataset (AGNews), so how close contextual calibration gets to a true ceiling elsewhere is unverified.

contamination

Not addressed. SST-2, AGNews, TREC, DBPedia, LAMA, and ATIS are all older, widely-circulated academic datasets that may well appear in GPT-3's pretraining corpus, and the paper runs no contamination check. It matters less for the paper's core before/after comparison (both arms see the same contamination), but it does mean the absolute accuracy numbers shouldn't be read as clean few-shot generalization scores.

what's missed

Only base LMs (GPT-2/GPT-3) with greedy decoding and single- or first-token label scoring — no instruction-tuned, RLHF, or chat-formatted models; no shot counts past 16; no retrieval-based (as opposed to random) example selection; and generation tasks are calibrated on only their first output token (acknowledged in the paper's own footnote as a simplification).

would raise confidence

Re-run the same bias measurements and calibration fix on modern instruction-tuned/chat models and sampling-based decoding; extend calibration past the first generated token; and check whether the three named biases (majority label, recency, common token) still account for the residual variance in the cases where calibration doesn't help (the 8-shot convergence cases above).

// bottom line

A precise, well-evidenced diagnosis of a real and previously under-quantified failure mode — GPT-3's few-shot accuracy swinging 40+ points on nothing but example order — paired with a genuinely cheap, no-label fix. But by the paper's own Table 1, contextual calibration narrows the sensitivity rather than closing it, and its central mechanism (a content-free probe over raw label-token probabilities) has never been shown to survive the move to instruction-tuned, chat-formatted models — read it as the founding diagnosis of prompt-order fragility, not a settled fix for it.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. Few-shot in-context learning's accuracy is dominated by an under-appreciated variable — the prompt's example order, selection, and format — not just which examples you pick: reordering the same four SST-2 examples alone swings GPT-3 2.7B from 54.3% to 93.4% accuracy.
  2. The instability traces to three concrete, measurable biases — majority label, recency, and common token — that all reduce to a predictable shift in the model's output distribution, one a single content-free probe (feeding the prompt "N/A" instead of a real test input) can estimate with zero labeled data.
  3. Contextual calibration reliably raises mean and worst-case accuracy (up to +30.0 points) and often shrinks variance, but by the paper's own Table 1, it narrows rather than eliminates the sensitivity — an early, precise diagnosis of the prompt-order/format fragility that keeps resurfacing, later in a different guise as order bias in LLM-as-judge evaluation.
