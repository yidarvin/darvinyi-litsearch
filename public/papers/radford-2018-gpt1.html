Improving Language Understanding by Generative Pre-Training

The first GPT. One decoder-only Transformer, generatively pre-trained on 7,000 books then lightly fine-tuned with task-aware input formatting, beats a zoo of bespoke task-specific architectures on 9 of 12 language-understanding benchmarks — the recipe GPT-2 and GPT-3 would simply scale.

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

By 2018, "transfer learning" in NLP mostly meant word embeddings — word2vec, GloVe, and, at the leading edge, the contextual vectors of ELMo — bolted onto a model architecture hand-designed for each benchmark. The unlabeled-text corpora the field had in abundance barely helped on the tasks where labeled data was scarce.

Two unsolved questions blocked anything more ambitious. First, which unsupervised objective transfers? Language modeling, machine translation, and discourse-coherence objectives each won on different tasks, with no consensus on which produces representations worth reusing. Second, even given good representations, how do you transfer them? The going answer was intricate and task-specific: every new benchmark got its own architecture, its own auxiliary losses, its own learning schedule — re-introducing exactly the per-task engineering that pre-training was supposed to remove.

GPT-1 asks a sharper question: can a single task-agnostic model, pre-trained once on raw text, transfer to entailment, question answering, semantic similarity, and classification with almost no change to its architecture? If yes, the bottleneck stops being clever task-specific design and starts being how much text — and how much model — you can throw at the language-modeling objective.

// how it works

The method

GPT-1 is two stages. Stage one is ordinary language modeling. Over an unlabeled corpus of tokens, maximize the likelihood of each token given a fixed window of k preceding tokens — a plain left-to-right next-token objective, no labels anywhere:

L1(U) = Σi log P(ui | ui−k, … , ui−1; Θ) // unsupervised pre-training: a standard autoregressive LM over a context window of size k

The corpus is BooksCorpus — over 7,000 unpublished books — chosen deliberately because long stretches of contiguous prose force the model to condition on long-range structure, unlike a sentence-shuffled corpus such as the 1B Word Benchmark, which is about the same size but destroys that long-range structure. The trained language model reaches a low token-level perplexity of 18.4. The network is a 12-layer decoder-only Transformer: masked multi-head self-attention with 768-dimensional states and 12 heads, 3,072-dimensional position-wise feed-forward layers, GELU activations, learned (not sinusoidal) position embeddings, and a byte-pair-encoding vocabulary with 40,000 merges. Trained with Adam (max LR 2.5e-4, cosine schedule) for 100 epochs on 512-token sequences.

Stage two is discriminative fine-tuning, and this is where the design is clever. The pre-trained weights are reused wholesale; the only new parameters are a single linear output head Wy plus embeddings for a few delimiter tokens. What lets one frozen-shape model handle structurally different tasks is task-aware input transformation: instead of building a bespoke architecture per task, GPT-1 serializes every structured input into one ordered token sequence the pre-trained model already knows how to read (a "traversal-style" approach). Entailment becomes premise $ hypothesis; similarity runs both sentence orderings and sums their representations; multiple-choice QA concatenates the document, question, and each candidate answer and scores them through a softmax. The architecture barely changes from task to task — see Figure 1.

One extra wrinkle: during fine-tuning GPT-1 keeps the language-modeling loss as an auxiliary objective, weighted by λ = 0.5, which the authors report improves generalization and speeds convergence — chiefly on the larger datasets:

L3(C) = L2(C) + λ · L1(C) // fine-tuning loss = supervised task objective L₂ plus a weighted auxiliary LM objective L₁ (λ = 0.5)
// what they found

The results

Across 12 datasets in four families, GPT-1 sets a new state of the art on 9, often beating ensembles with a single fine-tuned model.

Natural language inference. MNLI-matched 82.1, MNLI-mismatched 81.4, SNLI 89.9, SciTail 88.3, QNLI 88.1 — improvements of up to +1.5 (MNLI), +5 (SciTail), and +5.8 (QNLI) over the previous best. The lone exception is RTE, one of the smallest sets (2,490 examples), where GPT-1's 56.0 trails a multi-task biLSTM's 61.7.

Question answering & commonsense. Story Cloze 86.5 (+8.9) and RACE 59.0 (+5.7 overall; 62.9 middle-school, 57.4 high-school) — both tasks that reward long-range reasoning, where the contiguous-text pre-training pays off.

Semantic similarity & classification. CoLA (grammatical acceptability) leaps to 45.4 from a prior best of 35.0, evidence of linguistic structure absorbed during pre-training; SST-2 reaches 91.3, STS-B 82.0 (+1 point), and QQP 70.3 F1 (+4.2 over the strongest single-task ELMo baseline). The overall GLUE score is 72.8, up from 68.9.

The analysis is the part that aged best. Transferring more pre-trained layers helps monotonically — each Transformer layer contributes up to ~9% on MultiNLI for full transfer — so the representation is useful all the way up, not just at the embeddings. And when you track zero-shot heuristics (using the generative model to answer tasks with no fine-tuning at all) over the course of pre-training, performance rises steadily and smoothly (Figure 2, right): the language model is teaching itself task-relevant behavior simply by getting better at predicting text. The Transformer's zero-shot curves are also far less noisy than an LSTM's — the architecture itself aids transfer.

Ablations (Table below) confirm the recipe rather than any one trick. Removing pre-training entirely is catastrophic — a 14.8% average drop (74.7 → 59.9). Swapping the Transformer for a single-layer LSTM costs 5.6 points on average. The auxiliary LM objective is the most equivocal piece: it helps the larger NLI datasets and QQP but, averaged over this benchmark mix, the model is marginally better without it (75.0 vs 74.7).

// does it hold up?

The eval read

The result is real and the recipe is the right one — but the "9 of 12 SOTA" headline does a lot of compression, and a careful read of the tables tells a more textured story than the count.

The comparisons are method-vs-bespoke-architecture: GPT-1's baselines (ESIM+ELMo, CAFE, GenSen, the multi-task biLSTM+ELMo+Attn) are 2017–2018 task-specific models, some of them ensembles (ESIM+ELMo and CAFE at 5×, the RACE models at 9×; GenSen and the multi-task biLSTM are single models) pitted against a single fine-tuned GPT. That makes a handful of cells not apples-to-apples — and it cuts both ways: the win that matters is that one architecture beats many specialized ones, not the raw margins. Some of those margins are thin (SNLI +0.6, MNLI +1.5) and plausibly inside ensemble/seed noise, while on RTE, one of the smallest sets, GPT-1 actually loses (56.0 vs 61.7) — small-data fine-tuning is fragile, single runs are reported, and that fragility is acknowledged but not probed. Notably ELMo, the strongest contemporary pre-training competitor, only appears folded inside baselines; there is no clean ELMo-style pre-train-then-fine-tune head-to-head.

There is also no contamination analysis — standard for 2018, but worth flagging here specifically: the model is pre-trained on a corpus of fiction (BooksCorpus), and one of its largest gains (+8.9) is on Story Cloze, a fiction-based commonsense task. No overlap between pre-training books and the fiction benchmarks is measured. Finally, the auxiliary LM objective is shipped on by default despite an ablation that shows it slightly hurting the benchmark average — a defensible choice for larger datasets, but the blanket inclusion is under-justified.

baselines

Task-specific 2017–2018 architectures, some of them ensembles (5×/9×), vs a single fine-tuned model; fair for the era, but a few cells aren't apples-to-apples and the real win is one model beating many.

contamination

None measured. Fiction pre-training corpus (BooksCorpus) + fiction-based benchmark (Story Cloze, a +8.9 gain) is a latent leakage concern that goes unchecked.

what's missed

The "9/12" count flattens decisive wins (Story Cloze +8.9, CoLA +10.4) together with marginal ones (+0.6) and one regression (RTE). No variance/seed reporting on tiny sets; no head-to-head against ELMo's own transfer recipe.

would raise confidence

Seed variance on small tasks; a BooksCorpus↔benchmark overlap check; a clean ELMo pre-train+fine-tune comparison; scaling curves past 12 layers (the paper hints scale matters but doesn't push it — GPT-2/3 later did).

// bottom line

GPT-1 is a proof-of-concept that one generatively pre-trained decoder Transformer, plus task-aware input formatting and light fine-tuning, beats a zoo of hand-built architectures across most NLU tasks. The lasting contribution isn't the leaderboard deltas — some are thin and a couple of comparisons are against ensembles — it's the recipe, and the Figure-2 observation that task ability emerges smoothly from the LM objective alone. That observation is the thesis GPT-2 and GPT-3 would cash in by scaling.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. The entire GPT recipe is here: generatively pre-train a decoder-only Transformer on raw text, then transfer with task-aware input formatting and a single linear head — no per-task architecture. GPT-2 and GPT-3 changed almost nothing about this except scale and the removal of fine-tuning.
  2. Read the table, not the count. "9 of 12 SOTA" mixes decisive wins (Story Cloze +8.9, CoLA +10.4) with thin ones (SNLI +0.6) and one outright regression (RTE), and several baselines are ensembles.
  3. The most prophetic result isn't a benchmark number: zero-shot task ability rising smoothly with pre-training (Figure 2) is the first concrete evidence that improving the language-modeling objective alone buys general capability — the bet that defined the next two GPTs.
