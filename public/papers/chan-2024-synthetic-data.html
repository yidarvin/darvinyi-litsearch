Balancing Cost and Effectiveness of Synthetic Data Generation Strategies for LLMs

When you fine-tune on LLM-generated data, should you re-answer your questions, rephrase them, or evolve brand-new ones? A controlled sweep across math, SQL, and science QA shows the best choice flips with a single number — the ratio of teacher-query budget to seed-instruction count.

Yung-Chieh Chan, George Pu, Apaar Shanker, Parth Suresh, Penn Jenks, John Heyer, Sam Denton · Scale AI

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

Adapting an LLM to a new task runs straight into a data bottleneck. High-quality human data is the reliable lever, but it is, in the authors' words, "prohibitively expensive in many scenarios." The cheap alternative is to let a strong model generate the training data — and an entire zoo of recipes already exists for that: Self-Instruct, Evol-Instruct/WizardLM, MetaMath, OSS-Instruct/Magicoder, and more.

The trouble is that almost every one of those recipes is demonstrated in a single domain (usually math or code) at a single, generous scale. That leaves the practitioner's actual question unanswered: given my limited seed instructions and a fixed budget of queries to a teacher model, which recipe should I run — and does the answer change as my budget grows? Prior work optimizes for peak performance at scale, not cost-effectiveness under constraints, and rarely puts recipes head-to-head across tasks and data regimes.

This paper reframes synthetic-data generation as a resource-allocation problem. It groups the recipes into three representative strategies, sweeps them across three tasks and a grid of data constraints, and produces a practical decision rule for which strategy to spend on, and when.

// how it works

The method

The setting is knowledge distillation via supervised fine-tuning (SFT). You are given only a set of seed instructions Iseed = {qi} of size N, an augmenter model πaug, a teacher model πT, and a student model πS to train. Crucially, no ground-truth responses are assumed. Every generation strategy is a two-step pipeline: (1) transform the seed instructions with πaug, then (2) sample responses for them from πT. The three strategies differ only in step 1:

  • Answer Augmentation — keep the seed questions unchanged; sample diverse chain-of-thought responses from πT at temperature 0.7. Only the answers are new.
  • Question Rephrasing — reword each seed question with πaug (same underlying answer), then sample a fresh response. New phrasings, same problems.
  • New Question Evolution — condition πaug on the seed set to generate genuinely new questions with different final answers (with a self-verification step for answerability and format), then sample responses. New problems entirely.

The cost of each strategy is modeled by the number of queries it makes to πT and πaug: the ratio Answer Augmentation : Question Rephrase : New Question is taken to be 1 : 2 : 2 (the prompt-space methods pay for both an augmenter call and a teacher call). The single knob that organizes the whole study is the budget ratio — queries per seed instruction:

BR = qs // q = teacher/augmenter queries (final dataset size), s = number of seed instructions

A low BR means few queries relative to your seed set; a high BR means you are sampling the same small seed set many times over. The experimental grid: students are Llama 2 7B Chat (plus Mistral 7B in an ablation); the teacher and augmenter are both Llama 3.1 70B Instruct. Three tasks, one dataset each — GSM8k (grade-school math, 7,500 seeds), Spider (text-to-SQL, 7,000 seeds), ARC-C (science QA, ~3,000 seeds). Each is sharded to three seed sizes (100 / 1,000 / full) and crossed with seven query budgets from 1k to 100k, then evaluated on each dataset's ~1,000-example held-out test (exact-match accuracy; execution accuracy for Spider). The student starts far below the teacher, so gains are legible:

To read crossover points cleanly off noisy data, the authors fit an exponential-decay scaling law adapted from data-constrained scaling (Muennighoff et al., 2024). The idea: repeatedly querying the same seed set adds less information each time, so per-query accuracy gains decay.

Acc(S, Q) = E − ASα − BS + S·R*·(1 − e−Q/(S·R*))β // E = ceiling accuracy · A/Sα = gains from more seed data S · third term = gains from more queries Q, with decay

This decomposes accuracy into a ceiling (E), what's improvable by scaling the seed size (A/Sα), and what's improvable by spending queries (the decaying third term). It fits with R² > 0.98 across every task/method combination (optimized by L-BFGS on MSE), using a monotone running-max adjustment âs,q = maxs'≤s, q'≤q as',q' so each curve reflects the best accuracy obtainable up to that budget.

// what they found

The results

There is no single best recipe — the winner is set by the budget ratio. When BR is low (few queries relative to seed size), Answer Augmentation wins: spend on new responses to the prompts you already have. When BR is high, creating new prompts wins, and New Question Evolution generally beats Question Rephrasing in both cost and scalability.

The effect is sharpest with small seed sets. With only 100 seed instructions, the switch from "new answers" to "new questions" happens at a BR between 27 and 51 (roughly 3,000–5,000 samples). As the seed set grows, the crossover BR shrinks — averaging 17.6 at 1,000 seeds and 16.4 at the largest seed size. Figure 3 marks each crossover with a dashed line.

The most dramatic case is GSM8k with just 100 seeds: New Question keeps improving past 50,000 examples — over 500× the seed-set size — while the others plateau. At a 100k budget New Question reaches 53.5% versus Answer Augmentation's 32.1%, a 21-point gap from the same 100 seeds. The flip side: in data-rich regimes the choice barely matters. At 7,500 GSM8k seeds and a 100k budget the three methods land within ~2.4 points (Answer Aug 65.8%, Question Rephrase 67.6%, New Question 68.2%). As the paper puts it, augmentation choice and design details "matter substantially more in low to mid data regimes than in high data regimes."

Ablation: a weaker (cheaper) augmenter

Does the augmenter πaug need to be strong? It depends on the strategy. Question Rephrasing is robust to a weak augmenter (rephrasing is an easy task), but New Question is tightly coupled to augmenter capability. Swapping in Llama 2 7B — which itself scores only ~15% on GSM8k — as the augmenter drops New-Question accuracy by roughly 15 points at 10,000 samples relative to the stronger augmenters tested (Llama 3.1 70B, Llama 3.1 8B, Llama 3 8B). You can cheap out on the augmenter for rephrasing, not for new-question generation.

Ablation: does verifying answers help?

The teacher produces wrong answers for about 10% of instructions, so the synthetic data is noisy. Does filtering responses against ground-truth answers help? No measurable gain — in both the equal-size and filtered-size settings, training on verified responses does not significantly beat simply scaling up the (unfiltered) dataset. The authors offer two explanations: the student still learns from incorrect teacher responses (echoing Yu et al., 2023, where Llama 2 improved even on wrong GPT-3.5 answers), and filtering reduces diversity. (New Question's answers are unverifiable by construction, so verification was tested only on Answer Augmentation and Question Rephrasing, on Spider.)

// does it hold up?

The eval read

This is an unusually honest, decision-oriented study, and the central rule is intuitive and well-supported by a clean nine-setting sweep. But several things soften the precision of its headline numbers.

The comparison is internal: the three recipes are pitted against each other at matched sample counts and a shared teacher/student — fair as a relative ranking, but there is no external yardstick. There's no head-to-head against a real-data SFT run at the same budget (only a small appendix sanity check that synthetic ≈ real at equal size), and no comparison to an established pipeline (Evol-Instruct/WizardMath, Magpie, OSS-Instruct). So we learn the ordering of three in-house variants, not whether any of them is competitive with the field's strongest synthetic recipes. The 1:2:2 cost model is also a coarse proxy: it counts queries but ignores response/token length and the extra generation in New Question's self-verification step — and a token- or dollar-based accounting would shift every crossover line.

The crossover BRs are read off fitted curves, not raw points, and the fit applies a running-max adjustment â that forces monotonicity — explicitly to smooth over the fact that Answer Augmentation and Question Rephrasing accuracy actually drops at high BR from re-sampling the same seeds. That degradation is itself a practically important caveat the smoothed curves hide. And with n=1 run per cell and no error bars, the apparent precision of nearby crossover values (Spider's 8.3× vs GSM8k's 14.4×) is plausibly within noise.

baselines

Internal-only — the three recipes vs each other at matched counts; no real-data-at-budget run and no SOTA synthetic pipeline for comparison. Cost is a coarse 1:2:2 query ratio that ignores tokens and the self-verification overhead.

contamination

GSM8k, Spider, and ARC-C (and their public test splits) are almost certainly in Llama 3.1's pretraining — part of why the teacher hits 94.6% on GSM8k. Distillation transfers that possibly-memorized competence, and "new" evolved questions may echo memorized ones. No decontamination analysis.

what's missed

n=1 per cell, no error bars; crossover BRs come from curves smoothed by a running-max that hides the real high-BR accuracy drop; the whole study is one teacher, ~one student family (plus a single Mistral check), three tasks, one seed corpus each.

would raise confidence

Multiple seeds with error bars; real-data and strong-pipeline baselines at matched budget; a token/$-based cost model; decontamination of seed and test against the teacher's pretraining; and reporting the raw, non-adjusted curves so the high-BR overfitting is visible.

// bottom line

A practical, well-motivated decision rule — low budget-to-seed ratio → make new answers; high ratio → make new questions, and don't overthink the recipe when data is plentiful — backed by a clean sweep and a tight scaling-law fit (R² > 0.98). Trust the direction of the rule; treat the exact BR thresholds as ballpark, given a single teacher/student, n=1 cells, a coarse 1:2:2 cost proxy, and curves smoothed by the very adjustment that hides high-BR degradation.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. One number drives the decision: BR = queries ÷ seed instructions. Low BR → Answer Augmentation (new responses to existing prompts); high BR → New Question Evolution (new prompts). With 100 seeds the switch sits at BR ≈ 27–51; it falls to ≈ 16 as the seed set grows.
  2. New questions scale; old questions saturate — but only when data is scarce. From 100 GSM8k seeds, New Question keeps climbing past 50,000 samples (over 500× the seed size) to 53.5% while Answer Augmentation plateaus at 32.1%. In data-rich regimes all three converge within ~2–3 points, so don't over-engineer the recipe once you already have data.
  3. Spend where it counts, and skip verification. A weak, cheap augmenter is fine for Question Rephrasing but craters New Question (−15 pts with Llama 2 7B). And filtering teacher answers against ground truth gives no measurable gain — ~10% of teacher answers are wrong, yet the student still learns from them.
