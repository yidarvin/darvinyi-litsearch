What Will it Take to Fix Benchmarking in Natural Language Understanding?

A position paper arguing NLU evaluation is broken — top systems saturate every popular leaderboard while still failing trivial probes — and laying out four criteria a trustworthy benchmark must meet. Its sharpest claim: the field's fashionable fix, adversarial data collection, treats the symptom, not the disease.

Samuel R. Bowman, George E. Dahl

What problem is this solving?

By 2021 the standard engine of NLU progress — train on a crowdsourced, roughly IID benchmark, climb a leaderboard — had stalled in a specific and dangerous way. State-of-the-art systems were sitting at or above estimated human performance on essentially every popular benchmark: the GLUE leaderboard had approached or exceeded human scores on all nine of its tasks, and top systems on SQuAD 2.0 had long passed human annotators. There was almost no headroom left for a genuinely better system to show that it was better.

And yet those same chart-topping systems collapse on simple, targeted test cases — McCoy et al.'s HANS, Ribeiro et al.'s CheckList — that probe the very skills the leaderboards claim to measure. So the instrument is broken in both directions: it reports that the problem is nearly solved when it isn't, and it can no longer detect the improvements that remain. A broken metric is worse than no metric. It discourages newcomers and non-specialists from trying to contribute, and it hands specialists wide latitude to cherry-pick evaluation settings that quietly mask a lack of progress.

The community's reaction was to abandon IID benchmarks for adversarially-collected, out-of-distribution test sets — the DynaBench program asks crowdworkers to write examples that fool a reference model. That guarantees current models will score poorly, but Bowman and Dahl argue it obscures rather than measures the abilities we actually care about. Their paper steps back to the prior question nobody had pinned down: what, concretely, would a benchmark have to satisfy to deserve our trust?

// how it works

Four criteria, and a case against adversarial filtering

This is a position paper, not an experiment. Its contribution is a definition: a clean separation of task from benchmark, four criteria a good benchmark must satisfy, and an argument that the adversarial-filtering trend fails to meet them.

Task vs. benchmark

A task is a language skill in a fixed input–output format — for example, multiple-choice reading-comprehension question answering. A benchmark tries to evaluate that task by grounding it to a text domain and instantiating it with one concrete dataset and metric. The two are not the same, and there is no general way to prove a benchmark faithfully measures its task. Since we can only ever evaluate on concrete benchmarks, the entire job is to strengthen that correspondence as much as we can — which is exactly what the four criteria are about.

The four criteria

  • 1 · Validity. Out-scoring another system should be strong evidence of being better at the task. Minimally this requires reflecting the full range of linguistic variation in the domain, plausibly testing every behavior the task demands, and being free enough of annotation artifacts that a system can't reach near-human scores by shortcuts. The authors concede they "know of no simple test" to certify validity.
  • 2 · Reliable annotation. Test labels must be reliably correct, which means avoiding three failure modes: (i) careless mislabels, (ii) no clear label because guidelines are underspecified, and (iii) legitimate disagreement — examples that admit multiple reasonable labels. The third is the subtle one and drives one of the paper's best results (below).
  • 3 · Statistical power. A benchmark must be large and discriminative enough to detect any qualitatively relevant gap between two models. There is a trade-off with difficulty: an easy dataset needs to be enormous to retain power, while a hard, reliable one can stay modest.
  • 4 · Bias disincentives. A benchmark should, in general, favor a model without socially-relevant biases over an otherwise-equivalent model that has them — and should not reward systems that adopt harmful biases as performance-improving heuristics.

Why adversarial filtering isn't the cure

DynaBench belongs to the broader family of adversarial filtering: generate candidate examples, then keep only the ones a reference model gets wrong. Bowman and Dahl argue that collecting examples on which current models fail is "neither necessary nor sufficient" to build a useful benchmark. Worse, it manufactures a perverse incentive: a new model can top the leaderboard by being different without being better — producing different errors from the adversary rather than fewer of them, since the examples where its new mistakes would show up never enter the test set.

// the core objection

Adversarial filtering is "mode-seeking" rather than "mass-covering." By systematically discarding everything the adversary already solves, it shrinks coverage and diversity — making the validity criterion harder to satisfy, not easier. (The authors do endorse adversarial competitions — comparing how hard it is to fool different systems — as a healthy complement, as long as that's kept separate from using a model to filter the test set itself.)

// what they found

The evidence behind each criterion

Because this is a position paper, its "results" are the concrete numbers it marshals to show that current benchmarks fail the four criteria — and that no benchmark for any hard, broad-domain NLU task satisfies all of them.

Saturation is already here

GLUE's leaderboard approached or exceeded human performance on all nine of its tasks. When SuperGLUE solicited dataset submissions from the community in 2019, the organizers had to exclude the large majority because BERT already matched or beat a majority vote of human crowdworkers; of the eight tasks that left BERT clear headroom, all are now effectively saturated. SQuAD 2.0's state of the art has long exceeded its human annotators.

"Super-human" is often an annotation artifact

This is the paper's most quietly damaging point. Across several textual-entailment datasets, 20% of examples are significantly ambiguous (Pavlick & Kwiatkowski 2019), and 36% of Natural Questions short-answer annotations differ significantly from the majority answer (Kwiatkowski et al. 2019). When a benchmark is full of legitimately-disputable examples, a model that learns to predict the majority label can out-score individual humans who honestly report their own judgment — producing illusory super-human numbers that reflect no deeper understanding at all.

Statistical power gets expensive exactly when you need it

With a reliable, difficult dataset, detecting a 1-point absolute gain (80% → 81%) needs only a few thousand examples. But the same relative 5% improvement near the ceiling — 98% → 98.1% — requires two orders of magnitude more evaluation data (Card et al. 2020). As systems improve, benchmarks spend more and more time in this costly long tail.

// the price of a powered benchmark

Even on optimistic assumptions — 45 s to label an existing NLI example, one minute to write a new one, and only 10% of examples discarded for disagreement — a 10-way-annotated set of 500,000 examples costs over $1 million at $15/hr. (For scale, the paper notes GPT-3's training was publicly estimated above $10M.) Adequate power is buyable, but expensive enough to entrench the field's concentration on a few high-resource languages and tasks.

Bias metrics exist but go unreported

The state-of-the-art T5 model shows far more sensitivity to irrelevant gender cues than humans do on SuperGLUE's Winogender (DNC) diagnostic. The deeper problem is incentive design: even though SuperGLUE requires computing that bias metric to unlock test-set results, a large majority of papers reporting SuperGLUE numbers simply omit the unflattering bias figure. Measurement without an obligation to report it changes little.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

This is a position paper with no new experiments, so the real test is whether its diagnosis and its prescription survive the five years since. They came apart.

The diagnosis aged remarkably well. Benchmark saturation, annotation artifacts, and "super-human is an annotation-disagreement artifact" are now mainstream concerns, and the LLM era only sharpened them — MMLU and its peers fell within a couple of years, motivating harder exams like GPQA and Humanity's Last Exam and contamination-controlled suites like LiveCodeBench. The validity criterion — no near-human scores via shortcuts — reads as an early, precise statement of the train/test-leakage panic that now dominates LLM evaluation.

The prescription is the contested part. Bowman and Dahl bet against adversarial/OOD evaluation and for repairing static IID benchmarks by making them harder, larger, and better-annotated. The field did not take that fork cleanly: it pursued harder held-out exams, dynamic and contamination-resistant benchmarks, and human-preference arenas (Chatbot Arena) all at once. And the LLM era undercut the "much larger / much harder" remedy on its own terms — even careful, expert-written exams now saturate within a release cycle or two, which suggests more data buys less time than the paper hoped. The single biggest blind spot is scope: this is firmly an NLU-classification paper, and the dominant post-2022 problem — grading open-ended generation — sits entirely outside it. Nothing here addresses LLM-as-judge or rubric grading, where the paper's own "reliable annotation" worry comes roaring back, now with the judge as the unreliable annotator.

baselines

N/A — a position paper with no experiments of its own. Its evidence is borrowed from leaderboards and prior probing work (HANS, CheckList, Pavlick & Kwiatkowski). Persuasive, but it measures nothing new.

contamination

Not studied directly, but the validity criterion (no near-human scores reachable by shortcuts) is essentially an early framing of the train/test-leakage problem that would come to define LLM eval.

what's missed

Open-ended / generative evaluation and LLM-as-judge — the dominant regime after 2022 — are wholly out of scope. And the fourth criterion (bias) stays aspirational: the authors concede they know "no robust strategy" to guarantee unbiased eval data.

would raise confidence

An operational, runnable test for "validity" (the paper admits there is none), a per-task power/cost worksheet, and actual evidence that harder/larger static IID benchmarks outlast adversarial collection — rather than being preferable only in principle.

// bottom line

The diagnosis — NLU benchmarking is broken, and "super-human" is frequently an artifact of annotator disagreement — has only looked sharper with time. The cure (double down on bigger, harder, better-annotated static IID benchmarks; treat adversarial filtering as a distraction) is what history complicated: the LLM era saturated even careful benchmarks fast and pushed the community toward exactly the dynamic and OOD approaches the paper was skeptical of. Read it for the four criteria — still the cleanest checklist we have for asking whether a benchmark deserves trust.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. Four criteria for a trustworthy NLU benchmark — validity, reliable annotation, statistical power, and bias disincentives — with the claim that no benchmark for any hard, broad-domain NLU task satisfies all four.
  2. "Super-human" performance is frequently an illusion: when ~20% of entailment examples are genuinely ambiguous, a model predicting the majority label beats individual humans without understanding anything more.
  3. Statistical power is expensive and worsens with progress: detecting a 98% → 98.1% gain needs ~100× the data of an 80% → 81% gain, and a properly-powered 500k-example set runs past $1M to annotate.
  4. Adversarial filtering treats the symptom: it guarantees hard examples but is "mode-seeking," shrinking coverage and rewarding models that are different without being better.
