On Faithfulness and Factuality in Abstractive Summarization

A 2,500-judgment human study of five XSum summarization systems finds that more than 70% of single-sentence abstracts hallucinate content the source article never says — and that ROUGE cannot tell a faithful summary from a fluent, factually wrong one.

Joshua Maynez · Shashi Narayan · Bernd Bohnet · Ryan McDonald · Google Research
venue ACL 2020 citations 1,583 topic Benchmarks & Evals source ↗

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

By 2020, neural abstractive summarizers — RNN pointer-generators, convolutional seq2seq models, Transformers, and BERT-initialized encoder-decoders — had gotten fluent and scored well on ROUGE, especially on the recently-introduced eXtreme SUMmarization (XSum) task: compress a BBC article into the single sentence a journalist would have used as its intro. Nobody had run a rigorous, large-scale human study of whether that output was actually faithful to the source document it was supposedly summarizing. Isolated factuality checks existed (dual-attention consistency scores, NLI-based summary reranking, critiques of neural summarization's training-data artifacts), but none of them (a) defined a clean taxonomy of what a "hallucination" even is, (b) ran the same protocol across multiple architecture families on one dataset, or (c) checked whether the field's default metrics — ROUGE, and the newer BERTScore — track faithfulness at all.

XSum is a deliberately hard test case for this question. Its gold summaries are the BBC's own introductory sentences, written by journalists who freely bring outside context and background knowledge to the sentence — meaning even the human-authored references routinely say things the article body never states. That makes XSum simultaneously the sharpest test of abstraction (extractive shortcuts don't work on single-sentence compression) and the best magnifying glass for hallucination: if unfaithful content shows up even here, in a controlled, well-resourced study, it is very unlikely to be a minor implementation bug in any one system.

// the one-line gap

Abstractive summarizers were being scored on ROUGE — a metric that only ever measures word/n-gram overlap with a reference — with no systematic human check on whether their fluent, high-scoring output was actually true to the source article. This paper runs that check at scale, and proposes the first automatic metric shown to track it better than ROUGE does.

// how it works

The method

The paper first pins down a definition. Given a document D and its summary S, a span of S is a hallucination if it is not supported by D (paraphrases and legitimately inferable content don't count). It then splits hallucinations into two kinds: intrinsic hallucinations synthesize or misrepresent information that is present in the document — using the document's own entities and concepts, but getting the relationship wrong; extrinsic hallucinations introduce material the document never mentions at all, which may happen to be true (background knowledge) or simply invented. This intrinsic/extrinsic split is the paper's central conceptual contribution, and it's what makes the subsequent human annotation tractable and comparable across systems.

The testbed is XSum (226,711 BBC articles, split 204,045 / 11,332 / 11,334 train/val/test, each paired with a single journalist-written intro sentence). Five summarizers are evaluated on the same 500 randomly sampled test articles: PTGEN (See et al.'s RNN pointer-generator, copies + generates), TCONVS2S (Narayan et al.'s topic-aware convolutional seq2seq, XSum's original baseline), TRANS2S (a randomly-initialized Transformer seq2seq), GPT-TUNED (a GPT decoder-only LM fine-tuned for summarization), and BERTS2S (a Transformer seq2seq with both encoder and decoder warm-started from BERT-Base) — plus the human-written GOLD references themselves, scored the same way.

Three trained annotators independently judged each of the 500 × 5 = 2,500 document-summary pairs (after two pilot rounds to calibrate the intrinsic/extrinsic distinction), marking every unfaithful span and tagging it intrinsic or extrinsic. A separate follow-up pass then asked whether each hallucinated summary was nonetheless factual: annotators, given the source article plus external references (Wikipedia, Google Search), judged 1,869 hallucinated document-summary pairs true or false. Alongside the human study, the paper scores every summary with four automatic measures: ROUGE-1/2/L, BERTScore, a textual-entailment probability (a BERT-Large model fine-tuned on MultiNLI, scoring whether the summary is entailed by / neutral to / contradicts the full document), and a question-answering consistency check (round-trip: generate QA pairs from the summary, then see if a reading-comprehension model can answer them from the source document). The entailment and QA models are both repurposed from other tasks — a design choice the paper is explicit about, and one the eval section below returns to.

// what they found

The results

On raw automatic metrics, the ranking is exactly what you'd expect from 2020 pretraining trends: BERTS2S leads everything (ROUGE-1/2/L 38.42 / 16.96 / 31.27, BERTScore 78.85), the non-pretrained PTGEN / TCONVS2S / TRANS2S cluster in the low-to-mid 30s / low-20s, and GPT-TUNED trails badly (21.82 / 4.72 / 16.28, BERTScore 70.35) — poorly enough that the authors drop it from the human study entirely before the faithfulness question is ever asked of it.

The human study is where the automatic-metric story falls apart. Across every system tested — including the human-written GOLD references — between 73% and 79% of single-sentence summaries contain at least one hallucinated span:

GOLD was excluded from the factual follow-up study — the authors "presumably" treated human-written references as factual by default.) A few things stand out. PTGEN's copy mechanism trades intrinsic error for extrinsic error: it has the lowest extrinsic hallucination (63.3%) because copying straight from the source is hard to get factually wrong, but the highest intrinsic rate (19.9%) because copying without real document-level inference is exactly what produces misrepresentations. BERTS2S is best on every axis — least hallucinated overall (73.1%), most faithful (26.9%), and by far the most faithful-or-factual (34.7%, 7.4 points ahead of PTGEN) — which the paper attributes to pretraining exposing the model to enough world knowledge that its extrinsic guesses land true more often (6.6% of its hallucinations were factual, vs. 2.2% for PTGEN). Even so, over 90% of BERTS2S's hallucinations were still erroneous. And the GOLD row is the sobering baseline: human journalists' own intro sentences are only 23.1% strictly faithful, because XSum gold summaries are allowed to import outside context the article body never states.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

This paper is the empirical gut-punch the ROUGE critique thread had been waiting for. Lin & Hovy's 2003 n-gram co-occurrence paper and Lin's 2004 ROUGE package both validated only that overlap scores rank systems the way humans rank coverage — neither ever claimed to certify meaning, and both are honest that they don't. This paper is the first large-scale demonstration of exactly what that gap costs: every system in Table 1 posts respectable, cleanly differentiated ROUGE/BERTScore numbers, and Figure 1's BERTS2S output — "Zac Goldsmith has been chosen to contest the London mayoral election," which quietly drops the fact that Goldsmith merely won a primary — scores the highest ROUGE-1/2/L of the whole set (66.7 / 40.0 / 51.9) while still being unfaithful to the source. ROUGE cannot see that, by design; it was never asked to.

Baselines are a representative 2020 lineup — RNN, CNN, two Transformer variants, one pretrained-init model — but they're frozen at that moment. BART, PEGASUS and T5 (all roughly concurrent or slightly later, and purpose-built for summarization) never appear, and neither does anything at LLM scale; a reader can't tell from this paper whether stronger, more recent pretraining narrows the faithfulness gap or just gets better at automatic metrics again. More pointedly, GPT-TUNED — the paper's one genuinely open-ended, decoder-only baseline — is dropped from the human study entirely after scoring worst on ROUGE/BERTScore. That's a filtering decision made by the very metric the paper is arguing can't be trusted, inside a study whose whole point is that the metric misses what matters; we never learn whether GPT-TUNED's hallucination profile looks qualitatively different from the encoder-decoder models it was compared against and then excluded from.

There's no training-contamination axis here — this is an eval-only study — but there is a calibration risk worth flagging: the entire 2,500-judgment corpus comes from one dataset, and XSum's gold summaries are unusually background-heavy by construction (hence GOLD's own 76.9% "hallucination" rate). The paper is careful to say this, but the >70% headline number is easy to misread as a universal property of abstractive summarization rather than a number calibrated to XSum's single-sentence, journalist-authored genre; the paper itself argues the number should only get worse on longer summaries, but doesn't test that directly.

The proposed fix — textual entailment as an automatic faithfulness proxy — is a genuine advance (rs = 0.431 vs. ROUGE's 0.16–0.20) but far from a solved problem, and the authors are unusually candid about why. The entailment classifier is fine-tuned on MultiNLI, a corpus of isolated sentence pairs, then asked to score document-vs-summary entailment — a domain mismatch the paper names outright ("this entailment classifier is not optimal for the BBC article-summary pairs"). Entailment is also a coarse three-way signal (entail / neutral / contradict) computed over the whole summary; it doesn't localize which span is wrong the way the human annotation protocol does, and it can't obviously distinguish a subtly wrong number or misattributed name from a genuinely supported paraphrase if both come out "entailed." And because it's reference-less, it's explicitly gameable — the paper notes the first sentence of any document is trivially entailed by that document — which is why entailment is deployed here only as a reranker over four fixed systems' candidates (Table 5), not as a standalone leaderboard score.

baselines

Five representative 2020 systems (RNN / CNN / 2×Transformer / 1 pretrained-init), but no BART/PEGASUS/T5 or LLM-scale models, and GPT-TUNED — the one open-ended decoder-only baseline — is dropped from the human study after scoring worst on the very automatic metrics the paper is critiquing.

contamination

Not a training-contamination study; the closer risk is genre calibration — all 2,500 judgments come from XSum, whose background-heavy gold summaries push even human references to a 76.9% "hallucination" rate, so the >70% headline shouldn't be read as dataset-agnostic without the follow-up work the paper itself calls for.

what's missed

The entailment metric (rs=0.431/0.264) is better than ROUGE/BERTScore/QA but still only moderate/weak; it's trained on generic MultiNLI sentence pairs (not document-summary pairs), is reference-less and explicitly gameable, and scores a whole summary rather than localizing which span is unfaithful.

would raise confidence

The same protocol on a second, less background-heavy dataset (CNN/DailyMail); a summarization-tuned entailment model instead of MultiNLI; inter-annotator agreement reported alongside the headline rates rather than deferred to the appendix; and an entailment-guided training/decoding objective rather than reranking among four fixed candidates.

// bottom line

This is the paper that turned "the ROUGE score looks fine but the summary is wrong" from a suspicion into a number: 73–79% hallucination across every system tested, including the human-written references; near-zero correlation between that number and ROUGE/BERTScore; and a first working alternative — textual entailment — that's better but still far from solved. Beyond the numbers, its lasting contribution is the intrinsic/extrinsic hallucination vocabulary and the human-annotation protocol that the faithfulness-evaluation subfield (FactCC, QAGS, AlignScore, and eventually the general "LLM hallucination" framing) still builds on.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. Intrinsic vs. extrinsic hallucination is this paper's lasting vocabulary: misrepresenting content that's already in the document vs. inventing content that isn't there at all. That two-way split is now the standard way NLG faithfulness errors get categorized, far beyond summarization.
  2. The headline number is brutal, not marginal. Hallucination (I∪E) hit 73–79% of single-sentence summaries across every system tested, including the 76.9%-hallucinated human-written GOLD references — evidence that hallucination is the default output mode of abstractive summarizers, not an occasional glitch, years before "hallucination" became standard LLM-chatbot vocabulary.
  3. ROUGE and BERTScore barely track faithfulness (Spearman |rs| ≈ 0.16–0.20), and a BERT-based textual-entailment score does meaningfully better (0.431 / 0.264) but is still only moderate — quantitative proof that overlap metrics can't audit factuality, and the seed of the entailment/NLI-based and later QA- and LLM-judge-based factuality metrics that followed.
