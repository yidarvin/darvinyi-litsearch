BERT: Pre-training of Deep Bidirectional Transformers for Language Understanding

A single masked-language-model objective lets one deep Transformer condition on left and right context at every layer — replacing ELMo's shallow LSTM concatenation and GPT's left-to-right constraint, making pre-train-then-fine-tune the default recipe for NLP, and setting new state of the art on eleven tasks.

Jacob Devlin, Ming-Wei Chang, Kenton Lee, Kristina Toutanova · Google AI Language

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

By 2018 the recipe for using unlabeled text was clear — pre-train a language model on a huge corpus, then transfer it to a downstream task — but the two leading instances of that recipe were both stuck reading text in one direction. ELMo (Peters et al., 2018) trained a left-to-right and a right-to-left LSTM independently and concatenated their outputs as features: a shallow combination, never jointly bidirectional. OpenAI GPT (Radford et al., 2018) fine-tuned a left-to-right Transformer in which every token could attend only to the tokens before it.

That unidirectional constraint is natural for generation but actively harmful for understanding tasks — question answering, entailment, named-entity recognition — where a word's meaning depends on the words that come after it as much as before. The obvious fix, simply letting self-attention look both ways during language-model training, is self-defeating: in a multi-layer Transformer a token would indirectly "see itself" through the stack, so the model could predict the target word trivially and learn nothing. The missing piece was a pre-training objective that fuses left and right context in every layer without that leakage.

// how it works

The method

BERT's answer is the masked language model (MLM) objective. Rather than predict the next token, it randomly masks 15% of the WordPiece tokens in each input and trains the model to recover them from the surrounding both-sided context. Because the prediction targets are hidden, a token can attend in all directions without leaking its own identity — so the Transformer is genuinely deep bidirectional, not a stitched-together pair of one-directional models.

ℒMLM = − Σi∈M log P( xi | x\M ) // predict each masked token xi (set M = 15% of tokens) from the unmasked rest x\M — both left and right

One wrinkle: the special [MASK] token used in pre-training never appears at fine-tuning time, which would create a train/serve mismatch. So of the 15% of positions selected, BERT replaces 80% with [MASK], 10% with a random token, and leaves 10% unchanged — forcing the model to build a robust representation of every token rather than only the masked ones.

A second objective, next sentence prediction (NSP), takes two segments A and B and predicts whether B genuinely followed A in the corpus (50% of the time) or is a random sentence (50%) — intended to teach the inter-sentence relationships that QA and natural-language-inference tasks need. The aggregate representation for this and other sequence-level tasks is the final hidden state of a prepended [CLS] token.

BERT comes in two sizes: BERTBASE (L=12 layers, H=768 hidden, A=12 heads, 110M parameters — deliberately matched to OpenAI GPT for a controlled comparison) and BERTLARGE (L=24, H=1024, A=16, 340M parameters). Both are pre-trained on BooksCorpus (800M words) + English Wikipedia (2,500M words) — 3.3B words — for 1,000,000 steps at a batch size of 256 sequences × 512 tokens (128,000 tokens/batch, ≈40 epochs), using Adam at learning rate 1e-4. Pre-training BERTLARGE took 4 days on 16 Cloud TPUs.

Transfer is uniform: the same pre-trained weights initialize every downstream model, you add a single task-specific output layer, and you fine-tune all parameters end-to-end. Sentence-pair classification reads off [CLS]; SQuAD predicts answer-span start/end positions; sequence tagging (NER) reads each token's final hidden state — no task-specific architecture engineering.

// what they found

The results

BERT set new state of the art on eleven NLP tasks, often by wide margins. On the GLUE benchmark, BERTLARGE reached an 82.1 average across the nine tasks (BERTBASE 79.6) versus OpenAI GPT's 75.1 and the pre-GPT state of the art of 74.0; the official GLUE leaderboard composite reported in the abstract is 80.5%, a 7.7-point absolute jump. The per-task gains are uneven and revealing: CoLA (grammaticality) leapt from GPT's 45.4 to 60.5, and RTE from 56.0 to 70.1, while already-saturated tasks like SST-2 moved only to 94.9.

On reading comprehension, BERTLARGE scored 90.9 F1 / 84.1 EM on SQuAD v1.1 as a single model, and its best ensemble (augmented with TriviaQA data) hit 93.2 F1 / 87.4 EM — past the previous best ensemble (R.M. Reader, 88.5 F1). On the harder SQuAD v2.0 (with unanswerable questions) it reached 83.1 F1 / 80.0 EM (a 5.1-point jump). On SWAG commonsense inference, BERTLARGE scored 86.3, far above OpenAI GPT (78.0) and ELMo-based ESIM (59.2) — and, reading Table 4, edging past the single expert-human baseline (85.0) while still short of the 5-annotation human ceiling (88.0).

The ablations are where the mechanism shows. Replacing MLM with a left-to-right LM ("LTR & No NSP", same architecture and data) drops SQuAD dev F1 from 88.5 to 77.8 and MRPC accuracy from 86.7 to 77.5; the authors conclude this "demonstrate[s] the importance of the deep bidirectionality of BERT." Dropping only NSP costs QNLI (88.4 → 84.9) and SQuAD. And BERT works even frozen: concatenating the top four layers' activations as fixed features for CoNLL-2003 NER scores 96.1 dev F1, within 0.3 of full fine-tuning. The authors also report this is "the first work to demonstrate convincingly that scaling to extreme model sizes also leads to large improvements on very small scale tasks, provided that the model has been sufficiently pre-trained."

// does it hold up?

The eval read

BERT's central empirical claim — that bidirectionality, not scale or data, is the missing ingredient — rests on two pillars, and they are cleaner in spirit than in detail. The first is the head-to-head with OpenAI GPT. Sizing BERTBASE to match GPT's 110M parameters was the right instinct, but the two still differ in pre-training corpus (BooksCorpus + Wikipedia vs. BooksCorpus alone), in input format (BERT adds [SEP]/segment embeddings and the NSP task), and in training details — so the comparison bundles several changes behind a single "bidirectional" label. The stronger pillar is the within-paper LTR & No NSP ablation (Table 5): holding architecture and data fixed, swapping MLM for a left-to-right objective drops SQuAD F1 from 88.5 to 77.8. That one does isolate the objective, and it is the result the thesis really stands on.

Two seams are worth naming. The MLM objective introduces a [MASK] token that exists only at pre-training time — a train/serve distribution mismatch the 80/10/10 trick softens but never removes; XLNet and ELECTRA were built to attack exactly this. And the NSP result is the shakiest claim in the paper: BERT reports NSP helps, but its ablations were run at a single fixed compute budget, and RoBERTa later showed NSP is largely unnecessary once you train longer on more data with full-length sequences. So "NSP helps" is more precisely "NSP helps within this budget."

On evaluation hygiene: GLUE and the SQuAD/SWAG leaderboards limit test submissions (good), but SQuAD's passages are Wikipedia, which sits in BERT's pre-training corpus. There is no label leakage — the model never sees the answers — yet the pre-train/eval domain overlap is precisely what later contamination audits scrutinize, and some of the headline gain may reflect domain familiarity rather than better representations alone. Finally, the single GLUE number is a composite dominated by the large datasets (MNLI 392k, QQP 363k); the small tasks (RTE 2.5k, CoLA 8.5k) are noisy, and the authors themselves report averaging over random restarts to stabilize them — so a 56→70 RTE jump is a transfer story and a high-variance one.

baselines

Strong and current for 2018 — a size-matched GPT, ELMo, and task-specific SOTA on public leaderboards. But the GPT comparison conflates objective + corpus + input format, so it overstates how much is "bidirectionality" alone.

contamination

No label leakage. But SQuAD/GLUE source text (Wikipedia, BooksCorpus) overlaps the pre-training corpus — benign for the era, yet domain familiarity is uncontrolled.

what's missed

A single GLUE/SQuAD score hides high variance on the small tasks; NSP's claimed benefit is confounded with a fixed, relatively short training budget — and was later overturned by RoBERTa.

would raise confidence

An ablation cleanly separating bidirectionality from objective from data; longer-training runs (RoBERTa-style); variance bars on every small GLUE task, not just averaged restarts.

// bottom line

The within-paper MLM-vs-LTR ablation is convincing enough that the core thesis — deep bidirectionality is the missing ingredient — has held up. What aged is the surrounding recipe: NSP, the masking scheme, and the training length were all systematically improved by the next two years of work (RoBERTa, XLNet, ELECTRA). BERT was right about the idea and beatable on the execution.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. Bidirectionality without leakage. Masking 15% of tokens and predicting them from both-sided context lets one deep Transformer do what ELMo's shallow LSTM concatenation and GPT's left-to-right constraint could not — condition on full context in every layer.
  2. One model, eleven tasks. The same pre-trained weights plus a single output layer set new SOTA across GLUE (82.1 avg), SQuAD 1.1 (93.2 F1) and 2.0 (83.1 F1), and SWAG (86.3) — pre-train-then-fine-tune became the default recipe for NLP.
  3. The ablations outlast the leaderboard. Swapping MLM for a left-to-right LM collapses SQuAD (88.5 → 77.8), and frozen BERT features still hit 96.1 on CoNLL NER — but NSP's claimed benefit was later shown to be an artifact of a short training budget.
