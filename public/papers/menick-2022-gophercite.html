Teaching language models to support answers with verified quotes

DeepMind's GopherCite: a 280-billion-parameter QA model trained with reinforcement learning from human preferences to answer with a verbatim, machine-checked quote as evidence — and to say "I don't know" when it isn't confident.

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

By early 2022, large language models answered factual questions correctly often enough to be useful — but a user had no cheap way to tell a correct answer from a confident-sounding hallucination without independently fact-checking it. Linking to a whole web page (as LaMDA does) still leaves the reader to search that page for the relevant sentence. WebGPT, developed concurrently at OpenAI, has its model learn to issue its own search queries and stitch together a curated set of short quoted snippets. Neither directly optimizes for the reader being able to look at one sentence and immediately judge whether it backs up the claim.

Menick et al. take a narrower, more mechanical bet: make the model emit a literal, exact substring of a retrieved document as its evidence — not a paraphrase, not a link, not a page number — and constrain sampling so the "quote" is provably verbatim. If the claim and the quote are shown side by side, a rater (or an end user) can check supportedness at a glance instead of opening a source and reading it in full. The paper calls this task Self-Supported Question Answering (SQA), and their system for producing it, built by fine-tuning the 280B-parameter Gopher language model with reinforcement learning from human preferences (RLHP), is named GopherCite.

// how it works

The method

GopherCite generates an answer and its supporting evidence as a single string, using a special "Inline Evidence" syntax: %<Claim>%(Document title)%[Quote from document]%. Because the model is strictly left-to-right, this factors the joint distribution as P(answer | question) · P(evidence | answer, question) — the answer can be scored on its own, and the evidence is generated as a continuation conditioned on the claim already made. During decoding, the model's output is constrained so that whatever appears inside the quote brackets must be an exact, verbatim span from one of the retrieved documents — this is the mechanism that makes "verified" literal rather than aspirational.

Training follows a four-stage RLHF-style pipeline, run in repeated loops: (1) generate candidate answers — bootstrapped initially via few-shot prompting of the base Gopher model rather than human demonstrations, since a labelled inline-evidence dataset didn't exist — and have human raters judge each one as Plausible (a reasonable reply to the question) and Supported (the evidence is sufficient to convince the rater the whole answer is true); (2) supervised fine-tune Gopher on the Rated-Good examples for just 60 SGD steps, an aggressively early stop chosen specifically to keep the model close to its original distribution; (3) train a reward model (warm-started from the 7B Gopher variant) on 33,242 pairwise preference comparisons, following Christiano et al.'s RLHP setup, with an auxiliary loss predicting the binary Supported-and-Plausible judgment; (4) run reinforcement learning (synchronous A2C) against the reward model with a KL penalty back to the SFT policy. At inference time, the system retrieves the top-K documents from Google Search, draws N>K samples round-robin across those documents, and reranks with the reward model to pick the highest-scoring sample — the same reward model also powers an abstention mechanism: below a tunable score threshold, the system answers "I don't know" instead of guessing.

// what they found

The results

On held-out, leakage-filtered subsets of two QA datasets, GopherCite's best configuration produces a response human raters judge Supported & Plausible (S&P) 80.0% of the time on NaturalQuestionsFiltered (115 overlapping questions, best model: SFT reranked at top@64 over documents from Google) and 66.9% of the time on ELI5Filtered (121 questions, best model: RL reranked at top@16). Both numbers beat every handcrafted baseline they tried, including a FiD-DPR extractive-QA system paired with its best retrieved passage as evidence (58.3% S&P on NaturalQuestionsFiltered) and TF-IDF/ROUGE-matched evidence baselines. The ground-truth "Gold + GoldEvidence" answers from the NaturalQuestions dataset itself score 83.5% S&P — GopherCite's 80.0% approaches, without quite reaching, that ceiling, and in head-to-head human preference GopherCite's SFT@64 answers are preferred over Gold + GoldEvidence 49.5% of the time (a near-even split).

Reranking and RL both matter, but not equally on both datasets: reinforcement learning outperforms plain SFT decoding when only a single sample is drawn, but once reranking with a reward model is added, SFT@64 actually edges out RL@64 on NaturalQuestionsFiltered, while RL keeps its edge over SFT throughout on ELI5Filtered. The authors attribute the NaturalQuestions reversal to RL training losing sample diversity (which erodes the benefit of drawing many candidates to rerank) and to RL training data being weighted toward ELI5-style non-extractive questions. Scaling the generator from 1.4B to 7B to the full 280B Gopher produces clear, monotonic gains in both S&P and human preference — the 280B model is the strongest at every setting tested.

The most important negative result is in Section 3.7. Evaluated on the adversarial TruthfulQA benchmark, GopherCite still scores reasonably on its own Supported&Plausible metric (59.3% for SFT+top@16) but scores far lower — 22.2% — on TruthfulQA's own Truthful&Informative metric, barely above the raw few-shot-prompted Gopher baseline's 21.2%. The mechanism is structural, not a training bug: if a document exists that states something false in a way that reads as supporting evidence — e.g. an article about the Red Bull "gives you wings" slogan, or a satirical/fictional passage — the model can quote it verbatim and be rated "Supported" by a literal-minded rater even though the claim is false. The paper is explicit that this is a fundamental limitation of the approach in isolation, not a fixable side-effect: "not all claims supported by evidence are true."

// does it hold up?

The eval read

The headline evaluation is entirely human-rater-based, and the paper is unusually transparent about that rater pipeline — transparent enough to reveal a real wrinkle. All training data and the NaturalQuestionsFiltered evaluation come from a "super rater" pool of 113 crowd-workers, screened to 85% agreement with the researchers' own Supported/Plausible/Preferred judgments. But the paper states plainly that "for ELI5 and TruthfulQA evaluations, we opened the study to a wider pool of new raters, ensuring no overlap with the super rater pool," screened only by lighter attention checks rather than the 85%-agreement bar. That means the 80.0% NaturalQuestionsFiltered number and the 66.9%/59.3% ELI5/TruthfulQA numbers are not judged by the same rater-quality bar — a disclosed detail the paper doesn't fully reckon with when placing those scores side by side.

The evaluation sets are genuinely leakage-conscious: NaturalQuestionsFiltered explicitly filters out validation questions whose question, answer, or ground-truth Wikipedia document appeared in the training set (following Lewis et al.'s train-test-overlap analysis), leaving only 307 true holdout questions before further filtering down to 115 for apples-to-apples model comparison. That's a real, if severe, reduction — with only 115–121 questions per dataset and 90% confidence intervals reported via a normal approximation (Tables 1 and 2 report intervals from ±5.7 up to ±7.8 points), several of the headline comparisons (RL vs. SFT reranking, in particular) sit well within a single interval of each other and shouldn't be read as decisively separated.

The baselines are a mix of genuinely strong (FiD-DPR, a contemporaneous SOTA extractive QA system) and deliberately weak sanity checks (Gold + Random-Sentence scores only 3.5% S&P, exactly as intended, to calibrate the scale). That's reasonable practice, but the paper limits itself to "just this model" for the QA literature comparison "for brevity" — a single non-GopherCite ML baseline per dataset is thin for a paper making a strong "our approach beats prior systems" framing, especially given WebGPT (the most directly comparable contemporaneous system) is explicitly not compared quantitatively, only discussed qualitatively, because — as the authors themselves note — the two systems used different, non-comparable question subsets and rater pools.

baselines

Reasonable but thin: one non-GopherCite ML system (FiD-DPR) per dataset plus handcrafted sanity-check baselines; no head-to-head numbers against WebGPT or LaMDA, the two most relevant contemporaneous systems, despite extensive qualitative discussion of both.

contamination

Actively addressed and disclosed — NaturalQuestionsFiltered explicitly removes train-test overlap per Lewis et al., which is more diligence than most 2022 QA papers applied, though it shrinks the eval set to just 115–121 questions with correspondingly wide confidence intervals.

what's missed

"Supported" is rater-judged plausibility-of-evidence, not truth. Section 3.7's TruthfulQA result is the paper's own proof that Supported&Plausible and Truthful&Informative can diverge sharply (59.3% vs. 22.2% on the same answers) — the headline 80.0%/66.9% numbers should always be read with that caveat attached, not as a truthfulness score.

would raise confidence

A direct, same-question-pool comparison against WebGPT; larger held-out eval sets to tighten the wide confidence intervals; and a systematic characterization (beyond a handful of qualitative examples) of how often "supported" quotes are drawn from unreliable, satirical, or fictional sources in the wild, not just the adversarial TruthfulQA slice.

// bottom line

A well-executed, honestly-caveated RLHF system: the verbatim-quote mechanism is a genuinely useful trust primitive that lets a human check a claim in seconds instead of minutes, and the paper's own TruthfulQA experiment is a rare example of a lab rigorously demonstrating the boundary of its own headline metric rather than leaving that discovery to critics. But "Supported" is a proxy for "checkable," not for "true," the reported scores across datasets come from rater pools held to different quality bars, the eval sets stay small (~115–121 questions, wide confidence intervals), and the most relevant apples-to-apples comparison — against WebGPT — is never actually run.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. GopherCite fine-tunes a 280B Gopher model with RLHP to answer questions alongside a constrained-decoded, provably verbatim quote as evidence — human raters judge 80.0% of its NaturalQuestionsFiltered answers and 66.9% of its ELI5Filtered answers as Supported & Plausible, both above every handcrafted baseline tried.
  2. The same reward model that reranks candidates also powers abstention: declining to answer the least-confident 30% of questions lifts Supported&Plausible on attempted answers above 90% (NaturalQuestionsFiltered) and above 80% (ELI5Filtered).
  3. The paper's own TruthfulQA experiment is its most important result: a model can be "Supported" by a literal, verbatim quote while still being false — Truthful&Informative score is only 22.2% even though Supported&Plausible is 59.3% on the same answers, showing that citation-grounding alone does not solve truthfulness.
