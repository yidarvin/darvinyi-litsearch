Don’t Stop Pretraining: Adapt Language Models to Domains and Tasks

Continuing to pretrain RoBERTa-base on cheap, unlabeled domain and task text — no new architecture, no labels — closes much of the gap a genuinely broad 160GB pretraining corpus still leaves on the table.

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

By 2020, the standard NLP recipe was: pretrain one very large, very broad language model, then fine-tune it on whatever task you have. RoBERTa, the model this paper builds on, was trained on 160GB of English text drawn from books, Wikipedia, news, and Reddit-linked web pages — deliberately heterogeneous, on the theory that scale and diversity make domain-specific tailoring unnecessary. That theory was mostly untested at this scale. Plenty of prior work had shown that a domain-specific model — BioBERT on PubMed abstracts, SciBERT on scientific text, ClinicalBERT on clinical notes — beats a general model on that domain's tasks. But nearly all of those comparisons started from a smaller, less diverse base model (BERT trained on Wikipedia + BookCorpus, or trained from scratch on the domain corpus itself), so it was never clear whether the domain-specific gain was really about domain relevance, or just an artifact of comparing against a weaker or narrower baseline.

The authors' question is sharper: given a single strong, broad-coverage pretrained model, does a second, unsupervised phase of pretraining on domain-relevant text still help — and if the task itself has an even narrower unlabeled corpus, does adapting to that help on top of domain adaptation? No prior study had run this as a controlled comparison across multiple domains and tasks against one modern, genuinely broad-coverage LM, nor had anyone isolated whether such gains come from "more relevant data" as opposed to simply "more pretraining compute" — a confound the field had mostly waved past.

// how it works

The method

The recipe is deliberately simple: take an off-the-shelf RoBERTa-base checkpoint and continue its masked-language-modeling objective — no architecture change, no new pretraining tricks — on a second corpus, before fine-tuning on the labeled task. The paper studies two flavors of this second phase, at very different scales:

  • Domain-adaptive pretraining (DAPT) — continue pretraining on a large, unlabeled corpus that defines a broad domain: 2.68M full-text BioMed papers from S2ORC (7.55B tokens, 47GB), 2.22M full-text CS papers from S2ORC (8.10B tokens, 48GB), 11.90M News articles from RealNews (6.66B tokens, 39GB), and 24.75M Amazon Reviews (2.11B tokens, 11GB) — one domain-adapted model per domain, each trained for 12.5K steps (a single pass over that domain's corpus) on a v3-8 TPU.
  • Task-adaptive pretraining (TAPT) — continue pretraining on just the much smaller unlabeled text of the target task itself (hundreds to tens of thousands of documents, not billions of tokens) — orders of magnitude cheaper than DAPT, and something you'd do per-task rather than per-domain.

They evaluate on eight text-classification tasks spanning the four domains — ChemProt and RCT (BioMed), ACL-ARC and SciERC (CS), HyperPartisan and AGNews (News), Helpfulness and IMDB (Reviews) — deliberately mixing high-resource tasks (AGNews, RCT, Helpfulness, IMDB) with low-resource ones (ChemProt, ACL-ARC, SciERC, HyperPartisan, ≤5K labeled examples). To motivate why domain should matter at all for a model already trained on 160GB, they run a vocabulary-overlap analysis: comparing the top-10K most frequent words (top figure below) between each domain and a sample resembling RoBERTa's own pretraining sources.

Crucially, they also run a control that the domain-adaptation literature usually skips: for each task, they additionally continue-pretrain RoBERTa on a domain other than the task's own — a "¬DAPT" arm — to check whether gains come from domain relevance specifically, rather than merely from more pretraining exposure of any kind. Finally, for cases without domain-adaptive pretraining budget, they test a cheap automatic alternative: embed task and domain sentences with a lightweight bag-of-words model (VAMPIRE), then pull in the k nearest domain neighbors of each task sentence (via FAISS cosine search) as extra TAPT data — kNN-TAPT — compared against random domain sampling (RAND-TAPT).

// what they found

The results

DAPT helps on 7 of 8 tasks, and the size of the gain tracks the vocabulary-overlap analysis almost exactly: RoBERTa's own pretraining sources share 54.1% vocabulary with News and 34.5% with Reviews, but only 19.2% with CS and 27.3% with BioMed — and the biggest DAPT gains land on the domains RoBERTa's corpus knows least: +12.4 F1 on ACL-ARC (63.0 → 75.4) and +3.5 on SciERC (77.3 → 80.8), against a much smaller +0.4 on RCT (87.2 → 87.6) and no change on AGNews (93.9 → 93.9, the task closest to RoBERTa's own News-adjacent sources).

The ¬DAPT control confirms this isn't just "more pretraining helps": on ACL-ARC, adapting to an irrelevant domain (News, chosen as the least-overlapping alternative) only reaches 66.4 F1 — worse than DAPT's 75.4 and barely above the 63.0 RoBERTa baseline. On HyperPartisan, adapting to an irrelevant domain (CS) actually hurts, dropping to 76.4 versus RoBERTa's own 86.6.

TAPT alone — pretraining on nothing but the task's own unlabeled text — is competitive with DAPT despite touching a tiny fraction of the data, and the two combine: DAPT+TAPT wins on 7 of 8 tasks outright (HyperPartisan is a near-tie, 90.0 for DAPT+TAPT vs. 90.4 for TAPT alone, within a standard deviation).

Extra human-curated unlabeled data is worth even more, and a cheap automatic substitute gets most of the way there. In a simulated low-resource setting (RCT-500 — only 500 of RCT's ~180K labeled examples), pretraining on a larger pool of human-curated unlabeled task data (Curated-TAPT) plus DAPT reaches 83.8 F1 — 95% of the fully-labeled DAPT+TAPT model's 87.8 — using only 0.3% of the labeled data. When curated unlabeled pools aren't available, the automatic kNN-TAPT alternative (embedding-based nearest-neighbor retrieval from the domain corpus) climbs steadily toward DAPT's ceiling as k grows: on ACL-ARC, TAPT alone gets 67.4, 50NN-TAPT 70.7, 150NN-TAPT 73.3, and 500NN-TAPT 75.5 — essentially matching full DAPT's 75.4, while using roughly 2,000× less storage (24MB vs. 47GB) and running about 60× faster to train.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

The ¬DAPT control is the paper's best methodological decision — it's a genuine attempt to separate "domain relevance" from "just more pretraining tokens," which most of the domain-adaptation literature before and after this paper simply doesn't bother to test. That said, it's not a compute-matched ablation: the correct-domain and irrelevant-domain corpora differ in size (47GB BioMed vs. 11GB Reviews, for instance), and DAPT itself runs a fixed 12.5K steps per domain rather than a token-matched budget, so a domain seeing 8.10B tokens (CS) and a domain seeing 2.11B tokens (Reviews) aren't actually receiving comparable compute — some of the cross-domain gain-size story (the huge +12.4 F1 on ACL-ARC vs. the flat AGNews result) conflates "how far is this domain from RoBERTa's corpus" with "how much data/compute did this domain's DAPT run actually get."

The abstract's framing — "results using adaptive pretraining techniques are competitive with the state of the art" — is optimistic read against the paper's own appendix. Table 5's footnote lists dedicated SOTA systems the authors compare to (SciBERT, S2ORC-BERT, XLNet-large, Longformer, task-specific models), and DAPT+TAPT actually beats them on ACL-ARC (75.6 vs. 71.0) but trails by real margins elsewhere: RCT 87.8 vs. 92.9 SOTA, HyperPartisan 90.0 vs. 94.8 SOTA, AGNews 94.6 vs. 95.5, IMDB 95.6 vs. 96.2. "Competitive" is true task-to-task in direction of travel, not in absolute terms on several of the eight tasks.

There's also an unexamined leakage-adjacent concern: the CS domain's DAPT corpus is 2.22M full-text S2ORC computer-science papers, and the ACL-ARC and SciERC tasks are themselves built from computer-science papers — the paper never reports whether the specific labeled documents (or their unlabeled twins) appear inside the domain corpus used for DAPT. That wouldn't be "contamination" in the modern train-on-the-test-set sense, but it would inflate the apparent domain-relevance effect for exactly the two tasks that show the largest gains.

baselines

Single RoBERTa-base checkpoint throughout; on RCT, HyperPartisan, AGNews, and IMDB, DAPT+TAPT still trails dedicated prior SOTA systems (SciBERT/S2ORC-BERT/XLNet-large/Longformer) by 0.6–4.8 F1 points, despite the abstract's "competitive with SOTA" framing.

contamination

No classic benchmark leakage, but the CS and BioMed domain corpora (full-text S2ORC papers) plausibly overlap with the source documents behind ACL-ARC, SciERC, ChemProt, and RCT — the paper never checks or reports document-level overlap between its unlabeled domain corpora and its labeled task sets.

what's missed

DAPT's per-domain step count is fixed at 12.5K steps rather than token- or compute-matched, so domains differ in both "distance from RoBERTa's data" and "how much data/compute they actually got"; all eight tasks are single-document classification — nothing here speaks to generation, QA, or agentic tasks.

would raise confidence

A compute-matched domain-vs-random-domain ablation, a documented decontamination check between domain corpora and task source documents, at least one non-classification task, and — the big one — repeating the core comparison on a modern trillion-token-scale base model to see whether the vocabulary gap DAPT is exploiting has already closed.

// bottom line

This is a clean, comparatively well-controlled demonstration that a second, cheap phase of pretraining on domain- and task-relevant unlabeled text reliably buys real F1 — even against a genuinely broad 160GB baseline — and its human-curated-TAPT result (95% of full-data performance from 500 labeled examples) is arguably its most durable finding. But RoBERTa-base's 2019-era, still-narrow-by-today's-standards corpus is exactly the condition under which a vocabulary gap should be easiest to find and exploit; nothing here tells us whether the technique's value survives contact with a model already pretrained on trillions of tokens spanning most of Common Crawl, arXiv, and PubMed. That's precisely the question BloombergGPT's from-scratch bet — training on a proprietary financial corpus from zero rather than continuing to pretrain an existing general model — re-opened three years later, on the opposite side of the argument.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. A second, unsupervised phase of pretraining on domain-relevant text (DAPT) improves 7 of 8 tasks over a strong RoBERTa-base baseline, and the size of the gain tracks vocabulary overlap with RoBERTa's own pretraining sources — the more dissimilar the domain, the bigger the boost (+12.4 F1 on ACL-ARC, the least-overlapping domain tested).
  2. Task-adaptive pretraining (TAPT) — continuing to pretrain on just the task's own unlabeled text, vastly smaller and cheaper than DAPT — helps on its own and stacks with DAPT; DAPT+TAPT wins outright on 7 of 8 tasks.
  3. Extra human-curated unlabeled task data is worth more than either alone: on RCT-500, DAPT+Curated-TAPT reaches 95% of the fully-labeled model's performance using only 0.3% of the labeled data — and a cheap automatic kNN-based selection from the domain corpus closes most of that gap at roughly 2,000× less storage than full DAPT.
