The Arcade Learning Environment: An Evaluation Platform for General Agents

A single C++ interface to hundreds of Atari 2600 games, a disjoint train/test split borrowed straight from supervised-learning practice, and three competing ways to normalize scores across incomparable games — the paper that gave reinforcement learning its benchmark platform three years before DQN made that platform famous.

Marc G. Bellemare, Yavar Naddaf, Joel Veness, Michael Bowling

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

By 2012, "general" AI competency had no shared testbed. Competitions existed — General Game Playing, the Reinforcement Learning Competition, the International Planning Competition — but each measured a narrow slice of the problem, and evaluating an algorithm on a handful of hand-picked, parametrized benchmark problems is, in the authors' words, "by definition, flawed": it is prone to method overfitting (Whiteson et al., 2011) and it hides the amount of expert tuning effort a technique needs before it transfers to a new domain. A genuinely general agent needs to be tested on domains that are (i) varied enough to support a claim of generality, (ii) individually interesting enough to resemble real tasks, and (iii) built by an independent party so results aren't shaped by the algorithm designer's own biases.

The Atari 2600 turned out to be an unusually good fit for all three. Over 500 games were released for the console across essentially every genre — shooters, platformers, sports, puzzle, beat-'em-up — each designed by a different, independent studio to challenge a human player, none of them designed with any AI researcher's algorithm in mind. And the hardware itself is tiny by any modern standard: a 1.19 MHz CPU, a 160×210-pixel, 128-colour screen, and 128 bytes of RAM, cheap enough to emulate at up to 6,000 frames per second on 2012 hardware. That combination — hundreds of independently authored, human-scaled challenges running on hardware simple enough to iterate on quickly — is exactly the gap the paper sets out to fill: a single evaluation platform, plus a proposed experimental methodology for using it honestly.

// how it works

The method

The Arcade Learning Environment (ALE) wraps Stella, an open-source Atari 2600 emulator, in a single interface that turns "play this cartridge" into a standard RL problem: it tracks the game's score and detects when the game has ended, and it exposes either a raw 160×210 array of 7-bit-colour pixels or the console's 1,024-bit RAM as the observation, with 18 discrete joystick actions as the action space. It runs at 60 frames per second in real time and up to 6,000 frames per second flat-out, and — crucially for the planning experiments below — it can save and restore full emulator state, letting an agent use the simulator itself as a perfect generative model. All of it, including the benchmark agents described below, was released as open-source software.

The paper's second contribution is a train/test discipline copied deliberately from supervised learning. Five games — Asterix, Beam Rider, Freeway, Seaquest, and Space Invaders — were designated the training set, used to tune every hyperparameter (learning rate, exploration rate, discount factor, feature-abstraction level, search-tree budget). The testing set was built by filtering the 381 Atari 2600 games listed on Wikipedia at the time down to 123 that had their own Wikipedia page, a single-player mode, no adult content, and worked in ALE, then choosing 50 of those at random — so the 55-game evaluation set the paper reports on on is 5 tuned-on games plus 50 the agents had never touched during development. Each evaluation episode ran until the game ended or 18,000 frames (five real-time minutes) elapsed, with the agent choosing a new action every 5 frames (12 Hz); a full RL trial was 5,000 training episodes followed by 500 frozen-policy evaluation episodes, and every number below is averaged across 30 such trials per game.

Two families of benchmark agents demonstrate what ALE makes possible. For reinforcement learning, the authors ran SARSA(λ) with linear function approximation over five different hand-engineered feature representations of the screen or RAM — Basic and BASS (colour-presence grids, with BASS adding pairwise colour-pair features), DISCO (detects object classes and encodes their positions/velocities), LSH (locality-sensitive-hashing random projections of the raw screen), and RAM (the 1,024 memory bits directly, plus pairwise ANDs) — turning "what should an Atari agent see" into an ablation rather than a fixed design choice. For planning, the authors used the emulator's save/restore-state as a perfect forward model for breadth-first tree search and for UCT, the bandit-based Monte Carlo tree search algorithm of Kocsis & Szepesvári (2006), with one implementation optimization: children whose actions lead to identical emulator states (many of the 18 actions are no-ops in a given game) are merged to cut the effective branching factor.

The paper's third contribution — arguably its most durable — is a proposed answer to a problem the RL competitions above hadn't solved: once you have scores on dozens of unrelated games, how do you compare two algorithms with one number? Section 4 defines a normalized score under a chosen score range [r_min, r_max], then proposes three ways to pick that range (normalizing to a random-agent's score, to the range spanned by a pool of 37 simple baseline agents, or to the range spanned by the algorithms actually being compared) and three ways to collapse per-game normalized scores into a single summary (mean, median, or the full quantile / inverse-empirical-CDF curve).

// what they found

The results

On the 55-game evaluation set, learning agents beat the baseline policies (Random, Const, Perturb) on 40 of 55 games — the other 15 include cases like Double Dunk, Journey Escape, and Tennis, where the strongest "baseline" is simply refusing to act, since a passive agent avoids the negative reward an untrained one would rack up. Among the five feature representations, BASS was generally the strongest; DISCO — the one method that explicitly detects and tracks game objects — did reasonably well on the training games but its performance collapsed on the held-out test games, exactly the failure mode the train/test split was designed to expose. The RAM-based agent, despite observing the console's full internal state directly, never outperformed the image-based methods, suggesting the rendered screen carries structure that isn't easily recovered from raw memory bytes alone.

The planning agents make the gap between "learning from experience" and "planning with a perfect model" stark. Given the emulator itself as a generative model, breadth-first search and UCT together beat both the best learning agent and the best baseline on 49 of 55 games; on Asterix, UCT reached 290,700 against the best learner's 987, and on Zaxxon it reached 22,610 against 3,365. The catch is cost: even with the duplicate-action pruning optimization, a single UCT action selection took roughly 15 seconds on the hardware available in 2012 — planning with a perfect model solves far more of the suite than learning does, just nowhere near real time. The four games where search still lost — Freeway, Private Eye, Montezuma's Revenge, and Venture — are exactly the sparse-reward, long-horizon-planning games that would go on to define "hard exploration" as its own subfield of deep RL a few years later.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

Read in 2026, the most striking thing about this paper is how much of "the Atari benchmark" that the field actually uses was never proposed here at all. This 2013 paper's own evaluation set is 55 games (5 tuned-on, 50 held out, chosen at random from Wikipedia's list); the "57-game" subset that became canonical after DQN (2015) is a different, larger list that accreted informally from which games different labs happened to report, not a set this paper defined or endorsed. Citing "the Atari benchmark" without saying which games and which protocol is, ironically, exactly the kind of apples-to-oranges comparison Section 4's normalization machinery was built to prevent.

The scoring convention drifted even further. This paper explicitly considers and sets aside human-normalized scoring — the authors note that humans "often play games without seeking to maximize score" and worry about baking prior knowledge into the reference point — and instead builds its whole apparatus (Section 4) around normalizing to simple baseline agents (Random/Const/Perturb) or to the algorithms under comparison. DQN's 2015 Nature paper introduced human-normalized score (100 × (agent − random) / (human − random)) as the field's default aggregate, and nearly every deep-RL Atari paper since has reported it — reviving exactly the human-reference approach this paper flagged concerns about, without those concerns ever being resolved, just outvoted by convenience.

More consequential still: the ALE described here is a deterministic simulator with a fixed starting state per game. Averaging over 30 trials smooths out the SARSA agents' own stochastic exploration, but it can't detect an agent that has simply memorized one fixed action sequence from one fixed start — which is precisely what early deep-RL agents on this platform turned out to do. The field's patches for that — random no-op starts (up to 30 no-ops before an episode begins), "human starts" (beginning from a human-played state instead of the game's reset state), and eventually "sticky actions" (a small chance any commanded action is dropped and the previous one repeats, introduced by Machado et al.'s 2018 "Revisiting the Arcade Learning Environment") — all arrived years later, as fixes to a determinism problem this paper doesn't mention as a risk at all.

baselines

Random, Const (best fixed action per game), and Perturb (95%-fixed-action with random jitter), plus a single non-expert human's 5-episode average — fair for 2013, but explicitly not the human-normalized convention the field converged on two years later.

contamination

Not applicable in the modern data-leakage sense, but the disjoint 5-train/50-test split this paper proposes is itself an early train/test-contamination safeguard for RL — one that a fair amount of subsequent deep-RL work quietly abandoned by tuning hyperparameters directly on the games it reports.

what's missed

A fully deterministic environment with a fixed start state, unaddressed here, that let later agents rack up high scores by memorizing one trajectory rather than learning a robust policy — the exact gap no-op starts, human starts, and sticky actions were each invented to close.

would raise confidence

Re-running this paper's own released agents under the now-standard sticky-actions + human-starts protocol on the modern 57-game list, and reporting per-game variance rather than single 30-trial averages, would show how much of Table 1's margin over the baselines is real generalization versus an artifact of a friendlier, deterministic 2013-era evaluation.

// bottom line

What outlived this paper wasn't its scoring formula — it was the platform and the discipline underneath it: hundreds of independently authored games behind one interface, and a train/test split that treats "general competency" as something you have to hold out for, not just claim. The field kept that infrastructure for the better part of a decade, discarded this paper's specific normalization scheme in favor of human-normalized scoring, and then discovered — the hard way, through memorized deterministic rollouts — that the platform itself needed a determinism patch nobody had asked for in 2013.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. ALE's lasting contribution is the API — 160×210 7-bit-colour screens or 1,024-bit RAM, 18 actions, 60 fps real-time / 6,000 fps flat-out, plus save/restore-state for planning — sitting on top of hundreds of independently authored Atari 2600 games; that surface, not any particular game list, is what DQN, A3C, Double DQN, Rainbow, and dozens of later papers actually built on.
  2. Its proposed 5-game-train / 50-game-test split, borrowed from supervised-learning discipline, is the paper's real methodological bet — and Table 1's own result (DISCO's strong training-set performance collapsing on held-out games) is a live demonstration of exactly why the split matters.
  3. The field kept ALE and discarded this paper's own scoring apparatus: Section 4's random/baseline/inter-algorithm normalization and mean/median/quantile aggregation gave way to DQN's human-normalized score, and the platform's undiscussed determinism had to be patched years later with no-op starts, human starts, and sticky actions — a reminder that a benchmark's infrastructure can outlive its original proposed metric by over a decade.
