The AI Productivity Index (APEX)

Mercor grades frontier models on real deliverables from four high-paid knowledge jobs — investment banking, management consulting, big law, and primary-care medicine — scored against expert-written rubrics. Even GPT-5 (thinking = high) meets just 67.0% of the criteria, and investment-banking work tops out at just 63.0%.

Bertie Vidgen, Abby Fennelly, Evan Pinnix, … Brendan Foody, Dominic Barton, Cass R. Sunstein, Eric Topol, Osvald Nitski (Mercor · Harvard Law · Scripps Research)

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

Most AI benchmarks are scholastic: multiple-choice exams, math olympiads, contest code — clean to grade, easy to leaderboard, and almost entirely disconnected from the work people actually pay experts to do. They measure whether a model can answer a question, not whether it can produce a deliverable a managing director, a partner, or an attending physician would accept.

APEX targets that gap directly. The authors argue that — with a few exceptions like OpenAI's GDPval and Harvey's BigLaw Bench — benchmarks do not test whether frontier systems can deliver real-world value, the kind that shows up as productivity for a human professional. So they built an evaluation around four well-paid, document-heavy knowledge jobs and asked a blunt question: given the same brief and source files a junior professional would get, how much of an expert's quality bar does a frontier model actually clear?

The four jobs are investment banking associate, management consultant, big law associate, and primary care physician (MD). Each is a role where the output is a discrete, gradeable artifact — a valuation, a market sizing, a contract redline, a clinical plan — and where a wrong number or a missed clause has real consequences. This page covers APEX-v1-extended, the technical report that doubles the held-out set from n = 200 to n = 400 cases and overhauls the grading pipeline.

// how it works

The method

APEX is built by practitioners, not crowdworkers. Mercor sources experts from its talent platform, prioritizing people with both deep domain experience and data-labeling skill; candidates pass a 30–45 minute interview and a paid 1–2 hour assessment that tests whether they can write good prompts and rubrics before they are contracted. For APEX-v1-extended the expert pool grew from 76 to 137 contributors, with a mean of 7+ years of industry experience.

Each case has three parts: a realistic prompt drawn from the expert's day-to-day work, the source documents the model must reason over (PDF, XLSX, TXT, DOCX, CSV — up to 100,000 tokens, parsed with Reducto, nothing paywalled), and a prompt-specific grading rubric. The authors estimate the mean real-world time to actually do one of these tasks at 2.7 hours (ranging from 0.5 to 20). Prompts and rubrics each go through multiple rounds of human review before they enter the set.

Rubrics as "unit tests" for a deliverable

The core idea is to decompose the slippery notion of response "quality" into discrete, testable claims. Each criterion is an objective, specific, self-contained statement about the response, phrased as a descriptive claim that can be marked Pass or Fail — the authors' analogy is unit tests for code. A model's score on a task is simply the fraction of that task's criteria it satisfies. APEX-v1-extended averages 14.81 criteria per case (down from 29.09 in v1.0, after trimming redundant criteria and adding leaner new cases).

score(model, job) =  1|T| Σtask ∈ T  18 Σrun=1..8  criteria metcriteria total // mean fraction of Pass criteria, averaged over 8 sampled runs and all tasks in a job

Grading is done by an LM judge: each criterion is fed to the judge with the response and answered with a JSON {result: 0|1, reason: …}. Because a criterion is a short, self-contained statement, the authors argue it is far easier to judge reliably than an abstract concept like "helpfulness." APEX-v1-extended makes three deliberate changes to the v1.0 pipeline:

  • One judge, not a panel. A single model, Gemini 2.5 Flash, grades every criterion (v1.0 used a panel) — faster, cheaper, and, they argue, more transparent.
  • 8 runs, mean not median. Every prompt is sent to each model 8 times (up from 3), so scores are means with 95% confidence intervals rather than a 3-sample median.
  • Forced single-turn. A standard instruction is appended to every prompt telling the model not to ask follow-up questions and to produce a complete, final deliverable from the brief and attachments alone.

The n = 400 held-out set stays private for clean evaluation; Mercor open-sources a separate apex-v1-devset of n = 100 cases (25 per job, CC-BY) plus the eval harness so others can inspect the format.

// what they found

The results

Ten frontier models from Anthropic, Google DeepMind, OpenAI and xAI were evaluated in late November 2025, with thinking turned on (set to high where configurable) and temperature 0.7. The headline: GPT-5 (thinking = high) leads at 67.0%, followed by Gemini 3 Pro (high) at 64.3% and a tight cluster of o3, Grok 4 and Opus 4.5 around 63%. Even the best model leaves a third of expert criteria unmet — APEX-v1-extended's blunt conclusion is that frontier models still have substantial limitations on everyday professional work.

Scores vary sharply by job. Law is the most tractable (top score 77.9%); investment banking is the hardest, where the best model reaches only 63.0%, with management consulting (64.0%) and medicine (65.5%) in between. Despite the differing difficulty, model rank order is fairly stable across jobs. The differences between models are statistically significant overall (Friedman test, p < 0.000001); on pairwise paired t-tests with a Bonferroni correction, 35 of 45 comparisons (78%) remain significant — leaving 22% of model pairs statistically indistinguishable.

Re-ranking on the hardest tasks

Because tasks vary in difficulty, the authors also report z-scores — how many standard deviations above the field a model is, task by task. Here the spread is starker: GPT-5 scores 0.50 against the next-best 0.28 for Opus 4.5, which jumps from 5th to 2nd — evidence it is unusually strong on the hardest cases — while Opus 4.1 sits at −0.77. A robustness check on the open devset (same 8-run, Gemini-2.5-Flash methodology) reproduces the leaderboard: GPT-5 stays #1 (65.7% vs 67.0%), and ranks barely move, though GPT-5.1, Gemini 2.5 Flash and Sonnet 4.5 each drop more than 10 points between the two sets.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

APEX is one of the more honest attempts to grade frontier models on real professional output: the cases are expert-authored and source-grounded, the rubric is granular and quantitative, and the team reports confidence intervals and an omnibus significance test rather than a bare ranking. The setup is current — ten 2025-vintage frontier models with thinking enabled. But several design choices sit between the numbers and the conclusions, and the benchmark's name promises more than it measures.

The sharpest concern is the judge. A single model, Gemini 2.5 Flash — built by one of the four providers being ranked — grades every criterion for every model, including the three Gemini systems on the board and Gemini 3 Pro, which finishes second. The switch from a panel of judges to one judge was made for speed and "transparency," but it trades away exactly the diversity that guards against provider self-preference, and the report offers no LM-vs-human agreement statistic to show the judge's grades track expert grades on this set. The whole leaderboard rests on an unvalidated, single-vendor grader.

Two structural choices also flatter or distort. The criteria are binary and mean-pooled, so a disqualifying error — a wrong valuation, a missed indemnity clause — costs the same one point as a trivial omission, and no critical-criterion pass rate is reported; a 67%-of-criteria deliverable could still be unusable. And the forced single-turn format penalizes exactly the behavior a good associate would show: investment banking, the hardest job, is where a real analyst would ask scoping questions, but the harness instructs the model never to.

Finally, read the ranking with the error bars on. Run-to-run standard deviation averages 9.05 points — wider than most adjacent-model gaps — and o3, Grok 4 and Opus 4.5 sit within a point of each other with overlapping intervals. GPT-5's lead at the top looks real; positions 3 through 8 are largely within the noise band.

baselines

Strong and current: 10 frontier models across Anthropic, Google, OpenAI and xAI, thinking-high, temp 0.7, 8 runs/task with 95% CIs and a Friedman test (p < 1e-6).

contamination

Only the n=100 devset is public (CC-BY); the scored n=400 set stays private — which limits present-day leakage but also blocks independent auditing. Some models swing >10 pts between the two sets.

what's missed

A single Google judge (Gemini 2.5 Flash) grades Google models; no human-agreement or critical-criterion numbers; binary mean-pooled criteria hide disqualifying errors; single-turn only; "productivity" is never actually measured (the 2.7-hr task time is an expert estimate).

would raise confidence

Validate/diversify judges against human grades, report a critical-criterion pass rate, add a multi-turn setting and an end-to-end "would a professional ship this?" study, and publish the heldout-vs-public gap over time.

// bottom line

A genuinely useful, hard, expert-grounded benchmark with a sobering headline — the best frontier model clears only two-thirds of expert criteria, and banking work tops out at just 63.0%. But "Productivity Index" is a generous label for what is, mechanically, a single-turn, single-judge, rubric-pass-rate benchmark. Trust GPT-5-on-top; treat most of the rest of the ranking as a near-tie.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. On realistic deliverables from four high-paid knowledge jobs, the best frontier model (GPT-5, thinking-high) meets only 67.0% of expert rubric criteria, and investment banking — the hardest job — tops out at 63.0%. Frontier models are strong drafting tools, not autonomous professionals.
  2. The contribution is the construction: 137 vetted practitioners (7+ yrs experience) author source-grounded cases worth ~2.7 hours of real work each, then decompose "quality" into ~15 Pass/Fail criteria — "unit tests" for a deliverable — graded by an LM judge across 8 runs with 95% CIs.
  3. Read the leaderboard with its error bars: a 9-point run-to-run SD makes positions 3–8 a near-tie, and the soft spots are a single Google judge grading Google models and a private scored set. The name promises productivity; the measurement is rubric pass-rate on single-turn tasks.
