BioGPT: Generative Pre-trained Transformer for Biomedical Text Generation and Mining

A 347M-parameter GPT-2-style decoder, pretrained from scratch on 15M PubMed abstracts with its own domain vocabulary, beats BERT-family biomedical encoders on relation extraction and question answering — new state-of-the-art on four of six evaluated tasks. Its one uniquely generative capability, free-text generation, ships with no quantitative eval at all — and the paper's own demo output includes a confidently wrong COVID-19 drug claim.

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

By 2022, biomedical NLP had a lopsided toolkit. The BERT branch of pre-training had been thoroughly adapted to the domain — BioBERT continued-pretrains general BERT on PubMed text, PubMedBERT goes further and trains from scratch on PubMed with its own vocabulary — and both post strong results on discriminative tasks like named-entity recognition and classification. But BERT's bidirectional masked-language-modeling objective makes it fundamentally an encoder: it is not built to generate text, which rules it out for literature summarization, free-form answer generation, or reformulating extraction as generation.

Meanwhile GPT-style decoders had shown remarkable generative range in the general domain — GPT-2, then GPT-3 with in-context few-shot prompting — but transplanting that success into biomedicine had already been tried and had failed twice. Moradi et al. found GPT-3 to be a poor few-shot learner on biomedical tasks, and Gutiérrez et al.'s "Thinking about GPT-3 In-Context Learning for Biomedical IE? Think Again" reached the same conclusion for information extraction — plain prompting of a general-domain GPT does not transfer. The one earlier attempt to actually pretrain a GPT on biomedical text, DARE, used only 0.5M PubMed abstracts and solely for relation-extraction data augmentation, not as a general-purpose domain model. So going into this paper there was no generative biomedical foundation model built the way PubMedBERT had shown mattered for encoders: pretrained from scratch, on enough in-domain text, with a domain-native vocabulary.

// how it works

The method

BioGPT applies the PubMedBERT recipe to a GPT-2 architecture instead of BERT's. The backbone is a standard Transformer decoder trained with the ordinary next-token language-modeling objective — nothing architecturally new — but it is pretrained entirely from scratch on 15M PubMed abstracts (every PubMed item updated before 2021 that has both a title and an abstract), rather than continuing pretraining from general-domain GPT-2 weights. Just as importantly, BioGPT learns its own byte-pair-encoding vocabulary (42,384 tokens) from that corpus instead of reusing GPT-2's general-domain vocabulary, on the same logic PubMedBERT used for BERT: a domain-native tokenizer avoids wasting subword capacity on the wrong distribution of terms. The backbone follows GPT-2medium's configuration — 24 layers, 1024 hidden size, 16 attention heads — landing at 347M parameters (versus GPT-2medium's 355M; the gap is purely the different vocabulary-driven embedding and output-projection sizes). Pretraining runs 200k steps on 8×V100 GPUs with an effective batch of 524,288 tokens (1024 tokens/GPU × 8 GPUs × 64 gradient-accumulation steps), Adam with a peak learning rate of 2×10⁻⁴ and 20k warm-up steps, then inverse-square-root decay.

The harder design problem is using one generative model across four structurally different downstream tasks — end-to-end relation extraction (BC5CDR, KD-DTI, DDI), question answering (PubMedQA), document classification (HoC), and open-ended text generation. Rather than encode labels with special structured tokens the way prior sequence-to-sequence extractors do (REBEL's target format is literally <triplet> head <subj> tail <obj> relation), BioGPT reformats every label as a natural-language sentence the decoder can generate smoothly: a relational triplet ⟨dextropropoxyphene, mu-opioid receptor, inhibitor⟩ becomes "the relation between dextropropoxyphene and mu-type opioid receptor is inhibitor" (their best-performing "rel-is" template, one of three natural-language forms they compare); a QA example becomes "the answer to the question given the context is yes"; a classification label becomes "the type of this document is genomic instability and mutation."

To fine-tune without confusing the model about what to generate, BioGPT borrows the idea of continuous soft-prompt embeddings from prefix-tuning, but changes where they sit: instead of prepending virtual tokens at the very start of the input (prefix-tuning's convention), BioGPT inserts them between the source and the target, giving the final training sequence [source; prompt; target]. At inference, the source text plus the learned prompt is fed as the prefix and the decoder generates the target autoregressively — the same mechanism used for both fine-tuning and inference, shown schematically below.

The results

BioGPT and a general-domain GPT-2medium control are fine-tuned and evaluated on six tasks across five datasets, each against the strongest published baselines available at the time:

On BC5CDR, BioGPT's 44.98% F1 (49.44 precision / 41.28 recall) beats the strongest end-to-end competitor, REBEL, by 8.28 points, and even beats seq2rel — which is trained on train+validation data, unlike BioGPT — by 4.78 points once BioGPT is given the same train+val data (46.17% F1). Pipeline-based GLRE collapses from 23.99% F1 with ground-truth NER down to 8.05% when forced to use an open-source NER tool instead, underscoring the appeal of BioGPT's end-to-end formulation. On KD-DTI, BioGPT (38.42% F1) beats the task's own domain-specific baseline, Transformer+PubMedBERT-attn (24.19%), by 14.23 points, and still edges out REBELpt (33.32%) — a REBEL variant further pretrained on a large Wikipedia relation-extraction corpus BioGPT never sees — by 5.1 points. On DDI, BioGPT (40.76%) essentially ties REBELpt (40.56%), a 0.2-point margin that is the paper's thinnest win. PubMedQA is the standout: 78.2% accuracy against a prior best of 72.2% from BioLinkBERTlarge, a 6.0-point jump the authors call "a new record." HoC's gain is the smallest of the five headline results (85.12% vs. BioLinkBERTbase's 84.35%, +0.77). Altogether the paper reports state-of-the-art on four of the six evaluated task settings (BC5CDR, KD-DTI, and DDI relation extraction, plus PubMedQA).

A larger BioGPT-Large (1.5B parameters, GPT-2 XL configuration) doesn't uniformly help: it improves BC5CDR to 50.12% F1, but essentially matches (KD-DTI: 38.39% vs. 38.42%) or slightly regresses (HoC: 84.40% vs. 85.12%) the base model on the others — scaling the decoder alone is not a free lunch on these fine-tuning tasks. Two ablations back up the design choices: reformatting labels as natural-language sentences beats the structured special-token format used by prior seq2seq extractors on both BC5CDR (44.98 vs. 42.85 F1) and DDI (40.76 vs. 38.60 F1); and continuous soft prompts generally — though not dramatically — outperform hand-written hard prompts (best soft prompt 38.60% F1 at length 13 vs. best hard prompt "we can conclude that" at 38.16% F1, on KD-DTI).

The paper's other headline capability, free-text generation, is demonstrated only qualitatively: given drug/target-name prefixes (e.g. Bicalutamide, JAK-3, Psoralen, CP-673451) and topical prefixes (COVID-19, SARS-CoV-2, Omicron), BioGPT produces fluent, mechanistically specific biomedical continuations where general-domain GPT-2 drifts off-topic — treating "Psoralen" as a city name, degenerating into a number sequence for "CP-673451," or citing an unrelated "War on Terror" essay for "BIIB-021." No quantitative or human evaluation of generation quality or factual accuracy is reported for this comparison.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

The structured-task results are genuinely solid: the BERT-family baselines (BioBERT 2019, PubMedBERT 2020, BioELECTRa 2021, BioLinkBERT 2022) were current as of the paper's 2022 writing, and the relation-extraction comparisons against REBEL and REBELpt (themselves recent, strong sequence-to-sequence extractors) are a fair, non-cherry-picked fight. But two structural gaps sit underneath the headline numbers, and one of the paper's own qualitative examples is a live demonstration of the second.

First, the relation-extraction baselines (REBEL, GLRE, seq2rel) are all general-domain models with no biomedical pretraining, while BioGPT gets both a decoder-only generative architecture and in-domain pretraining as advantages simultaneously. Without a domain-pretrained encoder-decoder control (e.g. a BART or T5 continued-pretrained on PubMed), it's impossible to attribute how much of BioGPT's 5–14 point relation-extraction gains come from "generative decoding done right" versus simply "biomedical pretraining," which PubMedBERT already established works for a completely different architecture family.

Second — and more consequential — is the total absence of a factuality check on generation, the one capability a decoder uniquely offers over BERT-style encoders. The evaluation is a handful of author-selected before/after snippets, with no expert review, no fact-checking, and no held-out benchmark. That gap is not hypothetical: the paper's own COVID-19 demo has BioGPT state that, "as of March 11, 2020," there were "more than 800,000 confirmed cases and more than 800,000 deaths" — figures roughly two orders of magnitude too high for that date — and, more seriously, has BioGPT assert that hydroxychloroquine "has been approved by the US Food and Drug Administration (FDA) for the treatment of patients with COVID-19 in March 2020." The FDA only ever granted (and later revoked, in June 2020) a narrow emergency-use authorization, and hydroxychloroquine was never shown effective against COVID-19 in controlled trials. A biomedical text-generation model producing a confident, specific, wrong drug-efficacy claim — in the paper's own showcase examples, no less — is precisely the failure mode that matters most for a tool billed for "biomedical text generation and mining," and the paper does not measure or even acknowledge it.

baselines

Fair and current for the BERT-family comparisons (BioBERT, PubMedBERT, BioLinkBERT, BioELECTRa). But relation-extraction baselines (REBEL, GLRE, seq2rel) are all general-domain — no domain-pretrained seq2seq control exists to separate "generative decoding" from "biomedical pretraining" as the source of the gain.

contamination

No de-duplication or overlap check is reported between the 15M-abstract pretraining corpus (all PubMed before 2021) and the fine-tuning benchmarks' test documents — several of which (BC5CDR, PubMedQA, HoC) are themselves drawn from pre-2021 PubMed abstracts BioGPT may have already seen verbatim during pretraining.

what's missed

The generation capability — the paper's real differentiator from BERT-style models — has zero quantitative evaluation. The showcased examples include a confidently wrong hydroxychloroquine efficacy/approval claim and inflated COVID-19 case/death figures, undercutting the "biomedical text generation" framing rather than supporting it.

would raise confidence

A held-out factuality/hallucination benchmark for generation (ideally with clinician review), an explicit pretraining/test-split overlap audit, a domain-pretrained encoder-decoder control for relation extraction, and multiple-seed variance given some test splits are small (DDI's test set is 191 files; BC5CDR's is 500 documents).

// bottom line

BioGPT is a clean, well-executed proof that "from-scratch pretraining + domain vocabulary" — the recipe PubMedBERT validated for BERT — also works for GPT-style decoders, closing the gap that naive GPT-3 prompting had shown on biomedical extraction tasks in prior work, and doing so at a fraction of GPT-3's scale (347M vs. 175B parameters). The structured-task numbers earn their state-of-the-art claims. But the one place a generative model should distinguish itself — fluent, useful biomedical text generation — is evaluated with anecdotes rather than a benchmark, and the anecdotes themselves contain a textbook example of confident medical misinformation. Treat the relation-extraction and QA results as trustworthy; treat any claim about BioGPT's generation quality or factual reliability as unevaluated.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. Pretraining a GPT-2-style decoder from scratch on 15M PubMed abstracts with its own domain vocabulary — the same recipe PubMedBERT used for BERT — closes the gap that naive GPT-3 prompting had shown on biomedical extraction in prior work (Moradi et al., Gutiérrez et al.), at 347M parameters versus GPT-3's 175B.
  2. Reformatting every downstream task's labels as natural-language target sentences, rather than the special-token structured formats prior seq2seq extractors use, is what lets one decoder handle relation extraction, QA, and classification — and it measurably beats the structured format on F1 (44.98 vs. 42.85 on BC5CDR; 40.76 vs. 38.60 on DDI).
  3. New state-of-the-art on four of six evaluated tasks (BC5CDR 44.98% F1, KD-DTI 38.42% F1, DDI 40.76% F1, PubMedQA 78.2% accuracy) — but the generation capability that most differentiates BioGPT from BERT-style models is validated only anecdotally, and the anecdotes include the model confidently asserting a debunked hydroxychloroquine/COVID-19 claim.
