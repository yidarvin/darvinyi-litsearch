TAT-QA: A Question Answering Benchmark on a Hybrid of Tabular and Textual Content in Finance

Real financial-report tables never sit alone — they're wrapped in the prose that explains them. TAT-QA forces QA models to read both together and do the arithmetic, with the unit (thousand, million, billion, percent) scored as part of the answer. The paper's own model clears only about 64% of the human ceiling (58.0 vs. 90.8 F1), and most of the gap turns out to be about finding the right numbers, not doing the math with them.

Fengbin Zhu, Wenqiang Lei, Youcheng Huang, Chao Wang, Shuo Zhang, Jiancheng Lv, Fuli Feng, Tat-Seng Chua · National University of Singapore · 6Estates · Sichuan University · Bloomberg

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

By 2021, QA research had matured along three separate tracks: free text (SQuAD, CNN/DailyMail, HotpotQA), structured knowledge bases, and semi-structured tables (WikiTableQuestions, Spider). A handful of papers had started to combine table and text — most notably HybridQA, which links Wikipedia table cells to their linked wiki articles via hyperlinks — but the authors argue that connection is loose: a hyperlink, not a genuine semantic or numerical dependency between the two modalities.

Financial reports are a much tighter case of hybrid data: a table of dollar figures is directly explained, cross-referenced, and sometimes only made unambiguous by the paragraphs around it. The paper's own example is exact — a table lists "commercial cloud" nowhere as a line item, but the surrounding text states "our commercial cloud revenue … was $38.1 billion, $26.6 billion and $16.2 billion in fiscal years 2019, 2018, and 2017", which is the only way to answer "How much does the commercial cloud revenue account for the total revenue in 2019?" — you need the $38.1B from the text and the $125,843M total from the table, then divide, then get the scale (million vs. billion) right on both sides before you can even do the division.

No existing dataset stressed this combination: comprehensive table–text interdependence, real (not synthetic) financial-report language, and numerical reasoning where the correct scale of the answer is as easy to get wrong as the number itself. TAT-QA (Tabular And Textual dataset for Question Answering) is built to close exactly that gap.

// how it works

The dataset, and the baseline model built to test it

The authors downloaded roughly 500 financial reports released in the prior two years from annualreports.com, ran a table-detection model over them, and extracted table contents with Apache PDFBox. After keeping only tables with 3–30 rows and 3–6 columns, about 20,000 candidate tables remained. Annotators — about 30 university students with finance backgrounds, screened with a qualifying test and required to score ≥95% — then linked each candidate table to at least two surrounding paragraphs that describe, analyze, or complement it; tables that couldn't be linked this way were discarded outright (roughly two-thirds of candidates). Every submitted annotation went through a two-round check-then-approve pass by five separate verifiers.

For each valid hybrid context, annotators wrote at least six question–answer pairs "useful in real-world financial analyses," using common words rather than the table's own vocabulary. Answers come in three flavors — a single span, multiple spans, or a generated numeric/string answer — and for generated answers, annotators also recorded the derivation (an arithmetic expression or a set of counted items) and the answer's source (table, text, or both). The final release: 16,552 questions over 2,757 hybrid contexts drawn from 182 reports, split 80/10/10 by hybrid context (so no context's questions leak across splits) into train (13,215 Q, 2,201 contexts), dev (1,668 Q, 278 contexts), and test (1,669 Q, 278 contexts).

The questions cover eight reasoning types, and — unusually for a benchmark paper — the percentages are reported over the whole dataset, not just the illustrative example: Word Matching 38.06%, Composition 19.69%, Subtraction 16.17%, Set of spans 11.94%, Division 3.84%, Comparison 5.65%, Addition 2.37%, and Counting 2.28%.

Alongside the dataset, the authors propose TAGOP, a baseline model that reasons over both modalities in two stages. First, sequence tagging: the question, the table flattened row-by-row, and the associated paragraphs are fed together into a RoBERTa encoder, and every sub-token is tagged Inside/Outside via a per-token classifier — table cells and text spans whose sub-tokens get tagged "I" become the model's supporting evidence. Second, symbolic reasoning: an operator classifier reading the [CLS] vector picks one of ten aggregation operators (span-in-text, cell-in-table, spans, sum, count, average, multiplication, division, difference, change ratio) to combine the extracted evidence into a final value; for order-sensitive operators (difference, division, change ratio) a second classifier decides which of the top-two evidence values comes first. A fourth classifier, conditioned on the pooled table and paragraph representations, separately predicts the answer's scale (none, thousand, million, billion, percent) — the model's explicit answer to "how big a number is this, really?" All four classifiers train jointly, with the total loss the sum of four negative-log-likelihood terms (tagging, operator, scale, order).

// what they found

The results

TAGOP beats every adapted baseline by a wide margin. Against three families of prior QA models — a SQuAD-style reader (BERT-RC), a numerical-reasoning reader built for DROP (NumNet+V2), a tabular-QA model (TaPas for WikiTableQuestions), and a hybrid-QA model (HyBrider, repurposed from Wikipedia table-text linking) — TAGOP reaches 50.1 EM / 58.0 F1 on the test set, an 11.1-point absolute F1 jump over the next best (NumNet+V2, 37.0/46.9). HyBrider, designed for looser hyperlink-style table-text connections, is the weakest at 6.3/7.5, underscoring how much TAT-QA's tighter dependency structure breaks approaches built for HybridQA.

But every model — including TAGOP — remains far below the humans the authors sampled: on 50 test hybrid contexts (301 questions), human annotators scored 90.8 F1 (84.1 EM). TAGOP's 58.0 F1 leaves a 32.8-point gap to that ceiling. Breaking TAGOP's test performance down by answer type, it does best on multi-span answers (60.0 EM / 75.1 F1) and worst on arithmetic answers (42.5/42.5, where EM and F1 coincide because a generated number is either exactly right or not); by source, it does much better when the answer comes purely from text (43.3 EM / 68.7 F1) than purely from the table (47.8/49.3), and best when it has to fuse both (58.3/62.2).

An operator-ablation study (adding operators back one at a time to a span-only model) shows every operator helps on net, but two barely register: Sum and Multiplication add "little gain or even decline," which the authors attribute to how rarely those operators occur in the test set — a reminder that TAGOP's ten-operator design is being validated on very uneven per-operator sample sizes (Appendix Table 8 shows Multiplication is only 0.1–0.2% of questions, and the operator classifier scores 0% accuracy on it in the test set). A manual error analysis of 100 sampled TAGOP failures on the test set attributes 55% to wrong evidence and 29% to missing evidence — together 84% of all errors — versus only 9% wrong calculation, 4% unsupported calculation, and 3% scale error. In other words: TAGOP's ceiling is set almost entirely by whether it can find the right cells and spans, not by whether it can then do the arithmetic correctly.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

TAT-QA's construction pipeline is unusually well-documented for a 2021 benchmark — table-detection provenance, a two-round human verification process, and a released derivation label for every generated answer, none of which is typical even today. But the paper's own numbers point to two soft spots that matter for anyone using TAT-QA now, four-plus years and several LLM generations later.

First, the metric is stricter than it looks. The numeracy-focused F1 (adapted from DROP) is set to zero "unless the predicted number multiplied by predicted scale equals exactly the ground truth" — there is no partial credit for a correct derivation with a wrong final digit, and no credit at all for getting the number right but the scale wrong. The paper's own scale-error rate (3% of sampled failures) suggests this isn't the dominant failure mode for TAGOP specifically, but it does mean the headline F1 conflates two very different skills — arithmetic and unit tracking — into one binary pass/fail per question.

Second, the human ceiling that every later paper quotes (90.8 F1) rests on a genuinely small sample: 50 hybrid contexts, 301 questions, out of a 1,669-question test set — under a fifth of it, with no reported inter-annotator agreement statistic beyond the qualifying-test threshold used to hire annotators in the first place. It's a reasonable estimate, but it's being cited today with a precision the underlying sample size doesn't really support.

baselines

Fair for 2021 — BERT-RC, NumNet+V2, TaPas, and HyBrider were genuinely the closest available systems for textual, tabular, and hybrid QA at the time, each adapted with the same added scale classifier used by TAGOP. But all four predate the LLM era; the paper has no zero-/few-shot large-model comparison, so it says nothing about how a modern general-purpose model would do without task-specific fine-tuning.

contamination

Not a concern for TAGOP or the four 2021 baselines, all fine-tuned specifically on TAT-QA's training split. It is a live concern for anyone reporting an LLM's TAT-QA score today: the dataset and its GitHub repo have been public since mid-2021 and are now widely cited (560+ citations, including later financial-LLM papers like FinBen and FinanceBench that benchmark against it), making pretraining-corpus leakage plausible for any model trained after ~2022 — the paper itself predates this risk and so never addresses it.

what's missed

The metric scores final answers only; the labeled derivations and evidence sources are never used at evaluation time, so a model's published F1 can't distinguish "extracted the wrong cell" from "extracted the right cell, then miscalculated" — exactly the distinction the paper's own 55%/9% error breakdown shows matters most. Domain-knowledge questions (e.g., needing to know "gross profit margin = gross profit / revenue") are flagged by the authors as a source of difficulty but never separately quantified.

would raise confidence

A larger, IRR-reported human-agreement study rather than a 301-question spot check; a decontamination check (or a held-out post-2023 report slice) for anyone scoring modern LLMs on it; and either partial-credit scoring or a separate evidence-retrieval metric so "found the right numbers" and "computed correctly" aren't collapsed into one F1.

// bottom line

TAT-QA is a well-built, still-relevant stress test for grounding numerical answers in real hybrid financial documents — and its own error analysis is honest about what it's actually testing: evidence retrieval far more than arithmetic. Treat the 90.8 human ceiling as an estimate, not a precise target, and treat any LLM's TAT-QA score reported without a contamination check with proportionate skepticism.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. TAT-QA pairs 2,757 real financial-report tables with their surrounding paragraphs and 16,552 questions spanning eight reasoning types, from simple word matching (38.06%) to multi-step composition (19.69%) — and scores the answer's magnitude (thousand/million/billion/percent) as part of correctness, not as an afterthought.
  2. The paper's own TAGOP model reaches 58.0 F1 against a 90.8 human estimate — a 32.8-point gap — and its own error analysis attributes 84% of failures to wrong or missing evidence extraction, not faulty arithmetic. The benchmark is, in practice, mostly testing retrieval-and-grounding, not math.
  3. TAT-QA has outlived its original 2021 baselines: later financial-LLM benchmark papers like FinBen and FinanceBench still cite and benchmark against it, even as its small human-ceiling sample (301 questions) and lack of any contamination safeguard mean scores from post-2022 models should be read with real caution.
