Beyond Seeing: Evaluating Multimodal LLMs on Tool-Enabled Image Perception, Transformation, and Reasoning

A benchmark for the think-with-images paradigm — where a model must actively crop, edit, and enhance an image with tools, not just stare at it. 1,204 expert-authored tasks, 7,777 weighted rubrics, and a frontier that barely clears 18.68%.

// the gap

Vision as a workspace, not a backdrop

Almost every multimodal benchmark treats the image as a fixed slab of context: the model looks once, then reasons in text. The paper calls this thinking about images. But real users hand over photographs that are rotated, underexposed, cluttered, or poorly framed, where the deciding detail is a price in one corner of a menu or a serial number too small to read. Solving those tasks means thinking with images — cropping a region of interest, brightening a shadow, zooming, then folding the result back into a multi-step reasoning chain, often alongside a calculator or a web search.

This is the capability MLLMs are increasingly deployed into and the one no benchmark squarely measured. Static visual-QA suites (ScienceQA, MathVista, MMMU, ChartQA) never let the model touch the image; tool-use benchmarks that do (GTA, m&m's) restrict it to trivial crops without rubric grading; multi-turn dialogue sets (MMDU) skip genuine tool integration. In the paper's own Table 1, VisualToolBench is the only entry that checks every box at once: dynamic vision tools, rubric-based scoring, expert curation, reasoning, and multi-turn interaction.

// the reframe

Equipping a model with an image-editing tool turns the picture from passive perception into an interactive scratchpad. The question VisualToolBench asks is not "can the model see?" but "when seeing isn't enough, will it act on the image — and does that actually help?"

// how it works

1,204 tasks, six tools, a rubric for everything

VisualToolBench holds 1,204 open-ended tasks — 603 single-turn and 601 multi-turn — balanced across five domains (STEM, Medical, Finance, Sports, Generalist, each ≈20%), built on 2,893 images and graded against 7,777 weighted rubric criteria. Five task categories probe distinct skills: Region-Switch Q&A (stitch evidence from spatially scattered regions of one image) and Hybrid Tool Reasoning (combine vision tools with a calculator / interpreter / search) on the single-turn side; Follow-up Test (ask clarifying questions on a deliberately under-specified prompt), Temporal Visual Reasoning (track change across a sequence of images), and Progressive Visual Reasoning (chained, interdependent questions about one image) on the multi-turn side.

The toolset, and a perception trick

Models call a standardized six-tool API: python_image_processing, python_interpreter, web_search, calculator, browser_get_page_text, and historical_weather. The image tool is the cornerstone — arbitrary crop / edit / brightness-contrast manipulation, used as an iterative scratchpad. Crucially, tasks never name the tool to use; the model must infer when manipulation is needed. One implementation detail matters: dropping an encoded image into a tool-result message does not make it visible to the model, so after a vision tool runs the harness injects an extra user message carrying the transformed image, preserving genuine think-with-images. Models get a cap of 20 tool calls per task; human reference solutions usually need fewer than five.

How tasks earn their place

Every task is authored by a domain-expert contributor who supplies a prompt, image(s), a golden answer, a reference tool-use chain, and the rubrics. Quality control is a multi-stage gauntlet: the contributor grades three reference models (o3, Gemini-2.5-pro, o4-mini), and a task is admitted only if at least two of the three fail — guaranteeing it captures genuinely hard cases — then survives two independent rounds of review for realism, answer correctness, and necessity of dynamic image reasoning.

Rubric grading: partial credit and a hard gate

Each rubric criterion falls in one of five families — Visual Understanding, Truthfulness, Instruction Following, Reasoning, Presentation — and carries a contributor-assigned weight w ∈ {1..5}. An LLM judge (o4-mini, grading one criterion at a time, returning "Met"/"Not Met") scores each item. Two metrics fall out. Average Rubric Score (ARS) is the weighted fraction of satisfied criteria — partial credit:

ARS = Σi wi · siΣi wi // si ∈ {0,1} is whether the response satisfies rubric i (weight wi)

Average Pass Rate (APR) is stricter and binary: any criterion with w ≥ 4 is a critical rubric (typically truthfulness and key visual understanding), and a response passes a task only if it satisfies every critical rubric. APR is the headline number, and the gating is what makes it brutal.

// what they found

The frontier barely registers

Across 16 function-calling MLLMs, VisualToolBench is punishing. The best model, GPT-5-think, passes just 18.68% of tasks; GPT-5 reaches 16.96%, o3 13.74%, Gemini-2.5-pro 11.75%, and o4-mini 11.12%. Eleven of sixteen models score below 10% APR, and not one clears 20%. The open-source Llama-4 models sit near the floor (1.41% / 1.58%), and the Claude 4 family lands in the 4–6% band even with thinking enabled. Partial credit softens the picture but not by much — even the top ARS is only 0.4712 (GPT-5-think), meaning the best model satisfies under half the weighted rubric mass and almost never nails the full critical set. Multi-turn tasks are consistently harder than single-turn, as compounding turns multiply the chances to slip.

Tool use is the dividing line

The clearest signal is behavioral. APR rises with both tool-use proactivity (does the model invoke any tool?) and tool-call volume — OpenAI models cluster in the upper-right of both plots, while Claude, Gemini, Llama, and Nova stay low-tool and low-score. And the vision tool dominates: across the top three models, more than half of all tool calls are image manipulations — a striking 92.0% for GPT-5, 54.2% for o3, 55.0% for Gemini-2.5-pro. GPT-5 also packs multiple operations into a single vision-tool call, getting more perception per invocation than o3's higher raw count.

But tools aren't free — and the failures are perceptual

An ablation exposes divergent training. Stripping tools or weakening the system prompt costs GPT-5 roughly 11–14% APR (−11.4% weak prompt, −11.8% no vision tool, −14.4% no tools) — clear evidence it leans on iterative editing to offset weaker raw perception. Gemini-2.5-pro does the opposite: it scores +2.7% without tools, its stronger native vision actively hurt by unnecessary tool calls it was less trained to orchestrate. And the error analysis settles where the wall is: across GPT-5, Gemini-2.5-pro, and Claude-opus-4.1, visual-perception errors are 72–82% of all failures (71.73% / 78.01% / 82.11%), while calculation mistakes are rare (1.84–5.74%). Models mostly fail because they can't extract the right pixels — not because they can't reason or compute.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

This is a well-built, current benchmark — 16 frontier models including GPT-5, Claude Sonnet 4.5, and Gemini 2.5, original expert-authored images, and a clever re-ingestion fix that makes think-with-images actually testable. But the headline framing ("even the best clears only 18.68%") needs two asterisks, both stemming from how tasks were selected and graded.

First, the floor is partly engineered. A task is admitted only if at least two of {o3, Gemini-2.5-pro, o4-mini} already fail it, so the naturally-easy half of visual tool-use is filtered out by construction. The 18.68% therefore measures the residual hard tail, not "models solve 18% of real visual tasks." To the benchmark's credit, GPT-5 and GPT-5-think were not in the filter set yet still top the board — so the difficulty isn't merely overfit to the leader — but the absolute number is a selection artifact and shouldn't be read as an unconditional capability estimate.

Second, the judge is entangled with the benchmark. o4-mini is simultaneously a filter model, one of the 16 graded models (11.12% APR), and the grader for everyone. Single-rubric grading and a weak judge limit how much it can flatter itself, and the paper validates alignment (Table 8: o4-mini 0.8807 overall vs human labels). But that alignment is 0.9170 on objective rubrics and only 0.7396 on subjective ones — so the APR gate (critical = truthfulness / visual understanding, mostly objective) is probably trustworthy, while the ARS partial-credit numbers lean hardest on the judgments the judge is worst at.

baselines

Strong and current — 16 models spanning OpenAI, Anthropic, Google, Meta, Amazon, open and closed. The glaring omission is a human pass rate on the final set: reference trajectories exist, but no human APR is reported, so "models fail 80%+" has no human ceiling to sit against.

contamination

Low risk by design — original contributor images plus the ≥2/3-fail filter double as a leakage control, and the think-with-images structure resists memorization. The live web_search tool is the one open channel, but it's intrinsic to the tasks rather than a leak.

what's missed

APR collapses everything below "all critical rubrics" to a hard fail: a response that perceives perfectly but flubs one critical number scores the same zero as a blind guess. ARS (best 0.47) tells a far less catastrophic story, so the 18.68% understates partial competence. Only two trials, averaged, with no variance or CI; and the 20-call cap may itself bound scores.

would raise confidence

A human pass rate on the final benchmark; a cross-family judge panel for ARS (rather than o4-mini grading o4-mini); reported APR variance across trials; and an ablation showing how scores move on unfiltered tasks, to separate true difficulty from the selection filter.

// bottom line

The robust, defensible finding isn't the exact 18.68% — it's the shape of the failure: 72–82% of errors are perceptual, tool use is a trained skill that helps some families and hurts others, and frontier MLLMs still can't reliably turn an image into a workspace. Read APR as a difficulty-calibrated ranking, not an absolute capability gauge, and the paper holds up well.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. The first think-with-images benchmark. 1,204 expert tasks where the answer lives in pixels you have to actively crop, edit, or enhance — and the best frontier model (GPT-5-think) passes only 18.68%, with 11 of 16 models under 10%.
  2. The wall is perception, not reasoning. 72–82% of failures are visual-perception errors; calculation mistakes are under 6%. Even handed a cropping tool, models can't reliably extract the detail that matters.
  3. Tool use is a learned, family-specific skill. GPT-5 gains 11–14% from tools and routes 92% of its calls to image manipulation; Gemini-2.5-pro does better without tools. Proactive, multi-operation tool use — not raw vision — separates the leaders.
