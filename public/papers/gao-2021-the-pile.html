The Pile: An 800GB Dataset of Diverse Text for Language Modeling

EleutherAI's 825 GiB open corpus, stitched from 22 curated domains — the academic, legal, code, and dialogue text that pure web-crawl pretraining leaves out — built both to train large language models and to stress-test them.

Leo Gao, Stella Biderman, Sid Black, Laurence Golding, Travis Hoppe, Charles Foster, Jason Phang, Horace He, Anish Thite, Noa Nabeshima, Shawn Presser, Connor Leahy · EleutherAI

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

By late 2020 the recipe for a strong language model was settled: take a transformer, make it bigger, and feed it more text. The catch was the text. As models scaled, the demand for tokens outran any single clean source, so almost every large model — GPT-3, T5, Megatron — leaned on Common Crawl, a raw scrape of the open web, for the bulk of its training data. Common Crawl is huge and free, but it is also messy, repetitive, and skewed toward the kind of prose that dominates the open web: news, blogs, forums, marketing copy.

The authors' bet is that this monoculture is a ceiling. Prior work had shown that dataset diversity drives downstream generalization, and that a model can absorb a brand-new domain from a relatively small amount of in-domain text. If that's true, then a corpus assembled from many smaller, high-quality, mutually distinct sources should teach a model more than the same number of bytes of filtered web text — and should expose, by domain, exactly where a web-trained model is weak.

Two concrete gaps follow. First, there was no large, openly available, diverse training corpus: GPT-3's mixture was proprietary, and the public alternatives were either pure Common Crawl (CC-100, raw CC) or single-domain. Second — and the paper makes a point of this — no corpus built to train massive LMs had ever been seriously documented by its own creators. Web-scale training sets were shipped as opaque blobs, with their biases, toxicity, licensing, and topical coverage left for downstream users to discover the hard way. The Pile is an attempt to fix both at once: a curated, reproducible 800GB+ corpus, released with an unusually frank accounting of what is inside it.

// how it works

The method

The Pile is 825.18 GiB of English text combining 22 sub-datasets, 14 of which the authors built from scratch for this project. The new sources skew deliberately away from the open web: full-text biomedical papers (PubMed Central), arXiv in raw LaTeX, GitHub code, US court opinions (FreeLaw), Stack Exchange Q&A, patent backgrounds (USPTO), Ubuntu IRC logs, HackerNews, PhilPapers, YouTube subtitles, and NIH grant abstracts. To these they add curated existing sets — Books3, Project Gutenberg (PG-19), OpenSubtitles, English Wikipedia, DeepMind Mathematics, EuroParl, Enron Emails — plus their own improved web scrape, Pile-CC, extracted from raw Web Archive HTML with jusText rather than the lossy pre-extracted WET files, and OpenWebText2, a Reddit-upvote-filtered web set.

Crucially, the sub-datasets are not mixed in proportion to their raw size. Following GPT-3's practice, the authors up-weight components they judge high-quality by assigning each an epoch count — the number of times it is seen per pass over the Pile. Wikipedia is seen up to 3×, PG-19 2.5×, several academic sets 2×, while large-but-noisy GitHub and Pile-CC are seen 1×. The result is a 825 GiB raw corpus whose effective size during training is 1,254.20 GiB. By weight, the final mixture is led by Pile-CC (18.11%), PubMed Central (14.40%), Books3 (12.07%), OpenWebText2 (10.01%), and ArXiv (8.96%) — i.e. roughly a third academic, a third web, and a third books/code/dialogue/misc.

Measuring models in bits per byte

Because the Pile spans tokenizers and scripts, the authors evaluate with bits per UTF-8 byte (BPB) rather than perplexity, which is sensitive to tokenization. From a model's negative-log-likelihood loss ℓ, BPB rescales by the corpus's tokens-to-bytes ratio:

BPB = LTLB · ℓln 2 // LT = length in tokens, LB = length in UTF-8 bytes; for the Pile, LT/LB = 0.29335 GPT-2-tokens/byte

Two evaluation moves matter. To benchmark existing models, they score every off-the-shelf GPT-2 and GPT-3 (via the OpenAI API) on each Pile component, document-by-document. To prove the corpus trains better models, they train architecturally identical 1.3-billion-parameter models (the GPT-3 design) from scratch on three datasets held to the same size — the Pile, CC-100 (en), and a raw Common Crawl sample — each downsampled to ~40GB and decontaminated with the same 13-gram overlap filter used by GPT-3 against the evaluation sets. Finally, they spend a third of the paper documenting the corpus: language ID, document-length and bytes-per-token distributions, topic models, profanity rates, gender/religion/race co-occurrence and sentiment, and a per-component accounting of licensing and author consent.

// what they found

The results

Three findings carry the paper. First, existing GPT-2/GPT-3 models follow a clean scaling law on the Pile even though none were trained on it: on overall Pile test BPB (the size-weighted aggregate across components, not a simple per-component average), BPB falls with model size — the GPT-3 family’s line of best fit has slope -0.1674 and intercept 2.5516, with no sign of diminishing returns. The Pile works as a held-out generalization benchmark out of the box.

Second, where a web-trained model is weak is exactly where the Pile is different. Using a GPT-2 model trained on the Pile as a yardstick (normalized so OpenWebText2 reads zero), the authors measure how much harder each component is for off-the-shelf GPT-3 than for a model that actually saw Pile data. GPT-3 lags most on the components furthest from its web-and-books training — the top of Fig. 3 runs DM Mathematics, Enron Emails, PubMed Central, GitHub, and ArXiv (math, email, biomedical, code, academic) — and is essentially even on the web/book sets it already trained on (Books3, Wikipedia, Pile-CC, PG-19 sit near zero). In other words, the Pile's distinctive components are not redundant with GPT-3's web-heavy training data.

Third, at matched size, training on the Pile beats training on Common Crawl across the board. Holding all three corpora to ~40GB, the 1.3B Pile model improves on every single Pile component versus the CC-100 and raw-CC models — by enormous margins on the domains web data misses (DM Mathematics, EuroParl, GitHub, arXiv) and modestly on web-like text. On traditional benchmarks the picture is split: WikiText perplexity improves sharply (5.59 vs. 8.27 for CC-100 and 11.75 for raw CC), while LAMBADA is essentially flat (12.78 vs. 11.78 PPL; 50.1% vs. 49.7% accuracy). A telling side note: raw Common Crawl beats the perplexity-filtered CC-100 on Pile BPB, suggesting CC-100's aggressive Wikipedia-perplexity filtering throws away diversity — a caution for anyone over-filtering web data.

The documentation half of the paper adds texture rather than headline numbers: the Pile is 97.4% English (by fastText), the corpus as a whole is less profane than its own Pile-CC component, and co-occurrence probes surface familiar societal biases — adjectives like "military"/"criminal" skewing male and "married"/"happy" skewing female, "Buddhist" carrying the highest average sentiment and "Jew" the lowest, and every racial term the authors test carrying net-negative sentiment ("black" lowest at -0.15). These are offered as documentation for downstream users, not as fixes.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

The Pile's central claim — that a curated, diverse mixture beats filtered web crawl — is convincingly demonstrated, but only inside the box the authors drew. Every training comparison is run at 1.3B parameters on ~40GB corpora; "the Pile improves language modeling" is never shown at GPT-3 scale or at the corpus's real 825 GiB size. The authors are upfront that size-matching is "generous to CC-100" (which is ~1/3 the Pile's true size), which reframes the result honestly: this is evidence that better-mixed data wins at fixed budget, not that the Pile's sheer volume does. Whether the diversity advantage survives to 175B and to trillions of tokens is left to faith and to later work.

The benchmark-side results lean on a stack of proxies and assumptions. The zero-shot scaling law (Fig. 2) is plotted against assumed GPT-3 API sizes (ada/babbage/curie/davinci = 2.7B/6.7B/13B/175B) — sizes the paper itself flags as never publicized and merely “assume[d],” so the fitted -0.1674 slope sits on a guessed x-axis. The componentwise "GPT-3 underperformance" metric (Fig. 3) is itself a proxy built from a GPT-2-Pile model normalized to OpenWebText2; the authors flag that it "could be confounded by dataset-specific scaling effects," and indeed several components show GPT-3 implausibly beating the Pile-trained model. The bias and toxicity numbers are proxies too: a single profanity-classifier package (which flags the German word "die"), binary he/she co-occurrence, and SentiWordNet sentiment — useful documentation, but not measurements anyone should quote as ground truth.

On leakage, the authors deduplicate within the Pile and 13-gram-decontaminate the training comparison, yet explicitly warn that "some documents are duplicated across the train/validation/test splits." More structurally, parts of the Pile (Books3, Wikipedia, Pile-CC, PG-19) overlap GPT-3's own training data — which is precisely why those components read near-zero in Fig. 3 — so the corpus is not a clean held-out set for web-trained models. And the elephant in the room is licensing: Books3 (12% of the mixture) is derived from the Bibliotik private tracker, i.e. pirated books, and the authors candidly list five components collected with neither ToS compliance nor author consent, defending the whole on a self-authored fair-use argument that is explicitly "not legal advice." That argument has not aged well — Books3 was later pulled from the official distribution after copyright takedowns — and it makes the Pile an eval and training asset with a real legal asterisk attached.

baselines

Fair and well-chosen (CC-100, raw CC) but small: a single 1.3B model class at ~40GB, never GPT-3 scale or full-corpus size. The win is "better mixture at fixed budget," not "more data."

contamination

Within-Pile dedup is acknowledged imperfect (possible train/val/test overlap); several Pile components also overlap GPT-3's training data, so it is not a clean held-out benchmark for web-trained models.

what's missed

Scaling-law fit rests on unconfirmed GPT-3 API sizes; LAMBADA shows no gain (12.78 vs 11.78 PPL) despite the abstract's "improving downstream evaluations"; bias/toxicity are single-tool proxies; English-only (97.4%).

would raise confidence

A Pile-trained model at GPT-3 scale on the full corpus; held-out test sets with zero overlap with public web corpora; multiple toxicity/bias instruments; and a clean-licensing variant (which the community effectively forced later).

// bottom line

A landmark of open pretraining data and of dataset transparency — the diversity thesis is well-supported at small scale, and the per-component documentation set a norm the field badly needed. Read the headline as "a curated mixture beats filtered web crawl at matched budget," keep the assumed-size and proxy caveats in mind, and treat the Books3 licensing problem as load-bearing, not a footnote.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. Diversity beats volume at fixed budget. At a matched ~40GB and 1.3B params, a model trained on the Pile's 22-source mixture beats one trained on filtered Common Crawl on every Pile component, with the biggest gains on math, code, multilingual, and academic text.
  2. The Pile is also a benchmark. Off-the-shelf GPT-2/GPT-3 follow a clean BPB scaling law on it and visibly struggle on academic and symbolic domains — a built-in map of where web pretraining is thin.
  3. Curate with epoch weights, not raw size. Up-weighting high-quality sources (Wikipedia 3×, etc.) turns 825 GiB of raw text into 1,254 GiB of effective training data — a recipe many later open models reused.
  4. Document your data — and check its license. The paper normalized creator-side dataset documentation, but its own frankness exposes the cost: Books3 and four other components carried no consent or ToS basis, and Books3 was later removed over copyright.
