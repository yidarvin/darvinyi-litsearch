CUAD: An Expert-Annotated NLP Dataset for Legal Contract Review

A year-long effort by The Atticus Project: 510 commercial contracts from SEC EDGAR, hand-labeled by dozens of legal experts into 13,101 clause highlights across 41 review categories — a conservative ~$2M of expert labor turned into the first large public benchmark for contract review, and a clean test of whether NLP transfers to domains where labeled data is genuinely expensive.

Hendrycks, Burns, Chen, Ball — UC Berkeley · The Nueva School · The Atticus Project

What problem is this solving?

By 2021, large pretrained Transformers had surpassed humans on tasks ordinary people can do — SQuAD 2.0, SuperGLUE — but it was an open question whether they transfer to specialized domains, the ones where humans need years of training. The honest answer was "we don't know," and the reason was data: machine learning needs thousands of labeled examples, and in expert domains those examples can only be produced by trained professionals who are expensive and short on time. So unlike vision or translation, you can't just scrape the labels.

Contract review is the canonical example. Reading a contract to find the clauses that carry obligations or risk is one of the most tedious, repetitive jobs in law — firms spend roughly half their time on it, at billing rates of $500–$900 per hour in the US, so a single transaction can cost hundreds of thousands of dollars in review. The costs cascade downward: because review is so expensive, small businesses and individuals routinely sign contracts they never read, which invites predatory terms. Yet there was no large, public, machine-learning-ready dataset for contract review — the most related prior work (Leivaditi et al., 2020) covered a single contract type (leases), fewer categories, and over an order of magnitude fewer annotations, and the broader legal-NLP literature focused on judgment prediction or entity extraction rather than the clause-finding lawyers actually grind through.

CUAD fills that hole with a dataset deliberately built for machine learning by domain experts — and, because the labels are so costly, doubles as a probe of a more general question: when expert annotation is the bottleneck, how far does scaling models versus scaling data get you?

// how it works

The dataset and the task

CUAD — the Contract Understanding Atticus Dataset (pronounced "kwad") — is 510 contracts spanning 25 contract types (affiliate, license, supply, joint-venture, franchise, distributor, and more), drawn from the SEC's public EDGAR filing system. Legal experts labeled 13,101 clauses across 41 categories that lawyers single out when reviewing a contract, grouped into general information (party names, dates, governing law, license grants, renewal terms), "restrictive covenants" (non-compete, exclusivity, no-solicit — clauses that constrain how a business can operate), and "revenue risks" (uncapped liability, minimum commitments, revenue sharing).

The task is deliberately the lowest rung of contract review — what the paper calls contract analysis, or "finding needles in a haystack": for each of the 41 labels, highlight every span of the contract relevant to it. It is explicitly not the high-value counseling work of assessing what a clause's risk means for a business. And the needles are sparse — labeled text averages only about 10% of a contract, and roughly 0.25% per individual label.

The annotation pipeline is the real artifact

What makes CUAD expensive — and valuable — is the labeling. Law-student annotators first went through 70–100 hours of training (video instruction, live workshops, quizzes, practice contracts in the eBrevia review tool) and worked from over 100 pages of written annotation standards. Labeling ran as a seven-step pipeline — student label, keyword sweep for missed clauses, category-by-category report review, attorney review to consensus, and an "extras" pass against an AI tool's suggestions — with every annotation verified by three additional annotators. Across 9,283 pages reviewed at least four times each, at 5–10 minutes per page and $500/hour, the authors put CUAD's conservative pecuniary value at over $2 million.

Modeling: extractive QA over a sliding window

CUAD is cast as extractive question answering in the SQuAD 2.0 mold, so off-the-shelf QA models apply. Each "question" names a label category plus a one-to-two-sentence description ("Highlight the parts (if any) of this clause related to …"), and the model predicts the start/end token span to highlight — or the empty span, since most paragraphs match nothing. Contracts are processed with a sliding window to handle their length. Because more than 99% of windowed features contain no relevant label, training down-weights the empty features so positives and negatives are roughly balanced.

A predicted span counts as a match when its word overlap with a gold span clears a Jaccard threshold of 0.5:

J(A, B) = |A ∩ B||A ∪ B|  ≥ 0.5 // A = words in the gold annotation, B = words in the prediction (lower-cased, punctuation stripped)

Because relevant clauses are rare, the headline metrics are precision/recall-based: the area under the precision–recall curve (AUPR), and Precision @ 80% / 90% Recall — the precision attainable once the confidence threshold is loosened enough to recover 80% (or 90%) of the true clauses. Ten encoder QA models were fine-tuned (BERT, RoBERTa, ALBERT, DeBERTa) on an 80/20 random split of contracts, with a small grid search over learning rate ∈ {3·10⁻⁵, 1·10⁻⁴, 3·10⁻⁴} and epochs ∈ {1, 4}, on 8× A100 GPUs.

// what they found

The results

Performance is, in the authors' word, nascent — promising but far from solved. The best model, DeBERTa-xlarge, reaches 44.0% Precision @ 80% Recall and 47.8 AUPR, versus just 8.2% Precision @ 80% Recall for BERT-base. The metric improves sharply across model generations — a clean demonstration that the benchmark has headroom and tracks progress.

But the headline number hides a cliff. Precision holds reasonably up to about 80% recall, then collapses — at the harder operating point, Precision @ 90% Recall is 0.0 for nine of the ten models; only DeBERTa-xlarge is non-zero, at 17.8%. Even at the friendlier threshold, ~30% precision means a lawyer reads roughly two irrelevant clauses for every relevant one the model flags.

Data is the bottleneck, not model size

The most consequential finding is about what moves the needle. Model size alone is unreliable: ALBERT-xxlarge has 20× the parameters of ALBERT-base but only ~3% higher AUPR, and BERT-base (32.4) essentially ties BERT-large (32.3). What helps is model design (DeBERTa's disentangled attention far outruns BERT) — and, above all, data. Holding the model fixed at RoBERTa-base, an order of magnitude more training annotations lifts AUPR from 27.6% to 42.6% (+15 absolute) — a jump that rivals the entire 15.4-point spread between the worst and best architectures.

Tellingly, unlabeled domain data barely helps: pretraining RoBERTa-base on ~8GB of unlabeled EDGAR contracts with masked-language-modeling adds only ~3% AUPR (42.6 → 45.2). High-quality labels are far more valuable than orders of magnitude more raw text — and standard MLM doesn't effectively exploit the contract corpus.

Performance is wildly uneven by clause

Aggregate numbers also flatten enormous per-category variance. DeBERTa-xlarge nearly ceilings on boilerplate fields — Governing Law, Document Name, Parties, Agreement/Expiration Date — while high-stakes, variably-worded clauses like Covenant Not to Sue, IP Ownership Assignment, and ROFR/ROFO/ROFN sit near 20% AUPR. The clauses a reviewer least wants to miss are exactly the ones the model is worst at.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

CUAD is a genuinely important dataset, and its data-vs-model experiment is the part that has aged best. But the reported scores deserve a careful read, because the way the task is graded is forgiving in places that matter for legal work.

The headline metric tolerates the failure mode lawyers care most about. "44.0% Precision @ 80% Recall" is reported at a recall of 80% — i.e. the model is allowed to miss one in five relevant clauses. In contract review, a missed change-of-control, indemnification, or auto-renewal clause is precisely the error that causes harm, so the operating point a real reviewer needs is high-recall — and there the numbers fall apart: P@90%R is 0.0 for nine of ten models, and DeBERTa-xlarge's 17.8% implies wading through ~5 false positives per true positive. The paper's claim that "80% recall may already be reasonable for some lawyers" is asserted, not measured.

The match criterion is lenient for legal text. A prediction counts as correct at Jaccard ≥ 0.5 — half word-overlap with the gold span. But clauses carry their meaning in sub-parts (a carve-out, an exception, a liability cap amount), and the paper itself notes that "only a sub-part of a sentence is responsive." A 50%-overlap "hit" can capture the surrounding boilerplate while dropping the operative words.

The split invites leakage. Train/test is a random 80/20 over contracts, but CUAD is sampled from EDGAR, where contracts of the same type and even the same filer share heavy boilerplate. Near-duplicate clause language can straddle train and test, which would flatter apparent generalization; no type-disjoint or company-disjoint split is reported. And one category, Price Restriction, drew zero test examples from the random split, so 40 of 41 categories are effectively scored.

The distribution doesn't match the motivation. The access-to-justice framing invokes consumers and small businesses signing leases and terms-of-service — but EDGAR contracts are, by the authors' own description, "more complicated and heavily negotiated than the general population." That's a deliberate, defensible choice (it surfaces rare clauses), yet it means the benchmark over-represents M&A-style commercial agreements, and transfer to consumer contracts is untested.

baselines

Ten 2021 encoder QA models (BERT → DeBERTa); no generative, instruction-tuned, or retrieval baselines, and no measured lawyer baseline. Difficulty is calibrated to extractive QA of its moment.

contamination

Random 80/20 split over EDGAR contracts that share boilerplate by type and filer; no type-/company-disjoint split, so near-duplicate clauses can plausibly straddle train and test.

what's missed

P@80%R tolerates a 20% miss rate and a Jaccard ≥ 0.5 span match; P@90%R is 0.0 for 9/10 models. No inter-annotator agreement number is reported, and the "counseling"/risk level plus the released yes/no answers go unevaluated.

would raise confidence

Report P@95/99%R (recall is the safety axis), a measured lawyer recall/precision baseline + annotator κ, a filer-disjoint split, evaluation of the released yes/no risk answers, and an out-of-distribution consumer-contract test.

// bottom line

CUAD's lasting contribution is the data, not the model numbers — and the paper proves it: ~$2M of expert annotation is the moat, an order of magnitude more labels buys as much as switching architectures, and 8GB of unlabeled contracts buys only +3%. Read the headline scores as a 2021 extractive-QA floor on the easiest rung of contract review (find the clause, don't judge its risk), at a recall threshold a cautious lawyer wouldn't accept. The right takeaway is "expert-labeled data unlocks specialized domains," not "contract review is solved" — which is why CUAD became the canonical legal-NLP asset that LexGLUE-era benchmarks and LegalBench build on.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. The asset is the annotation, not the architecture. 510 EDGAR contracts, 13,101 expert-labeled clauses, 41 categories, a conservative ~$2M and a year of legal labor — built deliberately for ML because contract-review labels can't be scraped.
  2. Data, not model size, is the bottleneck. 10× more annotations lifts RoBERTa-base AUPR 27.6 → 42.6 (+15), matching the entire BERT → DeBERTa architecture gap, while 8GB of unlabeled contracts adds only +3% and 20× more parameters adds ~3%.
  3. Performance is nascent and recall-fragile. DeBERTa-xlarge tops out at 44.0% Precision @ 80% Recall (BERT-base 8.2%) but precision craters past 80% recall — P@90%R is 0.0 for nine of ten models. A useful first-pass highlighter, not a reviewer of record.
