Measuring and Narrowing the Compositionality Gap in Language Models

GPT-3 gets steadily better at recalling individual facts as it scales — but no better at composing two of them into a compound answer, a gap the paper pins at a roughly constant 40% and then narrows with a new prompting method, self-ask.

Ofir Press, Muru Zhang, Sewon Min, Ludwig Schmidt, Noah A. Smith, Mike Lewis · University of Washington / MosaicML / Meta AI / Allen Institute for AI

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

By late 2022, scaling GPT-3 was known to reliably improve single-fact recall and a wide range of downstream tasks. What wasn't clear was whether it also improved a model's ability to combine two facts it separately knows into an answer neither fact states alone — real compositional reasoning, not memorization. A question like "Who was president of the U.S. when superconductivity was discovered?" requires no new knowledge beyond two commonly-known facts (superconductivity's discovery year; who was president that year) — the only thing standing between a correct answer and a wrong one is whether the model actually composes them.

The paper's contribution starts with making that failure mode measurable rather than anecdotal. It defines the compositionality gap: the fraction of compositional (multi-hop) questions a model gets wrong despite answering both underlying sub-questions correctly when asked separately. That's a sharper diagnostic than raw accuracy, because it isolates composition failure from simple ignorance of the underlying facts — a model that never knew a fact was going to miss the compound question anyway, but a model that demonstrably knows both facts and still can't combine them is failing at something else entirely.

// how it works

The method

To measure the gap cleanly, the authors build Compositional Celebrities (CC): 8.6k automatically-generated 2-hop questions across 17 templated categories (e.g., "What is the calling code of the birthplace of Frida Kahlo?"), each combining a celebrity's birth fact (place, year) with a second fact about that place or year (capital, currency, calling code, that year's Master's Tournament winner, …). The combinations are deliberately unnatural — each individual fact is common, but the specific pairing is very unlikely to have appeared together in pretraining text, which lets the authors cleanly separate "does the model know fact A?" and "does it know fact B?" from "can it combine A and B?" The authors manually spot-checked that CC's exact question phrasings didn't already turn up in web searches, to keep the diagnostic honest.

compositionality gap = (both sub-answers correct) AND (2-hop answer wrong)both sub-answers correct // restricted to the subset of questions where the model already knows both underlying facts

Evaluated on GPT-3 (davinci-002) with a 2-shot prompt, the model answers 45.4% of CC's 2-hop questions correctly overall — reaching 84.6% on the easiest category but just 1.2% on the hardest (Birth Year / Literature Nobel Prize Winner), despite answering 80% of that category's sub-questions correctly in isolation. Figure 1 shows the headline result: plotting the compositionality gap across GPT-3 model sizes (Ada 0.35B through Davinci 175B) and across the InstructGPT-3 line (001/002/003), the gap holds at roughly 29–41% for the base GPT-3 family and 32–64% for InstructGPT-3, with no consistent downward trend as scale increases. Bigger models know more facts; they don't get proportionally better at composing them.

The paper's second contribution narrows that gap with self-ask: an "elicitive" prompt (in the spirit of chain-of-thought) that has the model explicitly ask itself follow-up sub-questions, answer each one, and only then state the final answer — scaffolded with literal markers ("Follow up:", "Intermediate answer:", "So the final answer is:") that make the decomposition mechanically parseable. Figure 2 contrasts this against direct prompting (answer immediately, wrong) and chain of thought (reason in one unstructured block, also wrong on this example) — only self-ask's explicit sub-question structure reaches the correct answer, William Howard Taft.

Because self-ask clearly demarcates where each sub-question begins and ends, the authors show it plugs directly into an internet search engine: whenever the model emits "Follow up: …", the sub-question is routed to a search API instead of being answered by the LM itself, and the returned snippet is spliced back into the prompt before generation continues — no fine-tuning, no architecture change, no prompt modification versus plain self-ask. This is the same "structured decomposition → external tool call" pattern that the RL-trained search agents in this graph (Search-R1, R1-Searcher, DeepResearcher) later automate end-to-end via reinforcement learning instead of a fixed prompt scaffold.

To stress-test decomposition on more varied questions than CC's 17 fixed templates allow, the authors also hand-write Bamboogle: 125 two-hop questions built by reading random Wikipedia articles and combining two unrelated facts from each (e.g., "What rocket was the first spacecraft that ever approached Uranus launched on?"), then filtering out any question a popular search engine could already answer directly via its featured snippet — ensuring the compound fact isn't already sitting on the open web in that exact phrasing, even though both underlying facts are sourced from Wikipedia.

// what they found

The results

Across Bamboogle, 2WikiMultiHopQA, and Musique (Davinci-002), self-ask beats every baseline, and adding the search engine improves it further still:

The self-ask jump over chain of thought is small on 2WikiMultiHopQA and Musique but large on Bamboogle — +11 points absolute (57.6 vs. 46.4), matching the paper's own framing of "a large 11% (absolute)" gain — which the authors attribute to Bamboogle's questions being far more varied and dissimilar to the few-shot demonstrations, a setting where CoT's unstructured reasoning has more room to go off the rails than self-ask's rigid decomposition. Plugging in the search engine adds a further +1.4 to +10.1 points across all three sets, with the biggest jump on 2WikiMultiHopQA (30.0 → 40.1). A plain search engine alone is nearly useless on compositional questions (0.0–2.2%) — unsurprising, since a single query can't resolve a two-hop compound fact, but it underscores that self-ask's value is in the decomposition structure, not just in having access to a search API.

Two more results round out the "narrowing" half of the paper. Against Kojima et al.'s zero-shot "Let's think step by step" elicitive baseline, self-ask wins decisively on CC (79.6% vs. 45.7% with InstructGPT-Davinci-002; 54.2% vs. 1.1% with plain Davinci). And against Least-to-Most prompting — which also decomposes questions but needs multiple forward passes with different prompts — self-ask matches or beats its accuracy (35.5 vs. 29.0 on 2WikiMultiHopQA; 16.3 vs. 16.8 on Musique) while generating far shorter outputs (569 vs. 844 tokens; 663 vs. 1020 tokens), over 30% faster since it decomposes and answers in a single forward pass with one prompt. The authors also report a cleaner output-format signal: on Bamboogle, 40% of chain-of-thought's final answers weren't in short, parseable form, versus 17% for self-ask and just 3% for self-ask + search engine.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

The headline "the gap doesn't shrink with scale" finding rests entirely on one model family: GPT-3 and InstructGPT-3, tested from 0.35B to 175B parameters. That's a real, carefully measured result within that family, but it's also the only family tested — there's no open-weights model (where the exact pretraining corpus is knowable, unlike GPT-3's) and nothing above 175B. The authors are explicit about this in their own Limitations section, which is a point in the paper's favor, but it means "scale doesn't close the compositionality gap" should be read as a finding about GPT-3-era OpenAI models circa 2022, not yet a settled property of language models in general.

On contamination, the paper is unusually careful for a 2022-era benchmark paper, and deserves credit rather than suspicion here: CC's question phrasings were manually spot-checked against web search to confirm they don't already appear online, and Bamboogle was explicitly filtered to exclude any question a search engine's featured snippet already answers — an active decontamination step, not an afterthought. The paper even flags its own future contamination risk in a footnote: because CC was publicly released before GPT-4 launched, the 23.0% compositionality gap they later estimate for GPT-4 "may be invalid" if GPT-4 was trained on CC itself. Explicitly disclaiming your own headline number for a newer model is a rare and honest move.

Where the evaluation is thinner: Bamboogle is only 125 questions, hand-written by the paper's own small author team, with no inter-annotator agreement or confidence interval reported on the 57.6%/60.0% self-ask numbers — plausible given the dataset's stated purpose (breadth of question style over statistical power), but it means single-digit-point differences on Bamboogle shouldn't be treated as precise. And the "search engine alone" baseline is a weak strawman by construction — first-result or featured-snippet only, with no LM reasoning over retrieved passages at all — so the paper doesn't rule out that a more sophisticated non-self-ask retrieval method (e.g., feeding passages to the LM the way FiD, already in this graph, does) might close some of the gap between "search alone" and "self-ask + search" without needing the self-ask decomposition specifically.

baselines

Comparisons (direct prompting, CoT, Least-to-Most, "let's think step by step") are reasonable and current for 2022, but the "search engine alone" baseline is snippet-only with zero LM reasoning — a weak comparison point that can't isolate how much of self-ask+search's win is the decomposition vs. just having retrieval at all.

contamination

Unusually rigorous for its era: CC was spot-checked against web search, Bamboogle was explicitly filtered to exclude search-engine-answerable questions, and the paper proactively flags that its own public CC release may have since contaminated GPT-4's training data, invalidating its own later GPT-4 estimate.

what's missed

The "gap doesn't shrink with scale" claim is tested on exactly one model family (GPT-3/InstructGPT-3, ≤175B) with no open-weights or newer-generation replication; Bamboogle's 125 hand-written questions carry no confidence interval.

would raise confidence

Replicating the scale sweep on an open-weights family (to rule out contamination confounds directly rather than by search-engine spot-check) and reporting variance on the small Bamboogle set.

// bottom line

The compositionality gap is a genuinely sharp diagnostic, and the paper is more careful about its own contamination risk than most benchmark papers of its era. But its central scaling claim is a single-family result, and Bamboogle — now a standard OOD benchmark cited by nearly every later RL-search-agent paper in this graph — is small enough that its numbers should be read as directionally informative, not statistically tight.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. The compositionality gap — the fraction of 2-hop questions a model misses despite knowing both underlying facts — stays at a roughly constant 29–64% across GPT-3 and InstructGPT-3 model sizes, with no consistent shrinkage as scale increases from 0.35B to 175B parameters.
  2. Self-ask (explicit "Follow up: … Intermediate answer: …" decomposition) beats chain of thought most on the most varied question set (+11 points on Bamboogle) and matches Least-to-Most prompting's accuracy while running over 30% faster, since it decomposes and answers in one forward pass.
  3. Self-ask's structured sub-questions plug cleanly into a real search engine with zero fine-tuning or prompt changes, adding up to +10.1 points further — the same "decompose, then call a tool" pattern later RL-trained search agents (Search-R1, R1-Searcher, DeepResearcher, ZeroSearch) automate via reinforcement learning instead of a fixed prompt.
  4. Bamboogle, the paper's 125-question hand-built benchmark, went on to become a standard out-of-domain multi-hop QA eval set for the entire later wave of RL search-agent papers — ZeroSearch reports on it directly alongside NQ, TriviaQA, and HotpotQA.
