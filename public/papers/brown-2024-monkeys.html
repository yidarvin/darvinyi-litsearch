Large Language Monkeys: Scaling Inference Compute with Repeated Sampling

Treat inference compute as a scaling axis: draw thousands of independent samples per problem and let a verifier pick the winner. Coverage — the fraction of problems any sample solves — grows nearly log-linearly over four orders of magnitude and fits an exponentiated power law, turning a cheap weak model plus 250 tries into a 56% SWE-bench Lite solver that beats the 43% single-shot state of the art.

Bradley Brown, Jordan Juravsky, Ryan Ehrlich, Ronald Clark, Quoc V. Le, Christopher Ré, Azalia Mirhoseini · Stanford / Oxford / Google DeepMind

What problem is this solving?

The last few years of LLM progress have been bought almost entirely with training compute — bigger models, larger datasets, longer pre-training runs — and that relationship is well charted by training scaling laws. Inference compute got far less attention. Larger models cost more per token, and prompting tricks like chain-of-thought spend extra tokens, but in practice users and systems still give a model exactly one attempt at a problem before reading off its answer.

Repeated sampling — drawing many independent candidate solutions and keeping the best — was known to help in isolated cases: AlphaCode kept improving up to a million samples per competitive-programming problem, and similar gains had shown up on math and puzzle benchmarks. But there was no systematic picture. How far does the benefit of more samples actually extend? Does it hold across model sizes, families, and levels of post-training? And critically: generating a correct solution somewhere in a pile of 10,000 samples is worthless if you can't tell which one it is. This paper sets out to characterize repeated sampling as a clean, second scaling axis — and to be honest about where it breaks.

// how it works

The method

The recipe is deliberately simple (Figure 1). For a given problem, sample the LLM k independent times at a positive temperature to get a collection of candidate solutions, then apply a domain-specific verifier to select a final answer. The effectiveness of this loop decomposes into two distinct properties:

  • Coverage — as k grows, what fraction of problems are solved by at least one sample? With an automatic verifier this is exactly the pass@k metric.
  • Precision — given the collection, how reliably can we identify a correct sample? This is easy when a proof checker or unit-test suite can score every candidate, and hard when (as in open-ended math word problems) it cannot.

They evaluate five pass/fail tasks: GSM8K and MATH (128 random test problems each, oracle-checked final answers), MiniF2F-MATH (130 Lean4 formal proofs, checked by the proof checker), CodeContests (competitive programming, hidden input/output tests), and SWE-bench Lite (real GitHub issues, the repo's own unit tests). MiniF2F, CodeContests, and SWE-bench have automatic verifiers, so coverage there converts directly into solve rate; GSM8K and MATH do not, and become the stress test for precision. Models span Llama-3-8B / 8B-Instruct / 70B-Instruct, Gemma-2B / 7B, and the eight Pythia models from 70M to 12B, with up to 10,000 samples per problem. SWE-bench Lite uses DeepSeek-Coder-V2-Instruct inside the open-source Moatless Tools agent, capped at 250 independent multi-turn trajectories.

To keep pass@k low-variance, they generate N samples once, count the correct ones Ci per problem, and use the unbiased estimator from the Codex paper rather than re-sampling:

pass@k = 1# problems  Σi  ( 1 − C(N − Ci, k)C(N, k) ) // chance that none of k draws is correct, averaged over problems (Chen et al., 2021)

The headline characterization is a scaling law for inference. Borrowing the function class the GPT-4 technical report used for training compute, they model the log of coverage c as a power law in the sample budget k, then exponentiate to predict coverage directly:

log(c) ≈ a·kb   ⟹   c ≈ exp(a·kb) // a, b ∈ ℝ fitted per (model, task); an "exponentiated power law"
// what they found

The results

Across all five tasks, coverage rises smoothly and substantially with the sample budget (Figure 2). Given a single attempt, GPT-4o beats the Llama and DeepSeek models on every task — but as samples accumulate, all three weaker models overtake GPT-4o's single-shot score. The flagship demonstration is on SWE-bench Lite: DeepSeek-Coder-V2-Instruct solves 15.9% of issues with one sample and 56% with 250, clearing the single-attempt state of the art of 43% (CodeStory Aide, a GPT-4o + Claude 3.5 Sonnet mixture) by 13 points. GPT-4o in the same Moatless harness manages only 24.67% at one attempt.

The effect is largest for the weakest models. On CodeContests, Gemma-2B's coverage climbs from a pass@1 of 0.02% to a pass@10k of 7.1% — a 300×-plus increase. On MATH, Pythia-160M goes from 0.27% to 57%. The one clean failure: every Pythia model scores zero coverage on CodeContests even at 10,000 samples, which the authors attribute to Pythia's thin diet of code in pre-training — you cannot sample your way to a capability the base model never acquired.

Repeated sampling also reshapes the cost frontier. Sampling a cheap, weaker model many times can beat one shot from an expensive one, in both FLOPs and dollars. On SWE-bench Lite, five attempts from DeepSeek-Coder-V2 solve more issues than single attempts from Claude 3.5 Sonnet or GPT-4o, at over 3× lower cost:

The coverage curves are not just monotone — they are predictable. For most model/task pairs the exponentiated power law c ≈ exp(a·kb) fits tightly (Figure 5); e.g. Llama-3-8B-Instruct on MATH fits with a = −1.33, b = −0.43 at a mean error of 0.003. Within a model family, coverage curves on a log-k axis trace S-curves with similar slopes but different horizontal offsets — a bigger model mostly buys you a leftward shift, i.e. the same coverage with fewer samples. The fit is imperfect where it matters least for the headline: Llama-3-8B-Instruct on MiniF2F-MATH is the worst case, with error 0.0297 ± 0.0157.

Then comes the catch. The whole story above quietly assumes a perfect verifier. On GSM8K and MATH, the only two tasks without one, the gap between coverage and usable performance blows open (Figure 7). With Llama-3-8B-Instruct on MATH, coverage rises from 82.9% at 100 samples to 98.44% at 10,000 — but the best practical selector (majority vote, reward-model best-of-N with ArmoRM-Llama3-8B, or reward-weighted majority vote) improves only from 40.50% to 41.41% over the same range. All three selection methods plateau around 100 samples while coverage keeps climbing; the rare correct solutions that drive coverage are exactly the ones majority voting and reward models cannot find.

Is the signal even there to exploit? A manual grade of 105 chains-of-thought from correct Llama-3-8B-Instruct GSM8K samples found over 90% are faithful — valid reasoning, not lucky guesses — even on problems the model solves ≤10% of the time. So the correct samples really are correct; the failure is in selection, not generation. The paper is also candid that automatic verifiers are themselves leaky: 11.3% of SWE-bench Lite problems have flaky test suites, and 35 of 122 CodeContests Python3 problems have provided "correct" solutions that fail their own tests.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

This is one of the more honest scaling papers — it foregrounds its own ceiling rather than burying it — but the eval framing rewards a careful read. The central tension is that coverage is not performance. The most quotable numbers (MATH coverage 98.44%, "coverage exceeds 95%") are oracle-verifier upper bounds. On the only two tasks where you can't cheat with a perfect checker, the actually-selectable accuracy barely moves — under one point (40.50% → 41.41%) across a 100× increase in samples. Read precisely, the paper makes two claims, not one: inference scaling works where you already have a verifier (code, formal proofs), and it largely does not yet work where you don't.

The flagship "exceeds SOTA by 13%" comparison is a cost story dressed as a capability story. It pits a weak model × 250 samples (coverage) against a strong model × 1 sample, which is fair on the axis the paper actually cares about — dollars and FLOPs — but is not a like-for-like capability claim. And the SWE-bench 56% leans on the benchmark's own hidden unit tests as the selector; on a genuinely novel issue with no gold test suite, you don't get to keep only the trajectory that happens to pass. The more you sample, the more chances you get to pass the tests without solving the task — and the paper's own audit (11.3% flaky SWE-bench suites, 35/122 leaky CodeContests problems) shows those tests admit both false positives and false negatives.

baselines

Fair and current for a 2024 paper (GPT-4o, Claude 3.5 Sonnet, the CodeStory Aide SWE-bench SOTA). But the headline compares coverage at k=250 to single-shot baselines — a budget asymmetry the cost table makes legitimate, not a matched-compute capability comparison.

contamination

Not analyzed. GSM8K/MATH/CodeContests plausibly sit in Llama-3/Gemma/Pythia pre-training; at 10k samples, low-probability "correct" generations could be memorized solutions surfacing, inflating coverage relative to genuine reasoning. The 90%-faithful CoT audit partly mitigates this but doesn't rule it out.

what's missed

"Verification doesn't scale" is shown for these selectors — plain majority vote and one 8B reward model (ArmoRM). A stronger or process-level verifier, or a trained best-of-N, might not plateau; the result bounds cheap selection, not verification in principle. Small eval subsets (128 GSM8K, 128 MATH, 130 MiniF2F, 250 SWE-bench attempts) put real sampling noise behind absolute figures like 98.44%.

would raise confidence

Full test sets with confidence intervals; a stronger/process verifier tested against the same plateau; an explicit contamination check; and a multi-turn vs. single-turn sampling comparison (they only sample independently). The de-flaked SWE-bench numbers in the appendix are reported as "similar," which helps.

// bottom line

Repeated sampling is a real, cheap, log-linear axis for inference compute — and the cost argument (weak × many > strong × one, at a third the price) is the durable contribution. But its payoff is gated entirely by verification. Where verifiers are perfect (proofs, executable code) it amplifies weak models past frontier single-shots; where they aren't (open-ended reasoning), coverage races ahead of what cheap selectors can extract — starkest on MATH, where selectable accuracy stalls near 41%. The paper's lasting value is less the 56% number than the precise diagnosis of that gap — which became the explicit target of the reasoning-model wave (o1, R1, RLVR) that followed.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. Inference is a scaling axis. Coverage (pass@k) rises smoothly with samples over four orders of magnitude and fits an exponentiated power law c ≈ exp(a·kb) — the inference-time analogue of training scaling laws, with bigger models acting as a leftward shift of the same curve.
  2. Weak + many can beat strong + one, cheaply. DeepSeek-Coder-V2 × 250 reaches 56% on SWE-bench Lite (vs. the 43% single-shot SOTA); × 5 matches Claude/GPT single-shot at over 3× lower cost. Repeated sampling buys a new performance/cost degree of freedom.
  3. The bottleneck is the verifier, not the generator. With an oracle, MATH coverage hits 98.44%; with majority vote or a reward model it plateaus near 41% past ~100 samples. The correct samples exist and are 90%+ faithful — repeated sampling only pays off as far as you can find the needle.
