What Disease does this Patient Have? A Large-scale Open Domain Question Answering Dataset from Medical Exams

61,097 multiple-choice questions pulled straight from professional medical board exams in the US, mainland China, and Taiwan, paired with a multi-million-token corpus of medical textbooks to search for evidence in — the paper that introduced MedQA, now the field's default "can a model practice medicine" benchmark.

Di Jin, Eileen Pan, Nassim Oufattole, Wei-Hung Weng, Hanyi Fang, Peter Szolovits

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

By 2020, pretrained models like BERT, RoBERTa, and ALBERT had "achieved nearly saturated performance on most of the popular [reading-comprehension] datasets" — but those datasets hand the model a passage already known to contain the answer. Real question answering rarely works that way: a system has to both find relevant evidence in a large text collection and reason over it, which is the harder setting the field calls open-domain QA (OpenQA). Existing free-form multiple-choice OpenQA sets such as ARC and OpenBookQA pushed in that direction, but the authors note their questions "require only elementary or middle school level knowledge" — e.g. "Which object would let the most heat travel through?" — a ceiling too low to stress a model that has already saturated on easier benchmarks.

The paper's answer is to source questions from an environment explicitly designed to be hard for domain experts: professional medical board examinations. A model attempting MedQA must retrieve the right evidence from a large medical-textbook corpus and perform the kind of multi-hop clinical reasoning a real exam-taker needs — read a patient vignette, identify the relevant findings, and rule out plausible but wrong diagnoses. Unlike consumer-health QA sets (LiveQA, MedicationQA) that retrieve an answer snippet from health websites, or span-extraction sets like BioASQ, MedQA is — per the paper's own comparison table — the only one of its contemporaries formulated as OpenQA with a multiple-choice answer format, sourced from real professional exams rather than consumer queries or automatically-mined records.

// how it works

The method

Each MedQA instance has three parts: a question (either a single fact lookup or a full patient vignette), several answer candidates, and access to a shared document collection the model must search for evidence. The questions and candidate answers were scraped from the National Medical Board Examination in three jurisdictions — the USA (USMLE), mainland China (MCMLE), and Taiwan (TWMLE) — from both real exams and freely-available mock tests. To comply with fair-use law, the authors shuffle each question's option order and randomly delete one wrong option for the USMLE and MCMLE sets, which "results in four options with one right option and three wrong options" (down from the original exams' five). Questions were split 80% / 10% / 10% into train / dev / test.

The document collection is the other half of the dataset. For USMLE (and TWMLE, which shares it), the authors assembled 18 English medical textbooks widely used by USMLE takers, OCR'd into 231,581 paragraphs and 12,727,711 tokens. For MCMLE they collected 33 official simplified-Chinese licensing-exam textbooks, OCR'd into 116,216 paragraphs and 14,730,364 characters. TWMLE questions, originally in traditional Chinese, are machine-translated to English via Google Translate and then answered against the same English textbook corpus as USMLE.

Two families of baseline are implemented. The rule-based methods score each answer option without training: PMI measures how strongly question n-grams and option n-grams co-occur in the document collection, and two information-retrieval variants (IR-ES, off-the-shelf Elasticsearch/BM25; IR-CUSTOM, a re-weighted BM25 with English MetaMap-based medical-term filtering) send "question + option" as a query and rank options by retrieval score. The neural family follows the DrQA recipe — a Document Retriever (the best IR system) surfaces the top-N passages, which a Document Reader then scores; readers tested range from a non-pretrained BiGRU max-pooling model (MAX-OUT) to fine-tuned BERT, RoBERTa, BioBERT, and ClinicalBERT variants for English, and BERT/RoBERTa variants with whole-word masking for Chinese.

The results

Every baseline struggles. The best method overall — BioBERT-Large on USMLE/TWMLE and RoBERTa-Large-WWM-EXT on MCMLE — reaches only 36.7% (USMLE test), 42.0% (TWMLE test), and 70.1% (MCMLE test) accuracy, against a four-option chance baseline of 25.0%. The gap between languages is stark: MCMLE is far more tractable than USMLE for every method tested.

For 68% of sampled USMLE questions, the retriever surfaces no usable evidence in its top 25 paragraphs at all — versus just 4% for MCMLE — which the authors trace to USMLE's much higher share of multi-hop "patient case" questions: 98.0% of sampled USMLE questions are this harder "Type 2" format (describe a patient, infer the diagnosis or next step) versus 25.0% for MCMLE and 31.0% for TWMLE, and Type 2 questions require chaining symptoms to a diagnosis before the retriever can even find the right textbook passage.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

MedQA's rigor is real for what it was designed to test in 2020 — but it carries structural properties that matter a great deal more now that it is a routine line in 2026-era medical-LLM model cards than they did as a 2020 retrieval-augmented-reading benchmark.

No human baseline, by the authors' own admission. The paper states plainly: "we did not include human performance for comparison due to the high variance of scores for human examinees (best medical students can earn almost full marks while worse ones cannot pass the exams)." That is a defensible call for 2020's retrieval-pipeline baselines, but it means MedQA ships with no anchor at all for "how hard is this for a person" — every later paper reporting a MedQA score against an implied human ceiling is importing an assumption this paper never measured.

The exam format was altered for copyright, changing item difficulty. To comply with fair-use law, USMLE and MCMLE questions had one of their original five options removed and the rest reshuffled, producing four-option items with a 25% chance floor instead of the real exam's 20%. That is a small but real distortion: the actual USMLE is harder per-item than the MedQA version of it, and every reported accuracy number is against the easier, modified item set rather than the exam a real physician would sit.

Provenance is now a contamination liability the 2020 paper never had to think about. USMLE and MCMLE questions were scraped from public, previously-indexed question-bank sites — the appendix names step1.medbullets.com, amboss.com, and lecturio.com for USMLE — the same kind of sites that large-scale web crawls used to pretrain today's frontier LLMs are likely to have ingested. A 2020 retrieval baseline had no way to "memorize" these questions; a 2024-era LLM pretrained on a fresh Common Crawl snapshot plausibly can, and this paper (reasonably, given when it was written) neither anticipated nor mitigates that.

The retrieval bottleneck means the headline score under-measures reasoning ability. With 68% of sampled USMLE questions returning no usable evidence in the top 25 retrieved paragraphs, the reported 36.7% conflates "the reader failed to reason" with "the retriever never found the right passage" — the paper's own error analysis shows this, but the headline accuracy number doesn't separate the two, so it systematically understates what a reader model could do if retrieval were solved (or if a model instead answers from parametric medical knowledge with no retrieval step at all, which is exactly how MedQA is used in most modern LLM evaluations).

baselines

Reasonable for 2020 — rule-based IR, a non-pretrained neural reader, and fine-tuned BERT/RoBERTa/BioBERT/ClinicalBERT variants — but the retrieve-then-read framing the paper evaluates is not how MedQA is used today, where models answer directly from parametric knowledge with no retrieval stage at all.

contamination

Not addressed in the paper (pre-dates contamination-era practice); questions were scraped from public exam-prep websites now plausibly present in web-scale LLM pretraining corpora, and the full labeled dataset ships with no held-out private split or canary.

what's missed

Any human baseline; an oracle-retrieval ceiling to isolate reader accuracy from retrieval failure; acknowledgment that the modified 4-option format changes difficulty relative to the real exam.

would raise confidence

A held-out, never-web-published test split; an oracle-retrieval control condition; a documented human-expert accuracy anchor; and a contamination audit given the dataset's public exam-site provenance and its heavy reuse in post-2022 model cards.

// bottom line

MedQA is a well-constructed retrieval-and-reasoning benchmark for the OpenQA setting it was built to test, with an honest error analysis that correctly diagnoses its own retrieval bottleneck. What it lacks — a human baseline, an unmodified item format, and any contamination safeguard for its web-scraped questions — matters far more today, now that MedQA is reported directly (usually without retrieval at all) as a headline "medical knowledge" number in frontier model cards it was never designed to support.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. Real board-exam questions, not consumer health FAQs. MedQA's 61,097 questions (12,723 USMLE English + 34,251 MCMLE simplified Chinese + 14,123 TWMLE traditional Chinese) come from actual medical licensing exams, paired with a 12.7M-token English and 14.7M-character Chinese textbook corpus to retrieve evidence from — an OpenQA setting, not reading comprehension.
  2. Even the best 2020 baseline barely cleared chance on English. BioBERT-Large scored 36.7% on USMLE test (chance is 25%) versus 70.1% on MCMLE with RoBERTa-Large-WWM-EXT — and the paper's own analysis traces most of that gap to retrieval: for USMLE's harder, multi-hop patient-case questions (98% of the USMLE sample), the retriever surfaces no usable evidence at all 68% of the time.
  3. No human baseline, and the format was altered for copyright — a real exam-difficulty distortion worth remembering every time a modern model card reports a MedQA number as if it were an unmodified medical-licensing exam.
