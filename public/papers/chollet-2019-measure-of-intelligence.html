On the Measure of Intelligence

A formal argument that intelligence is skill-acquisition efficiency over a scope of tasks — not raw task performance — followed by ARC, the Abstraction and Reasoning Corpus, as a concrete benchmark built to measure exactly that.

François Chollet

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

AI research has never had a rigorous, actionable definition of "intelligence" to aim at. Common-sense dictionary definitions aren't measurable; the Turing Test "completely opt[s] out of objectively defining and measuring intelligence" by outsourcing the judgment to unreliable human raters. In their absence, the field has defaulted to a proxy: build a system that beats humans at some specific, prestigious task — chess, Go, Dota 2, Atari — and treat that as evidence of progress toward general intelligence.

Chollet argues this proxy is broken, and not for a subtle reason: skill at a task can be "bought" with unlimited priors or unlimited training data, independent of any actual generalization power. IBM's Deep Blue beat Kasparov in 1997 through brute-force minimax search — "neither this knowledge nor the AI they had built could generalize to anything other than similar board games." OpenAI's Dota-playing "Five" trained on 45,000 years of self-play, could only play a restricted 16-character version of the game (real Dota 2 has over 100), and was reliably beaten by ordinary human players within days of its public release. AlphaGo and AlphaZero, as of the paper's writing, "have not yet found any application outside of board games." Each of these is a real capability achievement — and each tells you almost nothing about whether the underlying system could learn to do anything else. The paper's target is this gap between task-specific skill (crystallized, purchasable performance) and intelligence (the general-purpose process that acquires new skills efficiently) — a conflation the field keeps making because it has no formal way to tell the two apart.

// how it works

The method

The paper's central move is to formalize intelligence using Algorithmic Information Theory (Kolmogorov complexity). It sets up a task/intelligent-system interaction — a training phase where an "intelligent system" generates a "skill program" from experience, and an evaluation phase where that frozen skill program is scored on new situations — then defines Generalization Difficulty as the fraction of a solution's algorithmic complexity that is not already explained by the shortest program that merely performed well during training:

GDθT,C = H(SolθT | TrainSoloptT,C)H(SolθT) // H(·) is Algorithmic (Kolmogorov) Complexity — the length of the shortest program producing a given output; GD is bounded in [0,1] by construction

The same machinery defines Priors (how much of a sufficient solution is already explained by the system's starting state, before any training) and Experience (the relevant, novel information the system extracts from its curriculum, step by step). With those three pieces in hand, the paper's central claim can finally be stated formally, not just intuitively: "the intelligence of a system is a measure of its skill-acquisition efficiency over a scope of tasks, with respect to priors, experience, and generalization difficulty." Two systems that start from similar priors and receive similar experience are compared by how much skill each converts that experience into — the more efficient converter is the more intelligent one. Crucially, this makes skill an output of intelligence rather than a synonym for it: a system can reach high skill with low intelligence simply by being handed enormous priors or enormous experience, exactly the "buying" failure mode described above.

Translating the formalism into design guidance, the paper argues a genuine intelligence benchmark must explicitly declare and control for its priors, must feature tasks unknown in advance to test-takers ("developer-aware generalization"), and must not be gameable by throwing more compute or data at it. It then proposes exactly such a benchmark: the Abstraction and Reasoning Corpus (ARC), a set of visual grid puzzles — closely modeled on Raven's Progressive Matrices — built on an explicit, enumerated set of "Core Knowledge" priors (Spelke & Kinzler, 2007) intended to approximate innate human knowledge rather than acquired, culture-specific knowledge: object cohesion/persistence/contact, goal-directedness, small-number counting, and basic geometry (symmetry, rotation, containment, line-drawing).

// what they found

The results

ARC ships as 1,000 unique tasks total: 400 training tasks (a development/familiarization set) and 600 evaluation tasks, the latter split into a 400-task public evaluation set and a 200-task private evaluation set held back specifically to prevent developers from hard-coding solutions to known tasks. Each task gives a test-taker a small number of input→output grid demonstrations (3.3 on average) and asks them to produce the correct output grid for one to three new test inputs — grids range from 1×1 to 30×30 cells using 10 possible symbol colors, and a test-taker gets 3 trials per test example, with binary (correct/incorrect) scoring. A system's overall ARC score is simply the fraction of the 600 evaluation tasks it solves exactly.

Two design facts stand out. First, the priors are stated explicitly and kept deliberately close to innate human cognition rather than acquired/cultural knowledge — no language, no pictures of real-world objects, no facts a person had to be taught. Second, the paper reports that "ARC has been found to be fully solvable by humans": every task in the corpus was solved by at least one member of a group of three high-IQ humans working independently without practice or verbal explanation, establishing feasibility even though (as the paper is careful to flag) this is a small, informal solver pool rather than a large-sample psychometric study.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

Unusually for a paper this ambitious, the "Weaknesses and future refinements" section (III.2) is candid to the point of doing some of the critic's job in advance — worth reading closely rather than skipping past, since it's a rare case of a benchmark paper pre-committing to its own limitations in writing.

The most consequential gap is that the paper's elaborate Algorithmic Information Theory formalism and its actual benchmark are only loosely coupled. Generalization Difficulty, Priors, and Experience are all defined in terms of Kolmogorov complexity — a quantity that is formally uncomputable for any nontrivial program, by construction (it reduces to the halting problem). The paper says as much itself in III.2's opening bullet: "generalization is not quantified... we do not offer a quantitative measure of the generalization of the evaluation set." ARC's actual score — fraction of 600 tasks solved in ≤3 binary-scored trials — implements none of the GD/Priors/Experience machinery; it's a conventional pass/fail benchmark that the theory motivates rhetorically but doesn't operationalize. That's not a fatal flaw (the theory's value is arguably more conceptual than instrumental — a "North Star," the paper says explicitly), but it means ARC should be read as inspired by the formal definition rather than as a direct measurement of it.

Two more limits the paper names directly: dataset scale (1,000 tasks total, with the paper's own admission that "there may be some amount of conceptual overlap across many tasks," leaving room for shortcut strategies that a larger, more diverse set would dilute) and evaluation granularity (binary per-task scoring over only 3 trials, which the paper itself calls "overly close-ended," proposing a richer interactive alternative it doesn't implement). And the "Core Knowledge priors may not be well understood and may not be well captured in ARC" bullet is an honest acknowledgment that the entire fairness argument for comparing humans and machines rests on a psychological framework (Spelke & Kinzler) whose completeness is itself contested.

Six years on, there's a further irony worth naming that the paper couldn't have anticipated: ARC (and its 2025 successor ARC-AGI-2, released once the original saturated under heavy test-time-compute approaches) has itself become a target for exactly the "buy performance with unlimited resources" pattern the paper warns against — large program-synthesis search budgets and expensive test-time compute have driven scores up in ways that don't obviously reflect the efficient, few-shot skill-acquisition the paper set out to measure, a live illustration of how hard it is to keep a benchmark honest even when its designer explicitly anticipated the failure mode.

baselines

Not applicable in the usual sense — this is a conceptual/theoretical paper plus a first benchmark release, not a model comparison. The comparison being drawn is qualitative (skill-based AI "moonshots" vs. the paper's proposed alternative), not a table of baseline numbers.

contamination

Explicitly designed against it via a private 200-task evaluation split withheld from public release specifically to prevent developers from hard-coding or overfitting solutions — one of the paper's stronger methodological choices, later validated by the field's growing contamination concerns for other benchmarks.

what's missed

The formal AIT-based definitions (Generalization Difficulty, Priors, Experience, Intelligence itself) are uncomputable in practice and are never connected numerically to ARC's actual pass/fail score — a reader could walk away thinking ARC measures GD directly, when the paper itself says it doesn't.

would raise confidence

A large-sample human validity study (the paper relies on 3 informal solvers); any empirical approximation connecting solve rate to the paper's own generalization-difficulty formalism, which the paper flags as future work but never delivers; and — with the benefit of hindsight — a periodic re-examination of whether ARC's difficulty is holding up against compute-heavy solvers rather than genuinely efficient ones.

// bottom line

The diagnosis — that skill-based AI evaluation confuses purchasable performance with genuine generalization — is sharp, well-argued, and has aged unusually well; it anticipated debates about scaling, data contamination, and "capability vs. understanding" that the field is still having years later. The formal machinery built to operationalize that diagnosis is honest about its own incompleteness and remains more of a conceptual scaffold than a working measurement tool, while ARC — the paper's concrete deliverable — has proven durable enough to spawn two further iterations, which is a rare form of validation for a first-of-its-kind benchmark.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. Chollet's central argument: skill at a specific task (chess, Go, Dota) can be "bought" with unlimited priors or unlimited training experience, without any accompanying generalization power — so task-specific skill is a poor proxy for intelligence, which the paper instead defines as skill-acquisition efficiency over a scope of tasks, relative to priors, experience, and generalization difficulty.
  2. The formal definitions (Generalization Difficulty, Priors, Experience) are built on Algorithmic Information Theory / Kolmogorov complexity — mathematically precise, but uncomputable in practice, and never numerically connected to the paper's own benchmark; the paper is explicit that this link remains future work.
  3. The concrete deliverable is ARC: 1,000 visual grid-reasoning tasks (400 train / 400 public eval / 200 held-out private eval) built on an explicit, human-relevant set of "Core Knowledge" priors, solvable by humans without training, and specifically designed to resist the "buy performance" failure mode the paper diagnoses — a design that proved durable enough to be followed by ARC-AGI-2 in 2025.
