2019 · Nature · Post-training & RL

Grandmaster level in StarCraft II using multi-agent reinforcement learning

StarCraft II hides information behind fog of war, offers roughly 1026 possible choices at every step, and has no single best strategy — its strategic metagame is cyclic, like rock-paper-scissors, so naive self-play can chase its own tail forever. AlphaStar answers with a "League" of continually evolving agents — main agents, main exploiters, and league exploiters whose sole purpose is to keep the population from cycling — bootstrapped on human replays and evaluated blind on Blizzard's own ladder. It reached Grandmaster level, above 99.8% of ranked human players, in all three StarCraft II races using one architecture, one algorithm, and one set of hyperparameters.

Oriol Vinyals, Igor Babuschkin, Wojciech M. Czarnecki, Michaël Mathieu, …, Chris Apps, David Silver — DeepMind, Team Liquid

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

By 2019, AI had already run the table on classic board games with perfect information — chess and shogi fell to AlphaZero's from-scratch self-play, and that recipe traces straight back to Go. StarCraft II breaks nearly every assumption that lineage leans on. The game hides information behind fog of war and a scrollable camera: an agent, like a human, sees only the units its own units currently observe, and loses attribute detail on anything outside its camera view. Its action space isn't a small, discrete set of legal moves but a deeply structured one — at every step the agent must choose what action type to issue (out of several hundred), who among its units to apply it to, where on the map or minimap to target, and when to act again, a combination the paper puts at roughly 1026 possible choices per step. A full match runs about ten minutes of real time but tens of thousands of individual game steps and thousands of chosen actions, with no natural move-by-move turn structure for a tree search to exploit the way it does in chess or Go.

Worse, StarCraft has no single best strategy to converge to. Its metagame is cyclic and non-transitive — army composition A beats B, B beats C, yet C beats A — so naive self-play can chase its own tail indefinitely, endlessly rediscovering counters to counters without ever producing a policy that's robust against the whole space of plausible human play. A decade of StarCraft AI competitions (the paper names the AIIDE, SSCAIT, and SC2AI ladders) had produced bots that got around this by simplifying the game, granting themselves superhuman capabilities such as viewing the entire map at once or executing tens of thousands of actions per minute, or leaning on hand-crafted heuristic sub-systems — but none had come close to matching a skilled human's overall play in the unrestricted full game. The closest contemporaneous precedent, OpenAI Five in Dota 2, also simplified game rules, restricted the hero pool, hard-coded some sub-systems, and didn't limit its agents to a camera-constrained view the way AlphaStar does.

// how it works

The method

AlphaStar plays through essentially the same interface a human does. At each step it receives an observation of every currently visible unit and its attributes — imperfect, since hidden units and camera-obscured detail behave exactly as they do for a human player — and must issue a structured action: what type (move, attack, build, …), who it applies to, where it targets, and when the next action will be issued (Fig. 1a). Because a computer can otherwise act far faster and more precisely than any human, the authors impose deliberate constraints: a monitoring layer caps the agent to roughly 22 non-duplicate actions per 5-second window, and its decisions are subject to network-latency-like delays — about 110 ms between observing a frame and acting during real-time evaluation, and an average 370 ms between chosen observation points. These specific limits were developed in consultation with, and the resulting play style was endorsed by, StarCraft II professional player Dario "TLO" Wünsch, who is himself a co-author on the paper.

The policy itself is a single deep network — 139 million weights, though only 55 million are needed at inference — built from general-purpose components rather than StarCraft-specific engineering: a self-attention mechanism over the variable-size, unordered set of visible units; a new "scatter connections" mechanism that fuses that per-unit information with the spatial minimap/camera view; a deep LSTM that carries memory through the partially observed sequence; and an auto-regressive policy with a recurrent pointer network to handle the combinatorial, structured action space. Rather than start from random play, AlphaStar is first trained by supervised imitation on 971,000 human replays — players rated above 3,500 MMR, the top 22% of the ladder — one policy per race, each additionally conditioned on a "strategy statistic" z distilled from the replay itself (its build order and cumulative unit/building/upgrade counts). Human data isn't discarded once reinforcement learning starts, either: agents are penalized whenever their action probabilities diverge from the supervised policy, and are periodically rewarded for following a sampled z — a mechanism the paper's own ablations (Fig. 3e) call critical to reaching good performance at all, since naive exploration is "highly improbable" to stumble, unaided, onto a precise multi-thousand-step sequence like "build and effectively use air units."

The reinforcement-learning objective is just the match outcome (win, loss, or draw; +1, −1, or 0), optimized by an actor-critic policy gradient updated asynchronously and off-policy from a replay buffer. Because StarCraft's action space is so large, the current and a previous policy rarely match closely over long trajectories, so standard off-policy corrections behave poorly; AlphaStar's fix is a hybrid — the policy is updated with clipped importance sampling (V-trace, from IMPALA) while the value function is updated, less conservatively, with TD(λ) — plus a new self-imitation update the authors call UPGO, which nudges the policy toward the actions taken on trajectories that turned out better than the value function expected.

The centerpiece answering the cyclic-strategy problem is League training (Fig. 1b, c), a generalization of fictitious self-play that computes a best response against a non-uniform mixture of past opponents rather than just the latest self-play partner. The league holds three kinds of agent. Main agents use prioritized fictitious self-play (PFSP): opponents are sampled with probability weighted by a function of the agent's win rate against them, concentrating training on whichever past players are still giving it the most trouble, while a fixed fraction of games are still played against the current self to keep learning fast. Main exploiters play only the current main agents, existing purely to surface exploitable weaknesses the main agents must then patch. League exploiters use the same PFSP mechanism as the main agents but are never targeted by main exploiters, so their job is to hunt for weaknesses shared across the whole league rather than one agent. Both exploiter types are periodically reset back to the supervised policy to keep rediscovering fresh, diverse strategies instead of converging onto a single lineage. Every agent periodically freezes and adds a copy of itself to the league as a new "player" — over the full run this created almost 900 distinct players.

The league was trained with three main agents, three main exploiters, and six league exploiters — one of each per race, plus two league exploiters per race — twelve populations run as separate actor–learner setups in parallel, each on 32 third-generation TPUs over 44 days. Per training agent, the infrastructure ran roughly 16,000 concurrent StarCraft II game instances feeding 16 TPU-backed actor tasks, streaming experience to a single 128-core TPU learner processing about 50,000 agent steps per second.

// what they found

The results

Evaluated blind and anonymously on Battle.net — Blizzard's real matchmaking ladder, StarCraft II balance patch 4.9.3, European server, roughly 90,000 actively ranked players — AlphaStar Final reached ratings of 6,275 Match Making Rating (MMR) as Protoss, 6,048 as Terran, and 5,835 as Zerg, above 99.8% of ranked human players and at Grandmaster level, the top of Battle.net's seven leagues, in all three races using one shared architecture, algorithm, and hyperparameter set (Fig. 2a). The earlier training snapshots show how much of that came from imitation versus league training: AlphaStar Supervised — pure imitation learning, no reinforcement learning at all — averaged 3,699 MMR, already above 84% of human players, while AlphaStar Mid (27 days into league training) reached the top 0.5% before the full 44-day run pushed it to Grandmaster.

On the specific worry that this is just superhuman clicking, the paper reports effective actions per minute (EPM) for AlphaStar Final against the humans it actually played (Fig. 2c): its mean EPM sat close to the human mean in every race — 183 versus 174 in Terran, 187 versus 165 in Protoss, 211 versus 205 in Zerg — but its peak EPM was consistently far below the best humans it faced: AlphaStar's own max ranged 571–823 across the three races, while its human opponents' max ranged 873–4,166 — even AlphaStar's single highest max (823, in Zerg) fell short of every race's human max. An earlier, less-restricted prototype had already beaten professional players Grzegorz "MaNa" Komincz and Dario "TLO" Wünsch 10 games to 0 across two five-game series in December 2018 — though under looser conditions than the Grandmaster run (a single map and race match-up, an uncapped camera, and less action-rate restriction), so that earlier demonstration doesn't carry the same evidentiary weight as the blind ladder evaluation above.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

The "general-purpose" framing deserves the same scrutiny applied elsewhere in DeepMind's game-playing lineage. Reaching Grandmaster took 32 TPU v3 devices per agent for 44 days, across twelve simultaneously trained populations, backed by roughly 16,000 concurrent game instances per agent and a 128-core TPU learner — an infrastructure footprint the paper describes in detail but never totals into a single compute or dollar figure, which makes independent reproduction a non-starter outside a similarly resourced lab. And unlike the AlphaGo Zero/AlphaZero side of that lineage, AlphaStar is explicitly not tabula rasa: it's bootstrapped from 971,000 curated human replays, and the paper's own ablation (Fig. 3e) reports human-data usage as critical to reaching good performance — the supervised-only agent alone, with zero reinforcement learning, already beats 84% of human players. That's a real and useful engineering result, but it's a different, less austere achievement than "a single algorithm that masters the game from nothing but the rules," and the paper never reports a full-scale run with human data removed entirely, so there's no way to see from this paper alone how much of Grandmaster-level play League training would earn on its own.

The interface-fairness case also rests more on consultation and testimony than on independent measurement. The action-rate cap, delay, and camera constraints were, in the authors' own words, "developed in consultation with professional StarCraft II players and Blizzard employees," and the paper's sole piece of qualitative validation is a quoted endorsement from Dario "TLO" Wünsch — who is also a co-author on the paper, not a disinterested outside reviewer. The authors similarly note that AlphaStar can, in principle, target locations slightly more precisely outside the camera view than a human could (targets are resolved on the same 256×256 grid regardless of camera position), but that "the agent does not seem to exploit these extra capabilities" — a claim backed by the authors' own reading of AlphaStar's play, not an independent audit against a matched population of humans given the same targeting precision.

The Battle.net evaluation is, by the authors' own admission, a snapshot rather than a stress test: playing blind, anonymous, opt-in games "estimate[s] AlphaStar's strength under approximately stationary conditions, but do not directly measure its susceptibility to exploitation under repeated play" — real esports metagames are adversarial and iterative, and no human ever got to specifically study and prepare a counter-strategy against a known AlphaStar opponent the way they would against a rival professional. The per-matchup breakdown (Fig. 2b) is also thinner than the headline numbers suggest: several individual race-versus-race cells rest on as few as four to ten games (Protoss-versus-Zerg is 4 wins from 4 games; Zerg-versus-Terran is 10 from 15), so while the aggregate 30–60-games-per-race Grandmaster placement is reasonably powered, the finer race-matchup matrix is not. Finally, four co-authors — including both lead/senior authors — disclose a provisional patent application covering the manuscript's contents, worth flagging given how much of the paper's prose frames League training as a general-purpose contribution well beyond StarCraft.

baselines

The only available "baseline" is the Battle.net ladder itself — real, active human opponents at every skill tier — an ecologically valid test with no need for a hand-picked academic benchmark; but the matchmaking system chose AlphaStar's maps and opponents, and blind/anonymous play, while a sensible design choice to avoid meta-gaming, also means no one outside DeepMind can rerun the exact same test population.

contamination

Not leakage in the usual train/test sense, but a related concern: the 971,000-replay supervised-training corpus is drawn from the same MMR-ranked ladder population AlphaStar is later scored against, so its "human-like" priors (from patch 4.8.2–4.8.6 replays) are tuned to the very metagame — evaluated on patch 4.9.3 — it's then scored inside.

what's missed

No full-scale ablation removing human-replay initialization entirely, so League training's own contribution above imitation learning is never directly isolated; the interface-fairness claim leans on a co-author's testimonial rather than an independent audit; and several individual race-matchup cells in Fig. 2b carry single-digit game counts.

would raise confidence

A from-scratch (no human data) League-training run at matching scale; an independent, non-author panel or blinded professional-player study auditing interface parity; and a disclosed total compute/cost figure to make the reproducibility bar explicit.

// bottom line

AlphaStar is a genuine, well-earned result: one architecture, one algorithm, and one set of hyperparameters reaching Grandmaster rank across three structurally different StarCraft II races is real evidence that League training's exploiter mechanism generalizes as an antidote to self-play cycling, and blind Battle.net evaluation is about as ecologically valid a test as this kind of claim can get. But "Grandmaster" here means a ladder placement under blind, one-off, opt-in conditions, reached only after bootstrapping from nearly a million human games and a training budget — 32 TPUs per agent across twelve parallel populations for 44 days — that puts reproduction out of reach for almost anyone outside a similarly resourced lab. That makes it a meaningfully different kind of achievement from the tabula rasa framing of AlphaGo Zero and AlphaZero, even though all three papers share DeepMind's self-play-and-league lineage.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. League training — main agents trained by prioritized fictitious self-play, main exploiters that attack only the current main agents, and league exploiters that hunt for weaknesses shared across the whole population — is a general, reusable answer to the cyclic, non-transitive strategy spaces that make naive self-play chase its own tail; almost 900 distinct players were created over the 44-day run.
  2. Human replay data isn't a mere bootstrap convenience here: AlphaStar's own ablations call it critical, and the pure-imitation agent alone, with no reinforcement learning whatsoever, already beats 84% of human players — a meaningfully different starting point from AlphaGo Zero and AlphaZero's from-scratch self-play.
  3. Grandmaster level — above 99.8% of ranked players in all three races — took 32 TPU v3 devices per agent, twelve populations trained in parallel, and 44 days of wall-clock time, an infrastructure footprint the paper never totals but that keeps this class of result well outside the reach of most labs.
