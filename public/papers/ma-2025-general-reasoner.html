General-Reasoner: Advancing LLM Reasoning Across All Domains

Zero-RL reasoning got stuck in math and code — the only places you can cheaply check an answer. General-Reasoner breaks out with a 230K-question, multi-domain verifiable dataset and a compact 1.5B generative verifier that judges answer equivalence, lifting MMLU-Pro and SuperGPQA by ~10 points without sacrificing math.

Xueguang Ma, Qian Liu, Dongfu Jiang, Ge Zhang, Zejun Ma, Wenhu Chen · Waterloo · Vector Institute · TikTok · M-A-P

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

The breakout result behind the reasoning-model wave is "Zero" reinforcement learning — DeepSeek-R1-Zero showed you can run RL directly on a base LLM, with no supervised fine-tuning stage, and watch reasoning emerge. But every successful open replication — SimpleRL, DAPO, Open-Reasoner-Zero, DeepScaleR — lives almost entirely in mathematics and code. That is not an accident. Math and code are the two domains where you get the two ingredients Zero-RL needs for free: abundant data (endless competition and exam problems on the web) and cheap, reliable answer verification (exact numeric match, symbolic equality, unit tests).

The moment you leave that comfort zone, both ingredients evaporate. A physics or finance question's correct answer might be a LaTeX expression, a vector, a string with units, a percentage, or a sentence — forms that a rule-based checker either rejects outright or matches incorrectly. So the field quietly substituted "reasoning" with "math reasoning," and the resulting models do not transfer. The paper's sharpest motivating data point: S1/S1.1, trained on math, degrades MMLU-Pro by 4–6% — getting better at math made the model worse at general knowledge reasoning.

General-Reasoner attacks both bottlenecks at once. (1) Build a large, diverse-domain dataset whose answers are still verifiable. (2) Replace the brittle rule-based checker with a small learned verifier that can judge whether two answers mean the same thing, in any format — and is cheap enough to call inside every RL step.

// how it works

The method

The recipe has two halves: a dataset (WebInstruct-verified) and a reward model (General-Verifier), both feeding a standard GRPO loop in the Zero-RL setting.

1. Mining verifiable questions from the web

The dataset starts from WebInstruct — roughly 5 million naturally-occurring instructions crawled from high-quality sources like StackExchange and educational portals. Most of those aren't usable as RL tasks: they lack a clean, checkable answer. The pipeline (Figure 1) filters hard. Entries are traced back to their original web pages and re-crawled for precise question–answer pairs; anything without a clearly human-written answer (paywalled, interaction-gated) is dropped. Gemini-1.5-Pro then extracts the subset with verifiable short answers (~1M questions), and Gemini-2.0-Flash tags each with answer type, subject, and difficulty — letting them filter out sub-university math to rebalance away from the math-heavy long tail.

The final quality gate is a solvability filter: Gemini-2.0-Flash generates eight candidate solutions per question, and they discard any question where all eight fail (noisy / unsolvable) or all eight succeed (too easy to give RL signal). What survives is ~230K questions spanning Mathematics (33.9%), Physics (23.7%), Chemistry (10.4%), Business (9.99%), Finance (5.75%), Economics, History, Biology and more — with answers as Floats (29.4%), Expressions (21.4%), Multiple-Choice (13.4%), Integers, Strings, Lists, Booleans, Matrices.

2. A compact generative verifier

The core idea: a rule-based checker only knows string/numeric equality, so it rejects correct answers written differently — "4 + 8t, 1 + 2t, 17 − t" vs. the boxed "x = 4 + 8t, y = 1 + 2t, z = 17 − t", or "4.9 N·m" vs "4.9 J". General-Verifier is a 1.5B-parameter model (initialized from Qwen2.5-Math-1.5B) trained on the Gemini-generated solutions and equivalence annotations. Given the question q, the ground-truth g and the student answer s, it produces a chain-of-thought followed by a binary equivalence verdict:

ŷ ∼ P( y | q, g, s ),   ŷ = [ ycot , <sep> , ylabel ] // context-aware CoT verification → true / false equivalence, used as the RL reward

That verdict is the reward inside GRPO. The policy samples a group of G responses per question and updates with the clipped surrogate, normalizing each reward against the group's mean and standard deviation:

Âi = Ri − mean({R1…G})std({R1…G}) // GRPO group-relative advantage — no value network; the verifier supplies R

The reward shaping is deliberately simple: +1 if the verifier passes the answer, −0.5 if no answer can even be extracted (no \boxed{}), and a small length penalty −0.05 × min(10, |lengt − lenans|) to discourage rambling. Training is true Zero-RL — GRPO straight from Qwen2.5 (7B, 14B) and Qwen3 (4B, 14B) base models, up to 700 steps (Qwen2.5) or 400 (Qwen3) on 4×8 H100, built on verl. Average response length grows only from ~700 to ~1000 tokens.

// what they found

The results

Across 12 reasoning benchmarks, Zero-RL on the diverse dataset beats both the base and the instruction-tuned model — and, crucially, beats math-only RL on general benchmarks without losing math. On the Qwen2.5-7B backbone, General-Reasoner hits 58.9% on MMLU-Pro (base 47.7, instruct 57.0) and 34.2% on SuperGPQA. The 14B Qwen2.5 model reaches 66.6% MMLU-Pro (base 53.3, instruct 62.7), 43.4% GPQA-Diamond, 39.5% SuperGPQA. The headline efficiency claim: General-Reasoner-4B (Qwen3) already surpasses Qwen2.5-7B on MMLU-Pro (62.8 vs 58.9).

The strongest model, General-Reasoner-Qw3-14B, reaches 56.1% on GPQA and 54.4% on TheoremQA — exceeding GPT-4o's 50.0% and 43.6% on the same splits, from Zero-RL alone. On math (Table 3), the diverse-data model slightly out-scores the math-focused SimpleRL: the Qw2.5-14B variant averages 53.9 vs SimpleRL-14B's 50.7, and the Qw3-14B variant averages 58.8 (MATH-500 83.8, Minerva 68.0, AMC 70.0). And it does this without overthinking: where DeepScaleR-1.5B needs ~32K-token traces and 18 minutes on 4×H100 to score 35% on the MMLU-Pro CS split, General-Reasoner-4B finishes in 1.5 minutes at 61%.

Two ablations isolate the contributions. Data diversity (Table 4): on the 14B backbone, the full diverse dataset beats a math-only diet on every metric, including math itself (53.9 vs 48.6) — diversity doesn't trade off against math, it helps. The verifier (Figure 4 / Table 5): swapping the rule-based checker for the learned one during 120 steps of Qwen3-4B training lifts MMLU-Pro from 58.1 → 60.1 and GPQA from 37.9 → 39.4 — a steady gap that widens through training.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

The central claim — diverse verifiable data + a learned verifier extends Zero-RL beyond math without a math tax — is well-supported by the data-diversity ablation, which is the paper's cleanest experiment. Two other claims deserve more scrutiny, and two threats are left unmeasured.

The marquee numbers ride a very strong backbone. "Matches or beats GPT-4o" is true on exactly the two splits cherry-picked for it (GPQA, TheoremQA); GPT-4o still leads MMLU-Pro 74.6 vs 70.3. And the headline jumps are measured against the base model (MMLU-Pro 53.3 → 66.6, +13.3 at 14B). Against the already-strong instruct models the RL recipe's gains are far more modest — roughly +2 to +9 points on Qwen2.5 (MMLU-Pro +1.9 / +3.9, GPQA +5.0 / +2.0, peaking at +8.7 on TheoremQA-7B) — and on the Qwen3-14B backbone they shrink to small-and-mixed: General-Reasoner-Qw3-14B scores 70.3 MMLU-Pro vs the instruct model's 70.9 (a regression) and 56.1 GPQA vs 54.8. So "our RL recipe" and "the base model is already great" are entangled, and the paper never runs the backbone-controlled, matched-instruct ablation that would separate them.

The verifier is validated against the teacher that trained it. General-Verifier learns from Gemini-2.0-Flash's annotations, and Figure 2's quality metric is agreement with Gemini-2.0-Flash — so its "accuracy" is, by construction, fidelity to the supervisor, not to ground truth. If Gemini systematically mis-grades a format or domain, the verifier inherits the bias and silently rewards it during RL. No human-graded verifier accuracy is reported. (The math evals add a second model-judge: answer equivalence there is checked by GPT-4o.) Model-based rewards are also the classic reward-hacking surface; the short training (≤700 steps, response length only 700→1000 tokens) likely keeps hacking from surfacing, but it isn't probed.

"All domains" still means "short verifiable answers." Every eval — MMLU-Pro, GPQA, SuperGPQA (MCQ), TheoremQA (short-form) — has a checkable short answer. That's the same structural constraint as math, just spread over more subjects. Open-ended reasoning (multi-step proofs, long-form argument, anything without a boxed answer) is outside the evaluated frontier, so the title oversells the scope a little.

baselines

Fair within scale (base / instruct / SimpleRL / Open-Reasoner-Zero / Nemotron-CrossThink), but the closest all-domain baseline, Nemotron-CrossThink, is only reported on one 7B backbone and missing TheoremQA/BBEH. GPT-4o / o1-mini / DeepSeek-R1 are reference rows, not controlled comparisons.

contamination

Unaddressed and the biggest gap. Training is web-crawled from StackExchange / educational sites — the same sources MMLU-Pro and TheoremQA draw from. No decontamination against eval sets is reported. SuperGPQA's "not findable on Google" design partly mitigates it for that one benchmark.

what's missed

Mostly single greedy runs (only AIME averages 32 samples), so no variance/seed bars. Verifier accuracy is agreement-with-Gemini, not human-judged. The strongest results lean on Qwen3 with no backbone-controlled isolation of the RL gain.

would raise confidence

A decontamination report; human-graded verifier accuracy on a held-out set; the matched-instruct ablation on Qwen3; an open-ended (non-short-answer) reasoning eval; multi-seed variance on the headline tables.

// bottom line

A genuinely useful result — the data-diversity ablation alone justifies the paper, and the learned verifier is a clean fix for the "can't check non-math answers" bottleneck that kept Zero-RL boxed into math. Just read the GPT-4o-matching, instruct-beating headlines through the lens of a strong Qwen3 backbone and a verifier graded only against the model that taught it, with eval contamination left unmeasured.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. Verification, not data, was the bottleneck for general-domain RL. Swap the rule-based checker for a 1.5B learned verifier that judges answer equivalence, and Zero-RL works across physics, chemistry, finance and more — the same trick that unlocked math, generalized.
  2. Diversity doesn't cost you math. The cleanest result: training on the full diverse dataset beats a math-only diet on every metric including math (53.9 vs 48.6 at 14B). Breadth and depth weren't a trade-off here.
  3. Short and efficient beats long and overthought. ~1000-token traces match or exceed 32K-token DeepScaleR-style models — and General-Reasoner-4B already passes Qwen2.5-7B on MMLU-Pro, a real parameter-efficiency story.
