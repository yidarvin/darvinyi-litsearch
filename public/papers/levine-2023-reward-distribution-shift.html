A Baseline Analysis of Reward Models' Ability To Accurately Analyze Foundation Models Under Distribution Shift

Reward models are the linchpin of RLHF — yet every measurement that they "work" comes from test data drawn from the training distribution. Scale AI dials a reward model off-distribution and finds it breaks asymmetrically: out-of-distribution responses crater accuracy and calibration, while out-of-distribution prompts barely register — then borrows a classification energy score, using only the output logits, to flag when the reward model can no longer be trusted.

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

A reward model (RM) is the part of RLHF that turns "humans prefer this answer" into a number. You collect pairs of responses where one is preferred, train a model to score the preferred one higher, and then use that model two ways: to optimize the policy LLM during training, and — increasingly — at inference time to judge how well a fresh response adheres to the rewarded behavior. The RM is, in effect, the proxy for human judgment that the whole alignment pipeline leans on.

But every piece of evidence that a reward model "works" comes from a held-out test set drawn from the same distribution as its preference training data. Real deployments are not stationary. Users send prompts unlike anything in the preference set, and the policy generates responses that drift as it is optimized. In ordinary classification this is a well-known failure mode: under distribution shift, models lose accuracy and become miscalibrated — confident exactly when they should not be (Ovadia et al., 2019). Whether reward models inherit that fragility, and whether you can even detect when an RM has wandered off its training distribution, was essentially unmeasured.

Concurrent work had started to circle the question — Lightman et al. (2023) saw RM performance drop across different STEM question types, and Clymer et al.'s Generalization Analogies (2023) found degradation under several shift types — but both shifted prompts and responses together. This paper's contribution is to decompose the shift: hold one side in-distribution, push the other out, and watch accuracy, calibration, and detectability separately for prompt-shift versus response-shift.

// how it works

The method

The setup is deliberately a baseline: one off-the-shelf reward model — OpenAssistant's deberta-v3-large-v2 — evaluated on the Summarize-from-Feedback preference dataset (Stiennon et al., 2020), where each "response" is a candidate summary and the human label says which of two summaries is better. The RM is scored on two axes. Reward accuracy asks whether it ranks the human-preferred response above the other; the model is correct on an example when the preferred response gets the higher logit.

accrwd = 1N Σi 1[ Lrwd(pi, ripreferred) > Lrwd(pi, riother) ] // the RM is "right" when it scores the preferred response higher than the rejected one

The second axis is calibration — does the RM's confidence match its accuracy? Confidence is the max-softmax over the two response logits (a binary forced choice), and miscalibration is summarized by Expected Calibration Error (ECE) over 10 confidence bins: bin predictions by confidence, and measure the gap between average confidence and actual accuracy in each bin. A perfectly calibrated RM that says "80% sure" is right 80% of the time.

Two knobs for distribution shift

To study shift cleanly, the authors induce it two ways, each chosen to be controllable rather than realistic:

  • Natural (lingual) shift. English is in-distribution (it matches Summarize-from-Feedback). Out-of-distribution prompts and responses are produced by machine-translating the English into French, Spanish, and German with OPUS-MT. Crossing {ID, OOD} × {prompt, response} gives four cells — and crucially lets them shift the prompt and the response independently.
  • Artificial shift. Each word is perturbed with probability p — inserted, deleted, or replaced by a random same-language word — sweeping p from 0 to 1 over 10 trials per setting. This turns OOD-ness into a continuous dial. The authors are explicit that word-perturbation is not representative of any real shift, and warn that at high p the preference label itself may stop being meaningful; it exists only to trace the shape of the degradation curve.

Detecting shift from logits alone

To flag when an RM is operating off-distribution, the paper re-uses the classification Energy Score (Liu et al., 2020) but feeds it the two reward logits instead of class logits:

Srwd = −log( e Lrwd(p, r0) + e Lrwd(p, r1) ) // low energy ≈ in-distribution, high energy ≈ out-of-distribution — computed from output logits only

The design constraint is the point: this score needs only the output logits — no internal activations, no inference-time backpropagation, and no access to the RM's training set. That matters because reward-model training data is often unreleased and deployed RMs may expose nothing but their scores. The authors prefer Energy Score over Maximum Softmax Probability (MSP), citing the OOD-detection literature where energy reliably beats MSP.

// what they found

The results

Under the natural lingual shift, accuracy strictly degrades — but the damage is lopsided. Translating the response out of English costs roughly 6.6 points of accuracy and pushes ECE from 14.53% up to ~20%. Translating the prompt costs only ~2 points and, oddly, leaves calibration slightly better than the all-English baseline:

// Table 1 — averaged over French/Spanish/German OOD; ± stds in the paper are ≤0.73 pt (acc) and ≤0.56 pt (ECE).

The artificial-shift sweep makes the asymmetry sharper and surfaces the paper's headline calibration finding. As perturbation rises, accuracy falls monotonically for every condition, fastest when both sides are perturbed. The ECE panel shows a novel calibration paradigm for OOD responses: just off-distribution (near-OOD), the model stays overconfident while accuracy drops — ECE spikes to roughly 0.25 around p ≈ 0.3–0.4 — then far-OOD its confidence collapses and "catches up" with accuracy, so calibration looks excellent (ECE ~0.05, even better than the ID value). OOD prompts barely move any curve: even with completely random prompts, accuracy and confidence hold up. Reward models, in other words, are far more sensitive to the response than to the prompt.

Notably, the lingual numbers in Table 1 line up with roughly a 15% perturbation (near-OOD) on the artificial curve, not the far-OOD end. The authors conjecture this is because the RM's DeBERTa backbone was pre-trained on a multilingual corpus: cross-lingual correlations fold translated inputs into a shared representation space, so non-English text reads as only mildly OOD to the model.

Can you detect it?

The logits-only energy score detects shift, and — consistent with everything above — detects response shift far more readily than prompt shift. On the lingual data, AUROC for catching an OOD response is 72.84%, versus only 63.11% for an OOD prompt; with both shifted it reaches 77.12%. FPR@95 (false-positive rate at 95% recall, lower is better) stays high — ~60–72% — underscoring that this is a usable signal, not a sharp one.

The artificial sweep tells the same story more cleanly: detection AUROC climbs toward ~1.0 as perturbation grows, with the response and prompt+response curves rising fast while the prompt-only curve lags badly — subtle prompt shifts are the hardest thing to catch. (An appendix confirms MSP is the weaker detector, as the OOD literature predicts.)

// does it hold up?

The eval read

The honest framing in the title — "a baseline analysis" — is doing real work. The whole study rests on a single reward model (a ~0.4B-parameter DeBERTa-v3-large from OpenAssistant), a single preference dataset (Summarize-from-Feedback), and a single task (summarization). None of the headline patterns are shown to survive a change of model, scale, or domain, and the modern reality — large generative reward models and LLM-as-judge scorers — is entirely outside the evidence. The only OOD-detection method actually pitted against the energy score is MSP; the Mahalanobis / KNN / gradient detectors the paper cites are skipped on practicality grounds, so "energy is the right detector" is argued from the literature, not demonstrated here.

The more subtle issue is that the two shift constructions each smuggle in a confound. The lingual "OOD" is machine-translated, so an OOD response mixes genuine distribution shift with translation degradation and with the question of whether the human preference label even survives translation. That muddies the flagship asymmetry: perturbing the response alters the very text being judged, while perturbing the prompt only changes context — so "responses matter more than prompts" is partly mechanical, not purely a fact about reward models. And the celebrated far-OOD result — "calibration better than in-distribution" — is close to an artifact: on a binary forced choice, as inputs become gibberish, accuracy → 50% and confidence → 50%, so ECE is trivially small. The RM is not reliable far-OOD; it is appropriately clueless with low confidence, on data the authors themselves admit may carry meaningless labels.

What is genuinely valuable is the part that gets less fanfare: the near-OOD overconfidence spike (ECE up to ~0.25) is exactly the regime a real deployment lands in, and it is where the RM is most dangerous — wrong and sure of it. That, plus the clean prompt-vs-response decomposition, is the durable contribution.

baselines

One RM (OpenAssistant DeBERTa-v3-large), one dataset, one task. For detection, only MSP is run against the energy score — stronger OOD detectors (Mahalanobis, KNN, gradient methods) are cited but not evaluated.

contamination

No train/test leakage in the usual sense, but the lingual "OOD" is OPUS-MT output, so distribution shift is confounded with translation quality and with whether the preference label still holds after translation.

what's missed

"Better-than-ID calibration far-OOD" is largely a chance-collapse artifact of a binary forced choice, on data with dubious labels. The real, underweighted signal is near-OOD overconfidence. Detection of prompt-only shift (AUROC ~63%, FPR@95 ~71%) is barely above chance.

would raise confidence

Multiple and modern reward models (incl. generative/LLM-judge RMs); real shifts (topic, style, jailbroken responses) rather than synthetic perturbation + MT; separating translation quality from shift; and tying OOD detection to a downstream RLHF outcome — does flagging OOD actually curb reward over-optimization?

// bottom line

A clean, self-aware baseline that lands one durable, non-obvious insight — reward models fail asymmetrically, with OOD responses far more damaging than OOD prompts, and near-OOD overconfidence as the true danger zone — plus a logits-only detector for it. But the evidence base is narrow (one small RM, one dataset, synthetic and machine-translated shifts, MSP-only baseline), and the flashiest line about far-OOD calibration is mostly an artifact of chance. Read it as a hypothesis-generator about reward-model fragility, not a settled measurement.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. Reward models break asymmetrically under distribution shift: an OOD response drags accuracy from 72.3% to 65.7% and ECE from 14.5% to ~20%, while an OOD prompt barely moves either — even a fully random prompt. If you're watching for an RM going off the rails, watch the responses, not the prompts.
  2. The dangerous regime is near-OOD, not far-OOD: just past the training distribution the RM stays overconfident while accuracy falls, spiking ECE to ~0.25. Far-OOD it looks well-calibrated, but that is mostly a binary forced choice collapsing to chance with low confidence — not genuine reliability.
  3. You can flag untrustworthy inputs with only the output logits: the classification energy score, adapted to the two reward logits, detects response shift well (AUROC → ~1.0 as shift grows) without internal activations or the RM's training set — though it is weak on prompt-only shift (AUROC ~63%).
