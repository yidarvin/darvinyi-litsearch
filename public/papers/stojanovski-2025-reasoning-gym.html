REASONING GYM: Reasoning Environments for Reinforcement Learning with Verifiable Rewards

A library of 100+ procedurally generated, algorithmically verifiable reasoning tasks — from polynomial algebra to Rubik's cubes — that emit virtually unlimited training data at controllable difficulty. The same generators expose where frontier models fall off a "difficulty cliff," and RL-train a small model whose gains transfer across domains and to external benchmarks.

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

The recent leap in LLM reasoning — OpenAI o1, DeepSeek-R1, QwQ-32B — is powered by Reinforcement Learning with Verifiable Rewards (RLVR): instead of a learned reward model, you score a rollout by checking whether its final answer is correct. That makes the recipe only as good as its supply of checkable problems, and that supply is the bottleneck.

Today RLVR data comes from two places, both fragile. Hand-curated question–answer sets (GSM8K, MATH) are expensive, finite, and quickly saturated. Internet-scraped content is cheap but noisy, error-prone, and — as more of the web becomes model-generated — neither sustainable nor reliable. Worse, any fixed dataset can be memorized: once a benchmark leaks into pretraining, a high score may reflect recall, not reasoning. And a fixed dataset gives you no dial to make problems harder once a model masters them.

The missing piece is an open, scalable source of reasoning tasks that are (1) automatically verifiable, (2) generated fresh so no two instances repeat, and (3) tunable in difficulty so you can build curricula and probe the ceiling. Reasoning Gym (RG) is that source: a library of procedural generators rather than a frozen dataset.

// how it works

Generators, not datasets

RG ships over 100 task generators with paired verifiers spanning algebra, arithmetic, geometry, algorithmic thinking, formal logic, graph theory, pattern recognition / induction, ARC-style abstraction, code, cognition, and games/puzzles. Each task is not a fixed list of QA pairs but a generative algorithm exposed through three kinds of parameters:

  • Difficulty parameters directly scale complexity — graph node counts, polynomial degree, word length.
  • Structural parameters change the problem's nature — dimensionality, constraint types, proof depth.
  • Stylistic parameters vary presentation without changing difficulty — variable names, number formats, framing.

Three design principles hold the library together. (P1) Algorithmic Verifiability: every task is graded automatically with no human in the loop, which is what makes it safe to put in an RL reward. (P2) Large Solution Spaces: tasks are built so that guessing or template-matching pays poorly, rewarding generalizable strategies and blunting reward hacking. (P3) Parametric Difficulty Control: because difficulty is a knob, you can run a curriculum — raise the level only once the model is reliably solving the current one.

Because instances are sampled, not stored, no two are identical — exact-instance memorization is impossible and the ground-truth data-generating process is fully exposed. During RL the reward is a sum of an accuracy term (the verifier's correct/incorrect signal) and a small auxiliary format term that rewards well-formed output:

R = 1.0 · raccuracy + 0.2 · rformat // total training reward: a verifier-checked accuracy signal plus a lightweight formatting bonus (accuracy worth 1.0, format worth 0.2)

The experiments train Qwen2.5-3B-Instruct with GRPO (the critic-free policy-gradient method from DeepSeekMath), and evaluate both off-the-shelf frontier models and RG-trained checkpoints on held-out RG tasks and external benchmarks. The full experimental run cost roughly 1,500 A6000 GPU-hours on rented cloud hardware.

// what they found

A measuring stick and a training signal

As an evaluation, RG separates the field cleanly. On hard configurations, reasoning-optimized models form a leading group — o3-mini 63.51%, DeepSeek-R1 59.52%, Grok-3-mini 55.06% — well above general-purpose systems like Llama-4-Maverick (41.50%), QwQ-32B (40.52%), Claude-3.5-Sonnet (40.33%), Llama-4-Scout (30.01%), and Gemma-3-27B (20.26%). The ~22-point gap between the best reasoning and non-reasoning models suggests RLVR buys a step change, not a marginal gain. Tasks that render visual-spatial concepts in text (ARC, cognition, games) stay hardest for everyone.

The signature finding is the difficulty cliff: pushing a task from its easy to hard configuration collapses accuracy. For o3-mini the steepest drops are code −71.9%, graphs −33.8%, geometry −33.1%, and algorithms −25.6%; DeepSeek-R1 mirrors it (code −61.8%, graphs −29.6%, algorithms −27.9%). Difficulty is a parameter here, so these cliffs are measured, not anecdotal — evidence that current "reasoning" is often shallow template application that breaks under scale.

As a training signal, RLVR on RG data improves a small base model in three widening circles. Intra-domain: training on one slice of a category and testing on held-out tasks from the same category lifts every category measured (Acc@3):

A model trained only on algorithmic tasks gains +29.1 on algebra and +22.3 on geometry; logic training adds +13.3 on cognition and +9.1 on graphs; even games training transfers +21.8 to algebra. Notably, the Games category starts at 0.0% accuracy and reaches a measurable 3.3% after RLVR — bootstrapping a capability from scratch, albeit a tiny one.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

Read RG in two layers. As infrastructure — an open, contamination-resistant, infinitely scalable source of verifiable reasoning tasks with difficulty knobs — it largely delivers, and that alone is a real contribution. The capability claims built on top of it are directionally convincing but rest on a thin experimental base, so the magnitudes deserve caution.

The frontier-model evaluation and the RL-training experiments are two different studies wearing one title. The headline difficulty-cliff numbers come from API models scored zero-shot, where text-rendered spatial puzzles (ASCII art, FIGlet fonts, board diagrams) tax tokenization and output-parsing as much as reasoning — the paper itself flags "visual concepts in text format" as the hardest, which is partly a representation artifact, not pure reasoning failure. Meanwhile all the transfer evidence comes from a single small base model (Qwen2.5-3B-Instruct), on 50-example held-out sets over 3 seeds, reported as Acc@3 (three attempts) rather than Acc@1. The most striking result — Games 0.0 → 3.3% — is near noise in absolute terms.

The biggest missing control is a data baseline: there is no comparison against RL-training on an equivalent budget of existing curated data (e.g. MATH/GSM8K directly). So the external gains (MATH +9.7, BBH +7.66 — but BBH only reaches 16.34% from a very low 8.68% floor) show RG data helps a weak 3B model, not that procedurally generated data is better than the curated data it aims to replace. Some of the lift may be instruction-following / format alignment to the eval harness rather than new reasoning. Transfer tables also omit confidence intervals.

baselines

All RLVR results ride on one 3B model with no "equivalent existing data" control; the frontier eval mixes reasoning and non-reasoning models across different task configs. Acc@3 and n=50 holdouts inflate and widen the numbers.

contamination

RG's core strength: procedural generation makes exact-instance leakage near-impossible. But task-type familiarity (Sudoku, Rubik's, GSM-symbolic, base conversion) is almost certainly in pretraining, so zero-shot frontier scores aren't fully leakage-free even if no specific instance is.

what's missed

Verifiers grade the final answer, not reasoning faithfulness — a model can reach a checkable answer by an unsound path. The 0.2 format reward dominates the early reward spike, muddying "genuine skill acquisition," and text-rendering effects are entangled with the spatial-reasoning claims.

would raise confidence

Repeat transfer on larger base models; add a curated-data control at matched compute; report CIs on the transfer tables; and disentangle tokenization/format effects from reasoning on the text-rendered spatial tasks.

// bottom line

Reasoning Gym is best taken as an infrastructure paper — a clean, open, difficulty-tunable, contamination-resistant well of verifiable reasoning tasks, which is genuinely useful for both eval and RLVR. The cross-domain and external-transfer findings are promising signals, but on a single 3B model, small eval sets, and without a "more curated data" control, treat the specific gains as suggestive rather than settled.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. Procedural generation breaks the RLVR data ceiling. 100+ generators with automatic verifiers emit unlimited, exact-answer-checkable instances at tunable difficulty — so training data can't be memorized and curricula become trivial to build.
  2. Frontier "reasoning" is shallow under load. Reasoning models (o3-mini 63.5%, DeepSeek-R1 59.5%) beat non-reasoning ones by ~22 points, but hard configs cause steep cliffs (o3-mini code −71.9%, graphs −33.8%), and text-rendered spatial puzzles defeat everyone.
  3. RLVR on RG transfers — within a thin study. Training Qwen2.5-3B shows intra-domain (up to +11.7), cross-domain (algorithmic→algebra +29.1), and external gains (MATH +9.7, BBH +7.66), with curriculum beating fixed difficulty (+40.67 on Spell Backwards) — all on one small base model with no curated-data control.
