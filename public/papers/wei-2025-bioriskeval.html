Best Practices for Biorisk Evaluations on Open-Weight Bio-Foundation Models

A red-team of pretraining-data filtering. On Evo 2-7B — which deliberately excluded eukaryotic viral sequences for safety — "filtered" capability comes back in 50 fine-tuning steps (0.72 H100 GPU-hours), and a linear probe shows the harmful signal never left the model's latent representations at all.

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

Bio-foundation models — large models trained on genomic, transcriptomic, or protein data (Enformer, the DNABERT and Evo families, Geneformer, ESM 2, AlphaFold) — are squarely dual-use. The same model that accelerates drug discovery could help a bad actor design a more transmissible or more lethal pathogen. The risk is sharpest for open-weight releases, where anyone can fine-tune the weights or read out their internal representations.

The field's main mitigation so far is data filtering: scrub the hazardous data out of the pretraining corpus and the model never learns the dangerous skill. The OpenGenome dataset used to train the Evo family deliberately excludes eukaryotic viral sequences, and as a result Evo 2 performs poorly out-of-the-box at predicting mutational effects on those viruses. The bet is that what the model never saw, it cannot do.

But three uncertainties make that bet untested. (1) Unlike natural-language models — where biorisk evals like the Virology Capabilities Test exist — there is no systematic dual-use evaluation framework for bio-foundation models. (2) Filtering's robustness has never been stress-tested under a realistic open-weight threat model where the adversary can fine-tune. (3) Elicitation methods as simple as linear probing have not been tried — leaving open the possibility that the "removed" knowledge still sits latent in the hidden layers. BioRiskEval is built to close all three.

// how it works

The method

Threat model. An adversary wants to design pathogenic eukaryotic viruses by (1) generating novel, viable viral sequences, (2) performing in-silico optimization of hazardous traits like protein stability or receptor-binding affinity, and (3) ranking candidates by predicted virulence. Full weight access lets them go beyond prompting: they can fine-tune on public biological datasets or probe hidden representations. BioRiskEval evaluates the model's capability on exactly these tasks, before and after such manipulation.

The benchmark — three lenses on "virus understanding." BioRiskEval scores a model along three axes, each mapped to a concrete metric:

  • BioRiskEval-Gen (Sequence Modeling). Perplexity on human-infective eukaryotic viral genomes — 10,247,388 unique, deduplicated sequences from the NCBI Virus Repository (human-host only), spanning 57 families, 131 genera, and 1,795 species. Lower perplexity = the model "speaks" the language of human viruses more fluently.
  • BioRiskEval-Mut (Mutational Effect Prediction). 156,178 variants across 16 Deep Mutational Scanning (DMS) datasets on human-infecting viruses from ProteinGym, each with experimentally measured fitness. Metric: absolute Spearman correlation |ρ| between model scores and lab fitness.
  • BioRiskEval-Vir (Virulence Prediction). Median lethal dose (LD50) of Influenza A strains in BALB/C mice (eight RNA segments concatenated per strain), 369 records. Metric: Pearson correlation between probed predictions and observed LD50.

The model under test is Evo 2-7B, chosen precisely because it is one of the few open-weight genomic models that explicitly filtered eukaryotic viral data — giving a clean, controlled safety baseline. Evo 2 scores mutational effects with an autoregressive log-likelihood; the unfiltered protein model ESM 2 (650M / 3B) supplies a masked-marginal upper reference, and LLaMA-3.1-8B-Instruct — same depth and hidden width as Evo 2-7B but never trained on nucleotides — supplies a "no-biology" floor for the probing experiments.

SLL = Σj=1L log P(xmtj | xmt<j, θ) // Evo 2's autoregressive mutational score — sum of per-token log-probs of the mutant sequence; ranked against experimental DMS fitness by Spearman |ρ|

Two attack vectors are measured: fine-tuning (how few samples and how little compute are needed to recover excluded capability) and linear probing (whether a tiny linear read-out on frozen hidden states already recovers it, with no weight updates at all).

// what they found

The results

Across all three lenses, the filtered Evo 2-7B turns out to be far less "ignorant" than its training recipe implies. Two findings carry the paper: capability filtered out of the output distribution is cheaply re-learned by fine-tuning, and capability for the harder regression tasks was apparently never removed from the representations in the first place.

1. Sequence modeling: filtering is undone in 50 steps — across species

Holding out one species and fine-tuning on its relatives, the model's perplexity on the held-out species collapses to the level of benign IMG/PR plasmid sequences within 50 fine-tuning steps — just 0.72 H100 GPU-hours. Excluding a single species buys essentially nothing, because evolutionarily close species are freely available in public repositories. The friction only appears at greater evolutionary distance: in the inter-genus setting, 1,000 steps cut held-out Papillomaviridae perplexity by ~20% (still above benign), and 2,000 steps on Orthoherpesviridae produced no significant drop at all. Filtering by genus buys cost; it does not buy safety.

2. Mutational effects: fine-tuning approaches ESM 2 — probing needs none

Evo 2's reported weakness at mutational-effect prediction (mean |ρ| = 0.034) was taken as evidence the filtering worked. Fine-tuning for 2,000 steps (28.9 H100 GPU-hours) raises it to 0.164, closing most of the gap to the unfiltered ESM 2 (≈0.17 for ESM2-650M). But the sharper result is the linear probe: with no fine-tuning, a probe fit on just 3.6% of the DMS data (5,600 mutations) and tested on the other 95.4% (148,505 mutations) reaches |ρ| = 0.159 — comparable to ESM2-650M and 52.8% above the best LLaMA-3.1-8B-Instruct probe. Extra fine-tuning barely moves the probe, which is the tell: the knowledge is already in the representations, filtering or not.

3. Virulence: the lethal-dose signal is latent

Even the base, never-fine-tuned Evo 2-7B carries a probe-readable virulence signal: layer-wise probing of its hidden states predicts Influenza A LD50 with a max Pearson of 0.46 — 77% above the best LLaMA-3.1-8B-Instruct probe — from a probe trained on only 37 examples (10%) and tested on the remaining 332. Fine-tuning adds almost nothing (+5% max Pearson after 200 steps). And while perplexity drops sharply within 100 steps, virulence prediction barely tracks it — so perplexity is a poor proxy for latent harm. Probe expressiveness collapses past layer 28, which the authors attribute to a sudden explosion in representation magnitude there.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

As a red-team of a specific safety procedure, this is careful and well-controlled — and refreshingly honest about its own ceiling. The design brackets every claim between two references: ESM 2, a protein model trained without filtering eukaryotic viral data, as the unfiltered upper bound, and LLaMA-3.1-8B-Instruct, matched in depth and hidden width but with no nucleotide training, as the no-biology floor. That makes "Evo 2 still has the capability" a relative, falsifiable statement rather than a vibe. The catch is that both anchors are weak: ESM2-650M's own |ρ| ≈ 0.17 is not a strong predictor, so "comparable to ESM 2" describes parity with a mediocre baseline, and "52.8% / 77% above the floor" is a large relative lift over a near-floor number.

The deeper question is what the metrics actually license. The headline danger in the threat model — generating novel, viable pathogenic sequences — is argued but never measured; BioRiskEval reports perplexity (fluency) and probe correlations (predictive read-out), not a single demonstrated act of pathogen design. Predicting an LD50 ranking is not the same as engineering a more lethal virus, and the absolute numbers (mean |ρ| 0.164; virulence Pearson 0.46 from a 37-example probe, influenza-only) are modest with no confidence intervals or significance tests on the small virulence set. The authors say as much in their ethics statement: current Evo 2 is "not readily exploitable." Read this as evidence against a method (data filtering alone), not evidence that this model is dangerous today.

baselines

Well-chosen and controlled — unfiltered ESM 2 above, no-biology LLaMA-3.1-8B below — but both are weak anchors, so the large relative gains sit over low absolute bars.

contamination

Leakage is the finding: recovery works because evolutionarily close species are public. But that also means the eval can't cleanly separate "filtered capability" from capability inferable from permitted, related data; held-out taxa are close to training taxa.

what's missed

No generative pathogen-design eval, no wet-lab or surrogate validation that probe/log-likelihood gains translate to real capability, and n=1 model (only Evo 2-7B) with virulence limited to Influenza A (369 records).

would raise confidence

Test more filtered models; larger, multi-family virulence data with CIs; a true generation-of-viable-sequences task; and a head-to-head against a tamper-resistant baseline (durable unlearning, circuit breakers) instead of filtering alone.

// bottom line

A convincing demonstration that pretraining-data filtering is not tamper-resistant: excluded sequence-modeling skill is recoverable in 0.72 GPU-hours, and mutational/virulence signal is already latent in the frozen model (a linear probe needs zero fine-tuning). The absolute capability uplift stays modest and the scariest task — making a viable pathogen — is asserted, not shown. The right takeaway is "filtering alone isn't a safeguard," not "this model is a bioweapon."

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. Data filtering ≠ tamper resistance. Evo 2 excluded eukaryotic viral sequences, but fine-tuning on related public species restored held-out-species perplexity to benign-sequence levels in 50 steps (0.72 H100 GPU-hours).
  2. The knowledge was never really gone. A linear probe on the frozen Evo 2-7B predicts mutational effects (|ρ| 0.159, ≈ESM2-650M) and Influenza virulence (Pearson 0.46) with no fine-tuning — 52.8% / 77% above a no-biology LLaMA floor. Filtering removed the skill from the output distribution, not the latent representations.
  3. Evolutionary distance is the only real friction. Inter-genus recovery needed 1,000–2,000 steps for ≤20% gains, so filtering buys cost, not safety — and perplexity is a poor proxy for latent virulence.
  4. Honest scope. Absolute correlations stay modest and only Evo 2-7B is tested; the contribution is a reusable evaluation framework plus a warning to build safeguards beyond data exclusion, not a demonstrated pathogen-design capability.
