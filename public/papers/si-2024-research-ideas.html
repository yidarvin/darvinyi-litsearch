Can LLMs Generate Novel Research Ideas? A Large-Scale Human Study with 100+ NLP Researchers

A year-long, IRB-approved study runs the first adequately-powered, confounder-controlled head-to-head between an LLM ideation agent and 49 expert NLP researchers. Under blind review by 79 experts, the AI ideas come out significantly more novel — and the paper is unusually candid about why that result is fragile.

Chenglei Si, Diyi Yang, Tatsunori Hashimoto · Stanford University

What problem is this solving?

By 2024 a wave of "AI scientist" and research-agent papers claimed LLMs could autonomously generate and validate new research ideas. But none had actually shown the very first step — that an LLM can produce novel, expert-level ideas at all, let alone run the whole research loop. The optimism ran ahead of the evidence.

The reason the evidence was missing is that research ideation is one of the hardest things in ML to evaluate. Qualified experts are expensive and slow to recruit at scale; "is this idea good?" is deeply subjective; and even strong reviewers disagree about an idea before any experiments have been run. So earlier studies cut corners that quietly invalidate their conclusions — they used a handful of reviewers, constrained ideas to be short and underspecified, or replaced human judgement with LLM-as-a-judge. None ran the large-scale human comparison needed to answer the motivating question.

This paper takes the opposite, deliberately expensive route. Rather than propose yet another agent, it builds the missing measurement instrument: a controlled experiment that recruits over 100 highly-qualified NLP researchers, fixes the confounders that wreck small studies (topic mix, writing style, idea format), and asks one clean question — are the ideas an LLM agent generates as novel as those expert humans write?

// how it works

The method

The core is a three-condition, head-to-head comparison. The authors fix seven NLP topics drawn from the COLM call-for-papers — Bias, Coding, Safety, Multilinguality, Factuality, Math, and Uncertainty — and restrict everything to prompting-based research, because such ideas are concrete, cheap to execute later, and strike a fair balance between realism and being elicitable at scale. For each topic, ideas are produced under three conditions, 49 ideas each:

  • Human Ideas — proposals written by the recruited expert researchers.
  • AI Ideas — the agent's top-ranked proposals, taken directly from its own LLM ranker.
  • AI Ideas + Human Rerank — proposals where the first author manually picked the best of the agent's output instead of trusting the LLM ranker, to estimate the upper bound of AI idea quality.

The hard part is killing confounders. Everyone gets the same idea template (title, problem statement, motivation, proposed method, step-by-step experiment plan, test-case examples, fallback plan) and the same demonstration example. Topic distribution is matched exactly: humans pick a topic, and the agent generates a matching idea for it. A separate style-normalization LLM rewrites every proposal — human and AI — into one neutral voice; in a check, expert judges could tell AI from human only 50% of the time (chance). To make sure normalization didn't change meaning, the first author manually verified every human proposal's content was preserved.

The agent itself is deliberately minimal — the point is to probe today's LLMs, not to engineer a clever system. It runs on claude-3-5-sonnet and has three parts. Retrieval: it issues Semantic Scholar API calls (keyword query, paper lookup, references) to pull up to 120 related papers for RAG grounding. Generation: it over-generates 4,000 seed ideas per topic, on the bet that quality is rare and inference compute is cheap, then deduplicates them with sentence-embedding similarity. Ranking: a Swiss-tournament of pairwise LLM comparisons (5 rounds) scores the survivors — the Claude-3.5 pairwise ranker predicts ICLR accept/reject at 71.4% accuracy (vs. 61.1% for GPT-4o, 63.5% for Claude-3-Opus).

On the human side, the scale is real: 49 idea writers and 79 reviewers (24 overlap, none reviews their own idea) → 104 participants recruited via the OpenNLP Slack (1,426 researchers), NAACL 2024, and direct outreach, screened on Google Scholar. Writers were paid $300 (plus a $1,000 bonus for the top-5 ideas) and produced 902-word proposals over ~5.5 hours; reviewers were paid $25 per review and scored ideas on 1–10 scales for novelty, excitement, feasibility, and expected effectiveness, plus an overall score — yielding 298 unique blind reviews.

// what they found

The results

The headline holds up under scrutiny: AI-generated ideas are judged more novel than expert-written ideas (p<0.05), and the effect survives three different statistical treatments designed to absorb the obvious confounders. Treating each review as a datapoint (Test 1), novelty rises from 4.84 ± 1.79 (Human) to 5.64 ± 1.76 (AI) and 5.81 ± 1.66 (AI+Rerank), both significant at p<0.01. Re-running it per idea (Test 2, N=49) gives 4.86 → 5.62 (p=0.03) → 5.78 (p<0.01); per reviewer (Test 3) gives mean novelty gains of +0.94 (p<0.01) and +0.86 (p<0.01) over the human baseline.

The win is specific. AI ideas are also rated more exciting, but on feasibility humans come out slightly ahead (6.61 vs. 6.34 / 6.44, not significant) and expected effectiveness is a wash. On the bottom-line overall score, only the human-reranked condition clears significance (5.34 vs. 4.68, p<0.05) — the agent's own picks (4.85) are statistically tied with humans.

Then comes the other half of the paper — the part that complicates the headline. Probing the agent, the authors find two hard limits. Idea diversity collapses. Of 4,000 seed ideas generated per topic, only about 200 are non-duplicates; the fraction of new ideas in each fresh batch decays toward zero (Figure 4). Over-generate-and-rank has a low ceiling — more inference compute mostly buys repeats. And LLMs can't yet evaluate ideas reliably: the best automatic evaluator (the authors' own Claude-3.5 pairwise ranker) agrees with expert rankings only 53.3% of the time — below the human inter-reviewer consistency of 56.1%, with GPT-4o pairwise at 45.0% and the "AI Scientist" reviewer at 43.3%. That the human-reranked condition consistently beats the agent's own picks (the two top-10 sets overlap on only 17 of 49 ideas) is the same finding from another angle: a human in the loop is still doing real work.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

This is a genuinely careful study — the rigor (style normalization, matched topics, manual content verification, three statistical tests, a real cost in dollars and a year of work) is exactly what the field had been skipping. The result is real as stated. The question is how far "rated more novel under blind review" actually reaches, and the paper itself supplies most of the reasons to be cautious.

Start with the measurement instrument. Inter-reviewer agreement here is only 56.1% — barely above the 50% chance floor, and well under the 71.9% the same metric gives on ICLR-2024 LLM submissions or 66% at NeurIPS-2021. Judging an idea (no experiments, no results) is intrinsically noisier than judging a paper, so the yardstick measuring the novelty gap is itself shaky. A ~0.8-point difference read off a 1–10 scale with that much reviewer disagreement is statistically significant but not obviously large.

Next, what "novelty" is standing in for. The overall score correlates strongly with novelty (r=0.725) and excitement (r=0.854) but is essentially uncorrelated with feasibility (r=0.097). Reviewers, scoring un-executed proposals, reward what sounds new and exciting — precisely the axis where the AI wins — while the free-text reviews repeatedly flag AI ideas as vague on implementation, misusing datasets, missing baselines, making unrealistic assumptions, and being resource-hungry. A surface impression of novelty is the cheapest thing for a fluent LLM to manufacture.

And the human baseline is soft. Writers self-rated their submissions at roughly the top 43% of their past ideas — median-grade — and 37 of 49 invented the idea on the spot inside a 10-day window. So the finding is "AI beats experts' quickly-produced, median ideas," not "AI beats experts' best work." Finally, the result rests on a single backbone (Claude-3.5-Sonnet) in a single domain (prompting NLP), and the "AI" ideas quietly depend on humans: manual demo curation, manual content checks, and — for the only condition that wins overall — manual reranking.

baselines

Strong on paper — 49 researchers averaging 12 papers / 477 citations — but they submitted self-rated median ideas (top ~43%) written in 10 days. The agent's automatic ranker is a weak baseline: 53.3% agreement vs. 56.1% for humans, so the only condition that wins overall needed a human reranker.

contamination

The Claude-3.5 backbone may have trained on the related work it retrieves, and its ranker was calibrated on 1.2K ICLR-2024 submissions. The human side is well-guarded (style normalization, manual content checks, no same-institution review pairings), but leakage on the model side is unmeasured.

what's missed

Perceived novelty of a proposal ≠ research value. Feasibility is near-uncorrelated with the overall score (r=0.097), inter-reviewer agreement is only 56.1%, and whether these ideas produce good papers when executed is not measured in this study.

would raise confidence

The end-to-end execution study the authors propose (recruit researchers to actually build the ideas); more backbone models beyond Claude-3.5; idea formats beyond prompting NLP; and higher statistical power to pin down the feasibility/excitement effects this study was underpowered to resolve.

// bottom line

A landmark for evaluation rigor, with an honest, narrow result: today's LLM ideation agent writes proposals that experts rate as more novel — on a noisy instrument (56.1% agreement), against a median human baseline, on the one axis reviewers over-weight. The agent can't scale its own diversity (200 unique ideas from 4,000) or judge its own output (53.3% < 56.1%). "More novel proposal" is a real and surprising finding; "better research" is a claim the authors deliberately leave to the follow-up execution study.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. The first statistically significant evidence on LLM research ideation: an agent's ideas are rated more novel than expert NLP researchers' (novelty 5.64–5.81 vs. 4.84, p<0.01), robust across three statistical tests over 298 blind reviews.
  2. The win is narrow and on a shaky scale: it's on novelty and excitement — the dimensions reviewers over-weight (overall↔feasibility r=0.097) — not feasibility or demonstrated value; inter-reviewer agreement is just 56.1%, and humans submitted self-rated median ideas.
  3. The agent doesn't really scale: over-generating 4,000 ideas yields only ~200 non-duplicates, and the LLM ranker (53.3%) is worse than humans (56.1%) — so the result leans on a human in the loop, a single model, and a single domain.
