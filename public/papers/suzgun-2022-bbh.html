Challenging BIG-Bench Tasks and Whether Chain-of-Thought Can Solve Them

A curated 23-task subset of BIG-Bench — the tasks where no prior model had beaten the average human rater — and a demonstration that simply adding chain-of-thought prompting flips the outcome on most of them.

Mirac Suzgun, Nathan Scales, Nathanael Schärli, Sebastian Gehrmann, Yi Tay, Hyung Won Chung, Aakanksha Chowdhery, Quoc V. Le, Ed H. Chi, Denny Zhou, Jason Wei

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

BIG-Bench (Srivastava et al., 2022) threw over 200 diverse, deliberately hard tasks at language models and recruited human raters to solve the same tasks, establishing an "average human-rater" bar for each one. When Chowdhery et al. (2022) scaled PaLM to 540B parameters, it beat that bar on 65% of BIG-Bench tasks using standard few-shot prompting — good progress, but it leaves an unresolved question hanging over the other 35%: are those tasks genuinely beyond what large language models can do, or is the evaluation protocol itself the bottleneck?

The authors go looking for an answer by first isolating the hard core. Starting from the full 209-task BIG-Bench suite, they apply a cascading filter (excluding tasks with more than three subtasks, fewer than 103 examples, no human-rater baseline, or a non-multiple-choice/non-exact-match metric) down to 78 "clean" tasks, then keep only the 36 where the best previously reported model result still trails the average human rater. Of those 36, the authors manually set aside 13 as "extremely difficult for authors of this paper" — chess-move tracking, extremely long inputs, ambiguous moral-judgment scenarios — leaving 23 tasks (27 subtasks) that they name BIG-Bench Hard (BBH): the residue of BIG-Bench that standard prompting genuinely could not crack.

// how it works

The method

BBH's 23 tasks split into two rough families: 12 natural-language-understanding tasks (sarcasm detection, adjective ordering, translation-error spotting) and 11 algorithmic/arithmetic tasks (multi-step arithmetic, object tracking, Dyck-language balancing, navigation) that in principle admit a rule-based solution. Each task keeps a random 250-example evaluation slice (three tasks — Causal Judgement, Penguins in a Table, Snarks — use their full 187/146/178-example test sets instead), for 6,511 evaluation examples in total.

Two prompting regimes are compared head-to-head on the same tasks. Answer-only prompting follows the standard few-shot recipe (Brown et al., 2020) but strengthened with an explicit task instruction and the answer options spelled out in the prompt — a stronger baseline than BIG-Bench's own bare-exemplar setup. Chain-of-thought (CoT) prompting instead prepends three manually-written worked-solution exemplars per task, each opening with "let's think step by step" (Kojima et al., 2022), so the model is shown how to reason through the task before answering. Three model families are run under both regimes: PaLM (8B/62B/540B), InstructGPT (text-ada-001 through text-davinci-002), and Codex (code-cushman-001 through code-davinci-002). Decoding is greedy (temperature 0), and accuracy is scored by extracting the model's stated final answer via keyword matching ("the answer is") and comparing it to the gold label by exact match — a different protocol from BIG-Bench's own rank/likelihood classification, which the paper flags explicitly as a methodological difference.

6,511 eval examples × ~1.5K tokens/prompt ÷ 1K × $0.02 ≈ $195.33 // the paper's own estimate of the cost to run BBH once on text-davinci-002 at 2022 OpenAI API pricing — a small but telling detail: this is a benchmark built and priced around commercial, closed models
// what they found

The results

Averaged across all 23 BBH tasks, the average human rater scores 67.7% (max human rater: 94.4%), against a best-prior-BIG-Bench result of just 50.9%. Under answer-only prompting, none of the three model families gets close: PaLM 540B reaches 52.3% (beating the human average on 6/23 tasks), Codex 56.6% (5/23), InstructGPT 51.8% (4/23) — roughly matching what BIG-Bench itself had already reported.

Adding chain-of-thought prompting changes the picture sharply. PaLM 540B jumps to 65.2% (+12.9 points, human-beating on 10/23 tasks), InstructGPT to 68.4% (+16.6 points, 15/23), and Codex — the strongest model — to 73.9% (+16.7 points), surpassing the average human rater on 17 of the 23 tasks. That's still 20.5 points shy of the maximum human-rater score, so the paper is careful not to claim human-level performance — but it flips the benchmark's central framing: tasks that looked unsolvable under the field's default evaluation protocol turn out to be substantially solvable once the model is allowed to show its work.

The gains are not spread evenly. On the 11 algorithmic tasks, Codex's average jumps 28.5 points (45.9% → 74.4%) with CoT, more than double its 7.1-point gain on the 12 NLP-flavored tasks — CoT helps most exactly where multi-step decomposition matters. Individual task deltas with Codex include Tracking Shuffled Objects (+60.4 points), Multi-Step Arithmetic (+46.4), Navigate (+46.0), and Temporal Sequences (+19.8). But CoT is not universally helpful: on Causal Judgement, Ruin Names, and Snarks, CoT prompting underperforms answer-only across all three model families — these tasks lean on world knowledge (sarcasm, humor, causal presuppositions) that a reasoning trace alone can't supply.

The paper also traces the CoT effect against model scale (Figure 4): for both InstructGPT and PaLM, CoT prompting has negative or flat gain at the smallest model sizes (text-ada-001/babbage-001, PaLM-8B) and the CoT–no-CoT gap widens only once models get larger. On three tasks with otherwise flat scaling curves — Multi-Step Arithmetic, Tracking Shuffled Objects, Web of Lies — this produces a qualitative jump from near-random to well-above-random performance once both scale and CoT are present together (Figure 5), which the authors interpret through the lens of "emergent abilities" (Wei et al., 2022a).

// does it hold up?

The eval read

The core empirical claim — that answer-only few-shot prompting was underselling model capability on exactly the tasks BIG-Bench had flagged as hardest — is well supported and has aged well; BBH is now a standard component of reasoning suites precisely because the CoT effect it documents replicates broadly. But several aspects of the setup deserve a skeptical read before treating "17/23 beats the human rater" as a clean capability measurement.

First, the comparison is not apples-to-apples in effort. The authors manually wrote three chain-of-thought exemplars per task — a nontrivial amount of task-specific human engineering baked into the CoT condition — while the answer-only condition got a one-time, generic strengthening (instructions + options) rather than the same iterative, per-task tuning. Some of what looks like "CoT unlocks reasoning" could partly be "hand-crafted per-task prompts beat a generic template," a confound the paper doesn't isolate. Second, BBH's composition is itself a product of the authors' subjective judgment: 13 of the 36 candidate hard tasks were dropped as "extremely difficult for authors of this paper" to solve by hand, which is a defensible curation choice but also means the benchmark's difficulty profile was shaped by what a small group of ML researchers found tractable — not a neutral sampling of BIG-Bench's tail.

Third, and the paper is admirably upfront about this one: the "average human-rater" bar it repeatedly claims to beat is explicitly caveated in BIG-Bench's own design as noisy — raters worked a few hours a day over nearly a year on tasks whose formatting sometimes changed mid-collection, with internet access, and the paper quotes this caveat directly before concluding that "surpassing average human-rater performance score on a set of challenging tasks should not be confused with true language understanding or reasoning." That's a rare and welcome moment of self-restraint that the paper's own headline framing (and much of the field's subsequent citation of it) doesn't always carry forward.

baselines

Answer-only is strengthened once (instructions + options) but not iteratively tuned per task the way the CoT exemplars were — the two conditions differ in prompt-engineering effort, not just in the presence of a reasoning trace.

contamination

Not discussed (the paper predates the field's contamination norms). BBH's tasks, prompts, and even the Codex model outputs were published to GitHub immediately, and with 2,042+ citations it is now a heavily-reused static benchmark — plausibly present in later models' pretraining data, with no decontamination check offered by this paper or, typically, by papers that cite its numbers.

what's missed

The headline models (code-davinci-002, text-davinci-002) are closed OpenAI models that have since been deprecated and are no longer queryable, so the exact reported numbers are not independently reproducible today — an unusually literal reproducibility problem for a benchmark this widely cited.

would raise confidence

A same-effort answer-only baseline (equally hand-tuned per task) to isolate the CoT effect from the prompt-engineering-effort gap; a blinded or automated difficulty-filtering process in place of the authors' own 13-task exclusion call; and given later work (e.g., Schaeffer et al., 2023, "Are Emergent Abilities of Large Language Models a Mirage?") arguing some emergent jumps are artifacts of discontinuous metrics like exact match, a check of whether BBH's "emergent" tasks still look emergent under a continuous scoring metric.

// bottom line

BBH's finding that standard few-shot evaluation was quietly underestimating LLM reasoning ability is real and has held up as the field's default reasoning-eval methodology shifted toward CoT. But BBH-the-artifact is best understood as a hand-curated demonstration of that methodological point, not a neutral capability ruler: the task set was filtered by author judgment, the CoT prompts were hand-engineered by the same authors, and the "beats the human rater" framing rests on a human baseline the paper itself flags as loose.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. BIG-Bench Hard (BBH) is a hand-curated 23-task (27-subtask) slice of the 209-task BIG-Bench suite — specifically the tasks where, under standard few-shot prompting, no prior model had beaten the average human rater.
  2. Adding chain-of-thought prompting (three hand-written worked exemplars per task) flips that outcome: Codex with CoT beats the average human rater on 17/23 tasks (73.9% avg accuracy) versus just 5/23 with answer-only prompting (56.6%) — evidence that the standard single-shot eval protocol, not raw model capability, was the bottleneck on many "unsolvable" tasks.
  3. The CoT boost is scale-dependent and can look like a phase transition: on tasks like Multi-Step Arithmetic and Tracking Shuffled Objects, accuracy is flat and near-random until both sufficient model scale and CoT prompting are present together, at which point performance jumps sharply — the paper's case study in "emergent" task performance.
