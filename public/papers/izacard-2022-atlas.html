Atlas: Few-shot Learning with Retrieval Augmented Language Models

Can a language model learn knowledge-intensive tasks from a handful of examples without storing that knowledge in its own weights? Atlas pairs a dense retriever with a Fusion-in-Decoder reader, jointly pretrains both, and — at 11B parameters — beats 540B-parameter PaLM's 64-shot Natural Questions accuracy by outsourcing knowledge to a swappable document index instead.

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

By 2022, large language models — GPT-3, Gopher, Chinchilla, PaLM — had shown that few-shot in-context learning emerges reliably as parameter count and training data scale up. The implicit assumption behind that scaling recipe is that a model needs to memorize a lot of world knowledge in its weights to answer knowledge-intensive questions (question answering, fact checking) from just a few examples. Retrieval-augmented models — REALM, RAG, RETRO, FiD — had already shown they can match or beat much larger closed-book models on knowledge tasks with far fewer parameters, by looking answers up in an external corpus instead of memorizing them. But none of that prior work had shown retrieval-augmented models could actually do few-shot learning well; as the paper puts it, "it is unclear whether they work in few-shot settings."

Atlas's question, stated directly: can memorization be decoupled from generalization? If knowledge can be outsourced to a retrievable, swappable index, does a model still need hundreds of billions of parameters to be a good few-shot learner — or was raw scale mostly compensating for the lack of an external memory in the first place?

// how it works

The method

Atlas is two pretrained transformers wired together. The retriever is a Contriever-style dual encoder: query and documents are embedded independently, scored by dot product, and the encoder is pretrained with the MoCo contrastive loss on unsupervised data alone. The reader is T5 with the Fusion-in-Decoder modification: each of the top-k retrieved documents is encoded independently (avoiding the quadratic blow-up of concatenating everything into one long sequence), and the decoder cross-attends over all of the concatenated encoder outputs at once to generate the final answer.

The paper's real contribution is how to jointly train these two pieces using only (query, output) pairs — no document annotations required. It compares four loss functions that use the reader's own signal to teach the retriever which documents were actually useful: ADist distills the reader's cross-attention weights (scaled by value-vector norm) into the retriever's score distribution via KL divergence; EMDR² treats the retrieved documents as a latent variable and maximizes the marginal likelihood of the output under an EM-style objective; PDist (perplexity distillation) trains the retriever to match how much each document actually lowers the reader's output perplexity; and LOOP scores each document by how much worse the reader's prediction gets when that one document is left out of the top-k set. On top of the loss function, three self-supervised pretext tasks jointly pretrain retriever and reader together: prefix language modeling, T5-style masked language modeling, and Wikipedia title-to-section generation.

pretr(d | q) = exp(s(d, q) / θ)Σk exp(s(dk, q) / θ) // retriever's document distribution: s = query·document dot product, θ = temperature; the four losses differ only in what target distribution this is KL-matched against

In the retriever-loss ablation (64-shot / 1024-shot KILT subset: NaturalQuestions, Wizard of Wikipedia, FEVER), PDist edges out the other three at 64-shot (45.7% average accuracy vs. 43.0–43.7% for ADist/EMDR²/LOOP), while masked language modeling clearly beats prefix-LM and title-to-section generation as the pretext task (42.4% avg at 64-shot, 50.6% at 1024-shot). Both joint-pretraining ablations strongly outperform a "no joint pre-training" baseline (30.0% avg at 64-shot) and a non-retrieval closed-book T5 baseline (26.5%), confirming retrieval-aware pretraining — not just retrieval at inference time — is what drives the few-shot gains.

// what they found

The results

The main comparison: with only 64 fine-tuning examples, an 11B-parameter Atlas reaches 42.4% exact match on Natural Questions (45.1% with a Wikipedia-only index, per the abstract), against 175B-parameter GPT-3's 29.9%, 70B-parameter Chinchilla's 35.5%, and 540B-parameter PaLM's 39.6% — Atlas beats a model 50× its size by roughly 3 points while using in-context prompting for none of it (Atlas is fine-tuned on the 64 examples; the baselines are prompted). On TriviaQA-unfiltered, 64-shot Atlas hits 84.7% against PaLM's 81.4%. Full-dataset fine-tuning pushes further: Table 8 reports Atlas at 60.4% EM on NaturalQuestions and 79.8%/89.4% on TriviaQA filtered/unfiltered, both new state-of-the-art at publication.

On MMLU (57 multi-choice exam subjects), zero-shot Atlas-11B scores 47.1% overall; with full-dataset fine-tuning and transfer it reaches 66.0%, ahead of 175B-parameter GPT-3's in-context 53.9% (roughly 12 points) — matching the paper's own framing of "results on par or stronger than models with 15× more parameters on MMLU" — but behind 70B-parameter Chinchilla's separately-reported 5-shot in-context 67.5%. On FEVER fact-checking, 15-shot Atlas gets 56.2% accuracy, rising to 64.3% at 65-shot and 78.0% (80.1% with a FEVER-matched Wikipedia index) at full-dataset scale — within a few points of ProoFVer's dedicated 79.5% full-dataset SOTA. The index itself is compressible: the paper reports that a product-quantization-compressed index "achieves comparable performance as an uncompressed index while resulting in a 5x memory reduction," and swapping in a temporally-matched Wikipedia snapshot for NaturalQuestions adds another +3.6% EM.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

The headline few-shot comparisons carry a real asymmetry the paper discloses in its own table captions but doesn't foreground in the prose: "For the 64-shot setting, our model uses fine-tuning, while the other models use prompting" (Table 8; the same caveat appears for Table 7's MMLU numbers and Table 9's FEVER numbers). Fine-tuning on 64 labeled examples via gradient descent is a materially easier optimization regime than in-context prompting on the same 64 examples — the model gets to update its weights, not just condition on a prompt. Calling both "64-shot" invites a reader to treat them as the same task when they aren't quite. This doesn't make the numbers wrong, but it does mean "Atlas beats PaLM at 64-shot" is not the clean apples-to-apples few-shot comparison the abstract's framing implies.

More striking is the paper's own leakage analysis, tucked into Section 5.1 ("Interpretability and Leakage") rather than the results section. The authors check how often the literal text of the correct MMLU answer appears in Atlas's retrieved passages, and find MMLU accuracy climbs from 55% on questions where the answer text never appears in retrieval, up to 77% on questions where it appears more than 15 times — a 22-point swing driven by surface-level answer presence in the retrieved context, not obviously by reasoning. Manually inspecting 50 correctly-answered questions, the authors found only 28% had no obviously helpful retrieved information at all, while 26% contained a passage stating the answer "in a straightforward way" and 2% contained the verbatim question paired with its answer. They also directly estimate corpus leakage — questions whose text overlaps ≥75% with a retrieved CCNet passage — at 2.8%. That's a genuinely rare act of self-scrutiny for a benchmark paper, and it's presented candidly. But a 2.8% verbatim-leakage estimate coexists with a 55%→77% accuracy swing tied to partial answer presence, and the paper doesn't reconcile the two — the leakage check only catches near-exact matches, not the much larger "helpful passage present" effect its own Figure 3 documents.

Finally, the single most favorable head-to-head comparison in the paper — Atlas's 38.7 EM on 15-shot NaturalQuestions against Lazaridou et al. (2022)'s Gopher-280B-based pipeline (32.7% RAG-style, 38.4% ensemble), "despite having 25× fewer parameters" — is explicitly kept out of the main results tables: "This method (not shown in our tables) achieves exact match scores of..." A reader skimming Tables 7–10 would never see this comparison; it only exists in prose.

baselines

64-shot Atlas is fine-tuned; the GPT-3/Gopher/Chinchilla/PaLM baselines it's compared against are prompted in-context — a disclosed but under-emphasized asymmetry across Tables 7, 8, and 9.

contamination

The authors directly measure it (rare, commendable): 2.8% of MMLU questions verbatim-overlap a retrieved CCNet passage. But that estimate only catches near-exact text matches — it doesn't cover the much larger effect their own Figure 3 shows, where MMLU accuracy rises 22 points as the correct answer's text becomes more frequent (not necessarily verbatim) in retrieval.

what's missed

The most favorable Atlas-vs-prior-work comparison (beating a Gopher-280B search pipeline with 25× fewer parameters) is explicitly excluded from the results tables and only appears in prose. The abstract's 42.4%/45.1% NQ pair and Table 8's 60.4% full-dataset headline use different index configurations that aren't reconciled in one place.

would raise confidence

An in-context (non-fine-tuned) Atlas-64-shot number for a true apples-to-apples comparison against the prompted baselines, and a leakage estimate that accounts for partial/paraphrased answer presence in retrieval, not just ≥75% verbatim n-gram overlap.

// bottom line

Atlas's central claim — that jointly-trained retrieval can substitute for raw parameter count in few-shot knowledge tasks — is well-supported by the ablations (Tables 1–4) and holds up on its own terms. But the flashiest number in the abstract ("beats a 540B model") compares a fine-tuned retrieval-augmented model to prompted closed-book models, and the paper's own Figure 3 shows a meaningful chunk of MMLU accuracy is attributable to the retrieved passages simply containing the answer text — a form of leakage the authors measure honestly but don't fully account for in the headline numbers.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. Atlas jointly pretrains a Contriever-style dense retriever with a Fusion-in-Decoder T5 reader using only (query, output) pairs — no document annotations — via one of four retriever-training losses (ADist, EMDR², PDist, LOOP), with PDist and masked-LM pretraining winning the ablations.
  2. At 11B parameters, 64-shot fine-tuned Atlas beats 540B-parameter PaLM's in-context 64-shot Natural Questions accuracy (42.4% vs. 39.6%), and full-dataset fine-tuning reaches 60.4% EM — though the comparison mixes fine-tuning (Atlas) against in-context prompting (the baselines), a caveat the paper discloses in table footnotes rather than the headline text.
  3. The paper's own Section 5.1 leakage analysis is a model of self-scrutiny other benchmark papers should copy: MMLU accuracy swings from 55% to 77% based on how often the correct answer's text appears in retrieved passages, and the authors estimate 2.8% of questions verbatim-overlap their retrieval corpus — numbers worth reading before taking the headline accuracy figures at face value.
