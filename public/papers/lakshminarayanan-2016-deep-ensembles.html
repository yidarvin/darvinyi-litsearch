2017 · NeurIPS 2017 · Benchmarks & Evals

Simple and Scalable Predictive Uncertainty Estimation using Deep Ensembles

Skip the Bayesian machinery: train five ordinary neural networks from different random seeds, have each one output a mean and a variance instead of a single point estimate, and average them. That's the entire recipe — and it produced better-calibrated uncertainty than the approximate Bayesian methods of the day, at a fraction of the implementation cost.

Balaji Lakshminarayanan, Alexander Pritzel, Charles Blundell — DeepMind

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

By late 2016, the standard answer to "how do I get a neural network to know what it doesn't know?" was Bayesian: put a prior over the network's weights, compute (or approximate) the posterior given the training data, and use that distribution over weights to quantify uncertainty in predictions. The trouble is that exact Bayesian inference over the weights of a deep net is intractable, so the whole line of work reduces to a menu of approximations — Laplace approximation, MCMC, variational Bayes, stochastic-gradient Langevin dynamics, expectation propagation, and Monte Carlo dropout (Gal & Ghahramani, 2016) among them. Each requires nontrivial changes to the training procedure, extra hyperparameters, and often extra compute at test time; and the quality of the resulting uncertainty depends on both how good the approximation is and whether the chosen prior is even reasonable for the problem — "priors of convenience," as the paper puts it, can produce unreasonable uncertainty estimates no matter how careful the inference.

This paper's target is a very specific gap: is there a non-Bayesian way to get equally good — or better — predictive uncertainty, using nothing more exotic than the standard neural-network training pipeline? The authors frame uncertainty quality along two axes that matter in practice and are largely orthogonal to accuracy: calibration (do a model's confidence scores match its actual long-run correctness rate, measured via proper scoring rules like negative log-likelihood and the Brier score) and robustness to distribution shift (does the model get appropriately less confident, rather than staying confidently wrong, on inputs unlike anything it trained on). Getting both right with a method simple enough that any practitioner can drop it into an existing training pipeline — no new inference machinery, minimal hyperparameter tuning, and embarrassingly parallel training — was, at the time, an open problem.

// how it works

The method

The recipe has three ingredients, and the paper is explicit that the ensembling is what does the heavy lifting while the other two are cheap add-ons. First, each network is trained with a proper scoring rule as its loss — a scoring function whose expected value is uniquely maximized by the true data-generating distribution. Ordinary softmax cross-entropy for classification already is one; the paper's real departure is on the regression side, where instead of a network outputting a single value μ(x) trained to minimize MSE, it outputs both a mean μ(x) and a variance σ²(x), trained to maximize the Gaussian log-likelihood. This alone — before any ensembling — is what lets a network express "I don't know" as a wide predicted variance rather than silently absorbing noise into a point estimate.

L(θ) = log σθ²(x)2 + (y − μθ(x))²2σθ²(x) + const // Gaussian NLL used as the regression training criterion in place of MSE — a network that predicts an overconfident (too small) variance pays a steep penalty when it's wrong

Second, an optional smoothing step: generate an adversarial example for each training point via the fast gradient sign method (x′ = x + ε·sign(∇ₓ ℓ(θ,x,y)), with ε set to 1% of each input dimension's range) and add it to the loss. The authors' framing of this is more interesting than "make it robust to attacks" — they argue it's a computationally cheap way to smooth the predictive distribution in the single direction the network is most likely to be wrong, standing in for the (intractable) alternative of smoothing along all 2D directions in input space.

Third, the actual "deep ensembles" part: train M copies of the network (the paper's recommended default is M = 5) completely independently — random weight initialization, random data-batch order, no bootstrap resampling, no shared parameters, no interaction during training at all. That independence is the scalability claim: unlike MCMC or variational inference, which are inherently sequential refinements of a single posterior estimate, ensemble members can be trained in parallel on separate machines with zero coordination. At prediction time, the ensemble is treated as a uniformly-weighted mixture; for classification this is just averaging the softmax outputs, and for regression the mixture of Gaussians is further collapsed into a single Gaussian by moment-matching:

μ*(x) = M−1 Σm μθm(x)    σ*²(x) = M−1 Σm (σθm²(x) + μθm²(x)) − μ*²(x) // mean and variance of the M-component Gaussian mixture, giving a single closed-form predictive distribution for quantiles / NLL computation

The toy 1-D regression example below is the paper's own illustration of why each ingredient matters. A plain ensemble of MSE-trained networks (leftmost panel) only captures uncertainty implicitly, through disagreement in the point predictions — the shaded band barely widens away from the 20 training points. Switching a single network to NLL training (second panel) already produces a distribution that widens appropriately outside the data region. Adding adversarial training (third panel) smooths it further. The full recipe — an ensemble of 5 NLL-trained networks (fourth panel) — gives the cleanest picture: tight uncertainty near the observed data, and a visibly widening band as x moves away from where the model has evidence.

// what they found

The results

On UCI regression benchmarks (Table 1), deep ensembles are compared directly against two established Bayesian approximations — Probabilistic Backpropagation (PBP) and MC-dropout — using the exact same architectures and protocol from Hernández-Lobato & Adams (2015). The pattern is consistent: deep ensembles win or tie on NLL almost everywhere, sometimes by a wide margin, while occasionally trailing on RMSE — exactly what you'd expect from a method whose loss directly optimizes calibrated likelihood rather than squared error. On Naval Propulsion Plant, NLL improves from −3.73 ± 0.01 (PBP) and −3.80 ± 0.05 (MC-dropout) to −5.63 ± 0.05 for deep ensembles. On Kin8nm, NLL goes from −0.90 and −0.95 to −1.20 ± 0.02. On the large Year Prediction MSD dataset (515K examples, single train/test split), NLL improves from 3.60 (PBP) / 3.59 (MC-dropout) to 3.35. The counter-example the paper doesn't hide: on Yacht Hydrodynamics, RMSE is 1.58 ± 0.48 for deep ensembles versus 1.02 ± 0.05 (PBP) and 1.11 ± 0.38 (MC-dropout) — worse point-prediction accuracy even though NLL still improves (1.18 vs. 1.63 / 1.55), because the method is trading a bit of RMSE for substantially better-calibrated variance.

On MNIST and SVHN classification, the paper sweeps ensemble size M from 1 to 15 (MNIST, 3-layer MLP) and 1 to 10 (SVHN, VGG-style convnet) and compares four variants — plain ensembling, ensembling plus random-direction augmentation, ensembling plus adversarial training, and MC-dropout at matching M — across classification error, NLL, and Brier score simultaneously. Every ensemble variant beats MC-dropout at every ensemble size on all three metrics; adversarial training gives a further, consistent boost on MNIST at every M, but on SVHN it only helps at M = 1 and the benefit fades as the ensemble grows — the authors are candid that they don't fully know why ("If the classes are well-separated, adversarial training might not change the classification boundary significantly... further investigation is required").

The same known-vs-unknown-class setup, tested at scale on ImageNet by splitting ILSVRC-2012 into dog breeds (known) vs. non-dog categories (unknown) using an Inception network, shows the ensemble-size effect isn't just a toy-dataset artifact — both accuracy and uncertainty quality improve together as M grows:

Finally, the accuracy-vs-confidence experiment ties calibration to a decision rule a real deployment could use: filter test examples (a mix of known MNIST digits and unknown NotMNIST characters) to only those where the model's own confidence clears a threshold τ, and check whether accuracy on the surviving examples actually increases with τ, as a well-calibrated model should. All three ensemble variants climb steadily and end up in the high 80s% accuracy by τ = 0.9; MC-dropout's curve is comparatively flat and plateaus below 50% — meaning MC-dropout keeps handing out high-confidence labels on exactly the (unknown-class) examples it should be most unsure about, while the ensemble's stated confidence tracks its actual reliability.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

As a piece of methodology this has aged remarkably well — "deep ensembles" is still the default non-Bayesian uncertainty baseline cited in essentially every subsequent uncertainty-quantification or OOD-detection paper, and a large-scale follow-up study (Ovadia et al., "Can You Trust Your Model's Uncertainty?", NeurIPS 2019) stress-tested exactly this question across many competing methods under real dataset shift and found deep ensembles to be the most robust and best-calibrated approach of the field, years after this paper's original, comparatively small-scale experiments. That said, several parts of the original evaluation are worth reading critically rather than taking as a settled matter.

The compute/simplicity trade-off is real, and the paper doesn't fully quantify it. "Simple" is honest — no new inference machinery, no architecture change — but "scalable" undersells the cost: M = 5 means 5× the training compute and 5× the parameters to store and serve, versus a single MC-dropout network that needs only extra stochastic forward passes at test time. The paper's own framing (embarrassingly parallelizable training) is a real advantage over sequential Bayesian approximations like MCMC, but it never reports wall-clock or FLOPs comparisons against MC-dropout at matched compute budget, only matched "number of nets / number of MC samples" — a metric that favors ensembles trivially, since an MC-dropout forward pass is far cheaper than training and storing a whole additional network.

Is M = 5 actually enough, and is it principled? The paper's own ensemble-size sweeps (Figure 2, and the ImageNet table above) show diminishing but still-present returns from M = 5 to M = 10 and beyond on every metric — the curves haven't clearly flattened by M = 5, they've just gotten expensive to keep pushing. The choice of M = 5 as "the recommended default" reads more like a practical compute/benefit compromise than a value derived from any principled criterion, and diversity between ensemble members comes from nothing more than random initialization and data-batch order — the Discussion section itself flags "explicitly de-correlating networks' predictions... might promote ensemble diversity and improve performance even further" as future work the paper doesn't attempt.

The out-of-distribution tests are all "far" shifts. MNIST vs. NotMNIST (different alphabet entirely), SVHN vs. CIFAR-10 (digits vs. object photos), and ImageNet dogs vs. non-dogs are all large, easy-to-separate distribution shifts — precisely the regime where almost any uncertainty signal looks good. There's no near-OOD test here (e.g. one CIFAR-10 class held out from the rest, or a harder fine-grained shift) of the kind later OOD papers made standard, so the entropy-histogram result, while a real and useful demonstration, doesn't establish how the method behaves under the harder, more realistic shifts practitioners actually worry about.

baselines

Fair and well-chosen for its era — PBP and MC-dropout were the two leading practical Bayesian-NN approximations in 2016, and the regression comparison reuses their exact published protocol and datasets rather than re-implementing weaker versions. It does not compare against Hendrycks & Gimpel's MSP baseline or Guo et al.'s temperature scaling — both cited only in a closing "concurrent work" note — so it never shows how deep ensembles stack up against the other non-Bayesian alternatives that appeared the very same window. contamination

Not a concern: UCI regression uses the standard 20-fold (5-fold for Protein) splits from prior work, and every OOD pair (MNIST/NotMNIST, SVHN/CIFAR-10, ImageNet dogs/non-dogs) is a genuinely separate dataset or held-out category, not a disguised slice of the training distribution. what's missed

No near-OOD stress test; no wall-clock/FLOPs comparison at matched compute; the MNIST/SVHN/ImageNet classification curves appear to be single runs with no repeated-seed error bars (unlike the carefully 20-folded regression table); no ablation isolating how much of the gain is the NLL loss alone vs. adversarial training alone vs. ensembling alone beyond the toy example and the Appendix A variant table. would raise confidence

A principled criterion (or at least a cost-normalized curve) for choosing M instead of a flat recommendation; a near-OOD benchmark; repeated-seed variance on the classification results; and a direct empirical comparison against MSP and temperature scaling rather than a citation acknowledging they exist.

// bottom line

The core claim held up: a handful of independently-trained, NLL-trained networks, simply averaged, matches or beats the leading approximate-Bayesian methods of 2016 on calibration and out-of-distribution robustness, with an implementation any lab can run without new inference code. The honest cost is more parameters and more training compute than a single model, and the original OOD tests are all easy "far" shifts — but the method's staying power (and its clean win in later, harder dataset-shift studies) suggests the simplicity itself was the right bet.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. The recipe is three ingredients, and ensembling is the one that matters most: train M (default 5) neural networks independently from different random seeds, have each output a full predictive distribution (mean + variance for regression, softmax for classification) trained via a proper scoring rule rather than MSE, then average the ensemble as a mixture — no Bayesian inference machinery required, and every member trains in parallel with zero coordination.
  2. On UCI regression, deep ensembles beat Probabilistic Backpropagation and MC-dropout on NLL almost everywhere (e.g. Naval Propulsion Plant: −5.63 vs. −3.73/−3.80), occasionally at the cost of RMSE (Yacht: 1.58 vs. 1.02/1.11) — a direct trade of point-accuracy for calibration. On ImageNet, growing the ensemble from 1 to 10 networks drops top-1 error from 22.166% to 18.675% while NLL improves from 0.959 to 0.789.
  3. Ensembles express visibly higher, appropriately-growing uncertainty on out-of-distribution inputs (MNIST→NotMNIST, SVHN→CIFAR-10, ImageNet dogs→non-dogs) than MC-dropout, which stays overconfident even at matched ensemble/sample size — the property that made "deep ensembles" the default non-Bayesian uncertainty baseline, later confirmed as the most robust method under real dataset shift by Ovadia et al. (NeurIPS 2019).
