Proximal Policy Optimization Algorithms

Take the reliability of trust-region policy optimization — and throw away the conjugate gradients, the KL constraint, the second-order machinery. What's left is a single clipped objective you can drop into any policy-gradient codebase in a few lines. PPO became the default RL optimizer of the deep-learning era, and the engine under RLHF.

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

By 2017 the leading contenders for deep reinforcement learning each had a structural flaw. Deep Q-learning worked on Atari but, the authors note, "fails on many simple problems" and had not been shown to work on continuous control. Vanilla policy gradients are simple but "have poor data efficiency and robustness": they perform exactly one gradient update per sample of collected data, and trying to squeeze multiple updates out of the same trajectory "often leads to destructively large policy updates."

Trust Region Policy Optimization (TRPO) solved the destructive-update problem the right way — it maximizes a surrogate objective subject to a hard constraint on the KL divergence between the old and new policy — but it pays for that reliability with complexity. TRPO needs the conjugate-gradient algorithm, a linear approximation to the objective and a quadratic approximation to the KL constraint, and it "is not compatible with architectures that include noise (such as dropout) or parameter sharing (between the policy and value function, or with auxiliary tasks)."

The paper's goal is a method that is scalable (to large models and parallel implementations), data-efficient, and robust across problems without per-task hyperparameter tuning — while using only first-order optimization. In other words: TRPO's stability, with SGD's simplicity.

// how it works

The method

Start from the probability ratio between the new and old policy at a given action, rt(θ) = πθ(at|st) / πθold(at|st), so that r(θold) = 1. TRPO maximizes the surrogate LCPI = Êt[rt(θ) Ât] (the "CPI" superscript is for conservative policy iteration). Maximize that without a constraint and you get the destructive update problem back: the optimizer happily pushes rt arbitrarily far from 1 to chase a positive advantage.

PPO's central trick is to replace the constraint with a clip. The clipped surrogate objective is:

LCLIP(θ) = Êt [ min( rt(θ) Ât , clip( rt(θ), 1−ε, 1+ε ) Ât ) ] // ε = 0.2 — the only knob that defines the trust region

Read it term by term. The first argument of the min is the ordinary surrogate rtÂt. The second clips the ratio to the interval [1−ε, 1+ε], which "removes the incentive for moving rt outside" that band. Taking the minimum of the two makes the objective a pessimistic lower bound: as the paper puts it, "we only ignore the change in probability ratio when it would make the objective improve, and we include it when it makes the objective worse." So the policy is free to move toward better actions only up to the clip; past it, the gradient flattens to zero and there's nothing to gain by moving further.

Crucially, LCLIP equals LCPI to first order around θold, so for tiny steps PPO behaves like ordinary policy-gradient; the clip only bites once the policy has moved meaningfully. Figure 2 makes this concrete by interpolating along an actual PPO update direction: the clipped objective tracks the unclipped one initially, then bends away and penalizes the over-large step.

The algorithm, and why it's sample-efficient

The clip is what lets PPO do the thing vanilla policy gradients can't: multiple epochs of minibatch SGD on the same batch of data. Each iteration, N parallel actors each run the policy for T timesteps; advantages are estimated with truncated generalized advantage estimation; then the surrogate is optimized over the full NT samples for K epochs with minibatches (using Adam in practice). Reusing every sample K times instead of once is the source of PPO's data efficiency, and the clip is what keeps that reuse from blowing up the policy.

For networks that share parameters between policy and value function, the full per-iteration objective adds a value-function error term and an entropy bonus: LCLIP+VF+S = Êt[ LCLIP − c1 LVF + c2 S[πθ] ]. The paper also presents an adaptive-KL-penalty variant — optimize Êt[rtÂt − β·KL] and double or halve β depending on whether the realized KL overshoots a target dtarg — but reports it "performed worse than the clipped surrogate objective" and keeps it only as a baseline.

// what they found

The results

First, which surrogate? On 7 MuJoCo continuous-control tasks (1M timesteps, 3 seeds each, scores normalized so a random policy = 0 and the best result = 1, then averaged over the 21 runs), the clip cleanly beat both the no-constraint baseline and every KL-penalty setting. Clipping with ε = 0.2 scored 0.82 — the best of any variant — while the unconstrained objective scored a negative −0.39 (it destroys the policy on HalfCheetah).

Next, against the field. With ε = 0.2 fixed, PPO went head-to-head with TRPO, the cross-entropy method (CEM), an adaptive-stepsize vanilla policy gradient, A2C, and A2C with trust region across the same MuJoCo suite. The paper's verdict: "PPO outperforms the previous methods on almost all the continuous control environments."

On the harder showcase — a 3D Roboschool humanoid that must run, steer toward moving targets, and get back up after being pelted with cubes — PPO learns competent locomotion over tens of millions of timesteps, demonstrating it scales past toy control.

Finally, Atari. Across all 49 games of the Arcade Learning Environment, PPO was compared to well-tuned A2C and ACER on two metrics. By average reward over all of training (which rewards fast learning), PPO won 30 of 49 games, ACER 18, A2C 1. But by average reward over the last 100 episodes (final performance), the order flips: ACER won 28, PPO 19, A2C 1. So PPO is the clear sample-efficiency winner; ACER ekes out higher final scores — but, the abstract is careful to add, PPO does it while being "much simpler."

// does it hold up?

The eval read

PPO is one of the most-reproduced algorithms in machine learning, so the question isn't whether it works — it's whether this paper's evidence actually establishes what made it work. On that narrower question, the 2017 eval is thin by modern standards.

The headline continuous-control result rests on 3 random seeds per environment, aggregated into a single scalar with no variance reported. Deep-RL learning curves are notoriously high-variance across seeds; with only three, the per-environment rankings in Figure 3 carry wide error bars that the paper never shows. The "best result = 1" normalization is also within-comparison — it rescales each environment to whichever algorithm happened to do best there — so the aggregate scalar measures relative ordering on this exact suite, not absolute capability.

The famous ε = 0.2 is selected on the same 7 MuJoCo tasks it is then reported on, and it does not transfer: the Atari runs use a different, annealed clip (0.1×α). So the "robust without per-task tuning" framing is real for the clip idea but overstated for the specific hyperparameter — there is no single universal ε. And the baselines, while contemporary and reasonable (TRPO, A2C, ACER, CEM), are all tuned by the same authors; ACER and A2C are described as "well-tuned," but a fair head-to-head depends on equal tuning effort, which a reader can't verify. Notably, no DQN/Rainbow baseline appears on Atari despite Q-learning being named a "leading contender."

Theoretically, the clip is a heuristic. TRPO comes with a monotonic-improvement guarantee; PPO's "pessimistic lower bound" claim is informal, and the clip does not actually bound the KL divergence — it only zeroes the gradient locally, so across K epochs the realized policy can still drift. Subsequent reproductions found that PPO's strong numbers lean heavily on code-level details the paper omits (advantage normalization, value-function clipping, learning-rate annealing, orthogonal initialization), which is exactly the kind of thing a 3-seed, single-scalar eval cannot surface.

baselines

Contemporary and apt (TRPO, A2C, ACER, CEM, vanilla PG), but all author-tuned; "well-tuned" is asserted, not auditable. No DQN/Rainbow on Atari.

contamination

N/A in the LLM sense — these are RL benchmarks. The analogous risk is overfitting ε and code-level choices to the 7-task MuJoCo suite used for selection.

what's missed

No variance/error bars (3 seeds); the clip gives no monotonic-improvement guarantee and doesn't truly constrain KL; ε=0.2 doesn't transfer to Atari.

would raise confidence

More seeds with reported confidence intervals; an equal-tuning-budget protocol; ablation of the code-level optimizations; a held-out task suite for choosing ε.

// bottom line

PPO trades TRPO's theoretical guarantee for a dead-simple first-order clip that works well enough across domains and is trivial to implement — and that simplicity, not peak performance (ACER beats it on final Atari scores), is the real contribution. The 2017 evaluation is solid for its moment but light: three seeds, self-tuned baselines, and the code-level details that actually matter left unstated. Its true validation came afterward, when PPO became the workhorse of RLHF and the template every modern policy-optimization method — GRPO included — is measured against.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. The clip is the whole idea. min(r·Â, clip(r, 1−ε, 1+ε)·Â) forms a pessimistic lower bound that removes any incentive to push the policy ratio past 1±ε — giving TRPO-like reliability with plain first-order SGD and a few lines of code change.
  2. Sample efficiency comes from reuse. The clip is what makes it safe to run K epochs of minibatch SGD over each batch of N×T samples, instead of the single update vanilla policy gradients allow.
  3. The numbers, exactly. ε = 0.2 scored 0.82 on MuJoCo, beating every KL-penalty variant and the −0.39 no-clip baseline; on Atari PPO won fast-learning on 30/49 games but ACER won final performance on 28/49. PPO's edge is simplicity + sample efficiency, not peak score.
  4. It became the default. PPO is the optimizer under RLHF (InstructGPT, ChatGPT) and the direct ancestor of GRPO and the DeepSeek-style RL methods elsewhere in this atlas — arguably the most consequential single algorithm here.
