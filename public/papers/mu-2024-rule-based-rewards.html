Rule Based Rewards for Language Model Safety

Instead of paying annotators to teach a model when to refuse, write the safety policy as a list of yes/no propositions, have an LLM grade each one, and feed that score straight into PPO. RBRs hit a 97.1 safety–usefulness F1 versus 91.7 for the human-data baseline — with about 1% of the human labels.

Tong Mu, Alec Helyar, Johannes Heidecke, Joshua Achiam, Andrea Vallone, Ian Kivlichan, Molly Lin, Alex Beutel, John Schulman, Lilian Weng · OpenAI

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

The standard recipe for safety is RLHF: collect human comparisons of model responses to unsafe requests, train a reward model (RM) on them, then optimize the policy against that RM with PPO. It works, but the human-data layer is the weak point. Safety judgements are nuanced — whether and how to respond to a borderline request — and if the labeling instructions are underspecified, annotators fall back on personal bias. The paper gives a concrete failure: on self-harm prompts, some annotators preferred responses that pointed users to a US suicide hotline number, useless for users elsewhere.

The result is a model that is either over-cautious (refusing safe requests) or off-key (refusing in a judgmental, preachy style). Fixing it means re-writing instructions and recollecting or relabeling data — slow and expensive — and the data goes stale every time the content policy or model capabilities shift.

AI-feedback methods like Constitutional AI cut the human cost, but they swing the other way: a constitution of broad principles ("choose the less harmful response") hands the grader LLM enormous discretion over what "harmful" means. For a production deployment you need to enforce a specific policy — which exact categories to refuse, and in what tone — not a vibe. And both human-data and CAI-style methods share a structural flaw: they distill the behavior spec into a static comparison dataset used to train the RM, where detail can be lost or imbalanced before RL ever begins.

// the gap in one line

How do you enforce a precise, frequently-changing safety policy in RL — with the controllability of detailed human instructions but without the cost, lag, and lossy distillation of human preference data?

// how it works

The method

RBR replaces the safety slice of the reward signal with a small, interpretable function built directly from the policy — and crucially, it injects that function during PPO rather than baking it into the RM first. Four building blocks:

1 · Propositions and rules

The lowest-level unit is a proposition: a binary statement about a completion given the prompt — refuses ("contains an unambiguous statement of inability to comply"), apology ("contains a short apology"), judgmental ("criticizes the user's request or values"), complies, helpful, illogical_continuation. A rule then ranks completions for each target response type by which propositions should be true. The paper uses three response types — Hard Refusal (brief apology + statement of inability), Soft Refusal (empathetic, nuanced decline, e.g. for self-harm), and Comply — across a simplified four-area content policy: Erotic, Hate, Criminal, and Self-Harm.

hard_refusal: ideal: refuses=True judgmental=False complies=False less_good: refuses=True judgmental=True complies=False unacceptable: complies=True comply: ideal: complies=True helpful=True less_good: complies=True helpful=False unacceptable: complies=False

The key empirical bet: LLMs are far more accurate judging one narrow binary ("is there an apology?") than scoring a whole completion against a sprawling policy at once. So the complex policy is shattered into many easy classification tasks.

2 · Features from an LLM grader

Each proposition becomes a feature φi(p, c): a few-shot classification-prompt asks a grader LLM to output only yes or no, and the probability of yes is the feature value. Propositions are also bundled into class features (e.g. "ideal", "less_good") by multiplying the relevant proposition probabilities and normalizing. The experiments use 20 features for Hard-Refusal, 23 for Soft-Refusal, and 18 for Comply. The grader is a helpful-only SFT model (chosen for high precision on disallowed content).

3 · Fit a tiny linear reward, add it to the RM

The RBR itself is just a linear model over those features, and the total RL reward is the helpful-only RM plus the RBR:

Rtot(p, c) = Rrm(p, c) + Σi=1..N wi · φi(p, c) // helpful-only RM reward + rule-based reward · only safety-relevant prompts get the RBR term

Weights w are fit on synthetic comparison data so the total reward enforces the policy's rankings, via a hinge loss over every pair where completion ca should outrank cb:

L(w) = 1|DRBR| Σ(p, ca ≻ cb) max( 0,  1 + Rtot(p, cb) − Rtot(p, ca) ) // margin loss: the policy-preferred completion must beat the worse one by ≥ 1

Because there are only a handful of parameters (propositions + five class probabilities), fitting runs on a laptop in minutes — orders of magnitude cheaper than retraining an RM.

4 · Synthetic data, almost no human labels

The same proposition structure makes the training data nearly free to generate. A helpful-only model is prompted to produce completions with targeted traits (a perfect refusal; a judgmental refusal; a disallowed-content completion), giving exactly the ranked sets weight-fitting needs (DRBR, ≈6.7k prompts × 4 completions). The "ideal" completions double as SFT data. The only human input is a tiny Gold set — 518 hand-labeled completions (268 Comply, 132 Hard-Refusal, 118 Soft-Refusal) — used to tune the classification-prompts, about 3% of SFT and 1% of RM data.

// what they found

The results

The headline metric is an F1 between Not-Unsafe (safety) and Not-Overrefuse (usefulness) — a single number that punishes a model for being unsafe or for over-refusing safe prompts. On human evaluation of Large models, RBR-PPO scores 97.1, beating both the human-safety-data baseline (Human-PPO, 91.7) and the helpful-only baseline (95.8). The internal automated metric tells the same story (94.4 vs 91.1 vs 92.1).

The decisive contrast is how each baseline fails. Human-PPO reaches near-perfect safety (100.0% Not-Unsafe in human eval) but collapses usefulness — its over-refusal cost jumps almost 14 points (84.7% Not-Overrefuse). RBR-PPO buys nearly all of that safety (97.3% Not-Unsafe) while holding usefulness at 97.0%.

It works on different reward models. Bolting an RBR onto an over-cautious Human-RM cuts over-refusals by 16 points; onto an RM trained with outdated safety data it improves both safety and over-refusal by ~10 points. It is data-efficient: matching the human baseline to the same 518 completions ("Human-match RBR-PPO") does worse on both axes — the small RM slice can't teach the refusal boundary, whereas the RBR enforces it directly. And capabilities are untouched: MMLU, Lambada, HellaSwag, and GPQA are within noise of the baselines (e.g. MMLU 74.4 RBR vs 75.9 helpful). External benchmarks agree — on XSTest over-refusal RBR-PPO matches the helpful model (99.5%), and on WildChat unsafe prompts it reaches 95–96% safety by ModAPI / Not-Unsafe / Llama Guard.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

The core claim — match human-data safety at far lower over-refusal and ~1% of the labels — is well supported and shows up under both automated and human grading. But the evaluation has a real circularity and a narrow operating envelope that the headline F1 hides.

The most load-bearing caveat is self-grading. The internal Not-Unsafe / Not-Overrefuse / Hard-Refusal-Style metrics are computed from the same RBR propositions used as the training signal — thresholded instead of weighted, but the same classifiers. The authors are candid about this and mitigate it three ways: there's no prompt overlap between train and eval; they report the grader's Gold-set accuracy (93.6% for Large, but only 74.8% Medium, 68.1% Small, 43.8% XSmall); and they add genuinely external checks (XSTest graded by GPT-4, ModAPI, Llama Guard 2, plus a researcher human eval). Those external signals are what keep the result credible — but "correlated errors" between the reward and the metric are exactly the kind of thing that inflates a self-scored number, and the cleanest external safety figure (WildChat) actually favors Human-PPO (98–99%) over RBR-PPO (95–96%).

The human baseline is also not a fixed quantity. Their first Human-PPO over-refused on every Comply prompt; they diagnosed an imbalance (too few low-ranked refusals for Comply prompts) and manually patched the RM data with ~50 hand-written refusals before getting a usable baseline. That's an honest anecdote that doubles as the paper's thesis — distillation into RM data is lossy — but it means the human baseline RBR beats was itself hand-tuned, and a differently-tuned one might sit elsewhere on the frontier.

baselines

Fair and relevant (Helpful-PPO, Human-PPO on the same prompts), but the Human-PPO baseline required a manual data-fixing step, and the data-efficiency comparison ("Human-match") admits a different annotator pool — a ballpark, not apples-to-apples.

contamination

No prompt leakage between train and eval, but the internal metric is partly self-graded by the same RBR classifiers — correlated error is possible. External graders (GPT-4 XSTest, ModAPI, Llama Guard) and a human eval are the real backstop.

what's missed

Scope is benign-distribution over/under-refusal, not adversarial robustness — no jailbreak / red-team stress test. The content policy is a simplified 4-area toy (Erotic, Hate, Criminal, Self-Harm), soft-refusal style was weak by default, and quality is bounded by grader accuracy, which craters on smaller graders.

would raise confidence

An adversarial-attack evaluation; held-out safety categories absent from the RBR; a fully independent (non-researcher) human eval; a production-scale, comprehensive content policy; and analysis of biases the grader LLM may import into the reward (the authors flag this as an ethical risk).

// bottom line

A genuinely practical idea: turn a safety spec into composable, LLM-graded propositions and put them in the RL loop, where they're cheap to fit, fast to update, and don't lose detail through RM distillation. The over-refusal win is real and the capability cost is negligible. Just read it for what it is — a controllability and usefulness result on well-specified, decomposable safety behaviors under a self-consistent metric, not a robustness result against adversaries or a claim that holds for subjective, hard-to-proposition tasks (which the authors explicitly leave to future work).

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. Rules as reward, not as data. RBR's key move is injecting an LLM-graded, policy-derived reward directly into PPO instead of distilling the policy into a static RM dataset — avoiding the lossy, hard-to-rebalance distillation step that made the human baseline over-refuse on everything.
  2. Decompose to make the grader accurate. Shattering a complex policy into many narrow yes/no propositions is what makes LLM grading reliable enough to use as reward — and it makes targeted synthetic training data trivial to generate, cutting human labels to ~1%.
  3. The frontier, not just the average. RBR's value is balancing safety against over-refusal (F1 97.1 vs 91.7) at near-zero capability cost and laptop-speed updates — but it's demonstrated on decomposable, benign-distribution safety, and is silent on adversarial robustness and subjective tasks.
