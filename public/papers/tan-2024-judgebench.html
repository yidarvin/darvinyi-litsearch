JudgeBench: A Benchmark for Evaluating LLM-based Judges

A benchmark built from objectively-correct/incorrect response pairs — not human preference — to test whether LLM judges can actually tell right from wrong on hard knowledge, reasoning, math, and coding questions. Most judges, including GPT-4o with a vanilla prompt, land barely above a coin flip.

Sijun Tan, Siyuan Zhuang, Kyle Montgomery, William Y. Tang, Alejandro Cuadron, Chenguang Wang, Raluca Ada Popa, Ion Stoica · UC Berkeley & Washington University in St. Louis

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

LLM-based judges — models prompted or fine-tuned to compare two responses and pick the better one — have quietly become critical infrastructure. They rank models on leaderboards (Chatbot Arena, MT-Bench), they select the best candidate at inference time, and they supply reward signal during RLHF and self-improvement loops. Almost none of that infrastructure has been stress-tested for whether the judge is actually right. That gap compounds: a judge used as a verifier during best-of-N sampling or as a reward model during RLHF does not just misjudge a single output — a systematically wrong judge actively steers a model's training toward whatever the judge mistakes for correctness, so a benchmark that only checks stylistic agreement with humans cannot catch the failure mode that matters most for these downstream uses.

Every prior judge benchmark — MTBench, LLMEval, FairEval — asks the same question: does the judge's verdict match what a crowdsourced human annotator would have picked? That is a reasonable proxy when the two candidate responses differ mainly in tone, helpfulness, or instruction-following, because ordinary people are good at spotting those differences. It breaks down once the task requires expert reasoning to check — a multi-step math derivation, a subtly buggy code snippet, a factual claim requiring domain knowledge. Humans evaluating under time pressure gravitate toward surface cues (length, confident phrasing, plausible-sounding structure) rather than verifying the actual logic, so the "ground truth" these benchmarks are calibrated against is itself unreliable exactly where judging is hardest.

The authors formalize this as a three-part hierarchy a good judge should respect, in strict priority order: (1) instruction following — does the response actually address what was asked; (2) factual and logical correctness — is the content right; and only once both are satisfied, (3) stylistic alignment with human taste. Principles (1) and (3) are exactly what existing benchmarks test, and humans are reasonably reliable judges of both. Principle (2) — is this actually correct — is the one crowdsourced human preference cannot be trusted to arbitrate once problems get hard, and no prior benchmark isolated it. LLMBar came closest by using objective preference labels, but only for instruction-following, not factual/logical correctness. JudgeBench is built to close that specific gap: a benchmark where the "better" response is defined by verifiable correctness, not by what a rushed annotator happens to prefer.

// how it works

The method

The central design problem is: how do you get response pairs that are (a) hard enough that judging them actually tests reasoning, (b) free of confounds like length or stylistic bias, and (c) labeled with objective, not subjective, ground truth? The paper's answer rests on one observation: if a model struggles to consistently generate correct, coherent responses to a challenging question, it will also struggle to differentiate between its correct and incorrect responses. That single insight drives the whole pipeline.

Turning any verifiable dataset into a judge benchmark

Rather than pulling two responses from two different models — which introduces stylistic confounds and lets a judge win just by recognizing "which model wrote this" (self-enhancement bias) — JudgeBench samples k responses from a single strong model (GPT-4o) to the same question, grades each one for correctness against the dataset's own verifier, and keeps only questions where the k samples split into at least one correct and one incorrect response. It then pairs a correct response with an incorrect one to form the evaluation instance. Because both responses come from the same model on the same prompt, they are similar in style and length by construction, and the label (which one is correct) is definitionally objective rather than a matter of taste.

Four source datasets, one per category

The pipeline is applied to four existing datasets, each chosen because it already ships a ground-truth answer and an automated way to check correctness:

  • Knowledge — MMLU-Pro. 12,032 college-level multiple-choice questions across 14 disciplines with up to 10 answer options, a harder, decontaminated successor to MMLU.
  • Reasoning — LiveBench (reasoning subset). Problems drawn from Big-Bench Hard and Zebra logic puzzles; LiveBench refreshes its question pool monthly specifically to stay contamination-resistant.
  • Mathematics — LiveBench (math subset). Competition-style problems (e.g., AMC12, USAMO), excluding the "olympiad" subset; likewise refreshed monthly.
  • Coding — LiveCodeBench. Over 300 contest problems from LeetCode, AtCoder, and Codeforces, explicitly built to be contamination-free.

Correctness checking combines two signals to avoid punishing responses for formatting rather than substance. For MMLU-Pro and LiveBench, model outputs are parsed by regex and matched against the ground truth; for MMLU-Pro specifically, models are instructed to repeat their final letter choice five times in a row (e.g. "AAAAA") to make regex extraction robust. Because regex-only checking sometimes marks a genuinely correct answer wrong purely due to formatting slips, the authors add a second pass: GPT-4o-mini reads the question, response, and ground truth and independently judges correctness, and any response where the regex check and GPT-4o-mini disagree is discarded rather than trusted either way. Some additional randomized down-sampling on MMLU-Pro and LiveBench keeps category sizes balanced. The result is 350 response pairs: 154 Knowledge, 98 Reasoning, 56 Math, 42 Coding — all constructed from GPT-4o's own responses.

Scoring: two trials, order-swapped

LLM judges are known to exhibit positional bias — favoring whichever response happens to be shown first. JudgeBench evaluates every judge on each pair twice, once as (A=correct, B=incorrect) and once with the positions swapped, and aggregates the two verdicts into one decision.

verdict = A≻B  iff  trial₁(A,B)→A≻B  and  trial₂(B,A)→A≻B // any tie in either trial, or a flip between trials (judge picks A first, then B second), counts as an incorrect / inconsistent verdict — there is no partial credit

Because every pair has exactly one objectively correct response, the only two valid decisions are "correct wins" or "incorrect wins" — so a judge with no signal at all should score 50% by chance, which anchors every result below against a known random baseline.

Guarding against length bias and pipeline bias

Two validity checks matter here. First, because both responses in a pair come from the same model, the well-documented tendency of judges to prefer longer responses is largely neutralized by construction: across all 350 pairs, correct and incorrect responses average 562.29 and 561.16 tokens respectively (GPT-4o tokenizer) — a negligible difference. Second, using GPT-4o to generate every pair could bias the benchmark against GPT-4o specifically, since the pairs are, almost tautologically, the ones GPT-4o itself found hard to get right. The authors test this directly with a second version of the dataset built from Claude-3.5-Sonnet responses instead (270 pairs) — the results of that ablation are discussed under "does it hold up?" below.

// what they found

The results

The headline finding is stark: on JudgeBench, most LLM-based judges perform close to random guessing. The Vanilla judge running GPT-4o — just prompting the model to pick A or B with no explanation — scores 50.86% overall, statistically indistinguishable from a coin flip. Giving GPT-4o a better prompt (the Arena-Hard Judge, which asks it to draft its own reference answer first) lifts this to 56.57%, and Google's VertexAI Evaluation service (Gemini-1.5-Pro) manages only 44.57% — actually below random.

Fine-tuned judges fare even worse on average, and for a very concrete reason: many of them simply fail to produce a usable decision. PandaLM selects a tie on 479 of 700 judgments; Prometheus2-bgb-8x7b outputs an invalid, unparseable verdict on 215 of 700; several fine-tuned judges are inconsistent between the two swapped trials over half the time (JudgeLM-7B: 59.71% inconsistent, Prometheus2-7b: 52.29%). The one clear exception is Skywork's judges, fine-tuned on Llama-3.1-8B/70B, which are the strongest fine-tuned models by a wide margin (53.43% / 57.43% overall) and actually beat the corresponding prompted Arena-Hard judge using the same base model.

Reasoning-enhanced models pull far ahead

Freezing the Arena-Hard prompt and swapping in different underlying models tells a very different story from the "judges are basically random" headline. o3-mini (high reasoning effort) reaches 80.86% overall, including a perfect 100.0% on Coding; o1-preview reaches 75.43%; DeepSeek-R1 reaches 73.14%. Among non-reasoning general-purpose models, Claude-3.5-Sonnet leads at 64.29%, well above GPT-4o's 56.57% and far above Claude-3-Haiku's 33.14% — a 31-point spread between the best and worst general models on the same prompt and pipeline.

Reward models hold up better — and more consistently — than general judges

Purpose-built reward models, evaluated by simply comparing their scalar scores for the two responses, cluster in a narrower and generally higher band than prompted or fine-tuned LLM judges: Skywork-Reward-Gemma-2-27B scores 64.29% (matching Claude-3.5-Sonnet, one of the strongest general-purpose judges), Skywork-Reward-Llama-3.1-8B scores 62.29%, InternLM2-20B-Reward 63.43%, InternLM2-7B-Reward 59.43%, and GRM-Gemma-2B 59.43%. Notably, the Skywork 8B reward model beats the plain Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct model used as an Arena-Hard judge by a huge margin (62.29% vs. 40.86%) — training a small model specifically to verify, rather than to converse, buys a large amount of judging accuracy. The authors note the reward models' scores all sit in a comparatively tight 59-64% band despite ranging from 2B to 27B parameters, and argue this points to training-data quality (these models are largely trained on HelpSteer2 and Skywork-Preference-80K) mattering more for verification skill than raw model scale.

More agents does not mean a better judge

ChatEval, the one multi-agent judge evaluated, scores 34.00% overall — worse than the single-prompt Vanilla GPT-4o baseline (50.86%) and worse than every reward model tested. In ChatEval's setup, two GPT-4o instances take on "General Public" and "Critic" personas and debate for up to four rounds before each independently scores the two candidates from 1–10. On JudgeBench's hard, single-correct-answer pairs, that extra deliberation does not surface a decisive error the way it might on a subjective helpfulness comparison — instead it appears to dilute the model's own judgment relative to just asking it directly once. This is a useful negative result: multi-agent debate is often pitched as a way to make LLM evaluation more robust, but on questions where the actual bottleneck is whether either debater can correctly verify a subtle logical or coding error in the first place, adding more rounds of talk does not manufacture reasoning capability that was not there to begin with.

Judging tracks solving, with one sharp exception

An ablation directly compares each model's accuracy as a solver (asked to answer the question from scratch) against its accuracy as a judge on the same questions. For GPT-4o, Claude-3.5-Sonnet, Llama-3.1-405B, and Gemini-1.5-Pro, judge and solver accuracy track each other closely overall — e.g. Claude-3.5-Sonnet: 64.57% solver vs. 64.29% judge. The one systematic exception is Coding, where the solver consistently outperforms the judge for every model tested (e.g. Claude-3.5-Sonnet: 88.10% solving vs. 64.29% judging), while in Math the judge often clearly outperforms the solver (e.g. GPT-4o: 58.93% solving vs. 75.00% judging). The authors read this as evidence that spotting a logical slip in a math derivation is comparatively easy once you're shown two candidate derivations, whereas verifying whether code is subtly broken is genuinely harder than writing correct code in the first place.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

JudgeBench is a sharp diagnosis of a real problem — human-preference-calibrated judge benchmarks stop meaning much once the underlying task requires expertise to verify — and the same-model pair-construction trick is a genuinely clever, cheap way to manufacture hard, style-matched, objectively-labeled pairs from any dataset that already has a verifier. But several parts of the headline story deserve a harder look.

The "near-random" headline is mostly a story about reasoning effort, not judgment

The framing "even GPT-4o performs barely better than random guessing" is true of the Vanilla prompt (50.86%) but the paper's own Table 2 shows the ceiling moves enormously once you spend more test-time compute: o3-mini (high) hits 80.86%, o1-preview hits 75.43%, and DeepSeek-R1 hits 73.14% — all using the exact same Arena-Hard prompt as the "barely-random" GPT-4o run. That is a 30-40 point swing driven entirely by which underlying model is doing the judging, not by anything about judge architecture or prompting cleverness. The paper's Section 4.4 solver-vs-judge ablation makes the same point from a different angle: judge accuracy "closely mirrors" solver accuracy for every model except in coding. Put together, these two results suggest JudgeBench is measuring something close to general problem-solving capability on the underlying task, re-expressed as a discrimination test, more than it is measuring a distinct "evaluative" skill that a strong solver might lack. The paper gestures at this ("the ability of the judge to verify the solution pairs is highly correlated with its ability to solve the problem itself") but never reports the most direct baseline that would settle it: a "solve it yourself, then compare to whichever candidate matches" judge, with no comparative reasoning about the two responses at all.

Fine-tuned judges are compared against a moving target

Several of the fine-tuned judges evaluated here — PandaLM, JudgeLM, Auto-J — are trained on 2023-era crowdsourced or GPT-4-distilled preference data and built on small 7B–13B base models with 2048-token context windows, which the authors themselves had to truncate to fit long candidate responses. Their poor scores (PandaLM: 13.14%; JudgeLM-7B: 25.14%) are real, but they are as much an artifact of stale training data, small base models, and truncated context as they are evidence that "fine-tuning for judging doesn't work" — which the paper's own Skywork results contradict, since Skywork's judges (fine-tuned on current Llama-3.1-8B/70B with better data) are the strongest fine-tuned models and beat their own prompted-judge counterparts using the same base model. The comparison set skews toward judges that were state-of-the-art a generation or two before JudgeBench was built, which flatters the "fine-tuning underperforms prompting/scale" narrative more than a like-for-like comparison would.

The gold-answer construction narrows what "correctness" means

Because every pair is built from a dataset with a mechanically checkable answer (a multiple-choice letter, a final numeric answer, a passing test suite), JudgeBench necessarily tests judges on closed-form, verifiable correctness. That is exactly the class of problem where a strong solver can, in principle, sidestep judgment entirely by re-deriving the answer and pattern-matching it against each candidate — which is presumably why judge accuracy tracks solver accuracy so tightly. Real-world judge deployments (grading open-ended writing, chat responses, agent trajectories) are precisely the cases where no automatically-checkable gold answer exists — that's why a judge is needed instead of a script. JudgeBench's own construction pipeline is inherently limited to domains where "objectively verifiable ground truth" already exists, so it says little about judge reliability in the much larger space of genuinely open-ended comparisons, and readers should not generalize the 50-65% headline numbers to, say, judging creative writing or multi-turn dialogue quality.

Contamination is handled at the question level, not the pair level

The choice of source datasets is contamination-conscious: LiveBench and LiveCodeBench are explicitly refreshed on a rolling monthly basis to resist being scraped into pretraining data, and MMLU-Pro is a harder, shortcut-resistant filter over MMLU. That protects the underlying questions. It does not protect the response pairs and gold labels JudgeBench itself generates and publishes — the 350 GPT-4o-derived pairs (plus the 270 Claude-3.5-Sonnet pairs from the ablation) are fixed, public, and paired with a leaderboard on GitHub. Once released, this exact answer key is exactly the kind of artifact that tends to leak into later pretraining corpora, the same failure mode the source datasets were chosen to avoid — and unlike LiveBench/LiveCodeBench, JudgeBench itself has no stated refresh cadence to counter it.

The generator-bias check is real, but only a single data point

The authors do directly test whether using GPT-4o to generate every pair advantages or disadvantages GPT-4o as a judge, by rebuilding the dataset from Claude-3.5-Sonnet responses instead. The effect is large and exactly in the predicted direction: Claude-3.5-Sonnet scores 64.3% on GPT-4o-generated pairs but drops to 44.8% on its own generated pairs, while GPT-4o rises from 56.6% to 53.9% — still short of matching Claude's peak on the other split. That is good, transparent science, and it is exactly the kind of ablation a benchmark paper should run. But it is a single swap between two frontier models from two labs; it does not establish how the bias behaves for models that share heavy distillation lineage with the generator (e.g., GPT-4o-mini being trained on GPT-4o outputs), which is a more insidious version of the same confound the paper is trying to rule out.

baselines

Prompted baselines (Vanilla, Arena-Hard, VertexAI) and fine-tuned baselines are both reasonable choices, but the fine-tuned set (PandaLM, JudgeLM, Auto-J) is drawn from 2023-era models with small context windows the authors had to truncate — a genuinely unfavorable comparison against 2024 prompted frontier judges.

contamination

Source questions (MMLU-Pro, LiveBench, LiveCodeBench) are chosen for contamination resistance, but the 350+270 response pairs and gold labels JudgeBench itself releases are static and public, with no stated refresh plan — the same leakage risk the source datasets were picked to avoid, one level up.

what's missed

The metric folds "confidently wrong," "flip-flopped between trials," and "tied both times" into one undifferentiated "incorrect" bucket, so a well-calibrated judge that abstains on a genuinely hard pair scores identically to one that confidently guesses wrong. And because every pair has a mechanically checkable gold answer, the benchmark mostly tests closed-form verification, not open-ended judgment.

would raise confidence

A reported "just re-solve, then match" naive baseline to isolate how much of judge accuracy is generic solving ability; separating tie/abstain from wrong in the scoring; a periodic refresh of the released pairs; and a generator-bias sweep across more than two models.

// bottom line

JudgeBench correctly identifies that human-preference alignment is the wrong yardstick for judges once responses require expertise to verify, and its pair-construction recipe is a reusable, low-cost contribution independent of the specific benchmark it produces. But its "judges are near random" framing is largely reasoning-capability-driven (o3-mini and DeepSeek-R1 blow past 70-80%) and domain-driven (closed-form, checkable tasks where judging shades into re-solving), so the benchmark is best read as "how good is this model at verifying checkable answers" rather than "how trustworthy is this model as a general-purpose judge."

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. JudgeBench reframes what an LLM-judge benchmark should measure: not "does the judge sound like a human rater," but "can the judge tell an objectively correct response from a subtly wrong one" — and finds that on the latter, most judges, including a vanilla-prompted GPT-4o (50.86%), are barely better than chance.
  2. The construction trick — sample several responses from one strong model, keep only questions where the model gets some right and some wrong, then pair a correct with an incorrect response — is a general, cheap recipe for turning any dataset with a verifier into a hard, style-matched, objectively-labeled judge benchmark, with measured length bias reduced to a 1-token average difference (562.29 vs 561.16 tokens).
  3. Reasoning-enhanced models (o3-mini-high: 80.86%, o1-preview: 75.43%, DeepSeek-R1: 73.14%) and specialized reward models (Skywork-Reward-Gemma-2-27B: 64.29%) dominate plain prompted judges (GPT-4o Arena-Hard: 56.57%), and judge accuracy tracks solver accuracy closely except in coding — evidence that JudgeBench is measuring reasoning/verification capability more than a distinct "judgment" skill.
