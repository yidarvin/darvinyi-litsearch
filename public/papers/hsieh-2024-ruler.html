RULER: What's the Real Context Size of Your Long-Context Language Models?

Needle-in-a-haystack says every long-context model is nearly perfect. RULER disagrees: it adds multi-hop tracing and aggregation tasks to the retrieval test, and finds that of 17 models claiming 32K tokens of context or more, only about half actually stay above a usable performance bar once they get there.

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

By early 2024, "long-context" had become a headline model-card spec — 128K here, 1M there — and the near-universal way of backing up the claim was the needle-in-a-haystack (NIAH) test: bury a short "needle" fact somewhere in a long "haystack" of distractor text and ask the model to retrieve it. Models routinely score close to 100% on vanilla NIAH at their claimed lengths, and that number gets quoted as proof the context window works.

The RULER authors argue this proves much less than it looks like it proves. NIAH tests exactly one behavior — pull a single, syntactically obvious fact out of a haystack — and near-perfect NIAH scores coexist with models that plainly cannot reason well over their own long context in other ways: aggregating information spread across the input, tracking references across many hops, or answering questions where the relevant content isn't cleanly demarcated. A model that aces vanilla retrieval can still be functionally short-context for tasks that actually require using everything in the window.

Existing "realistic" long-context benchmarks (ZeroSCROLLS, L-Eval, LongBench, BAMBOO, LooGLE, InfiniteBench — summarized in the paper's own Table 1) have the opposite problem: they're built from real documents, so sequence length and task difficulty can't be controlled independently, and correct answers often leak from a model's parametric (pre-training) knowledge rather than from the context actually provided. RULER's premise is that evaluating long-context models needs a benchmark that is synthetic and flexible — length and complexity as free dials, minimal reliance on memorized facts — but that goes well beyond retrieval to interrogate whether the model can actually reason over what it's holding in context.

// how it works

The method

RULER is a synthetic benchmark with four task categories, each generated programmatically so sequence length and task complexity can be independently dialed up.

1 · Retrieval — beyond vanilla NIAH

RULER keeps the classic single-needle retrieval test (S-NIAH) but adds three harder variants: multi-keys NIAH (many needles inserted, only one is the target — the rest are hard distractors), multi-values NIAH (multiple values share one key; all must be returned), and multi-queries NIAH (multiple needles with distinct keys, all queried at once). Needles are word/number/UUID key-value pairs; the haystack is either repeated noise sentences or Paul Graham essays.

2 · Multi-hop tracing — variable tracking (VT)

A new task designed as a minimal proxy for coreference-chain resolution: a variable X1 is bound to a value, then a chain of assignments (X2 = X1, X3 = X2, …) is scattered through the haystack, and the model must return every variable name that ultimately points to the original value. Difficulty scales with the number of hops and the number of parallel chains.

3 · Aggregation — proxies for summarization

Two tasks stand in for summarization, where the answer depends on the whole context rather than one located span: common words extraction (CWE) samples words from a uniform distribution and asks for the most frequent ones, and frequent words extraction (FWE) samples from a Zeta (Zipfian) distribution parameterized by α, asking for the top-3 most frequent words as α is varied to control difficulty.

4 · Question answering

Existing short-context QA datasets (SQuAD, HotpotQA) are extended to long context by inserting the golden answer paragraph among paragraphs randomly sampled from the same dataset as distractors — a real-world-flavored version of NIAH where the question is the query and the golden paragraph is the needle.

The authors ran a preliminary task-correlation study — clustering 18 candidate task configurations by how correlated their per-model score vectors are across eight open models — and confirmed the four categories form distinct, non-redundant clusters, then trimmed down to 13 representative tasks for the main evaluation, each tested at six lengths (4K, 8K, 16K, 32K, 64K, 128K) with 500 generated examples per length.

Scoring: effective length, not just accuracy

Rather than reporting a single accuracy number, RULER defines a model's effective context length: the longest length at which its 13-task average score still exceeds a fixed threshold — Llama2-7B's own performance at 4K (85.6%). This "effective" length is reported alongside each model's claimed length, so the gap between the two becomes the headline comparison. For finer-grained ranking, RULER also computes two weighted-average scores across all six lengths, with weights either increasing (wAvg. inc) or decreasing (wAvg. dec) with sequence length, simulating usage patterns dominated by longer or shorter inputs respectively.

// what they found

The results

Across 17 long-context models (15 open-source plus GPT-4 and Gemini-1.5-Pro), the claimed-vs-effective gap is stark. Every model claims 32K tokens of context or more, but only about half actually clear the Llama2-7B threshold at 32K:

The pattern is not subtle: Gemini-1.5-Pro is the standout, its effective length exceeding the maximum 128K tested, and it tops both weighted-average rankings. GPT-4 and Llama3.1-70B both claim 128K but their effective length is 64K — half their advertised window. Models trained to extreme claimed lengths often fare worst of all: GradientAI/Llama3-70B (claimed 1M) is effective only to 16K, and LWM-7B (also claimed 1M) doesn't even clear the threshold at 4K. A large training context window turns out to be neither strictly necessary nor sufficient: Qwen2 (72B) reaches an effective 32K having been trained to only 32K (via inference-time length extrapolation), while Llama3.1 (70B) gets to the same 64K effective length via brute-force scaling of its training context to 128K — two different routes to a comparable result, while the 1M-trained models above land far short of either.

Why: task-by-task failure modes

Deeper analysis of Yi-34B-200K (which claims 200K and nominally performs well among open models) at lengths up to 256K exposes the specific ways NIAH-style perfection breaks down under RULER's harder tasks.

Specific failure modes the authors document in Yi-34B: non-robustness to needle type (UUID needles degrade far more than word/number ones); failure to ignore distractors (accuracy falls ~40 points at 256K when the haystack is packed with decoy needles); a tendency to copy verbatim from context rather than compute the answer (over 80% of Yi's output on the common-words-extraction task at 128K is just the in-context example copied back); unreliable multi-hop tracing that worsens with more hops or more chains; and frequent hallucination in long-context QA, where predictions at large context sizes start coinciding with the model's own no-context baseline — i.e., it stops using the context at all.

What actually helps

Ablating the LargeWorldModel (LWM) series at fixed 7B scale but varying training context (128K to 1M) shows larger training windows help overall, but the ranking is not strictly monotonic — LWM-1M actually underperforms LWM-512K at 256K, plausibly from insufficient adaptation to the new RoPE base frequency. Scaling Yi from 6B to 34B at a fixed 200K training length clearly improves both raw accuracy and the rate of degradation. And non-Transformer architectures — RWKV-v5-7B and Mamba-2.8B — trail the Transformer Llama2-7B baseline by a large margin up through 4K, the point where Llama2 itself starts to run past its own training length and degrade; the comparison is tested only up to 8K, but nothing in that short range suggests these alternatives have closed the architectural gap at the scales tested.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

RULER's core contribution — showing that claimed context length and functional context length are very different numbers — is well-evidenced and has aged into something close to a field standard: "effective context length via RULER" is now a common line in long-context model cards. But several of the design choices that make RULER practical also limit exactly what its numbers can be read to mean.

The effective-length threshold is a single, somewhat arbitrary reference point. "Effective length" is defined relative to Llama2-7B's own 4K performance (85.6%) — a specific, fairly modest 2023 model's score, not a principled task-difficulty anchor. Change the reference model and every "effective length" number in the paper's headline table shifts. The paper doesn't report how sensitive the claimed/effective gaps are to this choice, which matters since the entire framing ("only half hold up at 32K") rests on it.

Synthetic tasks reduce contamination risk but the paper is explicit that they're unvalidated proxies. The Limitations section states directly that variable tracking and frequent-words-extraction are meant as stand-ins for real long-context skills (coreference resolution, summarization), but "the lack of easy-to-evaluate realistic long-context tasks prevents us from verifying the validity of these proxies" — the authors' own words, not an external critique. A model could conceivably specialize for RULER's synthetic distributional quirks (Zeta-distributed word frequencies, essay-shaped haystacks) without that translating cleanly to messier real documents.

Depth-of-needle position isn't controlled or reported. The well-documented "lost in the middle" phenomenon — models finding needles near the start/end of context more easily than the middle — is explicitly out of scope here: RULER reports one aggregate number per length rather than a depth-resolved curve, which the authors themselves flag as a limitation they plan to address (citing concurrent work like LV-Eval that does report depth). A model could ace RULER's aggregate score while still failing badly on needles buried at specific depths.

baselines

17 models spanning 6B to 132B-MoE parameters and both closed (GPT-4, Gemini-1.5-Pro) and open weights — a genuinely broad, current-for-2024 sweep, plus a useful non-Transformer arm (RWKV, Mamba). No o1/R1-class reasoning models, since none existed at submission.

contamination

Low by construction — synthetic key-value pairs, essay haystacks, and Zipfian word lists are close to memorization-proof, which is the paper's central selling point over realistic-document benchmarks. The QA category (SQuAD/HotpotQA-based) is the one partial exception, since those source documents are public.

what's missed

No depth-of-needle breakdown (acknowledged limitation); no short-context (<4K) evaluation, so it's unclear whether "effective length" failures reflect long-context-specific weaknesses or a model that was already mediocre at the tested tasks even at short lengths; no formal validation that VT/aggregation scores correlate with performance on real long-document tasks.

would raise confidence

A sensitivity analysis showing how the claimed/effective gap moves under different threshold references; depth-controlled variants of each task category; and a correlation study between RULER's synthetic-task scores and held-out realistic long-document benchmarks to validate the proxy tasks the paper itself flags as unverified.

// bottom line

RULER's headline finding — that needle-in-a-haystack near-perfection dramatically overstates functional context length, and that only about half of models claiming 32K+ actually deliver at 32K by a fixed threshold — is a real, useful, and since-corroborated correction to how the field reported long-context capability. The four-category task design is a sound way to probe beyond retrieval without inviting memorization. The honest caveats are the threshold's arbitrariness, the unvalidated realism of the synthetic proxy tasks (which the authors flag themselves), and the absence of depth-of-needle reporting — meaning RULER is best read as a strong relative ranking tool and a useful stress test, with its absolute "effective length" numbers taken as one reference point among several rather than a physical constant of the model.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. Needle-in-a-haystack overstates long-context capability. RULER adds multi-hop variable tracking, aggregation (common/frequent-words extraction), and harder multi-needle retrieval to the standard NIAH test — four synthetic, length-and-complexity-controllable task categories, trimmed to 13 representative configurations via a correlation study.
  2. Claimed length and effective length diverge sharply. Of 17 models tested (4K–128K), only about half hold up at their claimed 32K+ context by RULER's threshold (Llama2-7B's own 4K score); GPT-4 and Llama3.1-70B are effective to 64K against a 128K claim, and some 1M-claimed models (LWM-7B, GradientAI/Llama3-70B) are effective well under 32K.
  3. Read the number as a relative ranking, not a physical constant. "Effective length" depends on a single reference-model threshold the paper doesn't stress-test, and the synthetic proxy tasks (variable tracking, word-frequency extraction) are explicitly unvalidated stand-ins for real long-document skills — strong tool, but the authors' own limitations section is worth reading before quoting an absolute number.
