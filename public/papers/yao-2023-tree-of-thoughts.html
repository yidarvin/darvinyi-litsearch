Tree of Thoughts: Deliberate Problem Solving with Large Language Models

Generalize chain-of-thought into a searchable tree: let the LM propose several candidate "thoughts" per step, self-evaluate them in language, and run BFS or DFS with lookahead and backtracking. On Game of 24, GPT-4 with chain-of-thought solves 4% — Tree of Thoughts solves 74%.

Shunyu Yao, Dian Yu, Jeffrey Zhao, Izhak Shafran, Thomas L. Griffiths, Yuan Cao, Karthik Narasimhan · Princeton & Google DeepMind

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

For all their range, scaled-up language models still generate one way: token by token, left to right. Every choice is locally greedy and irrevocable — there is no mechanism to keep several options open, look ahead before committing, or back out of a bad start. The authors frame this as the LM running only on cognitive "System 1": fast, associative, automatic. What it lacks is "System 2" — slow, deliberate search.

Chain-of-Thought (CoT) prompting helps by inserting intermediate reasoning steps z₁…zn between question and answer, but it still samples one continuous chain. Self-Consistency (CoT-SC) ensembles k independent chains and takes a majority vote — but it only votes on final answers, never explores alternatives within a chain, and the "most frequent" rule only works when the output space is small (e.g. multiple choice). Neither can do what a human problem-solver does on a hard puzzle: try a branch, judge whether it's going anywhere, and if not, abandon it and try another.

The cost of greedy decoding is concrete. On Game of 24, the authors show ~60% of CoT samples are already doomed after the first step — the first three tokens (e.g. "4 + 9") rule out any path to 24, yet the model marches on. The gap this paper targets: give an off-the-shelf LM a way to explore, evaluate, and backtrack over intermediate thoughts — without any extra training.

// how it works

The method

Tree of Thoughts (ToT) reframes problem solving as search over a tree, drawing directly on Newell & Simon's 1950s view of problem-solving as navigating a combinatorial space. Each node is a state s = [x, z₁…zi]: the input plus the partial sequence of thoughts so far, where a "thought" is a coherent chunk of language — a line of arithmetic, a writing plan, a filled-in word. Any instantiation of ToT answers four questions:

1. Thought decomposition. How big is a thought? Problem-dependent: an intermediate equation for Game of 24, a whole paragraph plan for Creative Writing, a single word for Crosswords. Small enough that the LM can sample diverse candidates, big enough that the LM can meaningfully evaluate one.

2. Thought generator. From a state, propose k next-step candidates either by (a) sampling i.i.d. from a CoT prompt — good when the thought space is rich and diversity comes free (Creative Writing) — or (b) proposing sequentially with a "propose prompt" so the LM avoids duplicates when the space is constrained (Game of 24, Crosswords).

3. State evaluator. This is the novel move: use the LM itself as the search heuristic, in language. Classical search heuristics are either hand-programmed (Deep Blue) or learned (AlphaGo); ToT proposes a third option — the LM deliberately reasons about how promising a state is. Two strategies: (a) value each state independently (e.g. label a Game-of-24 partial "sure / maybe / impossible" via a few lookahead trials plus commonsense — "1 2 3 are too small to reach 24"), or (b) vote across states by comparing them in one prompt (better when quality is hard to score absolutely, like passage coherency). Either can be sampled multiple times to trade compute for a more robust heuristic.

4. Search algorithm. With generation and evaluation as primitives, plug in a classical search. ToT uses two simple ones — BFS, which keeps the b highest-valued states at each step (used for the shallow Game-of-24 and Creative-Writing trees, depth T ≤ 3), and DFS, which dives into the most promising state, prunes a subtree when the evaluator deems it hopeless, and backtracks to the parent (used for the deeper Crosswords tree). A* and MCTS are left as future work.

s = [x, z1…i]   →   St = arg max S⊆S′t, |S|=b  Σ s∈S V(pθ, s) // one BFS step: expand every kept state, score the frontier with the LM evaluator V, keep the b best

Conceptually IO prompting, CoT, CoT-SC, and self-refinement all fall out as special cases of ToT — trees of limited breadth and depth. The framework's selling points are modularity (swap the base LM, generator, evaluator, or search independently), adaptability, and convenience: no training, just a pre-trained LM and prompts.

// what they found

The results

Three deliberately hard tasks, all run on GPT-4 (Chat Completion, temperature 0.7, May 2023). In each, deliberate search opens a large gap over greedy prompting.

Game of 24 — search dominates sampling

Reach 24 from four numbers using +−×÷, scored over 100 hard games (4nums.com, indices 901–1000). Plain prompting is hopeless: IO 7.3%, CoT 4.0%, CoT-SC (k=100) 9.0%. ToT with breadth b = 1 already hits 45%, and b = 5 reaches 74%. Crucially this beats oracle sampling baselines — best-of-100 IO is 33% and best-of-100 CoT is 49%, both far below ToT exploring a comparable number of nodes. The error analysis is the punchline: most CoT failures are sealed at step one.

Creative Writing — more coherent, by GPT-4 and by humans

Write a 4-paragraph passage that ends each paragraph in a given random sentence. With no ground truth, coherency is scored two ways. By a GPT-4 1–10 judge (5 samples averaged), ToT 7.56 beats CoT 6.93 and IO 6.19. In a blind pairwise human study over 100 passage pairs, raters preferred ToT over CoT 41 times vs CoT over ToT only 21 (38 ties). Iterative refinement is especially effective here as a "third" thought generator, lifting IO 6.19 → 7.67 and ToT 7.56 → 7.91.

Mini Crosswords — DFS, pruning, and backtracking earn their keep

5×5 crosswords (20 test games) push ToT to its deepest search (up to 10 word-steps, DFS capped at 100 steps). IO and CoT stall below 16% word-level success; ToT reaches 78% letters / 60% words / 20% games (4 of 20 solved). The ablations show where the gains live: removing pruning drops words to 41.5%, and removing backtracking — a greedy fill — collapses it to 20%. An oracle "best DFS state" readout would hit 35% games, exposing that the simple output heuristic, not the search, is the ceiling here.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

The headline comparison is unusually honest. The authors don't just beat vanilla CoT (4%); they beat oracle best-of-100 CoT (49%), so the win isn't "ToT samples more." But that fairness comes with a real asterisk: cost. Their own Table 7 shows ToT runs Game of 24 at $0.74/case (5.5k completion tokens) vs CoT best-of-100's $0.47 and IO best-of-100's $0.13 — and on Creative Writing ToT costs roughly 5× the tokens. The 74% is bought with deliberate compute; the right framing is a quality-for-compute trade, and the paper is upfront that this is a knob, not a free lunch.

The bigger caveats are scope and scale. Everything is GPT-4-only (a single model, frozen to a May 5–16 2023 snapshot), on three small hand-built tasks with test sets of 100, 100, and just 20 games. The tasks were selected because GPT-4 fails them under standard prompting — which makes the gains vivid but says little about tasks the model already handles. The appendix admits as much: on GSM8K and StrategyQA, where GPT-4 is already strong, ToT barely moves the needle (CoT 86 → ToT 90 on GSM8K; 82 → 83 on StrategyQA). And the state evaluator is visibly imperfect — it prunes solvable crossword boards because GPT-4 doesn't recognize rare words, so the heuristic itself caps performance.

Contamination is a mild concern (Game-of-24 puzzles and crossword games are scraped from public sites), but the near-zero CoT scores argue against memorization. The human evaluation is the softest link: coherency was judged by "a subset of the authors" rather than independent raters — order was randomized and blind, but it isn't an arms-length study.

baselines

Strong and honest — not just CoT (4%) but oracle best-of-100 CoT (49%), CoT-SC (9%), and iterative-refine. The missing baseline is any task-specialized solver, which the authors deliberately exclude to test the LM as a general problem-solver.

contamination

Puzzles scraped from public web (4nums.com, GooBix), so leakage is possible — but GPT-4's 4–9% standard-prompt scores make memorization unlikely. Adjacent crossword games are similar, so only 20 spaced games are tested.

what's missed

Token/$ cost (ToT ≈ $0.74/case and ~5× tokens on writing); single model; tiny test sets (100/100/20); only tasks GPT-4 already fails; the LM evaluator itself is a performance ceiling (prunes solvable boards). No fine-tuning explored.

would raise confidence

Multiple model families and sizes; larger held-out test sets; external blind human raters; real-world long-horizon tasks (coding, agents); and an isolation of evaluator quality from search quality.

// bottom line

A genuine capability unlock on problems that need exploration, and — more durably — a conceptual template: cast LM inference as tree search with the model as its own language-space heuristic, generator, and pruner. The empirical case rests on three small GPT-4 tasks at real token cost, but the framing (System-1→System-2, LM-as-heuristic, plug-in BFS/DFS) is what propagated. ToT became a reference point for the test-time-compute, agent, and reasoning-RL waves that followed.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. Search beats sampling when initial steps are pivotal. ToT took GPT-4 on Game of 24 from 4% (CoT) to 74%, and beat oracle best-of-100 CoT (49%) — because ~60% of CoT runs are dead after the first thought, and only branching + backtracking can recover.
  2. The key idea is the LM as its own heuristic. Instead of programmed (Deep Blue) or learned (AlphaGo) search heuristics, ToT has the model evaluate states in language ("sure/maybe/impossible", or by voting) — flexible, training-free, and inspectable, but only as good as the model's self-assessment.
  3. It's a framework, not a trick, and it isn't free. IO/CoT/CoT-SC are special cases; you choose thought size, generate-vs-vote, and BFS-vs-DFS per problem. The price is compute (~$0.74/case, up to ~5× tokens), and it mostly helps on tasks the base model can't already do.
