Visual Instruction Tuning

The first attempt to extend instruction tuning into the vision-language domain: bolt a frozen CLIP vision encoder onto Vicuna through a single trainable linear projection, train the whole thing on 158K instruction-following examples that GPT-4 generated from nothing but image captions and bounding boxes, and get LLaVA — a chatbot that reaches 85.1% of GPT-4's quality on its own synthetic multimodal instruction benchmark and, ensembled with GPT-4 as judge, a new 92.53% state of the art on ScienceQA.

Haotian Liu, Chunyuan Li, Qingyang Wu, Yong Jae Lee

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

By April 2023, instruction tuning had already remade text-only LLMs: take a base language model, fine-tune it on machine-generated instruction/response pairs, and it goes from a next-token predictor to something that follows arbitrary human requests — InstructGPT/ChatGPT, FLAN-T5, FLAN-PaLM, Alpaca, Vicuna. None of that had happened yet for vision-language models. The existing large multimodal models — Flamingo, BLIP-2, OpenFlamingo, KOSMOS-1 — were trained on image-text pairs (captioning-style objectives), not on instructions, and the paper's own comparisons show it: asked "what is unusual about this image?", BLIP-2 and OpenFlamingo just describe the scene instead of answering the question.

The blocker was data, not modeling: there was no multimodal analogue of the instruction-following datasets that made text-only instruction tuning work, and hand-collecting one — pairing images with diverse, richly-annotated instructions and responses — is slow and expensive. The paper's core move is to sidestep human annotation almost entirely: use a strong language-only GPT-4 (which cannot see images at all) to generate multimodal instruction data by feeding it symbolic text stand-ins for an image — its caption(s) and object bounding boxes — and prompting it to produce conversations, detailed descriptions, and complex reasoning Q&A as if it could see the picture.

// how it works

The method

Two contributions, glued together. First, a data-generation pipeline: for each COCO image, the authors feed GPT-4 (or ChatGPT, in early ablations) the image's existing captions plus object bounding boxes as a purely textual stand-in for visual content — GPT-4 never sees the pixels — and, seeded by a handful of hand-written in-context examples, generate three response types: conversation (multi-turn Q&A about the visual content), detailed description, and complex reasoning (step-by-step answers requiring inference beyond what's literally stated). This produces LLaVA-Instruct-158K: 158K samples total — 58K conversation, 23K detailed description, and 77K complex reasoning.

Second, the model: a pre-trained CLIP ViT-L/14 vision encoder produces a grid of visual features Zv for an input image Xv; a single trainable linear projection matrix W maps those features into the same embedding space as the language model's word tokens, producing a sequence of visual tokens Hv that are prepended alongside the language instruction tokens. The language model itself is Vicuna, chosen for having "the best instruction following capabilities in language tasks among publicly available checkpoints" at the time.

Hv = W · Zv,  with Zv = g(Xv) // the entire vision→language bridge is one trainable matrix W; the CLIP encoder g(·) stays frozen throughout

Training runs in two stages on 8×A100s, following Vicuna's hyperparameters. Stage 1 (feature alignment pre-training): both the vision encoder and the LLM are kept fully frozen; only W is trained, on 595K image-text pairs filtered down from CC3M, converted into single-turn "briefly describe this image" instructions, for 1 epoch (lr 2e-3, batch size 128). This is framed explicitly as training a vision tokenizer compatible with a frozen LLM, not as teaching the LLM anything new. Stage 2 (end-to-end fine-tuning): the vision encoder stays frozen, but now both W and the LLM's weights are updated, for 3 epochs (lr 2e-5, batch 32) on two possible targets — the full 158K multimodal chatbot data, or the ScienceQA training split reformatted as single-turn reasoning-then-answer conversations.

The default model size is 13B parameters; a 7B variant is also trained and evaluated. For ScienceQA, the model is prompted to predict a free-form reasoning explanation before its final multiple-choice answer, and trained for 12 epochs.

// what they found

The results

Because no prior work directly measured multimodal instruction-following quality, the paper builds its own benchmark, LLaVA-Bench, in two flavors, and grades every model's answer with GPT-4 itself: GPT-4 is fed the image only as ground-truth text (captions + boxes), asked to produce a reference answer, and then asked to score a candidate model's answer against that reference on a 1–10 scale, reported as a relative score against the GPT-4 reference.

LLaVA-Bench (COCO) — 30 randomly-selected COCO-Val-2014 images, 90 questions across the three response types — is used for an ablation over training data composition (Table 4 in the paper): training on the full 158K mix reaches an overall relative score of 85.1%; dropping conversation-type data down to only "Detail + Complex" drops it to 81.9% (−3.2); using only a small sampled mix of Conv + 5% Detail + 10% Complex drops it further to 80.5% (−4.4); using conversation data alone drops it to 73.8% (−11.3); and — the most telling ablation — skipping instruction tuning entirely collapses the score to 21.5% (−63.6), the clearest evidence in the paper that the instruction-tuning step, not just scale or architecture, is doing the work.

LLaVA-Bench (In-the-Wild) — 24 more challenging images (indoor/outdoor scenes, memes, paintings, sketches) with 60 manually-curated questions — is used to compare LLaVA against BLIP-2 and OpenFlamingo directly. LLaVA reaches an overall relative score of 67.3% ± 2.0, versus BLIP-2's 38.1% ± 1.0 (a reported +29% gap) and OpenFlamingo's 19.1% ± 0.4 (a reported +48% gap); on the complex-reasoning subset specifically LLaVA hits 81.7% ± 1.8.

On ScienceQA (21K multimodal multiple-choice questions), LLaVA fine-tuned alone reaches 90.92% accuracy — close to the prior SoTA, MM-CoTLarge, at 91.68%. The paper then tries two ways of combining LLaVA with a separate, text-only GPT-4 (2-shot in-context-learning, 82.69% accuracy alone): a "GPT-4 complement" scheme (fall back to LLaVA whenever GPT-4 declines to answer) reaches 90.97%, and a "GPT-4 as judge" scheme (whenever LLaVA and GPT-4 disagree, re-prompt GPT-4 with both answers to pick a final one) reaches 92.53% — a new state of the art, which the paper calls the first documented case of GPT-4 being used for model ensembling.

Ablations on ScienceQA design choices (Table 8) are all modest: using CLIP's last-layer features instead of the layer before it costs 0.96 points; predicting the final answer before the reasoning (instead of after) costs 1.15 points; skipping Stage-1 pre-training and training on ScienceQA from scratch costs 5.11 points, the largest single ablation effect in the paper; and dropping from 13B to 7B costs 1.08 points.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

The headline evaluations all route through GPT-4, wearing three different hats in the same paper: GPT-4 generates the 158K-sample training corpus, GPT-4 (blind, text-only) produces the reference answers for LLaVA-Bench, GPT-4 grades LLaVA's answers against those references, and GPT-4 is then directly ensembled into the ScienceQA SoTA number. That's a tightly closed loop — a model judging (and partly authoring) the training signal and the test signal for a system built to imitate it — and the paper never checks whether the GPT-4 judge's 1–10 scores correlate with independent human preference. The specific "bag of patches" failure the authors themselves surface (LLaVA answers "yes" when asked if strawberry-flavored yogurt is in a fridge that contains none) shows the judge-based score can miss ungrounded, fluent-sounding hallucination — exactly the failure mode a reference-comparison LLM judge, which never looks at the actual pixels either, is least equipped to catch.

The benchmarks themselves are small: LLaVA-Bench (COCO) is 30 images / 90 questions, LLaVA-Bench (In-the-Wild) is 24 images / 60 questions. The In-the-Wild numbers report a standard deviation across 3 inference runs, which is honest, but a benchmark this size still leaves wide confidence intervals on any given percentage-point gap. And on the data-composition ablation (Table 4), the paper doesn't report variance at all, so a swing like 85.1% → 81.9% between two training-data mixes on a 90-question benchmark is presented as clean signal without any indication of how much of it is dataset-selection noise.

There's also an unstated train/eval boundary: the 158K instruction-tuning corpus is built from COCO images, and LLaVA-Bench (COCO) separately draws its 30 evaluation images from COCO-Val-2014. The paper never states that these two image sets are disjoint — a detail that matters given the eval and a chunk of the training data live in the same underlying image collection.

baselines

BLIP-2 and OpenFlamingo are legitimate baselines in the sense that they're the era's leading open multimodal models, but neither was instruction-tuned — so the comparison mostly demonstrates that instruction tuning beats no instruction tuning, which the paper already shows more directly with its own ablation (85.1% → 21.5% without it). There was no other instruction-tuned multimodal model to compare against yet, which is a real gap in the field the paper is filling, but it also means LLaVA's central claim is validated against a category of model it was never really competing with.

contamination

Not addressed. Both the 158K training corpus and LLaVA-Bench (COCO) draw images from COCO; the paper never states the two are disjoint. Stage-1 pre-training data (filtered CC3M) is separately sourced and lower risk.

what's missed

A grading rubric built on comparison-to-a-GPT-4-reference rewards fluency and plausibility, not factual grounding in the actual image — there's no dedicated hallucination or object-existence check (the kind later formalized by benchmarks like POPE), so a confidently wrong answer about image contents can still score well if it reads naturally.

would raise confidence

A human-preference correlation study for the GPT-4 judge; variance/CIs on the Table 4 ablation, not just point estimates on 90 questions; an explicit train/eval image-disjointness statement; and a grounding- or hallucination-specific eval alongside the chat-quality score.

// bottom line

The architectural claim — that a single trainable linear layer is enough to bridge a frozen vision encoder and a frozen-then-fine-tuned LLM, given the right instruction data — holds up well and was quickly validated by the field adopting it wholesale. The evaluation claims are shakier: they lean on GPT-4 grading GPT-4-flavored outputs against GPT-4-authored references, on benchmarks small enough that the ablation deltas are hard to trust as more than directional, with no check for whether the model is actually looking at the image or just producing plausible text conditioned loosely on it.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. LLaVA connects a frozen CLIP ViT-L/14 encoder to a Vicuna LLM through one trainable linear projection matrix, trained on 158K instruction-following examples that a text-only GPT-4 generated from nothing but image captions and bounding boxes — no human wrote multimodal instructions for this dataset.
  2. Instruction tuning is the dominant effect: on the paper's own ablation, dropping it collapses the LLaVA-Bench (COCO) relative score from 85.1% to 21.5%, a bigger swing than any architectural or data-mix choice tested.
  3. The evaluation methodology this paper popularized — GPT-4 as judge, scoring against a GPT-4-authored reference — became a field-wide default almost overnight, but the paper itself never validates that judge against human ratings, and its own qualitative failure case (hallucinating yogurt flavors) shows exactly the blind spot that method has.
