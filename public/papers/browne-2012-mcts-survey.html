// the gap

What problem is this solving?

Monte Carlo Tree Search didn't arrive as one paper — it congealed out of several independent 2006 results. Rémi Coulom coined the term while describing Monte Carlo evaluations for tree-based search; independently, Levente Kocsis and Csaba Szepesvári married the UCB1 multi-armed-bandit algorithm to tree search to give the Upper Confidence bounds applied to Trees (UCT) algorithm; and Sylvain Gelly and colleagues showed UCT applied to computer Go worked with "remarkable success" in their program MoGo. This survey's own Table 1 lays that chain out in full.

By late 2011, five years on, MCTS/UCT had been picked up, renamed, hybridized, and re-validated by dozens of independent groups across dozens of games and planning problems — RAVE and AMAF for Go, SP-MCTS for single-player puzzles, coalition reduction for multiplayer games, HOP and Information Set UCT for hidden-information card games, and on into non-game domains like scheduling and constraint satisfaction. Each result arrived with its own terminology and its own narrow empirical comparison; nothing connected "which enhancement helps in which kind of domain" across the whole literature. The one prior survey in the field, Rimmel et al.'s 2010 "Current Frontiers in Computer Go," covered only the Go-specific slice.

This paper's stated aim is to fill that gap directly: to be, in its own words, "by far the most comprehensive survey of MCTS methods to date" — pulling together almost 250 references, the majority published in the preceding five years "at a rate of almost one paper per week," into one taxonomy spanning the core algorithm, its variations, its enhancements, and its applications.

// how it works

The method

The survey's organizing spine is the core algorithm itself (Section 3), which every later variant is defined as a modification of. Each iteration repeats four steps, shown in Figure 2 below and formalized as the four-line MCTSSEARCH pseudocode (Algorithm 1): Selection — starting at the root, a tree policy recursively descends the existing tree, picking the "most urgent" expandable node; Expansion — one or more child nodes are added to the tree; Simulation — a default policy (in the simplest case, uniform-random play) runs the domain forward from the new node to a terminal state; Backpropagation — the terminal outcome is propagated back up through every node visited this iteration, updating each one's visit count N(v) and total reward Q(v). The tree policy (selection + expansion) and the default policy (simulation) are the two levers every enhancement in the paper turns out to be tuning; backpropagation itself is policy-free bookkeeping. Because intermediate states never need a hand-crafted evaluation function — only the final outcome of a playout matters — MCTS sidesteps the hardest problem in classical depth-limited minimax search for domains like Go, where no reliable static evaluator exists.

Section 3.3 then devotes its own subsection to the single tree-policy instantiation that dominates everything downstream: Kocsis and Szepesvári's UCT, which treats each node's child selection as an independent multi-armed bandit problem and picks the child maximizing:

UCT = Xj + 2Cp√(2·ln n ⁄ nj) // child-selection rule (Kocsis & Szepesvári, 2006 — refs [119],[120]); n = parent visit count, nⱼ = child j's visit count, Xⱼ = child j's mean simulated reward. Cp trades exploitation (Xⱼ) against exploration (the square-root term); Kocsis & Szepesvári show Cp = 1/√2 satisfies the Hoeffding inequality for rewards in [0,1].

The survey cites this UCT paper more than any other single source in the taxonomy that follows, and Sections 4–6 are largely organized around variations and enhancements layered on top of it. Section 4 ("Variations") catalogues alternative problem settings the base algorithm was adapted to: single-player search (SP-MCTS, FUSE), multiplayer and multi-agent settings (coalition reduction, Ensemble UCT), real-time domains, nondeterministic and imperfect-information games (HOP, Sparse UCT, Information Set UCT, MCCFR), recursive self-nesting approaches (Nested Monte Carlo, NRPA), and sample-based planners drawn from the AI-planning literature (FSSS, UCTSAT, ρUCT). Section 5 ("Tree Policy Enhancements") catalogues alternatives to plain UCB1 for the selection step — UCB1-Tuned, Bayesian UCT, first-play urgency, progressive bias, the whole AMAF/RAVE family (all moves as first / rapid action value estimation, which shares statistics across transpositions to converge faster), and game-theoretic solvers (MCTS-Solver, MC-PNS). Section 6 ("Other Enhancements") covers everything outside the tree policy: simulation-policy tweaks (contextual Monte Carlo, learned pattern-based rollouts, "last good reply"), backpropagation weighting schemes, and parallelization strategies for exploiting multiple cores — leaf, root, and full-tree parallelization with either a global mutex or per-subtree local mutexes, shown below.

// what they found

The results

The survey's own two summary tables are its clearest statement of scope. Table 3 cross-references every named MCTS variation and enhancement discussed in the paper against every combinatorial game it had been tried on; Table 4 does the same for "other domains." Between them — by direct count of the rows and columns as printed — they tabulate 66 named variants and enhancements against 34 combinatorial games and game-playing programs (Go, Phantom Go, Hex, Havannah, Amazons, Arimaa, Shogi, Chess, CadiaPlayer, and 25 others) and 35 other games and non-game domains (Tron, Ms. Pac-Man, Poker, Backgammon, Settlers of Catan, security games, mixed-integer programming, scheduling, constraint satisfaction, and more) — a 66×69 grid marking, per cell, whether that enhancement has been tried in that domain.

The paper's own reading of that grid (Section 8, "Summary"): "UCT is by far the most widely used MCTS technique, and Go is by far the domain for which most enhancements have been tried, followed by Havannah and General Game Playing." It also notes that "MCTS enhancements are generally applied to combinatorial games, while MCTS variations are generally applied to other domain types" — the tree-policy tinkering concentrated on abstract board games, while the higher-level algorithmic adaptations (nondeterminism handling, multi-agent settings) concentrated elsewhere. Table 1 below, reproduced from the paper, gives the historical chain that got the field to this point.

Altogether the paper states it cites "almost 250 articles, the majority published within the last five years, at a rate of almost one paper per week" as of its October 2011 writing — a genuinely comprehensive sweep for a five-year-old subfield, and the reason it became the default citation for "what is MCTS" for the decade that followed.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

Judge this the way the other pure literature-survey pages in this atlas get judged: not on benchmark rigor (it runs no experiments of its own) but on completeness, organization, and how well it has aged.

On completeness and organization, it holds up remarkably well for what it is. The two-table taxonomy is unusually rigorous for a 2012 survey — most contemporaneous reviews summarized a literature in prose; this one built an explicit variant-by-domain matrix and let readers see the actual coverage gaps (heavy concentration on Go and abstract combinatorial games, comparatively thin coverage of the "other domains" table). The paper is also candid about negative results where it has them — discussing the "Score Bonus" backpropagation-weighting scheme, it notes plainly that the idea "did not improve playing strength" when tested on Sums of Switches, rather than only reporting positive findings, which is not guaranteed in a citation-hungry field.

Where it doesn't hold up is exactly where a paper frozen in October 2011 couldn't have held up: it has no way to anticipate what UCT-based MCTS would become. Sections 5–6 spend most of their length on the AMAF/RAVE family, progressive bias, and hand-tuned rollout policies as the primary route to stronger play — reasonable framing at the time, since simulation quality genuinely was the main lever available. Four years later, AlphaGo (2016) and then AlphaGo Zero (2017) — both of which cite this exact survey as their canonical MCTS reference (Browne et al., DOI 10.1109/TCIAIG.2012.2186810, appears in both papers' own Nature reference lists) — replaced the entire rollout-policy-plus-heuristics stack this survey catalogues with a single learned policy/value network guiding UCT-style search, and MuZero (2019) went further still and learned the search's own dynamics model. None of that is a fault of the survey — it's simply outside the five-year window it explicitly scoped itself to — but it does mean the "Research Directions" discussion in Section 8 reads today as a snapshot of a field about to be overtaken by an approach it could only gesture at.

A second, more structural limitation: the two summary tables record only whether an enhancement was tried in a domain, occasionally qualified with a "?" for an uncertain result — never whether it actually improved on that domain's prior state of the art. That's a map of the literature's activity, not a ranking of what worked; a reader looking for "which enhancement should I use for my domain" still has to go read the underlying ~250 cited papers to find out.

baselines

Not applicable in the benchmark sense — this is a synthesis of roughly 250 prior papers, not a new experiment. Its coverage claim is credible: the two cross-tabulated tables (66 variants × 69 domains) are unusually systematic for a review of this era, and it explicitly positions itself as superseding the narrower, Go-only 2010 survey it cites (Rimmel et al., "Current Frontiers in Computer Go").

contamination

Doesn't apply in the data-leakage sense; the closer analogue is survey-selection bias — a synthesis of published, MCTS-labeled work will tend to under-report negative results, though the authors do include at least one explicit "did not improve playing strength" finding (the Score Bonus backpropagation scheme), which is a reassuring sign they weren't only cataloguing wins.

what's missed

Everything the field did after October 2011, most importantly the deep-learning + MCTS fusion: AlphaGo (2016), AlphaGo Zero (2017), AlphaZero (2018), and MuZero (2019) replaced hand-crafted rollout policies and enhancements like RAVE and progressive bias with learned value/policy networks — a direction this survey's "future work" discussion could only lightly gesture at. Two of those four papers (AlphaGo, AlphaGo Zero) cite this survey directly as their MCTS reference.

would raise confidence

A living or periodically re-drawn successor — the way some newer survey pages in this atlas keep re-revising their taxonomy — rather than a single frozen 2012 snapshot; or at minimum a companion "MCTS after AlphaGo" addendum tying the taxonomy's enhancement families (RAVE, progressive bias, rollout policies) to what learned value/policy networks turned out to replace.

// bottom line

The correct citation for "what is MCTS, and what had the field tried with it by 2012" — its four-phase decomposition and its UCT formalization are still exactly how the algorithm is taught and cited today, including inside the very papers (AlphaGo, AlphaGo Zero) that went on to make MCTS famous outside games research. Don't read its "Research Directions" section as current guidance, though: it's a pre-deep-learning snapshot of a field that was, unknown to its authors, about five years from being transformed by exactly the kind of learned evaluation function Section 3.1 explains MCTS was designed to avoid needing.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. MCTS is one loop of four steps — selection (tree policy descends via UCB1/UCT), expansion, simulation (default policy), backpropagation — repeated until a compute budget runs out; the paper's four-line Algorithm 1 pseudocode is the whole thing.
  2. UCT (Kocsis & Szepesvári, 2006) — picking each child to maximize Xj + 2Cp√(2 ln n / nj) — is "by far the most widely used MCTS technique" by this survey's own count, and this paper is a large part of why "MCTS" and "UCT" get treated as near-synonyms in the literature that followed.
  3. The survey catalogues 66 named variants and enhancements across 69 games and domains from roughly 250 references — genuinely comprehensive for its 2012 moment — but it predates, and its "future work" section could not anticipate, the AlphaGo-era fusion of MCTS with learned value/policy networks that made the algorithm famous far beyond the games-research community that produced it.
