CRMArena: Understanding the Capacity of LLM Agents to Perform Professional CRM Tasks in Realistic Environments

Nine customer-service tasks, three professional personas, and a real Salesforce sandbox populated by an LLM data-generation pipeline that domain experts rated realistic 90% of the time — and even the best agent, o1 with hand-crafted tools, clears only 64.3% of tasks.

Kung-Hsiang Huang, Akshara Prabhakar, Sidharth Dhawan, Yixin Mao, Huan Wang, Silvio Savarese, Caiming Xiong, Philippe Laban, Chien-Sheng Wu

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

Enterprises want LLM agents inside their Customer Relationship Management (CRM) systems, but nobody can benchmark that honestly with real customer data — privacy rules block it. The workaround so far has been synthetic proxies for "work" in general: WorkBench, WorkArena, and τ-bench. CRMArena's authors argue all three fall short in the same two ways: the objects they model (tables, records) and the dependencies between them are far simpler than a real business schema, and the tasks themselves — navigating pages, filtering lists — are shallower than what a service agent, analyst, or manager actually does day to day. Table 1 in the paper makes this concrete: where WorkBench models 5 objects with essentially no cross-object dependencies and WorkArena models 7 objects at 0.86 dependencies/object, CRMArena models 16 objects at 1.31 dependencies/object — and is the only one of the three that is both grounded in a real-world environment (an actual Salesforce org) and validated by domain experts.

The fix is to stop trying to invent a toy CRM and instead mirror a real one. CRMArena generates synthetic-but-schema-faithful data for Salesforce's actual Service Cloud schema, uploads it into a genuine Salesforce sandbox organization, and defines nine tasks pulled directly from interviews with CRM professionals — spanning the Service Manager, Service Agent, and Service Analyst personas that make up day-to-day CRM work.

// how it works

The method

The pipeline has four stages, shown end-to-end in Figure 1: CRM data generation, query instance generation, agent benchmarking, and an expert study that validates the whole thing.

Two design choices do the real work of making the data feel real without using real customer records. First, object connectivity: the 16 objects (Account, Contact, Case, Order, Product, Knowledge Article, and so on) are generated to mirror Salesforce's actual Service Cloud schema, so a Case is genuinely linked to an Account, a Contact, and a User the way it would be in production. Second, latent variables — hidden causal factors invisible in the deployed sandbox that shape how the visible data was generated. A SHOPPINGHABIT variable governs when and what a customer buys; a SKILL variable determines whether an agent can resolve a case or must transfer it, which is what makes the Transfer Count Understanding task solvable at all — without a latent skill signal, there would be no principled reason for some cases to get transferred and others not.

Generation runs in mini-batches of 10 (a single LLM call can't produce, say, the 20,000+ PriceBookEntry rows needed), with a two-phase deduplication strategy (in-context "don't repeat" instructions, then string-exact-match cleanup) and a dual verifier: a format verifier rejects mini-batches missing required fields, and a content verifier checks task-specific feasibility — for Named Entity Disambiguation, it confirms a paraphrased product name is neither too far from the original nor confusable with something else the customer bought. The finished database is uploaded to a clean Salesforce Simple Demo Org without the latent variables baked in as visible fields, mirroring the fact that a real company wouldn't have access to that hidden structure either.

The nine tasks split across three personas:

Query instances come from 14 hand-written seed templates with placeholders (time period, product, extrema), ground-truth answers computed directly against the hidden latent variables, then paraphrased by an LLM for surface diversity — 130 instances per task × 9 tasks = 1,170 total queries, with 30% deliberately constructed as unanswerable ("false presupposition") queries that a well-calibrated agent should answer "None." Agents interact through either raw SOQL/SOSL queries (general-purpose Salesforce query languages) or 27 hand-crafted Python wrapper functions (task-specific tools), tested under three agentic frameworks — Act (direct action prediction), ReAct (thought + action), and Function Calling.

Realism itself is checked, not assumed: ten CRM professionals recruited via User Interviews were given sandbox access, assigned five sampled query instances each, and asked to rate the org and its objects against real systems they use daily.

// what they found

The results

The topline number, per the paper's own framing: state-of-the-art agents succeed in under 58% of tasks with ReAct (general-purpose SOQL/SOSL access only) and under 65% even with manually-crafted function-calling tools. Concretely, the best configuration anywhere in Table 2 is o1 under Function Calling at 64.3% overall; the best configuration without hand-built tools is o1 under ReAct at 57.7%. Every other model, in every framework, scores lower — most mid-sized and large non-reasoning models land somewhere in the teens-to-mid-50s depending on framework, while the smallest model tested (Llama 3.1 8B) scores near zero under all three (2.5% Act, 3.1% ReAct, 0.0% Function Calling).

The most actionable finding is that function-calling tools are not a uniform upgrade. Stronger models like GPT-4o and Claude 3.5 Sonnet benefit from hand-crafted functions, but weaker models frequently score worse with them than with plain ReAct — they can't reliably invoke the functions correctly, so structure becomes a liability rather than scaffolding. A controlled ablation (Table 4) removing categories of functions makes this precise: pulling CALCULATION + DEPENDENT functions (ones that both compute something and rely on another function's output) costs GPT-4o −26.7 points, but actually helps GPT-4o-mini (+4.0) and Claude-3-Sonnet (+3.3) — the same functions that are load-bearing scaffolding for a strong model are a source of failure for a weaker one.

DeepSeek-R1 is the paper's odd case: a strong reasoning model that nonetheless struggles with tool-calling specifically — 35.1% under ReAct, but only 9.0% once forced into a function-calling protocol, which the authors attribute to poor instruction adherence and poor use of execution feedback. Consistency is also a real problem across the board: the paper's passk metric (the probability all k independent trials of a task succeed, averaged over all tasks) degrades at nearly the same rate for Act, ReAct, and Function Calling as k grows from 2 to 10 — meaning none of the three frameworks meaningfully improves an agent's reliability across repeated attempts, even where they differ in single-shot accuracy. On cost, GPT-4o is the most efficient configuration tested: $0.182 per query and 5.4 turns under ReAct, versus $0.228 and 6.9 turns for Claude 3.5 Sonnet on the same setting.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

Grounding the benchmark in a real Salesforce sandbox with expert-validated synthetic data is a genuinely clever way around the "no benchmark can touch real enterprise data" wall — but a few choices in how that data and those experts were used deserve a second look.

baselines

The model roster (GPT-4o family, Claude 3.5/3 Sonnet, Llama 3.1, o1, DeepSeek-R1) and three agentic scaffolds (Act/ReAct/Function Calling) is a reasonably broad late-2024 snapshot, but it's prompting-only — no fine-tuned or memory-augmented CRM agent is tested, so the paper can't say how much of the 64.3% ceiling is a model-capability limit versus a scaffolding limit. It's also a Nov-2024 vintage benchmark with no GPT-5/Claude-4/Gemini-2.5-class model in the comparison — expected given the publication date, but it means the leaderboard numbers are already dated; the durable result is the qualitative one (function-calling isn't a free win, and it can actively hurt weaker models).

contamination

Classic answer-leakage is structurally blocked — the shoe-company org, its cases, and its ground truths are generated fresh, not scraped from any pretraining corpus. But the data generator is gpt-4o itself (the paper's own footnote: "we utilize gpt-4o as the LLM for data synthesis and content verification for its cost efficiency"), which means the synthetic case notes and knowledge articles are stylistically shaped by one specific model family. The paper doesn't test whether that family (or its successors) does unusually well partly because the text "sounds like" its own writing — a subtler self-preference confound than leakage, and one worth checking before treating GPT-4o's strong Function-Calling score as pure task-solving skill.

what's missed

The expert-realism study is 10 people, all recruited through one platform (User Interviews) and pre-screened to use Salesforce CRM "several times a day" — a sensible qualification bar, but the paper reports no inter-rater agreement statistic alongside the headline "90% rated it Realistic or better," so there's no way to tell whether that consensus was tight or driven by a couple of outlier raters. The benchmark's own Limitations section admits it covers only three of several CRM personas (explicitly excluding sales reps), so "professional CRM tasks" is narrower than it sounds — this is customer-service CRM, not full CRM. And Knowledge QA is graded by F1 against a reference answer, a lexical-overlap metric known to penalize correct-but-differently-phrased responses; the paper reports no human-judge spot-check to confirm F1 is actually tracking correctness on this task the way exact-match cleanly does for the other eight.

would raise confidence

Publishing inter-rater agreement (not just percentages) for the expert realism study; testing at least one agent from outside the "family" that generated the underlying data, to probe self-preference bias directly; extending the persona coverage the paper itself flags as future work — which its own six-months-later follow-up, CRMArena-Pro, does by adding personas and multi-turn interactions; and a human-expert score on the same 1,170 queries, so 64.3% has a known ceiling to be read against rather than standing alone.

// bottom line

CRMArena's real contribution is methodological: prove that a real enterprise sandbox plus an LLM-synthesized, schema-faithful, expert-validated dataset can stand in for data that privacy rules will never let a benchmark use directly. The function-calling-hurts-weak-models finding is concrete and actionable for anyone building CRM agents today. But the 64.3% headline should be read as a snapshot of one persona-slice (customer service, not full CRM), one data-generation family, and late-2024 models — not a durable capability ceiling.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. CRMArena grounds 9 CRM tasks across 3 personas (Service Manager, Service Agent, Service Analyst) in a real Salesforce sandbox populated by an LLM pipeline that models 16 interconnected objects and hidden latent variables — and 90% of domain experts rated the result "Realistic" or better.
  2. Even the best configuration (o1 with hand-crafted function-calling tools) clears only 64.3% of the 1,170 test queries overall; without hand-built tools, the best agent (o1 under ReAct) reaches 57.7%, and every other model scores lower still.
  3. Function-calling tools are not a uniform upgrade: they help strong models (GPT-4o, Claude 3.5 Sonnet) but can actively hurt weaker ones — removing calculation-heavy dependent functions cost GPT-4o 26.7 points while helping GPT-4o-mini and Claude-3-Sonnet.
