Can Large Language Models Be an Alternative to Human Evaluations?

Hand the model the same instruction, sample, and question you would give a human rater, parse the 1–5 Likert score it writes back, and call it a day. This is the paper that named "LLM evaluation" and showed text-davinci-003 and ChatGPT reproduce expert English teachers' rankings — the seed of the entire LLM-as-a-judge era.

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

Human evaluation is the gold standard for generated text, and also its weakest link. It is unstable — Karpinska et al. (2021) showed that low-quality Amazon Mechanical Turk crowds produce ratings so noisy you cannot tell two systems apart, and that AMT workers cannot even distinguish GPT-2-generated stories from human-written ones, while trained English teachers clearly can. It is unreproducible — you cannot re-hire the same annotators, and the ones you can re-hire have already seen the task and rate differently the second time. And it is slow and expensive — recruiting raters, writing guidelines, and waiting for results takes weeks.

Automatic metrics (BLEU, ROUGE, perplexity) sidestep all of that but only measure surface overlap; they are useless for the qualities — fluency, cohesion, relevance, likability — that actually motivate human evaluation in the first place. So the field was stuck between a method that measures the right thing badly and a method that measures the wrong thing cheaply.

Chiang and Lee's observation: instruction-tuned LLMs (GPT-3, InstructGPT, ChatGPT) had just learned to follow arbitrary task instructions zero-shot. A human-evaluation protocol is a task instruction. So why not give the model the exact instruction, sample, and rating question the human rater receives, and read off its answer? Submitted to ACL on 13 January 2023, this was — by the authors' account — the first paper to propose and validate using an LLM as a stand-in for the human rater itself.

// how it works

The method

The recipe is deliberately minimal — that is the point. Take the human-evaluation packet verbatim: a short task description, the sample to be judged, and a question asking for a rating on a 1–5 Likert scale ("How grammatically correct is the text of the story fragment? (on a scale of 1-5, with 1 being the lowest)"). Feed that text to the LLM, let it freely generate a response, and extract the numeric score with a few simple parsing rules. The only thing that changes between human evaluation and LLM evaluation is the identity of the rater.

score = Parse( LLM( instruction ⊕ sample ⊕ question ) ) // identical inputs to the human rater; only the rater is swapped. Sample 3 times to mimic 3 annotators.

Crucially the instructions are not tuned for the model — they are lifted from prior human-evaluation studies. To imitate having three annotators, they sample three answers from each LLM (ChatGPT, queried through the web UI with no parameter control, gets only one). They test four LLMs of increasing capability: T0, text-curie-001, text-davinci-003 (the strongest InstructGPT), and ChatGPT.

The validation strategy is a head-to-head against real experts on two very different tasks:

  • Open-ended story generation. A GPT-2 medium model fine-tuned on the WritingPrompts dataset generates 200 stories; 200 human-written stories share the same prompts. Each is rated on four attributes — grammaticality, cohesiveness, likability, relevance. The litmus test: a good evaluator should rate human-written stories higher than GPT-2's, the way English teachers do (and AMT crowds famously don't).
  • Adversarial attacks. Three classic synonym-substitution attacks — TextFooler, PWWS, BAE — perturb AG-News titles against a fine-tuned BERT classifier. 100 benign/adversarial pairs per attack are rated for fluency and meaning preservation. These are abnormal, often ungrammatical texts the model is unlikely to have seen — a stress test of whether the judge generalizes.

For the human side they deliberately avoid AMT and hire three certified English (ESL) teachers through Upwork — experts used to grading student essays — formatting their packet identically to the LLM's.

// what they found

The results

On stories, capability is the dividing line. The three English teachers rate human-written stories higher than GPT-2's on all four attributes (the ground truth). T0 and text-curie-001 show no clear preference — curie even rates the GPT-2 stories slightly higher on grammaticality (3.23 vs 3.19), and T0 on cohesiveness (3.02 vs 2.98). But text-davinci-003 and ChatGPT behave like the teachers: both rate human-written stories higher on every attribute, and Welch's t-test confirms every gap is statistically significant.

Two behavioral quirks fall out. text-davinci-003 grades generously (high absolute scores), while ChatGPT is fastidious and scores low — but both preserve the ranking, and the authors argue this is what matters: the absolute number encodes an evaluator's personal bias, so as long as you hold the evaluator fixed across systems the comparison is valid. ChatGPT can also explain its ratings, citing specific sentences; it sometimes refuses to rate likability ("I am an AI and I do not have the ability to experience enjoyment") and rates violent or impolite stories low, an artifact of safety tuning.

Do they agree story-by-story, not just on average?

Aggregate ranking is the easy bar; per-item agreement is the hard one. Kendall's τ between the teachers and text-davinci-003 is only weak-to-moderate: 0.14 / 0.18 / 0.19 on grammaticality / cohesiveness / likability for human-written stories, rising to 0.38 only on relevance (and 0.12 / 0.14 / 0.22 / 0.43 on GPT-2 stories). All are positive and significant (p < 0.05), but relevance — a near-mechanical check of whether the prompt's content appears in the story — correlates far better than grammaticality, which the instruction never actually defines.

Is it stable?

Reassuringly, yes. Adding a persona ("You are a human worker hired to rate…") or asking the model to explain itself shifts davinci's scores by < 0.1 on grammar/cohesion and ≤ 0.25 on the others — never flipping the human-vs-GPT-2 ranking. Dropping the sampling temperature from T = 1.0 to T = 0 nudges average ratings slightly down (argmax decoding is a touch harsher) but again never changes the ordering, and inter-sample agreement rises as temperature falls.

Adversarial text

On the second task ChatGPT passes a sanity check perfectly — asked whether two identical titles preserve meaning, it returns 5.00 — and then agrees with the teachers' overall picture: adversarial titles are far less fluent and meaning-preserving than benign ones, and BAE attacks are cleaner than TextFooler/PWWS. The judge is more lenient than the teachers on the artificial text, but the rank order survives.

The economics seal the argument. Hiring one English teacher to rate 200 stories cost US$140 and the whole human study took over a week end to end; the best InstructGPT did the same job for under US$5 in a few hours — and spared the raters from reading violent or hateful text.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

As a proof of concept it is genuinely prescient — and the foundation MT-Bench, AlpacaEval, and every rubric benchmark were built on. But read as evidence, the case is thinner than the headline "LLM evaluation is consistent with expert human evaluation" suggests, and the gap matters because the field took the headline and ran.

The biggest tell is the difference between what is claimed and what is measured. The strong result is ordinal and aggregate: the LLM preserves the system-level ranking (human-written > GPT-2). The weaker, quieter result is that item-level agreement — Kendall's τ of 0.12–0.22 on three of four attributes — is barely above noise. An evaluator can nail "system A beats system B on average" while disagreeing with humans on most individual examples, and that is roughly what happens here outside of relevance.

The reference itself is thin. The "ground truth" is three English teachers on two tasks (200 stories, 100 adversarial pairs per attack). The teachers' own inter-annotator agreement is low on subjective attributes and on model-generated text — the gold standard is shaky in exactly the regimes where you most need a judge. And the generators are easy marks: distinguishing human prose from GPT-2 medium is a low bar, which is why the result is really "the strong models clear a low bar that T0 and curie don't," not "LLMs grade at human level."

Most consequentially, the failure modes that later work made central go unprobed. There is no self-preference test: the judges (davinci, ChatGPT) grade a weak, different-family generator (GPT-2), never their own or a same-family rival — so the paper cannot speak to the self-enhancement bias MT-Bench would document months later, even though that is the bias that bites hardest in real LLM-as-judge pipelines. Verbosity and position biases are likewise untouched. And the grammaticality episode — one teacher rejecting ChatGPT's habit of counting punctuation as grammar errors — quietly exposes that the "rubric" is underspecified and each rater, human or model, applies its own private standard.

baselines

The human "ground truth" is just 3 ESL teachers with low agreement on subjective traits; the generator under test is GPT-2 medium — a weak, easy-to-beat target that doesn't probe near-human-quality grading.

contamination

WritingPrompts and AG-News are public and plausibly in the LLMs' pretraining; not a scoring leak (the task is rating, not answering), but the judge's priors about "good text" may be shaped by data they've seen. Unexamined.

what's missed

No self-preference / same-family test, no verbosity or position-bias probe, and per-item agreement (τ ≈ 0.12–0.22) is weak — the validation establishes ranking fidelity, not item-level fidelity, and only on a 2-task slice.

would raise confidence

More evaluators and tasks; stronger and same-family generators; reporting item-level agreement as the headline; meta-evaluating the judge against many human annotators; and open/local judges — the closed text-davinci-003 / ChatGPT have since been deprecated, so the study's own reproducibility pitch no longer holds.

// bottom line

A landmark first step that correctly anticipated where evaluation was going: a capable instruction-following LLM can stand in for a human rater and recover system rankings cheaply, reproducibly, and fast. Just don't over-read it — the evidence is two tasks, three teachers, and a weak generator, it validates aggregate ranking far more than per-story agreement, and the judge biases that dominate modern practice are exactly the ones it never tested.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. The origin of LLM-as-a-judge. Give the model the same instruction, sample, and rating question a human gets, parse its 1–5 answer. This paper named the idea ("LLM evaluation") and showed it works — months before MT-Bench formalized it into a leaderboard methodology.
  2. Capability gates it. text-davinci-003 and ChatGPT track expert English teachers (rating human-written stories above GPT-2's on all four attributes); the weaker T0 and text-curie-001 don't. A judge is only as good as the model behind it.
  3. It validates ranking, not item-level agreement. Stable across instructions and temperature, and cheap (under $5 vs $140 + a week), but per-story Kendall's τ is weak (0.12–0.22) outside relevance — and self-preference, verbosity, and position biases were left for later work to discover.
