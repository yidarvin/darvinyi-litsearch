On the Dangers of Stochastic Parrots: Can Language Models Be Too Big?

The position paper that coined “stochastic parrot.” It argues that scaling language models ever larger buys fluent text without any grasp of meaning — at environmental, financial, and social costs borne mostly by the people who benefit from the models least.

Emily M. Bender, Timnit Gebru, Angelina McMillan-Major, Shmargaret Shmitchell

What problem is this solving?

By early 2021 the dominant story in NLP was simple: bigger is better. In three years the field had gone from BERT (340M parameters, 16 GB of text, 2019) to GPT-3 (175B parameters, 570 GB) to Google’s Switch-C (1.57 trillion parameters, 2021) — and each jump bought new state-of-the-art numbers on English leaderboards. Scale had become a proxy for progress, and progress a proxy for understanding.

This paper is not another model. It is a deliberate step back to ask the question the leaderboard race never asked: how big is too big? The gap it targets is an accounting failure. The benefits of scaling were measured obsessively; the costs — carbon, dollars, undocumented training data, encoded bias, and the risk of mistaking statistical fluency for comprehension — were not measured at all. The authors argue that a field optimizing hard on one unaudited metric (benchmark score) while ignoring the bill is not doing careful science, and that the people who pay that bill are disproportionately those least likely to see any upside. The contribution is a structured risk analysis and a coined term — the stochastic parrot — that reframes what these systems actually are.

// how it works

The method

There is no experiment here. The “method” is a disciplined argument, built across four axes, that an LM’s scale is itself a risk surface. The authors marshal existing evidence rather than running new models — the rhetorical move is to make the unpriced costs of scaling legible.

1. Environmental and financial cost. Training and tuning ever-larger models consumes energy and money that scale with size. The paper foregrounds the carbon and dollar bill and argues it should be the first thing weighed, not an afterthought — especially because the communities that bear the environmental harm are not the ones reaping the benchmark gains.

2. Unfathomable training data. Datasets have grown past the point where anyone can document or audit them. Size does not guarantee diversity: web text overrepresents the already-overrepresented (younger, wealthier, Anglophone, men), and naive filtering of “bad words” further silences marginalized voices. Static snapshots freeze a worldview even as society’s norms move (value-lock), and the bias baked into the corpus is inherited by every model trained on it. Collecting data faster than you can document it, the authors warn, incurs documentation debt.

3. Down the garden path: form ≠ meaning. Drawing on Bender & Koller (2020), the paper argues LMs only ever manipulate linguistic form; they have no access to meaning, communicative intent, or a model of the world. Striking leaderboard gains on “meaning-sensitive” tasks therefore do not demonstrate natural-language understanding — treating them as if they do misdirects research effort.

4. The stochastic parrot. The four threads converge on the paper’s defining image. Because humans reflexively impute intent to fluent text, the danger is not that the model understands us but that we mistake its output for understanding:

The results

A position paper’s “results” are its claims and the numbers behind them. The sharpest are about cost. Citing Strubell et al. (2019), the paper notes that while the average human is responsible for an estimated 5 tonnes of CO₂e per year, training a single Transformer (big) model with neural-architecture search was estimated to emit 284 tonnes of CO₂, and that training one BERT base model on GPUs — without hyperparameter tuning — consumes roughly the energy of a trans-American flight. The moral weight comes from who pays: the environmental cost, the authors write, “doubly punishes marginalized communities that are least likely to benefit… and most likely to be harmed.”

The data argument is just as concrete. GPT-3’s training set includes a filtered slice of Common Crawl — “petabytes of data collected over 8 years of web crawling” — at 570 GB of text (Table 1). At that scale no team can read, document, or audit what went in, so the corpus’s skew toward hegemonic viewpoints and its encoded stereotypes pass silently into the model. The authors name this documentation debt and recommend “only creating datasets as large as can be sufficiently documented.”

From the parrot framing they derive a catalogue of follow-on harms: bias amplification as models absorb a hegemonic worldview; value-lock, where a static model reifies older, less-inclusive norms even as movements like Black Lives Matter reshape language; exposure to derogatory and abusive language; and — most consequentially — fluent synthetic text used for manipulation and disinformation at scale, because readers reflexively treat coherent strings as the intent of an accountable speaker. They cite a real machine-translation failure in which Facebook’s system rendered a Palestinian man’s Arabic “good morning” as “hurt them” (in English) and “attack them” (in Hebrew), leading to his arrest by Israeli police.

The closing prescription (“Paths Forward,” §7): weigh environmental and financial cost first; budget for dataset curation and documentation (data statements, datasheets); run pre-mortems and adopt value-sensitive design early; build an evaluation culture that looks past average-case leaderboard numbers; and fund research directions that do not depend on ever-larger LMs.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

This is an argument, not an experiment, so the honest evaluation is not “are the baselines fair?” but “do the arguments hold up — and where do they lean on the strongest available framing?” On its central forecast, the paper has aged well: bias amplification, disinformation at scale, undocumented training corpora, and hype outrunning capability are now mainstream concerns, and “stochastic parrot” became the field’s shorthand for the gap between fluency and understanding. That predictive track record is the best evidence in its favor.

Two load-bearing claims are softer than the rhetoric. First, the carbon numbers: the headline 284 t CO₂ is borrowed from Strubell et al. and comes from an extreme neural-architecture-search run, not a single training job. Later analyses (e.g., Patterson et al., 2021) argued such figures overstate the cost of a routine train by a large margin — one to two orders of magnitude — once efficient accelerators, cleaner datacenters, and train-once economics are counted. The directional point (scale costs energy, and the field under-reports it) is sound and helped normalize compute/carbon reporting; the specific number is worst-case. Second, the “no understanding” thesis is a philosophical stance (form vs. meaning, after Bender & Koller) asserted rather than demonstrated. It is the paper’s most contested claim: in-context learning, chain-of-thought, and emergent benchmark behavior have made “they only manipulate form” hard to defend as a flat empirical statement, and the paper offers no test that would distinguish genuine understanding from very good form-manipulation — which makes the claim difficult to falsify even as the deeper point about grounding and accountability stays live.

The paper is also lighter on the trade-off than on the costs. It does cite one cost-per-capability figure — via Strubell, that buying 0.1 BLEU on English–German translation with neural-architecture search costs roughly $150,000 in compute — and explicitly frames a “risk/benefit” analysis. But across its social-harm axes (bias, disinformation) the costs stay qualitative: it can tell you scaling is risky, not how much risk accrues per unit of capability. Its recommendations — “document sufficiently,” “weigh cost first” — are directionally right but lack operational thresholds, and the field’s uptake (datasheets, data statements, model cards) has been uneven. Context worth knowing: this is the paper at the center of Timnit Gebru’s and Margaret Mitchell’s departures from Google, and “Shmargaret Shmitchell” is a pseudonym — history that explains its cultural weight but is orthogonal to whether its arguments are correct.

baselines

Not an empirical paper. Its “baseline” is the scaling-is-progress consensus it argues against; evidence is synthesized from prior work (Strubell on energy, Bender & Koller on meaning), not originally measured.

contamination

N/A — no benchmark. But the headline 284 t CO₂ figure is a worst-case NAS estimate, not a representative single-model train; later work argued such numbers overstate routine training cost by a large margin.

what's missed

Beyond one borrowed figure ($150k per 0.1 BLEU), the social-harm costs (bias, disinformation) stay qualitative, not quantified; the “no understanding” thesis is asserted, not tested, and comes with no falsifiable experiment; Table 1 caps at 2021-era scale (Switch-C), predating GPT-3.5/4.

would raise confidence

Operational thresholds for “sufficiently documented,” falsifiable predictions, and direct engagement with the strongest capability evidence rather than treating form-manipulation as a settled fact.

// bottom line

Five years on, the harms it forecast largely arrived and its coinage entered the field’s vocabulary — but its most-quoted carbon figure leaned on a worst-case estimate, and its central “no understanding” claim is a philosophical stance it never turned into a test. Read it as the foundational harms-side counterweight to the scaling narrative, not as a measurement.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. “Stochastic parrot” is a thesis, not an insult. An LM stitches together linguistic form by probability with no reference to meaning; fluent output is not evidence of understanding, and the real risk is that humans reflexively supply the meaning the model lacks.
  2. Scale has an unpriced bill. Bigger models cost carbon, money, and auditability — ~284 t CO₂ for one NAS-tuned Transformer, 570 GB of undocumentable web text in GPT-3 — and those costs fall hardest on the communities least likely to benefit. Weigh them first, and only build datasets you can document.
  3. It aged into the field’s conscience. The harms it predicted — bias, disinformation at scale, hype outrunning capability — largely materialized, which is why it’s load-bearing; but its carbon numbers were worst-case and its “no understanding” claim was asserted, never operationalized into a test.
