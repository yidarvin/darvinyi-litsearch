Self-Consistency Improves Chain of Thought Reasoning in Language Models

Stop decoding one chain of thought greedily. Sample many, then vote. This one-line change to the decoder — no training, no verifier, no extra data — lifts GSM8K by +17.9 on PaLM-540B and turns chain-of-thought from a fragile single-shot trick into the test-time-compute recipe behind maj@k and cons@k.

Wang, Wei, Schuurmans, Le, Chi, Narang, Chowdhery, Zhou · Google Research, Brain Team

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

Chain-of-thought prompting (Wei et al., 2022) had just shown that adding a few worked-out reasoning exemplars to a prompt unlocks multi-step reasoning — but it still decoded the answer the old way: greedily. The model generates exactly one reasoning path, token by token, and whatever answer that single path lands on is the answer. That makes the whole apparatus brittle. One early arithmetic slip, one wrong branch in the reasoning, and the answer is wrong with no way to recover; the model commits to its first plausible line of thought and never reconsiders.

The fixes on offer all bolted extra machinery onto the model. Cobbe et al. (2021) trained a separate verifier to re-rank generated solutions; LaMDA's authors collected human annotations to train a re-ranker; others fine-tuned on annotated reasoning paths. Every one of these is task-specific and demands additional training, labeled data, or a second model. None of them improve reasoning at the moment of decoding, off the shelf.

The gap this paper targets is narrow and practical: can you make chain-of-thought reasoning substantially more reliable without training anything? No verifier, no extra annotations, no fine-tuning — just a smarter way to read the answer out of a model you already have.

// how it works

The method

Self-consistency rests on one observation about how people solve hard problems: a complex question usually admits many different routes to the same correct answer. If you can reach the right answer by several distinct lines of reasoning, then the right answer should be the one that the most reasoning paths agree on. Wrong paths, by contrast, tend to fail in idiosyncratic, mutually-disagreeing ways. So instead of trusting one chain, generate a crowd of them and let them vote.

Concretely it replaces greedy decoding with a three-step sample-and-marginalize procedure. (1) Prompt the model with the same chain-of-thought exemplars as before. (2) Rather than greedily decode a single path, sample a diverse set of reasoning paths from the decoder — using ordinary temperature, top-k, or nucleus sampling. (3) Each sampled output is a (reasoning path ri, answer ai) pair; marginalize out the reasoning path by taking a majority vote over the final answers — keep the most consistent answer.

a* = arg maxa Σi=1m 𝟙(ai = a) // pick the answer the most sampled paths agree on — majority vote over m outputs, with each reasoning path ri summed out as a latent variable

You can do something fancier — weight each answer by the model's probability of generating its path, P(ri, ai | prompt), normalized by length — but the paper finds it barely matters. On PaLM-540B/GSM8K the unweighted majority vote scores 74.4 while the normalized weighted sum scores 74.1; the model treats its sampled paths as roughly equally likely, so plain voting is enough. The headline numbers use 40 sampled paths, averaged over 10 runs.

Three properties make this matter. It is unsupervised and task-agnostic — no training, no labels, no verifier. It is a self-ensemble: a single model voting with itself, not an ensemble of different models or prompts. And it is a strict drop-in — anything that already does chain-of-thought can switch its decoder and get the gain. The one constraint: the final answer must come from a fixed answer set so that votes can be tallied (it doesn't directly apply to open-ended generation).

// what they found

The results

Across four models spanning a 27× range of scale — UL2-20B, LaMDA-137B, GPT-3 (code-davinci-001/002, 175B), and PaLM-540B — self-consistency improves chain-of-thought reasoning on every model and nearly every task, with no fine-tuning. The paper's own headline gains over greedy CoT, best model per task: GSM8K +17.9, AQuA +12.2, SVAMP +11.0, StrategyQA +6.4, ARC-challenge +3.9. PaLM-540B alone — the model in the table below — runs a touch lower on three of the five (Δ +12.5/+7.6/+3.5 on AQuA/SVAMP/ARC-c), since the abstract's per-task figures each draw from whichever of the four models scored best.

The most striking pattern is that the gains grow with scale. On the small UL2-20B the lift is a modest +3–6 points, but on LaMDA-137B and GPT-3 it jumps to +9–23 points — and even on the already-strong PaLM-540B and GPT-3 code-davinci-002, which start above 56% on GSM8K, self-consistency still adds +12–18 points on the hardest tasks (AQuA, GSM8K). On PaLM-540B, GSM8K climbs 56.5 → 74.4 and on GPT-3 code-davinci-002 it climbs 60.1 → 78.0. Despite being unsupervised, these set new state-of-the-art on almost every task — beating prior results that required task-specific fine-tuning with thousands of examples plus a trained verifier.

More paths help, with diminishing returns: accuracy rises steeply over the first ~10 samples and keeps creeping up to 40, where it saturates (Figure 2). Self-consistency also beats the obvious alternatives head-to-head — it outperforms sample-and-rank at the same sample budget, beam search decoding, and prompt-order / multi-prompt ensembles. It is robust to the sampling strategy and temperature, survives imperfect, equation-only, and even zero-shot prompts, and — notably — helps even on tasks where chain-of-thought normally hurts relative to standard prompting. Finally, the vote's agreement rate is correlated with accuracy, so "% consistent" doubles as a cheap, label-free uncertainty estimate.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

This is an unusually clean empirical paper, and the baselines are the right ones: it compares against greedy chain-of-thought (the honest baseline — the thing everyone was actually doing), and then goes further to beat sample-and-rank, beam search, and prompt/multi-prompt ensembles at matched budgets, plus the task-specific finetuned-plus-verifier prior SoTA. The scale sweep across four models is genuine evidence that the effect is not a quirk of one model family.

But the headline numbers hide a cost that the framing soft-pedals. Self-consistency buys accuracy with 40× the inference compute — 40 forward passes where greedy used one. Every reported gain is, implicitly, a gain at 40× the FLOPs, and the paper never reports a compute-matched comparison: would a single larger model, or one model run once with that same compute, do as well? Figure 2 partly rescues this — most of the lift arrives by ~5–10 samples, so the knob is tunable — but the marquee +17.9 leans on the full 40.

Two scope limits deserve flagging. First, the method only works when the answer is from a fixed set so votes can be tallied; open-ended generation needs a consistency metric the paper doesn't supply. Second, majority vote is only as good as the crowd: when a model is systematically biased toward a wrong answer, the votes concentrate on that wrong attractor and self-consistency confidently entrenches the error rather than fixing it. "Consistency" correlates with accuracy (Figure 5) but is not identical to it — and on the OOD 4-letter symbolic task, UL2-20B stays pinned at 0.0 with or without voting. Contamination, finally, is unmeasured: these 2022 models on GSM8K/CommonsenseQA may carry some pretraining exposure, which the absolute numbers can't disentangle (though the relative lift over greedy is the real claim and is contamination-neutral).

baselines

Strong and fair — greedy CoT, sample-and-rank, beam search, prompt/multi-prompt ensembles, and finetuned-plus-verifier SoTA, across four models from 20B to 540B.

contamination

Not measured on the 2022-era benchmarks, but the headline claim is the relative lift over greedy decoding, which is unaffected by leakage.

what's missed

No compute-matched comparison (every gain is at 40× FLOPs); majority vote can't fix a model biased toward a wrong answer, and the method needs a fixed answer set.

would raise confidence

Accuracy-vs-FLOPs curves against a larger single-pass model, and per-task analysis of when voting entrenches rather than repairs errors.

// bottom line

A training-free decoding change that turned chain-of-thought from a brittle single-shot trick into a reliable self-ensemble — at the price of N× sampling and a fixed-answer-set requirement. It holds up because the relative gains are large, consistent, and reproduced across scale; the asterisk is that it is the first clear "spend test-time compute to buy accuracy" lever, and the paper measures the accuracy without ever pricing the compute.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. Sample many chains, take the majority answer. No training, no verifier, no extra data — just a decoder swap that lifts GSM8K by +17.9 on PaLM-540B (56.5 → 74.4) and on GPT-3 (60.1 → 78.0).
  2. Diversity is the mechanism. Hard problems have many correct routes and idiosyncratic wrong ones, so the right answer is the consensus attractor; gains grow with model scale (+3–6 at 20B vs +9–23 at 137B+) and saturate around 40 sampled paths.
  3. It priced test-time compute. The cost is N× inference and a fixed-answer-set requirement, but it became the default scaling lever — the direct ancestor of maj@k / cons@k voting in the RLVR reasoning era (e.g. DeepSeek-R1's cons@16).
