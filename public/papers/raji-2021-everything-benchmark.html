AI and the Everything in the Whole Wide World Benchmark

A 1974 Sesame Street storybook sends Grover to a museum promising "everything in the whole wide world" — and of course it can't deliver. This position paper argues that ML's "general" benchmarks, ImageNet and GLUE chief among them, make the same impossible promise: a finite, convenience-built dataset is sold as a measure of general visual or linguistic ability it cannot possibly have. The diagnosis is construct validity.

Inioluwa Deborah Raji, Emily M. Bender, Amandalynne Paullada, Emily Denton, Alex Hanna

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

A handful of benchmarks have been elevated into something they were never built to be. ImageNet and GLUE began as datasets for concrete tasks — large-scale object recognition, a suite of English sentence-classification tasks — and ended up serving as proxies for "visual understanding" and "language understanding" in the abstract. State-of-the-art numbers on them are read, by creators and community alike, as milestones on the road to flexible, general-purpose AI. When Microsoft can frame "DeBERTa surpassing human performance on SuperGLUE" as "an important milestone toward general AI," the leaderboard has quietly become a claim about cognition.

The authors' objection is not that benchmarks are useless — they explicitly affirm their value — but that the rhetoric of generality wrapped around a finite dataset is a category error. Their term for it is construct validity: how well an experimental setup (here, a dataset plus a metric) actually represents the thing it claims to measure. As Bowman and Dahl had just noted, validity "is difficult to fully formalize, and we know of no simple test that would allow one to determine if a benchmark presents a valid measure of model ability" — so it gets neglected. This paper is an argument that for "general" benchmarks, the construct-validity gap isn't a rough edge to file down; it's structural, and no amount of scaling closes it.

// the central analogy

In Grover and the Everything in the Whole Wide World Museum (1974), Grover tours rooms like "The Carrot Room," "The Tall Hall," and "The Things That Can Tickle You Room," reaches a door marked Everything Else, opens it — and steps back outside. A finite, arbitrarily-partitioned collection can't contain everything, and pretending it does is the joke. The claim: ML does this with a straight face.

// how it works

The argument

This is a position paper, so the "method" is a chain of reasoning rather than an experiment. It builds in three moves: a history of how benchmarking got here, a definition of the standard it's failing, and two case studies that fail it.

1 · The Common Task Framework, and how it drifted

Modern benchmarking descends from the Common Task Framework (CTF), championed in the mid-1980s by Fred Jelinek at IBM and Charles Wayne at DARPA for speech recognition and machine translation. Its three ingredients: (a) a public training set of labeled examples, (b) a set of competitors who must infer a prediction rule, and (c) a scoring referee. Crucially, the CTF was invented for tightly-scoped, practically-oriented tasks — ASR, MT — where validity means the benchmark reflects the real-world job asked of the system. The authors argue the field has stretched this machinery to cover "performance in the abstract," divorced from any application, which "completely subverts the intention of its introduction."

2 · Construct validity as the missing standard

Borrowing from measurement theory (O'Leary-Kelly & Vokurka, 1998), they frame the question as: how well does the benchmark dataset, and its metric, represent the task it claims to stand for? Reliability is about repeatability; validity is about hitting the right target at all. A benchmark can be perfectly reproducible and still measure the wrong construct.

⟨ dataset + metric ⟩  — represents? —▶  the claimed task / ability // construct validity = the strength of that arrow. For "general" benchmarks, the authors argue it is structurally weak.

3 · Two case studies

They scope the analysis to dataset-based benchmarks pitched at general capability, and pick the two canonical ones. ImageNet was described by its creators as "the most comprehensive and diverse coverage of the image world," and retrospectively as an "attempt to map the entire world of objects" — large-scale recognition as the field's "north star." GLUE / SuperGLUE are presented as "evaluation framework[s] for research towards general-purpose language understanding technologies." In both, the authors show the gap between the generality claimed and the specificity of what was actually built — then organize the failure into three buckets: limited task design, de-contextualized data, and inappropriate community use.

// what they found

Three ways the "general" benchmark breaks

Limited task design — tasks of convenience, not theory

If these benchmarks really probed general ability, you'd expect their subtasks to be chosen systematically, against a theory of the capability being measured. Instead they look like "samples of convenience." GLUE's tasks were curated from ~30 proposals gathered in an informal survey of NLP colleagues, filtered by practical heuristics ("licensing issues, complex formats, and insufficient headroom") down to the final eight or nine — "a collection of tasks authors from the NLP community perceived as interesting problems at the time." ImageNet's labels were lifted near-whole-cloth from 12 WordNet subtrees (mammal, bird, fish, reptile, amphibian, vehicle, furniture, musical instrument, geological formation, tool, flower, fruit) — an ontology built in another field for another purpose, which later turned out to carry derogatory and offensive categories in its "person" subtree. Worse, GLUE quietly fuses two different things — linguistic competence and open-ended world knowledge / commonsense reasoning — as if they were "equivalently scoped problems," and then calls the result comparable to "the human ability to understand language."

De-contextualized data — no dataset is neutral

"General" framing acts as cover for not reporting a dataset's limits. ImageNet is geographically lopsided: in the 2011 version, about 45% of images were sourced from the US and over 60% from a handful of Western countries in North America and Europe, while only 1% came from China and 1.2% from India — the two most populous countries on the planet. Run the same pipeline on Hindi-language image queries (de Vries et al.) and you get a drastically different dataset, even for everyday concepts like "wedding" or "spice." On the language side, GLUE/SuperGLUE test one specific language — English — not "language" in the abstract, with all the annotation artifacts and embedded viewpoints that come with any single dataset. The supposed diversity is thin: GLUE is two single-sentence tasks, two similarity/paraphrase tasks, and four inference tasks; SuperGLUE is four QA tasks, two inference tasks, one word-sense-disambiguation task, and one coreference task.

Inappropriate community use — the leaderboard becomes the field

Chasing SOTA is, they argue, "a very peculiar way of doing science" — empirical and incremental rather than hypothesis-driven. Kiri Wagstaff's critique is the sharp edge: "80% accuracy on Iris classification might be sufficient for the botany world, but to classify as poisonous or edible a mushroom you intend to ingest, perhaps 99% (or higher) accuracy is required." Averaging x% across datasets "tells us nothing at all useful about generalization or impact," because the same number means different things in different places. Benchmarks also steer the whole field's methods — chess pushed AI toward minimax tree search; ImageNet made the deep-learning era possible; BERT was first proven on GLUE. And the framing licenses commercial overreach: Microsoft's "milestone toward general AI" copy, and Baidu literally cheating on the ImageNet benchmark to inflate marketing claims. Poor performance on minority subgroups can hide inside an aggregate "general" score — the exact failure Gender Shades exposed in deployed face systems.

What to do instead

The fix is not to add more rooms to the museum. Two paths: present benchmarks honestly as tests of concrete, well-scoped, contextualized tasks; and when you genuinely want to probe broad capability, reach for evaluation methods that aren't a single leaderboard number — test suites, audits, and adversarial testing (map the problem space, e.g. CheckList, Gender Shades); system-output analysis (error, disaggregated, and counterfactual analysis); ablation testing; and analysis of model properties orthogonal to outputs (energy, memory, stability under perturbation). The point of these, pointedly, "isn't to show which systems can 'solve' them but to understand which aspects of the problem space remain challenging."

// does it hold up?

The eval read

As a critique of evaluation, this paper has to be judged as an argument, not an experiment — and as an argument it's well-aimed and durable. Its central move (separating reliability from validity, and naming the construct-validity gap) gave the field a vocabulary it has used ever since; the geodiversity numbers and the GLUE-curation history are concrete and verifiable, not vibes. But a few things are worth flagging if you're tempted to treat it as the last word.

First, it argues entirely by two hand-picked exemplars — ImageNet and GLUE — chosen precisely because they pitch generality hardest. That's fair for an existence proof ("here is a structural problem"), but it's not a survey, and it can't tell you how prevalent or severe the problem is across the benchmark ecosystem, nor weigh the cases where a "general" benchmark did drive transferable progress. Second, the proposed alternatives (test suites, audits, disaggregated analysis, ablations) are sketched in an appendix and asserted to be better, but they're not themselves validated here — and several have their own construct-validity problems (who decides which test items, which slices, which perturbations?). The honest reading is that they trade one hard measurement problem for a more transparent set of them, which is progress, but not a solved evaluation. Third, the paper is light on the genuine upside of a shared general benchmark: comparability and coordination. "Convenience sample" and "Schelling point that let a thousand labs compare results" can both be true; the paper leans hard on the former.

None of this is fatal — a position paper's job is to reframe, and this one does. But "general benchmarks have a construct-validity problem" is the start of a research program (how to measure it, how much it costs you, what replaces it), and four years on, much of that program is still open.

evidence base

An argument from history + measurement theory + two case studies, leaning on secondary sources (Shankar et al. for geodiversity, Wagstaff for metric critique). No new experiment — appropriate for the genre, but it means severity/prevalence are asserted, not quantified.

case selection

Only ImageNet and GLUE/SuperGLUE — the strongest "general" claimants. Great for an existence proof of the flaw; silent on counterexamples and on benchmarks that did yield transferable gains.

the alternatives

Test suites, audits, output analysis, ablations, property analysis — all plausible and underused, but unvalidated in-paper and carrying their own (more transparent) validity choices.

what would raise confidence

A systematic audit across many benchmarks scoring construct validity; a worked example showing the alternatives catching a failure a leaderboard missed; and an honest accounting of what's lost (comparability) when you drop the single number.

// bottom line

The diagnosis is right and has aged well: a finite dataset can't measure an open-ended "general" ability, and calling it general mostly serves to hide what the dataset leaves out. The prescription — scope your benchmark honestly, and use richer methods when you actually want to probe breadth — is the harder, still-unfinished part.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. Construct validity is the question to ask of any benchmark. Not "is it reproducible?" but "does this dataset + metric actually measure the ability you're claiming?" For benchmarks sold as "general," the answer is structurally no.
  2. "General" is doing rhetorical work. ImageNet is ~45% US images and ~1% China/India; GLUE is eight-ish English tasks chosen by informal survey. The generality is asserted, and the asserting conveniently excuses not reporting the limits.
  3. Leaderboards reshape the field, and the number hides things. Aggregate SOTA can bury subgroup failures (Gender Shades) and gets recycled into "milestone toward general AI" marketing — and even cheating (Baidu on ImageNet).
  4. The cure isn't a bigger museum. Scope benchmarks to concrete tasks; for broad capability use test suites, audits, disaggregated/counterfactual analysis, and ablations. Benchmark to survey the landscape, not to win a race.
