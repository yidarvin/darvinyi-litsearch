Fantastically Ordered Prompts and Where to Find Them: Overcoming Few-Shot Prompt Order Sensitivity

The same four few-shot training examples, merely reordered, swing GPT-3's accuracy from near-chance to near-state-of-the-art. This paper maps that fragility across eight model sizes and fixes it without a single labeled validation example — by sampling the language model's own generations to build a synthetic probing set, then ranking candidate orderings by the entropy of their predictions on it.

Lu, Bartolo, Moore, Riedel, Stenetorp

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

Brown et al.'s GPT-3 had just shown that a handful of in-context examples can substitute for gradient-based fine-tuning — but every reported number assumed the practitioner's one chosen prompt was representative of what the model could do. This paper isolates a variable nobody had studied in detail: holding the training-example selection and the prompt template completely fixed, and varying only the order in which the same few examples are concatenated, how much does accuracy move? Composing a prompt already has two other known-sensitive knobs — which examples you pick, and how you linearize them into text — but ordering had been treated as a free, structurally inert choice.

The answer, worked out across four sizes of GPT-2 (0.1B–1.5B) and two sizes of GPT-3 (2.7B, 175B) on eleven established text-classification datasets, is that order alone can be decisive. GPT2-XL (1.5B) can exceed 90% four-shot accuracy on SST-2 with the right permutation of just four examples — comparable to a fully-supervised model trained on more than 60,000 labeled samples — but the identical model, given the identical four examples in a different order, falls back to essentially random performance. The paper's sharpest single number: one specific permutation's accuracy drops from 88.7% to 51.6% purely from swapping the underlying model from GPT2-XL (1.5B) to GPT2-Large (0.8B) — the same ordering that is "fantastic" for one model is not even guaranteed to transfer to a slightly smaller one, let alone a much larger one. Scale helps, but doesn't close the gap: variance visibly narrows with size on SST-2, yet "although increasing the model size somewhat addresses it, the problem is still present for some text classification tasks (Subj in Figure 1) for models with billions of parameters" — the paper's own conclusion from the box plots below.

There's no shortcut through cross-model correlation either. Ranking all 24 possible orderings of one fixed 4-example set by accuracy, then computing the pairwise Spearman correlation between every pair of model sizes, gives numbers close to zero throughout — GPT-3 175B and 2.7B correlate at just ρ = 0.05. A permutation hand-picked for a small model tells you almost nothing about whether it will help a larger one, and there's no way to search offline for one universally "good" ordering. Since standard practice was to pick a single prompt, run it once, and report the resulting number, a published few-shot score could just as easily be reporting an unlucky draw as a genuine capability.

// how it works

The method

A fully-supervised setting could just use a held-out development set to pick the best-scoring ordering among candidates. But in the true few-shot regime — where, following Perez et al. (2021), a large labeled validation set is exactly what's unavailable — using one to select prompts quietly reintroduces the supervision the whole paradigm is supposed to avoid. The paper's fix repurposes the one resource a language model always has: its own ability to generate text. For a randomly-drawn set of n training examples (n = 4 in most experiments, giving 24 possible orderings), every permutation is used as a context to sample new continuations directly from the model itself — at a temperature of 2, with blocked n-gram repetition to encourage diversity, capped at 128 generated tokens. Each generation is then inverse-parsed by the same template into a synthetic (sentence, label) pair — but only the sentence half is trusted; the generated label is discarded outright, since nothing guarantees it's correct. Pooling the kept sentences across every one of the n! orderings yields an unlabeled artificial validation set, the "probing set."

With a probing set in hand — no ground-truth labels anywhere in the pipeline — each candidate ordering cm is scored by how well-behaved its predictions look on that synthetic data, using two entropy-based statistics. Global Entropy (GlobalE) aggregates the model's predicted label across the whole probing set into a single distribution pvm per class v, then takes its Shannon entropy — a high score means the ordering isn't collapsing every probing input into one majority label. Local Entropy (LocalE) instead averages the per-example predictive entropy across the probing set — a high score means the model isn't uniformly, indiscriminately confident on arbitrary inputs, which the paper treats as a symptom of poor calibration rather than genuine task competence. Both scores are computed for all 24 orderings, and the top k = 4 highest-entropy orderings by either metric are kept as the "performant" prompts actually used at test time — the model's raw, uncalibrated predictions under those orderings, nothing else changed.

// what they found

The results

Across eleven text-classification datasets and four orders of magnitude of model scale (0.1B to 175B parameters), selecting the top-4 orderings by GlobalE or LocalE delivers an average 13% relative improvement over the 24-permutation random baseline, and up to 30% relative improvement on the models/tasks that suffer the most variance to begin with — with no cases of meaningfully negative impact. On GPT-2 0.8B / SST-2, for instance, the random-ordering baseline is 74.5±10.3% accuracy; GlobalE-selected orderings reach 84.8±4.1% — both a higher mean and roughly half the standard deviation. An "Oracle" upper bound, which is allowed to pick the best 4 of the 24 orderings using real validation labels (something the true few-shot setting disallows), still tops both at 88.9±1.8% on the same cell, showing there's headroom the entropy statistics don't fully close. The method also holds at the largest scale tested: prompt selection using GlobalE improves GPT-3 175B's accuracy on CB by 4.9 points, in a setting the paper explicitly notes was budget-constrained — only 2 random example sets with 12 permutations each, rather than 5 sets of 24, "due to a limited computation budget" on the paid OpenAI API.

Two further checks support the approach. First, entropy-based ranking is robust to the K in top-K selection: sweeping K from 1 to 24 gives a negative-sloped accuracy curve for every one of the eleven datasets (Figure 8), meaning the ranking itself is doing real work rather than picking noise; the authors settle on K = 4 because it was the most stable choice across datasets in their preliminary runs, even though K = 1 sometimes does as well. Second, against a natural labeled alternative — splitting the same 4-shot training set in half and picking the top orderings by validation performance on the held-out half — entropy-based probing still wins across every GPT-2 size tested on SST-2 (e.g. GPT-2 0.8B: 75.1±6.8% for the split-training-set baseline vs. 84.8±4.1% for GlobalE), despite using strictly less information (no labels at all, versus labels on half the training set).

// does it hold up?

The eval read

This graph already has zhao-2021-calibrate (published two months earlier) diagnosing essentially the same instability — GPT-3's few-shot accuracy swinging wildly on prompt configuration — and fixing it with contextual calibration: a single content-free forward pass that estimates and removes a static output-distribution bias. This paper's fix is a genuinely different lever, not a restatement of Zhao's: instead of correcting the model's output probabilities after the fact, it searches before inference over which of the n! candidate orderings to commit to, scored entirely by the entropy of the model's own generations on synthetic data — no assumption about *why* an ordering is good, no fitted correction applied to the logits, and (unlike calibration) no claim about the mechanism at all. The paper is explicit about this agnosticism: "we find no common denominator between performant sample orders," and a good ordering for one model size doesn't transfer to another (ρ = 0.05, GPT-3 175B vs. 2.7B) — calibration's fitted bias-correction is at least interpretable and portable across test inputs under a fixed prompt; entropy-selected ordering is neither.

The two methods don't stack the way you'd hope, and this paper shows it in its own results. They run Zhao's contextual calibration directly on their own order-sensitivity setup (2-shot SST-2, GPT2-XL 1.5B, all 24 permutations) and report the outcome plainly: "although calibration leads to much higher performance, the variance remains high." Concretely, uncalibrated accuracy across the 24 orderings spans roughly 51.6–85.2%; calibration shifts the whole distribution upward, but a real spread survives in their own Figure 6. That is a rare, valuable data point in this literature — one contemporaneous eval paper empirically testing a rival contemporaneous fix on the exact same axis, in the paper itself, rather than in a follow-up years later. It means calibration and ordering are correcting related but distinct failure modes: calibration re-centers a static, order-independent bias in the softmax; entropy-based selection instead avoids orderings whose synthetic-data behavior looks pathological, but leaves the model's raw (uncalibrated) output untouched. Neither paper tests the natural next experiment — calibrate the output of an already entropy-selected ordering — so whether the two compose to close the remaining gap is still open.

Compute cost is asymmetric, and this paper doesn't cost it out. Zhao's calibration is one extra forward pass per prompt configuration, amortized over every subsequent test input — essentially free. Entropy-based ordering is combinatorial by construction: scoring requires generating a probing set from the model at temperature 2 for every one of the n! candidate orderings (24 sampling passes at n = 4, each up to 128 tokens), then re-scoring the pooled probing set under each candidate ordering to compute GlobalE/LocalE — a cost that the paper itself flags doesn't scale past small n ("there can be up to 64! possible orders" for larger example counts) and that forced them to cut GPT-3 175B experiments down to 2 example-sets of 12 permutations rather than the usual 5 sets of 24, "due to a limited computation budget" against the paid API. The 13% average relative gain is real, but it buys it with meaningfully more inference-time compute than calibration's near-free fix, and the paper never states that tradeoff in FLOPs or dollars.

baselines

Same-model, same-example-set A/B across all 24 orderings — clean and consistent throughout Table 2. The one asymmetry worth flagging: the Oracle upper bound is computed using real validation labels the true few-shot setting explicitly forbids, and it sits meaningfully above both LocalE and GlobalE in nearly every cell (e.g. GPT-2 0.8B SST-2: 84.8 GlobalE vs. 88.9 Oracle) — real headroom the entropy statistics leave on the table.

contamination

Not addressed. SST-2, TREC, AGNews, DBPedia, CB, and RTE are the same widely-circulated academic datasets Zhao et al. and Brown et al. use, and no contamination check is run. It matters less for the paper's internal ordering comparisons (every arm sees the same possible contamination), but the absolute accuracy numbers — especially GPT2-XL's "comparable to 60,000-sample supervised training" claim — shouldn't be read as clean generalization.

what's missed

Only classification tasks with short, single-token-scorable labels (eleven datasets, mostly sentiment/topic/entailment); no generation tasks, no instruction-tuned or chat models (the paper predates them); and the combinatorial method is demonstrated only at small shot counts (mainly 4-shot) — its cost at the 16–32+ shot regime common in modern many-shot in-context learning is untested. The paper's own related-work section also flags a direct challenge to its premise: Liu et al. (2021) found that once training examples are chosen by nearest-neighbor retrieval rather than at random, "the order in which they are provided in the prompt has little to no effect on performance" — this paper's whole phenomenon may be an artifact of naive random example selection rather than an unavoidable property of in-context learning.

would raise confidence

Test entropy-based ordering jointly with contextual calibration to see whether the combination actually closes the spread their own Figure 6 leaves open; report the wall-clock/API-cost overhead of the probing pipeline against calibration's near-free fix; and re-run the core comparison with retrieval-selected (not randomly-selected) examples, to check whether order sensitivity survives outside the worst-case random-selection setting the paper studies.

// bottom line

A careful, well-quantified diagnosis that prompt order alone — nothing else changed — can be as decisive as which examples you pick, paired with a fix that is more genuinely zero-label than Zhao et al.'s contextual calibration and orthogonal to it in mechanism. But the paper's own Figure 6 shows the two fixes don't simply stack, its combinatorial search cost is never priced against calibration's near-free forward pass, and its central premise — that random example selection is representative of practice — is directly challenged by the retrieval-based results the paper cites from Liu et al. Read it as the sharpest early evidence that "prompt order" and "output-distribution bias" are related but distinct failure modes, not as a closed case on either.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. Holding the training examples and template fixed and varying only their order can swing GPT-family few-shot accuracy from near-chance to matching a model trained on 60,000+ labeled examples — and the effect barely shrinks with scale on some tasks (Subj at 175B) and doesn't transfer across model sizes (a specific ordering's accuracy fell from 88.7% to 51.6% just from changing GPT2-XL to GPT2-Large; cross-size Spearman correlation of orderings is as low as ρ = 0.05).
  2. The fix needs zero labeled data: sample synthetic probing examples directly from the model itself under each candidate ordering, then rank orderings by the entropy of their own predictions on that synthetic set (GlobalE / LocalE) — the top-4 orderings by this metric buy an average 13% (up to 30%) relative accuracy improvement across eleven datasets and four orders of magnitude of model scale.
  3. This is a genuinely different lever than Zhao et al.'s contextual calibration (two months earlier, same underlying instability) — and the paper's own Figure 6 shows the two don't simply stack: calibrating across 24 orderings on GPT2-XL still leaves real variance. Order-sensitivity and output-distribution bias are related failure modes, not the same one, and neither fix alone (nor the untested combination of both) has been shown to close the gap.
