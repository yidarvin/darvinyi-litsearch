Robots at Work

Before Acemoglu and Restrepo traced robots' local fallout across US commuting zones — and named this paper their own closest precursor — Graetz and Michaels asked the same question at the country-industry level: using International Federation of Robotics panel data spanning 17 countries and 14 industries from 1993–2007, and a novel instrument built from each industry's 1980 occupational exposure to robot-replaceable tasks, they estimate that robot densification raised annual labor productivity growth by about 0.36 percentage points, left aggregate hours worked statistically unchanged, but cut low-skilled workers' share of hours relative to their middle- and high-skilled peers.

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

By the mid-2010s, "what will robots do to jobs" was a question answered almost entirely by theory and anecdote. A growing macro literature on secular stagnation and the falling labor share (Karabarbounis & Neiman, Elsby, Hobijn & Sahin) worried out loud about automation; popular accounts (Brynjolfsson & McAfee's The Second Machine Age, Ford's The Lights in the Tunnel) argued robots would displace workers en masse; and a UK survey found 30% of respondents believed a robot could take their job within 20 years. But almost none of this rested on directly observed robot adoption. The empirical literature on automation that did exist studied conventional ICT — computers, software, communications equipment — not the specific, autonomous, reprogrammable machines the IFR calls industrial robots, and at the level of firms or industries within a single country (Bartel, Ichniowski & Shaw; Doms, Dunne & Troske), not across the developed world.

What was missing was a data source that measured actual robot deployment, comparably, across many countries and industries, paired with a strategy for ruling out the obvious alternative story — that industries adopt robots because they are already becoming more productive for unrelated reasons, not the other way around. The International Federation of Robotics' annual survey of robot suppliers offered exactly the first ingredient: a "Gold Standard" definition (an automatically controlled, reprogrammable, multipurpose manipulator programmable in three or more axes) that rules out single-purpose equipment and yields comparable delivery counts back to 1993. What the paper adds is the second ingredient — two new instruments for robot adoption built from industries' pre-existing occupational structure, rather than from another country's contemporaneous adoption trend (the strategy Acemoglu and Restrepo would later apply to US commuting zones, citing this paper's use of IFR data as their point of departure).

// how it works

The method

The paper opens with a small task-based model of firms' technology choice: within an industry, robots and workers can perform a shared set of tasks, but robots can only be used on the subset — share α(i) — that industry i's production allows. Firms that adopt robots at all pay a fixed cost φ, then hire robots and workers up to a cost-minimizing ratio set by robots' rental rate ρ:

R(i)L(i) = α(i)1 − α(i) · ρ−σ // cost-minimizing robots-to-labor ratio: rises with an industry's replaceable-task share α(i), and as robots get cheaper (ρ↓), amplified by the task substitution elasticity σ

A falling robot rental rate ρ therefore raises labor productivity in robot-using industries, lowers their relative output price, and — whether it raises or lowers relative employment depends on whether the elasticity of substitution between industries (ε) exceeds the elasticity within an industry's task bundle (σ). The model's punchline for the empirics: industries with a higher replaceable-task share α(i) should adopt robots earlier and more intensively as robot prices fall, which motivates instrumenting robot adoption with that pre-existing task structure rather than with realized adoption itself.

The core data merge IFR robot-delivery counts with EU-KLEMS industry accounts (value added, hours, capital stocks, TFP) across 14 industries in 17 countries, 1993–2007 — the US, fourteen European countries, South Korea, and Australia — yielding robot density (the stock of robots per million hours worked), which rose from a 17-country average of 0.58 in 1993 to 1.48 by 2007, concentrated in transport equipment, chemicals, and metal industries, and highest in Germany, Sweden, Belgium, and Italy.

To address reverse causality — industries might adopt robots because they are already becoming more productive — the paper instruments robot densification two new ways. The first, "replaceable hours," takes the IFR's own taxonomy of tasks robots could perform by 2012 and maps it back onto 1980 US occupations, then computes the share of each industry's 1980 hours performed by occupations that would later become replaceable — a technology-supply-side measure fixed decades before robots became common. The second, "reaching & handling," measures how much of an industry's 1980 physical-task hours involved reaching and handling (the specific manipulation that robotic arms are built for) relative to other physical tasks. Both instruments are interacted implicitly with the economy-wide decline in robot prices, in the spirit of a Bartik shift-share design; because the underlying industry characteristic dates to 1980, well before robots diffused, it is meant to be orthogonal to the industry-specific shocks of 1993–2007. The preferred functional form uses the percentile of the change in robot density rather than the raw change, since raw changes are heavily right-skewed — the mean change in the top quartile of adopters was over ten times that of the third quartile.

// what they found

The results

The headline: country-industry pairs that densified robots faster from 1993–2007 saw significantly larger gains in value added per hour worked. The baseline OLS coefficient is 0.36 (s.e. 0.23), rising to 0.66 (0.24) once controls and country/industry trends are added; the 2SLS estimates instrumented with "replaceable hours" run 0.88–1.05 and with "reaching & handling" 0.69–1.02 (first-stage F-statistics of 16–42, comfortably above weak-instrument thresholds), with both instruments jointly giving nearly identical estimates to either alone. The 2SLS estimates sit 2.5–3× above OLS — the authors read this as classical measurement error in robot density attenuating the OLS coefficient toward zero, rather than reverse causality (which would push OLS up, not down).

Translated into an aggregate: taking the conservative OLS estimate (β=0.35, country+industry trends), the paper calculates that in the absence of 1993–2007 robot densification, labor productivity in robot-using industries would have been about 16% lower; scaled by those industries' roughly one-third share of value added, economy-wide productivity would have been about 5.1% lower — equivalent to robots adding ≈0.36 percentage points a year to labor productivity growth against a 2.4% mean. For comparison, that is close to steam's estimated 0.35-point contribution to British productivity growth (Crafts 2004) — though sustained over a period four times as long — and below ICT's estimated 0.6–1.0-point contribution to EU/US productivity growth, 1995–2005 (O'Mahony & Timmer 2009).

Beyond productivity, robot densification also raised total factor productivity (OLS 0.26–0.47, 2SLS 0.62–0.79) and lowered output prices (OLS −0.38 to −0.51, 2SLS −0.40 to −0.72) — both consistent with the model's predictions. On labor, the preferred OLS/2SLS estimates show no significant effect on aggregate hours worked (small, imprecisely estimated coefficients, mostly negative in the 2SLS but statistically indistinguishable from zero). Splitting hours by skill tells a sharper story: robot densification raised the share of hours worked by high- and middle-skilled workers while cutting low-skilled workers' hours share by roughly 5.7 points (OLS) to 8.6–8.9 points (2SLS) across the observed range of robot-density change — a skill-biased pattern, but one that (unlike ICT) does not polarize the labor market against the middle. On wages, the preferred specification finds robots raised average hourly wages — 0.039 (0.017) OLS, 0.084 (0.021) 2SLS, both statistically significant. Twenty-four case studies of Universal Robots' customers add a firm-level cross-check: payback periods of 2–18 months, implying annual returns on the order of 25–200%.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

This is the paper Acemoglu and Restrepo would later call their own closest precursor, and it earns that status for being the first to instrument robot adoption at all — but its design differs from theirs in ways that matter for what it can and can't say, and on one key outcome it points the opposite direction.

First, the identification strategy leans on a forward-looking definition standing in for a purely exogenous shock. The "replaceable hours" instrument classifies 1980 US occupations as robot-replaceable using the IFR's own 2012 taxonomy of what robots could by then do — meaning the instrument is built by looking at which tasks robots actually ended up being good at, then projecting that back onto industries' pre-existing task mix. That's a reasonable way to get decades-old, plausibly pre-determined variation, but it is not the same as an instrument constructed purely from technological supply factors unrelated to the outcome; an industry's 1980 occupational structure could easily correlate with unobserved secular trends (general capital deepening, competition from imports, organizational modernization) that also raised productivity over 1993–2007, independent of robots specifically. The paper's controls for ICT/non-ICT capital changes and routine-ness/offshorability narrow this, but don't close it.

Second, the 2.5–3× gap between the 2SLS estimates (0.88–1.05) and the OLS estimates (0.36–0.66) is attributed entirely to classical measurement error attenuating OLS toward zero. That's plausible — robot density is almost certainly measured with noise — but it isn't the only story consistent with the data: instrument-compliant industries (those whose adoption is driven by 1980 task structure) could simply have a larger local average treatment effect than the average industry, which would produce the same pattern without any measurement error at all. The paper doesn't have a way to distinguish these two explanations.

Third, and the most striking point of contrast with Acemoglu & Restrepo directly: this country-industry panel has only ~238 observations (17 countries × 14 industries, unbalanced) against their 722 US commuting zones — a coarser, lower-powered design, but one spanning 17 countries rather than one. And on wages, the two papers disagree in sign: this paper's preferred 2SLS estimate is +0.084 (0.021) — robots raising average wages — while Acemoglu & Restrepo's commuting-zone 2SLS estimate is −0.77% per additional robot per thousand workers. These aren't necessarily contradictory — a national industry-level design nets out worker reallocation across regions and sectors that a local commuting-zone design would pick up as a sharp local decline even while the national average rises — but neither paper's data can test that reconciliation, since neither observes both the geographic and the country-level margin at once. A reader relying on either paper alone would come away with the opposite belief about what robots do to pay.

baselines

OLS and 2SLS agree on sign and both are precisely estimated, but the 2SLS-over-OLS gap (2.5–3×) is explained solely by measurement-error attenuation, without ruling out that instrument-compliant industries have a genuinely larger treatment effect (a LATE-vs-ATE story the paper can't distinguish from its own data).

contamination

Not leakage in the ML sense, but a close analogue: the "replaceable hours" instrument is built using the IFR's 2012 definition of what robots turned out to be able to do, applied backward to 1980 occupations — closer to using a late snapshot of the outcome's own technology to construct a "pre-determined" regressor than a clean, independent supply-side shock.

what's missed

Where displaced low-skilled hours go — the paper shows their industry-level share falls while aggregate hours stay flat, but never traces the reallocation into other industries, regions, or non-employment; and the sample stops at 2007, before the 2008 crisis and the far larger 2010s robot buildout led by China.

would raise confidence

A version of this same country-industry design with sub-national (not just national) resolution, to test directly whether "wages rise nationally" and "wages fall in the most-exposed commuting zones" (Acemoglu & Restrepo) can both be true at once — plus an instrument that doesn't require a 2012 robot taxonomy to define 1980 "replaceability."

// bottom line

This is the paper that opened the empirical study of industrial robots' economic effects, and its 0.36-point productivity contribution has held up as a credible, if conservative, benchmark. But its country-industry panel trades geographic resolution for international breadth, its instrument leans on a forward-defined notion of "replaceability," and its finding that robots raise average wages sits in direct tension with the commuting-zone finding — that robots cut wages — from the very paper that calls this one its closest precursor. Both are probably capturing something real; neither data set can show how the two fit together.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. Using IFR robot-delivery data across 17 countries and 14 industries (1993–2007), instrumented via industries' 1980 occupational exposure to robot-replaceable tasks, robot densification is estimated to have raised annual labor productivity growth by about 0.36 percentage points (against 2.4% mean growth) — a 2SLS estimate (0.88–1.05) that runs 2.5–3× above the OLS estimate (0.36–0.66), a gap the authors attribute to measurement error rather than reverse causality.
  2. Robots left aggregate hours worked statistically unchanged, but cut low-skilled workers' share of hours by roughly 5.7 (OLS) to 8.6–8.9 (2SLS) percentage points relative to middle- and high-skilled workers — and, unlike the commuting-zone estimate in Acemoglu & Restrepo's paper (which calls this one its closest precursor), this country-industry design finds robots raised average wages rather than cutting them.
  3. Robots also raised total factor productivity and lowered output prices, consistent with the paper's task-based adoption model; 24 real-world case studies of Universal Robots' customers cross-check the productivity story with 2–18 month payback periods, implying 25–200% annual returns.
