2018 · EMNLP · Benchmarks & Evals

XNLI: Evaluating Cross-lingual Sentence Representations

Extends MultiNLI's English development and test sentence pairs into 14 more languages by professional human translation — 112,500 annotated pairs across 15 languages, including low-resource Swahili and Urdu — then benchmarks four ways of getting an English-trained NLI system to work everywhere else: translate the training data, translate the test input, or align multilingual sentence encoders directly.

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

By 2018, natural language inference (NLI) had become the field's favorite sentence-understanding test bed, but only in English: SNLI and MultiNLI had grown crowdsourced training sets into the hundreds of thousands of examples, and both were being used to evaluate everything from attention architectures to reusable sentence encoders. Almost none of that machinery had been checked outside English. Building fresh, million-example NLI corpora for every language a production system might encounter is not realistic, so cross-lingual language understanding (XLU) — train primarily on one language, evaluate on others — is the only scalable path, yet there was no serious benchmark to tell whether it actually worked for a linguistically demanding task like inference. What existed was thin: the Reuters cross-lingual document-classification corpus operated at the document level (too coarse for sentence-pair reasoning), was badly class-imbalanced, and shipped no target-language dev set; Cer et al.'s SemEval semantic-textual-similarity set covered only four languages; and the closest prior attempt at cross-lingual textual entailment (Agić & Schluter, 2018) translated just 1,332 SNLI pairs into four languages — useful, but far too small and narrow to serve as a standard. Nothing covered typologically diverse, lower-resource languages like Swahili or Urdu at any scale. XNLI's gap, stated plainly: build a large, sentence-level, many-language, human-validated evaluation set for NLI specifically, then use it to find out which of the available strategies for cross-lingual transfer — machine-translate the data, or align multilingual sentence encoders directly — actually holds up.

// how it works

The method

Building the corpus meant solving a subtler problem first: MultiNLI's test portion is kept private, so XNLI couldn't just translate it. Instead the authors collected 7,500 new English NLI pairs — 750 from each of MultiNLI's ten genre sources (Face-to-Face, Telephone, Government, 9/11, Letters, Oxford University Press, Slate, Verbatim, plus Fiction drawn from the 1922 novel Captain Blood) — using the identical crowdsourcing pipeline as MultiNLI, including a validation pass where four extra annotators re-label every pair and a majority vote sets the gold label (a three-vote consensus was reached on 93% of the data). With that validated English base in hand, professional translators (via the One Hour Translation platform) translated the premises and hypotheses separately — so no cross-sentence context leaks into the hypothesis translation — into 14 target languages: French, Spanish, German, Greek, Bulgarian, Russian, Turkish, Arabic, Vietnamese, Thai, Chinese, Hindi, Swahili, and Urdu. Labels are simply copied over from the English source rather than re-annotated per language. That gives 7,500 × 15 = 112,500 annotated pairs. The deliberate choice to translate an already-labeled English test set, rather than have each language's crowdworkers write independent hypotheses, is the corpus's central design decision: it guarantees the label distribution and task difficulty stay matched across languages by construction, at the cost of every non-English example being English content wearing a translation.

With the corpus built, the paper compares four ways to get an English-trained NLI system working in the other 14 languages. Two rely on machine translation: TRANSLATE TRAIN machine-translates the English MultiNLI training data into each target language and trains a separate classifier per language; TRANSLATE TEST instead keeps one English classifier and machine-translates the test input into English at inference time. The other two avoid translation entirely by aligning multilingual sentence encoders: an English encoder and classifier are learned first on MultiNLI (the classifier consumes the standard [u, v, |u−v|, u∗v] feature vector over premise/hypothesis embeddings u, v, feed-forward with 128 hidden units and 0.1 dropout), then each target-language encoder is trained to mimic the fixed English encoder's output on parallel sentence pairs — after which the untouched English classifier is applied directly to the target-language embeddings (Figure 1). X-BiLSTM uses a 512-unit bidirectional LSTM trained in-domain on MultiNLI (compared in both last-hidden-state and max-pooling variants); X-CBOW instead uses pretrained multilingual continuous-bag-of-words embeddings with no NLI fine-tuning, testing transfer rather than in-domain alignment.

Lalign(x, y) = ‖x − y‖2 − λ · ( ‖xc − y‖2 + ‖x − yc‖2 ) // λ = 0.25 (best in their sweep) weights the contrastive term; x_c, y_c are randomly-sampled "negative" sentence embeddings that push aligned pairs apart from unrelated ones

The alignment loss needs parallel sentence data per language pair: United Nations corpus text for French/Spanish/Russian/Arabic/Chinese, Europarl for German/Greek/Bulgarian, OpenSubtitles2018 for Turkish/Vietnamese/Thai, and the IIT Bombay corpus for Hindi — each with 500K+ pairs, capped at 2M. The two low-resource languages get far less: Urdu draws on Bible/Quran transcriptions plus OpenSubtitles and LDC corpora for only 64K pairs, and Swahili scrapes just 42K pairs from Global Voices and a Quran transcription corpus — an order of magnitude less parallel data than the other twelve languages, which matters later.

// what they found

The results

The English ceiling for the shared classifier is 73.7% (BiLSTM-max) — every cross-lingual method is judged against that number. Across all 14 other languages, TRANSLATE TEST (machine-translate the test input to English, then use the English classifier) wins outright: with BiLSTM-max it scores 70.7% on Spanish, 70.4% on French and Bulgarian, down to 64.4% on Thai and 59.3% on Urdu — consistently a few points ahead of TRANSLATE TRAIN (68.8% Spanish, 68.3% French, down to 56.6% Urdu) for every single language. The paper's own multilingual sentence-encoder method, X-BiLSTM (trained in-domain on MultiNLI, then aligned to each target language via the Lalign loss), is competitive with TRANSLATE TRAIN — 68.9% Greek, 68.7% Spanish, 55.7% Swahili — but still trails TRANSLATE TEST by as much as 6 points on Swahili, despite needing no machine-translation system at inference time. X-CBOW, the transfer-learning baseline built on pretrained (not NLI-fine-tuned) multilingual bag-of-words embeddings, is weakest everywhere: 60.7% Spanish down to 50.4% Swahili. The paper notes that the roughly two-point gap between X-BiLSTM-max and X-BiLSTM-last pooling is maintained fairly consistently across all 14 target languages — evidence, the authors argue, that a stronger English encoder also transfers better. Translation quality tracks final accuracy closely too — three of the four languages with the best machine-translation BLEU scores (Table 3) also clear 70% XNLI accuracy, while Swahili and Urdu, with the least parallel training data (42K and 64K sentences, versus 500K+ for the rest), post the lowest scores under every method. Figure 2 shows why: X-BiLSTM's XNLI dev accuracy for French, Arabic and Urdu tracks its alignment loss closely as training progresses — except for Urdu, which visibly overfits partway through, consistent with its much smaller parallel corpus.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

XNLI's central methodological choice — translate an already-labeled English test set, rather than have speakers of each language write independent examples — is exactly the design pattern that a survey of multilingual benchmarks would later put on trial. The paper is unusually candid about the risk it's taking: its own related-work section cites Mohammad et al. (2016) finding that translation alters sentiment annotations, and Smith et al. (2016) showing that classifier degradation from English-to-Spanish translation is largely a cultural-difference artifact, then states outright, "one of the limitations of the XNLI corpus is that it does not capture these differences, since it was obtained by translation." That's a rare admission for a benchmark paper to write down in 2018. But the paper's own check on the claim is thin: it hand-inspects a single flipped example (an entailment relation becomes a contradiction in the Chinese translation of "upright" → "sitting upright"), then runs a re-annotation exercise on only two of the fifteen languages — English and French, the two closest and best-resourced — finding 85% and 83% recovery of the original gold label respectively. Nothing was verified for Swahili, Urdu, Thai, or any of the languages that actually post the largest accuracy drops in Table 4, which is precisely where translation artifacts (register shifts, missing idiom, or outright mistranslation under a smaller professional-translator pool) would be expected to bite hardest if they exist at all. The paper's own correlation between XNLI accuracy and machine-translation BLEU score is presented as evidence that models transfer imperfectly, but it's equally consistent with the test data itself quietly becoming a different, harder task once translated into a typologically distant, lower-resource language — the experimental design can't tell those two explanations apart. This is close to a direct preview of the diagnosis MultiLoKo (Hupkes & Bogoychev, already in this graph) would generalize seven years later: it names XNLI explicitly, alongside PAWS-X and XCOPA, as one of the "older multilingual NLU sets" whose translate-the-benchmark construction makes evaluation "multilingual on the surface, not in content" — because 100% of the non-English test data originated as an English sentence, no such benchmark can measure whether a model actually understands something a native speaker of that language would say, only whether it can process a translation of something an American crowdworker said.

baselines

All four systems (TRANSLATE TRAIN, TRANSLATE TEST, X-BiLSTM, X-CBOW) were the right comparison set for 2018's pre-Transformer toolbox, but every one is now obsolete: contemporary multilingual evaluation just runs a pretrained encoder (mBERT, XLM, XLM-R) zero-shot directly on the XNLI test sets, skipping the paper's translation-vs-alignment comparison entirely. The baselines section is now read for historical interest, not as a live comparison.

contamination

Too early for LLM-era decontamination concerns, but a real, unaddressed risk downstream: web-scraped pretraining corpora assembled years later can contain reposted or re-translated XNLI/MultiNLI sentences, silently contaminating "zero-shot" evaluations of later encoder and LLM models on this exact test set — a problem the 2018 paper had no way to anticipate or guard against.

what's missed

A model's accuracy on translated-Swahili XNLI says nothing about a genuinely Swahili idiom, register, or culturally specific reference with no English equivalent — the corpus can't test what it never contains, since every non-English example is definitionally a translation of an English sentence.

would raise confidence

A small, natively-authored companion test partition for at least the lowest-resource languages (Swahili, Urdu, Thai) would let future work directly measure the "translationese gap" the paper's own French/English spot check hints at but never quantifies for the languages where it matters most.

// bottom line

XNLI is the founding instance of the "translate the test set" playbook for multilingual NLU — cheap, label-preserving by construction, and honest enough to flag its own translation-fidelity risk in a single paragraph the field mostly moved past. Eight years and a large-language-model generation later, MultiLoKo's headline finding that translated benchmarks are "multilingual on the surface, not in content" reads less like a new discovery and more like a citation of the limitation XNLI's own authors wrote down in 2018 and never fully resolved.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. XNLI translated MultiNLI-style dev/test sentence pairs — not independently-written new ones — into 14 additional languages via professional human translation, producing 7,500 × 15 = 112,500 annotated NLI pairs; the choice to translate an already-labeled set (rather than crowdsource fresh hypotheses per language) was made specifically to keep label distributions and difficulty matched across languages, at the cost of every non-English example originating in English.
  2. TRANSLATE TEST (machine-translate the input to English, then apply the English classifier) beat every multilingual-encoder approach in every one of the 15 languages, including the paper's own novel sentence-alignment method (X-BiLSTM) — and the same translate-and-classify pattern remains a strong, if computationally heavier, baseline today.
  3. The paper's own related-work section already names, in 2018, the exact risk its benchmark successors would spend years re-discovering: translating a test set doesn't fully preserve a language's original difficulty or cultural nuance, and can quietly turn "multilingual evaluation" into "English evaluation wearing a translation" — the diagnosis MultiLoKo later generalizes across the whole translate-the-benchmark family.
