MathVista: Evaluating Mathematical Reasoning of Foundation Models in Visual Contexts

The first benchmark to ask whether a model can do math when the math lives in a picture — a function plot, a geometry diagram, an IQ-test puzzle. 6,141 problems, 31 source datasets, and a headline gap: GPT-4V's 49.9% leads every rival but still trails humans by 10.4 points.

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

By late 2023 the math-reasoning leaderboard was a text-only world. GSM8K, MATH, MathQA — the benchmarks everyone reported on — handed the model a string of words and asked for a number. The most capable models were already saturating the easy end: GSM8K, the authors note, had hit performance saturation. But a huge fraction of real mathematical reasoning is not textual at all. It lives in a bar chart an analyst is reading, in the diagram beside a geometry problem, in the function plot on an exam, in the puzzle figure on an IQ test.

The handful of vision-language datasets that touched math each covered only a thin slice. ChartQA was charts; Geometry3K and GeoQA were geometry diagrams; FigureQA and DVQA were synthetic plots. Each fixed one task or one visual context. And the big general-purpose VQA datasets — VQA, GQA, VizWiz — contain only a small sliver of questions that genuinely require arithmetic or quantitative reasoning, drowned in a sea of "what color is the bus." So nobody had asked the question systematically: can a foundation model look at an arbitrary mathematical image and reason about it?

That gap matters because the downstream applications are exactly the visual ones — an assistant fielding logical queries over statistical dashboards, a tutor working through a geometry figure, a research aide reading the tables and plots in a paper. MathVista is built to turn "math in visual contexts" into a single measurable axis: a taxonomy of 5 tasks × 7 reasoning types spread across a dozen kinds of visual context, so that progress (and failure) can be located precisely.

// how it works

The method

MathVista is built taxonomy-first. The authors define five tasks — figure QA (FQA), geometry problem solving (GPS), math word problems (MWP), textbook QA (TQA), and visual QA (VQA) — and seven reasoning types — algebraic, arithmetic, geometry, logical, numeric commonsense, scientific, and statistical. Every example is then tagged with rich metadata (task, reasoning type, grade level, visual context, answer type) so models can be profiled cell by cell rather than on one aggregate number.

The 6,141 examples come from three streams:

  • 9 existing MathQA datasets (geometry, tabular and abstract-scene word problems, college-curriculum textbook QA) → 2,666 examples, capped at ≤400 per source for balance.
  • 19 existing VQA datasets. The team reviewed 70+ VQA datasets, auto-filtered candidates that look mathematical (numeric answers or quantity words), then had three expert annotators majority-vote which truly require math reasoning — 4,949 candidates pruned down to 2,739.
  • 3 brand-new datasets, 736 examples (12.0% of the benchmark), made to fill gaps no source covered: IQTest (228 puzzle figures, logical/inductive reasoning), FunctionQA (400 function plots, algebraic reasoning), and PaperQA (107 academic figures/tables/charts drawn from papers posted to Hugging Face in August 2023).

Annotation was tight: among the 736 freshly curated questions, only 6 drew disagreement among three independent reviewers (a 99.2% consistency rate), and automatic reasoning-type labels matched expert labels on 94.1% of examples. The benchmark is split into a 1,000-example testmini for development and a 5,141-example test set whose answer labels are withheld behind an online evaluation server to deter contamination.

The quietly important contribution is the evaluation protocol. Modern LMMs answer in long, chatty prose, which breaks the template-matching graders older benchmarks used. MathVista replaces them with a three-stage pipeline: generate the response, then use an LLM (GPT-4) as an answer extractor to pull the short answer out of the prose, then normalize it (to an option letter or a number) and score exact-match accuracy. On a 200-example check the extractor was >99.5% accurate — making free-form generation deterministically gradable.

// what they found

The results

The team evaluated 12 foundation models — three text-only LLMs (ChatGPT, GPT-4, Claude-2), the same LLMs augmented with image captions and OCR, seven open-source LMMs, and two proprietary LMMs (Multimodal Bard and GPT-4V) — against random/frequent-guess baselines and a human baseline on testmini.

The headline: GPT-4V reaches 49.9% overall, the best of any model, 15.1 points above the second-best LMM, Multimodal Bard (34.8%) — but still 10.4 points below the human baseline of 60.3%. The supporting cast is just as telling:

  • Text matters less than vision — but not nothing. The best text-only model (2-shot CoT GPT-4, given the question but no image) scores 29.2%, beating frequent-guess (26.3%) by reasoning over the question wording alone. The low ceiling confirms the benchmark genuinely needs the picture.
  • "Perceive, then compute" gets you most of the way. Feeding GPT-4 an external caption + OCR text and letting it write Python (PoT) lifts it to 33.9% — essentially matching Multimodal Bard without any native vision.
  • Open-source LMMs basically can't do visual math. IDEFICS, mPLUG-Owl, miniGPT4, InstructBLIP, LLaVA and friends land between 19.8% and 26.1% — at or below the 26.3% frequent-guess line.

The fine-grained view is where MathVista earns its keep. GPT-4V doesn't just win on average — it surpasses the human baseline on geometry problem solving (50.5 vs 48.4), textbook QA (65.2 vs 63.2) and algebraic reasoning (53.0 vs 50.9), and on structured visual contexts like function plots, geometry diagrams, scatter plots and tables. Yet it collapses where humans coast: logical reasoning (21.6 vs 40.7) and numeric commonsense (20.1 vs 53.8) — the IQ-puzzle and everyday-number-sense problems. The failures are perceptual and reasoning-bound, not just compute-bound. The paper also documents emergent GPT-4V behaviours the benchmark surfaces: self-verification, gains from self-consistency, and goal-directed multi-turn dialogue.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

MathVista is a genuinely useful map of where multimodal math reasoning was hard in late 2023, and its LLM-as-extractor protocol was widely copied. But the single most-quoted number in the paper — GPT-4V at 49.9%, "within 10 points of human, superhuman on some tasks" — rests on a setup that deserves scrutiny.

First, the asymmetry in how GPT-4V was scored. Every other model ran through the deterministic generate→extract→score pipeline. GPT-4V, which had no API at the time, was evaluated by hand in the ChatGPT playground chatbot — no temperature control, no fixed system prompt, no reproducible model snapshot, and a human in the loop typing each prompt. The marquee result is the one number in the paper that can't be reproduced by re-running a script.

Second, contamination is structural. 28 of the 31 source datasets are years-old public benchmarks (VQA, GQA, ChartQA, Geometry3K, IconQA, TabMWP, AI2D…). Withholding the test answer labels stops leaderboard gaming, but it does nothing about the fact that the underlying images and questions were on the open web for years — and were plausibly scraped into GPT-4V's and Bard's web-scale pretraining. (That last step is an inference the paper doesn't test: it withholds test labels but never probes whether the closed models memorized public items.) Only the 736 genuinely new examples (12%) are clean by construction.

Third, the human baseline is weak, which is what manufactures the "superhuman" cells. The 60.3% comes from Amazon Mechanical Turk workers with a high-school diploma, answering five questions in twenty minutes — non-experts, time-boxed, on material that includes college curricula and scientific reasoning. That is a floor, not a ceiling. "GPT-4V beats humans on geometry" really reads "beats rushed crowdworkers," and the gaps that support it (e.g. algebraic reasoning 53.0 vs 50.9) are within a couple of points on a 1,000-example slice with no error bars.

baselines

12 models, but apples-to-oranges: every model except the headline GPT-4V ran the deterministic pipeline; GPT-4V was hand-scored in the playground. Open-source LMMs (19.8–26.1%) barely clear the 26.3% frequent-guess line.

contamination

28 of 31 sources are long-public datasets, plausibly scraped into web-scale pretraining (an inference the paper doesn't test); withholding test labels blocks gaming, not memorization of public images/questions. Only 12% (736 ex.) is newly created.

what's missed

All headline numbers live on testmini (1,000 ex.), so per-category cells are small and carry no confidence intervals; "GPT-4V > human" on algebra/geometry is within noise — over a time-boxed crowdworker ceiling, not an expert one.

would raise confidence

Score GPT-4V through the same automated pipeline on the full test set; report per-cell CIs; add a holdout/perturbation contamination probe; rebuild the human baseline with untimed domain experts.

// bottom line

MathVista made "math in visual contexts" a single, measurable axis and gave the field a taxonomy and an LLM-extraction protocol it reused for years. But the marquee result — GPT-4V at 49.9%, a hair under human and superhuman in spots — leans on a hand-scored chatbot run, a 1,000-example slice without error bars, and a crowdworker ceiling, atop datasets the models likely trained on. Read it as a directional map of late-2023 weaknesses, not a precise scoreboard — and remember newer models have since largely saturated it.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. MathVista reframed mathematical reasoning as inherently multimodal: 6,141 examples from 31 datasets, organized as 5 tasks × 7 reasoning types over a dozen visual contexts — the first systematic test of whether models can reason mathematically over figures, plots and diagrams.
  2. The late-2023 verdict: GPT-4V (49.9%) lapped every open-source LMM and beat Bard by 15.1 points, yet trailed humans by 10.4 — and cratered on logical/puzzle reasoning (21.6%) and numeric commonsense (20.1%), exposing perception-bound, not just compute-bound, failures. Error analysis pins ~half of wrong explanations on hallucinated visual facts.
  3. Its most durable contribution is methodological: the LLM-as-answer-extractor pipeline that turns chatty free-form generations into deterministic accuracy, plus a withheld-label test split and online server — a template later visual-reasoning benchmarks (MMMU, OlympiadBench) adopted.
