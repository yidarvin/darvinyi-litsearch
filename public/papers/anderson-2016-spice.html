SPICE: Semantic Propositional Image Caption Evaluation

A caption-evaluation metric that parses candidate and reference captions into scene graphs — objects, attributes, relations — and scores an F1 over matched semantic tuples, catching synonymous-but-lexically-different captions that n-gram metrics like BLEU, ROUGE and CIDEr miss.

Peter Anderson, Basura Fernando, Mark Johnson, Stephen Gould

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

By 2016 the standard way to score an automatic image captioning system was BLEU, ROUGE, METEOR or the then-year-old CIDEr — all of them fundamentally n-gram overlap measures, comparing a candidate caption against a handful of human reference captions by counting shared words or word sequences (CIDEr weights those n-grams by TF-IDF and sums their cosine similarity; that's exactly the surface-form matching this paper takes aim at). The authors open with two deliberately paired examples that expose why that's the wrong yardstick. First: "A young girl standing on top of a tennis court" versus "A giraffe standing on top of a green field" — two captions describing completely different images that nonetheless score highly similar under any n-gram metric, because both share the 5-gram "standing on top of a." Second, the mirror-image failure: "A shiny metal pot filled with some diced veggies" versus "The pan on the stove has chopped vegetables in it" — two captions of the same image that convey almost identical meaning but share no words at all, so n-gram metrics score them as dissimilar. N-gram overlap, in other words, is neither necessary nor sufficient for two sentences to mean the same thing. The paper's hypothesis is that what a human evaluator actually checks is a caption's semantic propositional content — is there a girl, is she young, is she standing, is there a court, is it tennis, is she on top of it — and that an automatic metric should score exactly those propositions rather than surface n-grams.

// how it works

The method

SPICE turns both the candidate caption c and the set of reference captions S into scene graphs — a representation already established in image-retrieval work and reused from Visual Genome — through a two-stage pipeline. Stage one runs a PCFG dependency parser (pre-trained, using Universal Dependency relations) over the raw caption to get its syntactic structure. Stage two maps that dependency tree onto a scene graph using a rule-based system: a modified version of the Stanford Scene Graph Parser with nine linguistic rules that extract lemmatized objects O(c), attributes K(c) ⊆ O(c) × A (e.g. the adjectival modifier "young" attaches to the object "girl"), and relations E(c) ⊆ O(c) × R × O(c) (e.g. "girl" —on-top-of→ "court"). SPICE departs from the original parser in two ways: it drops the duplication of object nodes for plural/numeric modifiers (numeric modifiers become object attributes instead, so a caption never gets penalized twice for one wrong count), and it adds a rule guaranteeing every noun appears as an object node even with no identifiable relations, so it's never silently dropped from scoring.

Given a scene graph G(c) = ⟨O(c), E(c), K(c)⟩, the function T(G(c)) := O(c) ∪ E(c) ∪ K(c) flattens it into a set of logical tuples — each with one, two, or three elements representing an object, an attribute, or a relation. For the "young girl standing on top of a tennis court" example, T yields tuples like (girl), (court), (girl, young), (girl, standing), (court, tennis), (girl, on-top-of, court). A binary matching operator ⊗ then finds tuples shared between the candidate's tuple set and the (unioned) reference tuple set — two tuples match if their lemmatized word forms are equal or, reusing METEOR's synonym-matching machinery, if they fall in the same WordNet synset. Precision, recall, and the SPICE score itself are then a plain F1 over matched tuples:

SPICE(c, S) = F1(c, S) = 2 · P(c,S) · R(c,S)P(c,S) + R(c,S) // P(c,S) = |T(G(c)) ⊗ T(G(S))| / |T(G(c))| · R(c,S) = |T(G(c)) ⊗ T(G(S))| / |T(G(S))|

Two design choices are worth noting. Unlike Smatch (the AMR-parsing metric SPICE otherwise resembles), there is no partial credit for a tuple that matches on one element but not another — the authors argue common relations like "in" or "on" applied to the wrong pair of objects arguably deserve zero credit, not half. And unlike CIDEr, SPICE uses no cross-dataset statistics (no corpus-level IDF weighting), so it needs no reference corpus to calibrate against and applies identically to small and large datasets. Because the score decomposes cleanly into object/attribute/relation subsets — and attributes further into arbitrary categories via word lists — SPICE can also answer diagnostic questions no n-gram metric can, like "which caption generator understands color?" or "can caption generators count?" // what they found

The results

The headline number: on the 2015 COCO Captioning Challenge (15 competition entries plus human captions, scored against human judgments collected via Amazon Mechanical Turk before any of this was released), SPICE reaches a system-level Pearson's ρ of 0.88 with overall human quality judgment (M1 — "percentage of captions evaluated as better or equal to human"), versus 0.43 for CIDEr and 0.53 for METEOR (BLEU-1 0.24, BLEU-4 0.05, ROUGE-L 0.15). SPICE leads on every one of the five human-judgment dimensions the challenge measured except M4 (caption detail), where it's still the only metric with a positive, significant correlation (0.46) — BLEU and ROUGE-L actually penalize more detailed captions (−0.54 and −0.63 for BLEU-1/4, −0.55 for ROUGE-L), while CIDEr and METEOR aren't statistically significant there. Most strikingly, SPICE is the only metric that ranks the human-written captions first among all 16 entries — CIDEr ranks them 7th, METEOR 4th (Figure 3, red dots). Ablating the WordNet synonym matching (SPICE-exact) only costs a little — 0.84 versus 0.88 on M1 — though the authors expect synonym matching to matter more when fewer reference captions are available.

At the finer-grained caption level the gap narrows a lot. Kendall's τ against graded human quality scores on Flickr 8K: SPICE 0.45, CIDEr 0.44, METEOR 0.42, BLEU-1/ROUGE-L 0.32, BLEU-4 0.14 — against an inter-human ceiling of 0.73. On the Composite dataset (Flickr8K + Flickr30K + COCO): SPICE 0.39 vs. CIDEr 0.36, METEOR 0.35. The authors are upfront that this is "only a modest improvement" per-caption; SPICE's real advantage shows up once judgments are aggregated across many captions, as at the system level. On PASCAL-50S accuracy (correctly picking the human-preferred caption in a pair), SPICE, METEOR and CIDEr are close overall (78.8 / 80.8 / 78.9 with 5 references), but SPICE is specifically best on model-vs-model (MM) pairs — 68.2% vs. CIDEr's 64.6% and METEOR's 66.8% — which the authors flag as the pair type that matters most, since telling two machine-generated captions apart is the actual point of using a metric during model development. METEOR wins on human-incorrect (98.1%) and human-model (94.2%) pairs; BLEU-1 wins when both captions are human-correct (64.9%).

Because SPICE's tuples subdivide cleanly, Table 2 breaks 2015-challenge entries down by object/relation/attribute F-score and further into color/count/size attribute subcategories. The best system (the MSR entry, which used trained visual detectors) actually exceeds the human F-score baseline on color (0.063 vs. 0.055) — but no system shows a convincing ability to count: the best non-random count F-score is 0.033, several systems score 0.000, against a 0.095 human baseline.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

The evaluation protocol itself is careful for its time: the methodology was fixed before touching the COCO test captions, correlations are computed against real human judgments collected independently by the COCO organizers (not the authors), and the same evaluation code and datasets (Flickr 8K, Composite, PASCAL-50S, 2015 COCO Challenge) used by prior metric papers are reused so the comparison is apples-to-apples. But the paper's own design choices open two structural cracks that it names but never closes. First, scoring runs entirely downstream of a rule-based dependency parser — a PCFG parser plus nine hand-written extraction rules borrowed (with tweaks) from the Stanford Scene Graph Parser. Every SPICE score is therefore only as reliable as that parser's output: a parse error silently becomes a scoring error, with no way for a reader of the number to tell the two apart. The authors are candid that "significant challenges still remain in semantic parsing" and even say they tried AMR-style parsing with the Smatch metric and found it "performed poorly" as a caption representation — yet the paper never measures how much of SPICE's residual disagreement with human judgment (0.88, not 1.0, at the system level) is attributable to parser error versus genuine semantic mismatch. They explicitly punt that separation to future work ("using human annotators to establish an upper bound... given perfect semantic parsing"), which means a captioning system trained against SPICE has no principled way to know whether a bad score reflects a bad caption or just a parser failure on an unusual sentence.

Second, the paper includes an unusually self-aware "Gameability" section (3.3) that names its own weak spot: SPICE "neglects fluency," scoring only propositional content, so a generator could in principle produce ungrammatical or disfluent text that still hits the right objects/attributes/relations and score well. The authors suggest pairing SPICE with a fluency metric like surprisal — but never build or test that combination, so the risk is stated, not measured. Nothing in the paper checks whether a model explicitly optimized against SPICE (e.g., via a scene-graph-shaped reward) degenerates into keyword-stuffed, non-fluent captions the way BLEU/CIDEr-optimized RL captioners were already known to.

baselines

Fair for 2016: BLEU-1/2/4, ROUGE-L, METEOR and CIDEr, run with the official MS COCO evaluation code across four independent human-judgment datasets (2015 COCO Challenge, Flickr 8K, Composite, PASCAL-50S). The comparison set is complete for its era — every metric in common captioning use at the time is included.

contamination

Low risk in the usual sense (it's a metric, not a trained model), but the system-level correlation is computed over only 16 entries (15 systems + human), a small n for Pearson's ρ — several p-values sit right at the edge of significance (e.g. M4's 0.070), and the COCO organizers' human judgments were collected on a single fixed 1,000-image subset, so the headline 0.88 rests on one snapshot of one challenge.

what's missed

SPICE can only reward propositions already present in the reference set — a genuinely correct but novel description not covered by any reference still scores zero, the same reference-coverage ceiling that limits CIDEr and BLEU, just moved from n-grams to semantic tuples. The color/count/size diagnostic breakdown (Table 2) is also built on hand-curated word lists rather than a validated ontology, so its "can models count?" verdict is only as good as that ad hoc taxonomy.

would raise confidence

An ablation isolating parser error from real semantic mismatch (the paper's own proposed "perfect parsing" upper bound); a larger, more diverse system pool for the Pearson correlation than 16 entries; an actual empirical test — not just a named risk — of whether optimizing a captioner directly against SPICE degrades fluency.

// bottom line

SPICE is a genuine methodological advance — it's the first captioning metric to move scoring from surface n-grams to a decomposable semantic representation, and the system-level correlation gap over CIDEr and METEOR (0.88 vs. 0.43/0.53) is large and well-supported. But its entire value proposition is conditioned on a rule-based parser it openly admits is imperfect, and on a fluency-blindness the authors flag but don't stress-test — both of which the field would go on to probe (by 2021, CLIPScore's comparison table still shows SPICE trailing a reference-free CLIP similarity on several of these same benchmarks).

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. SPICE reframes caption evaluation as matching sets of semantic tuples — objects, attributes, relations — extracted via dependency parsing and rule-based scene-graph construction, instead of matching n-grams. That directly targets the exact blind spot BLEU/ROUGE/METEOR/CIDEr share: two captions can convey the same meaning with zero shared words, or convey completely different meanings while sharing a long common phrase.
  2. It wins decisively at the system level — 0.88 Pearson's ρ with human judgment versus 0.43 for CIDEr and 0.53 for METEOR on the 2015 COCO Captioning Challenge, and it's the only metric that ranks human-written captions above every model. The caption-level edge is much smaller (Kendall's τ 0.45 vs. 0.44 for CIDEr, 0.42 for METEOR on Flickr 8K), and on PASCAL-50S it's roughly tied with CIDEr overall while specifically leading on model-vs-model pairs (68.2% vs. 64.6%) — the comparison that matters most for picking a better captioning system.
  3. Because scoring runs entirely through a rule-based dependency parser, SPICE inherits that parser's brittleness as a structural risk, and the paper's own "Gameability" section names a fluency-blindness that a caption generator could in principle exploit — neither risk is empirically measured here, both were left as open questions for the field (and CLIPScore's 2021 head-to-head shows SPICE still behind a reference-free CLIP similarity on several of the same benchmarks).
