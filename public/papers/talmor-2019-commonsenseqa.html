CommonsenseQA: A Question Answering Challenge Targeting Commonsense Knowledge

A five-way multiple-choice QA benchmark generated by grounding crowd-worker prompts in ConceptNet subgraphs, so that answering each question requires background knowledge that is never stated in the question itself.

Alon Talmor, Jonathan Herzig, Nicholas Lourie, Jonathan Berant
venue NAACL 2019 citations 2,558 topic Benchmarks & Evals source ↗

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

By late 2018, QA benchmarks had mostly converged on a "read a passage, find the answer" format — SQuAD, TriviaQA, MS MARCO, CNN/DailyMail. Those datasets test reading comprehension, but the answer sits explicitly in the given context, so the questions require very little of the world knowledge people actually draw on when they answer things. The datasets that did target commonsense more directly ran into a different problem: the Winograd Schema Challenge is a mainstay of commonsense evaluation but has only ~150 hand-authored examples, and COPA (Choice of Plausible Alternatives) has just 500 dev / 500 test questions — both too small to train or reliably benchmark modern neural models at scale. The paper's own related-work discussion also flags a sharper failure mode: SWAG, a larger crowdsourced commonsense dataset about what happens next after an event, looked hard when it shipped, but was solved almost immediately — BERT-style pretrained LMs reached near-human performance on it within months, because the task turned out to be learnable from surface/stylistic regularities in how crowd workers write plausible-vs-implausible continuations, not from genuine background knowledge.

So the design target is specific: a benchmark that (a) is large enough to fine-tune and evaluate neural models on, (b) is answerable by a person with no additional context, and (c) is constructed so that the correct answer can't be picked out by surface cues or annotation artifacts alone — the same failure mode that Gururangan et al. (2018) documented in NLI datasets and that made SWAG collapse.

// how it works

The method

CommonsenseQA is generated from ConceptNet 5.5 (Speer et al., 2017), a graph knowledge base of 32 million (concept, relation, concept) triplets such as (gambler, CapableOf, lose money). The generation pipeline has three stages:

1. Subgraph extraction. First the triplet pool is filtered down: relations that are too generic (e.g. RelatedTo) or already well studied in NLP (e.g. IsA) are dropped, leaving 22 relation types; concepts longer than four words or not in English are dropped; and pairs with too-low edit distance are dropped. That leaves 236,208 triplets. From these, the authors group triplets that share a source concept and relation into question sets — one source concept plus three target concepts, all connected to it by the same relation (e.g. river —AtLocation→ {waterfall, bridge, valley}), yielding roughly 130,000 triplets across 43,000 question sets.

2. Crowdsourced question authoring. For each question set, an Amazon Mechanical Turk worker sees the source concept and the three target concepts and writes three questions — one per target — such that only that target is a correct answer among the three. Workers are explicitly told not to use words strongly associated with the answer (e.g. not "open" when the answer is "door"), which is the mechanism meant to block the surface-cue shortcut that sank SWAG. Each worker then adds two more distractors per question: one pulled from ConceptNet (a semantically related but wrong concept) and one authored freehand — bringing every question to five candidate answers. A separate pool of workers verifies each question (2 verifiers per question, kept only if at least one answers correctly), which filters out 15% of formulated questions.

3. Optional context. For every question and each of its five candidate answers, the authors also issue a Google search query (question text + candidate answer) and keep the first 100 result snippets, giving 500 web snippets per question — used later only by the retrieval-augmented BIDAF++ baseline, not by the core task.

The result is 12,247 final questions kept out of 16,242 formulated, at a fully-loaded cost of $0.33 per question. Two train/dev/test splits (80/10/10) are released: a random split, and a question-concept split where source concepts never overlap across the three sets. The paper reports the random split is empirically harder — about five points lower across all trained models — because a memorizing model can no longer rely on having seen the same source concept (with a different correct answer) during training, so the random split is treated as the primary evaluation setting.

// what they found

The results

Human accuracy, measured from five non-expert annotators per question with majority vote, is 88.9%. The strongest baseline — BERT-LARGE fine-tuned on CommonsenseQA — reaches 55.9% on the random split (63.6% on the question-concept split), well below humans but far above the 20% random-guess floor for a five-way question. GPT (the original OpenAI GPT-1) is next at 45.5% / 55.5%. Untrained baselines (cosine similarity over GloVe/Numberbatch embeddings, a 1-billion-word LM scored two ways) all land in the low-to-mid 20s–30s, barely above chance. ESIM-family and bilinear/compare models trained on the dataset without large-scale pretraining top out around 30–40%. BIDAF++, the one baseline that uses the retrieved web snippets as context, scores only 32.0% / 38.4% — using extra text does not obviously help, "hinting that they do not carry a lot of useful information," in the authors' words.

The paper also runs a diagnostic "SANITY" condition: keep the questions but swap the four curated distractors for randomly-drawn ConceptNet distractors instead. Under SANITY, BERT-LARGE's accuracy jumps to 92.3% (random split) / 93.2% (question-concept split) — essentially solved. A category-level breakdown of BERT-LARGE's errors (Table 6, 100 sampled dev questions) shows the model does well when a surface lexical clue points to the answer (77.7% accuracy on that subset), and does much worse on questions requiring negation/antonym understanding (42.8%), factoid knowledge (38.4%), fine answer-granularity distinctions (35.4%), or a conjunction of conditions (23.8%). A learning-curve experiment training BERT-LARGE on subsets of the data extrapolates to roughly 75% dev accuracy at 100k training examples (8× the current dataset size) — still short of the 88.9% human ceiling.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

The most important number in this paper isn't 55.9% — it's the SANITY jump to 92.3%. Swapping the four hand-curated distractors for random ConceptNet neighbors turns a 56%-accuracy task into a 92%-accuracy task for the exact same model on the exact same questions. That is the authors honestly showing their own hand: a large share of what makes CommonsenseQA hard is the adversarial selection of distractors that share a relation with the answer, not some irreducible requirement for "commonsense reasoning" in the abstract. It's a legitimate design choice — hard distractors are what force a model to discriminate rather than guess — but it means the benchmark is measuring "can you tell apart ConceptNet-similar concepts" as much as it's measuring general world knowledge, and the two are not obviously the same skill.

The category breakdown in Table 6 is the paper's strongest piece of self-diagnosis, and it points the same direction: BERT-LARGE gets 77.7% right when a surface clue is present versus 23.8% on conjunction-style questions — a 54-point spread that suggests a meaningful chunk of the reported 55.9% comes from lexical/associative shortcuts already present in pretrained representations, the same class of shortcut the paper explicitly designed the task to avoid. Unlike SWAG, though — which used an adversarial-filtering procedure to discard candidate endings a fine-tuned classifier could already solve — CommonsenseQA's distractor selection is entirely human-driven (ConceptNet-neighbor sampling plus crowd verification), so there's no equivalent guarantee that a fine-tuned LM can't find and exploit patterns invisible to the human verifiers.

There's also a provenance concentration worth flagging: only 122 workers formulated all 12,247 questions, and the paper states 10 of them wrote more than 85% of the total. A benchmark whose linguistic surface is dominated by a handful of annotators' personal phrasing habits is exactly the setup that produced the "annotation artifacts" problem the paper itself cites (Gururangan et al., 2018) as a cautionary precedent for NLI datasets — a model could in principle be picking up on writing idiosyncrasies of ten Turkers rather than commonsense per se, and the paper doesn't test for this directly.

baselines

Fair for November 2018: BERT-LARGE and GPT-1 were the strongest available pretrained LMs, alongside carefully-chosen NLI/QA architectures (ESIM, bilinear, compare-aggregate). No adversarial-filtering pass against a fine-tuned model was applied to the released set, unlike SWAG's construction.

contamination

Not applicable at publication (pre-large-scale-LM-pretraining-on-benchmark-data era), but the dataset has since become a common component of instruction-tuning and eval mixtures — any "zero-shot" claim on CommonsenseQA against a modern model needs an explicit train-set decontamination check that this 2018 paper obviously couldn't anticipate.

what's missed

No test for annotator-fingerprint exploitation (10 workers wrote >85% of questions); no adversarial filtering against a strong LM before release; no analysis of whose "common" sense the U.S.-centric AMT worker pool encodes.

would raise confidence

A fine-tuned-model adversarial-filtering pass before the final release (SWAG-style); a second, disjoint worker-pool eval to check the 10-annotator concentration doesn't drive the reported gap; a held-out replication of the SANITY diagnostic on new questions.

// bottom line

CommonsenseQA is a genuinely useful scale-up of commonsense QA past Winograd/COPA-sized datasets, and its own SANITY ablation is an unusually candid piece of self-critique for a benchmark paper — most datasets don't ship the experiment that shows how much of their difficulty is an artifact of distractor selection. That transparency is exactly why later work should treat the 55.9%→88.9% gap as a floor on "true" commonsense difficulty rather than a precise measurement of it.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. 12,247 five-way multiple-choice questions generated by grounding crowd-worker prompts in ConceptNet subgraphs (a source concept plus three semantically-related targets sharing one relation), specifically to force questions that need background knowledge beyond what's stated — unlike SQuAD/TriviaQA-style reading comprehension.
  2. Best baseline (fine-tuned BERT-LARGE) reaches 55.9% accuracy versus 88.9% human accuracy on the primary random split — but the paper's own SANITY check (swap curated distractors for random ones) pushes the same model to 92.3%, showing the measured gap is driven substantially by adversarial distractor selection, not raw task difficulty.
  3. Became the standard commonsense-reasoning benchmark for the chain-of-thought and self-consistency line of work (it's cited directly by Wang et al.'s Self-Consistency paper) as a held-out test of reasoning gains beyond direct prompting.
