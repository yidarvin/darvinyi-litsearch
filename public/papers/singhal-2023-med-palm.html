2023 · Nature · Benchmarks & Evals

Large Language Models Encode Clinical Knowledge

Google's PaLM 540B — instruction-tuned into Flan-PaLM, then prompt-aligned into Med-PaLM — reaches 67.6% on USMLE-style MedQA, a 17-point jump over prior state of the art. But the paper's own physician panel still ranks the model below clinicians on consensus, harm, and completeness. Its lasting move: dragging medical-LLM evaluation off the multiple-choice leaderboard and into human-rated, safety-aware long-form answers.

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

By late 2022, large language models were posting eye-catching numbers on medical exam questions, but nobody could say what those numbers meant for medicine. The evaluations available were thin in two ways. First, they were almost entirely multiple-choice — a model picks A/B/C/D and you score accuracy — which rewards pattern-matching to answer keys and says nothing about whether a free-text answer is factual, complete, or dangerous. Second, the long-form alternatives leaned on automated text-overlap metrics like BLEU, which compare a generation to a single reference string and badly mis-measure clinical quality.

Medicine is the wrong domain to evaluate sloppily. A wrong answer here doesn't cost a leaderboard point; it can lead a patient to the wrong action. Answering a medical question well demands three distinct skills the authors keep returning to — reading comprehension of the clinical context, recall of the right medical fact, and reasoning over that knowledge — and none of those are visible in a single accuracy number. What was missing was a benchmark broad enough to span professional exams, research literature, and the questions real consumers actually type into a search bar, paired with a way for clinicians to judge model answers along the axes that matter in the clinic: scientific consensus, potential harm, bias, and completeness.

// how it works

The method

The paper has two halves: a benchmark and a model. The benchmark, MultiMedQA, bundles seven medical question-answering datasets — six existing (MedQA/USMLE, MedMCQA, PubMedQA, plus MMLU's six clinical subtasks, LiveQA, MedicationQA) and one new: HealthSearchQA, 3,375 consumer health questions harvested from real search-engine queries. The mix spans formats (multiple-choice vs. long-form), domains (open vs. closed-book), and sources (board exams, biomedical literature, consumers), so a model can't game it with one trick.

On top of accuracy, the authors build a human-evaluation framework: a panel of nine clinicians (UK, US, India) rates long-form answers along twelve axes — agreement with scientific consensus, extent and likelihood of harm, evidence of correct/incorrect comprehension, retrieval, and reasoning, inappropriate or missing content, and possibility of bias — and five lay users rate helpfulness and whether the answer addresses intent. Each answer gets a single rating, blinded to its source.

The model side starts from PaLM 540B and its instruction-tuned sibling Flan-PaLM, evaluated at 8B/62B/540B. Multiple-choice performance is squeezed out with three prompting strategies stacked together: few-shot exemplars hand-written with clinicians, chain-of-thought, and self-consistency — sample many reasoning chains and take the majority answer (they fixed 11 chains per question).

ŷ = argmaxa Σi=1m 𝟙[ answer(ci) = a ]m ,  m = 11 // self-consistency: marginalize over m sampled chain-of-thought decodes ci, keep the plurality answer

Strong multiple-choice scores didn't fix the long-form answers, so the authors add their named contribution: instruction prompt tuning. Rather than fine-tune 540B parameters (prohibitively expensive) or replace the hard prompt, they learn a small soft prompt — 100 tokens, ~1.84M trainable parameters — that is prefixed in front of the full hard prompt (instructions + few-shot exemplars + question), with the rest of the model frozen. Trained on just 40 clinician-curated exemplars for 200 steps, this soft prefix nudges the model toward safer, more grounded medical phrasing. The result is Med-PaLM: instruction-prompt-tuned Flan-PaLM 540B.

// what they found

The results

On multiple choice, Flan-PaLM 540B set a new state of the art across the board. On MedQA (USMLE, 4 options) it hit 67.6% — surpassing the prior best, PubMedGPT (50.3%), by 17.3 points, and DRAGON (47.5%) by 20.1 — and scored 62.0% on the harder 5-option set. On MedMCQA it reached 57.6% (prior SOTA Galactica, 52.9%); on PubMedQA, 79.0% (prior SOTA BioGPT, 78.2%) — though here the single-rater human ceiling is itself only 78.0%, so there's little headroom left.

Two ablations matter. Scale roughly doubled accuracy from 8B to 540B, with the 8B model barely above chance on MedQA. Self-consistency drove the MedQA jump (60.3% few-shot → 67.6%, a >7-point gain) — but it actually hurt PubMedQA (79.0% → 75.2%), and plain chain-of-thought never beat few-shot. The gains are real but dataset-specific, not a universal recipe. A selective-prediction probe using self-consistency vote counts as a confidence signal reached 82.5% on MedQA once the model was allowed to defer on 45% of questions.

The more important findings are in the human evaluation, where multiple-choice success did not translate. On the 140 long-form consumer questions, clinicians judged 92.9% of expert answers aligned with scientific consensus — but only 61.9% of Flan-PaLM's. Instruction prompt tuning closed almost the entire gap: Med-PaLM reached 92.6%. Potential harm followed the same arc — answers judged as potentially leading to harm dropped from 29.7% (Flan-PaLM) to 5.9% (Med-PaLM), versus 5.7% for clinicians — as did bias (7.9% → 0.8%) and missing content (47.2% → 15.1%). Lay-rated helpfulness rose from 60.6% to 80.3%, still short of clinicians' 91.1%.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

The headline "+17% SOTA on MedQA" is doing more rhetorical work than evidentiary work. The models it beats — PubMedGPT (2.7B), DRAGON (360M), BioGPT (355M), Galactica (120B) — are one to three orders of magnitude smaller than Flan-PaLM 540B. The authors fairly note MedQA is out-of-domain for the biomedical models, but a "state of the art" claim that compares a 540B general model to a 355M domain model is mostly a statement about compute, not a medical method. The genuinely method-driven result is instruction prompt tuning, and that is validated only on the long-form side.

There's also a quiet branding conflation worth naming. Every multiple-choice number — including the celebrated 67.6% — belongs to Flan-PaLM, not Med-PaLM. "Med-PaLM" the model is evaluated only on 140 long-form questions. So the paper's two famous claims (the exam SOTA and the safety alignment) come from two different models on two different evaluations, and the title's "Med-PaLM" gets credit for both.

The human evaluation, the part that actually moves the field, is thin where it counts: 140 questions, a single clinician rating per answer, nine raters total, and self-described "subjective" axes. The authors openly say the AHRQ harm scale's validity doesn't transfer to this setting, and they report no inter-rater reliability for the main study. And one number cuts against the safety narrative: instruction prompt tuning increased inappropriate/incorrect content (Flan-PaLM 16.1% → Med-PaLM 18.7%, vs. clinicians' 1.4%) — alignment for completeness produced longer answers with more chances to be wrong, a trade-off the "closes the gap to clinicians" framing skips past.

baselines

SOTA comparisons are against 355M–120B biomedical models; the jump partly reflects ~200× scale, not a medical technique. The real method (instruction prompt tuning) is only tested on long-form answers.

contamination

MedQA/MedMCQA/PubMedQA/MMLU are public and could sit in PaLM's 780B-token web corpus. The rebuttal — Chowdhery et al.'s contaminated-vs-clean delta — is dataset-level, not per-question decontamination of these specific medical sets.

what's missed

Multiple-choice accuracy is the headline, but the model's own long-form answers still trail clinicians on consensus, retrieval, and helpfulness — and Med-PaLM regressed on incorrect content. Self-consistency helps MedQA yet hurts PubMedQA.

would raise confidence

Per-question contamination checks; multi-rater human eval with reported inter-annotator agreement; consistent reporting of Med-PaLM (not Flan-PaLM) across every axis; and evaluation in real clinical workflows rather than expert-curated vignettes.

// bottom line

A landmark that reframed medical-LLM evaluation — from a multiple-choice leaderboard to a physician-rated, harm-aware human study — and showed a cheap soft prompt can dramatically improve answer safety. But the famous 67.6% is a Flan-PaLM exam score on possibly-contaminated public questions, while the safety story rests on a small, single-rater long-form study where the model still trails clinicians and even got worse at avoiding incorrect content. Read it as a methodology contribution and an honest limitations section, not as evidence the model is clinic-ready — which the authors themselves are careful to say.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. The benchmark and human-eval framework are the real contribution. MultiMedQA (seven datasets, incl. the new 3,375-question HealthSearchQA) plus twelve clinician-rated axes — consensus, harm, bias, completeness — pushed medical-LLM evaluation past multiple-choice accuracy and BLEU.
  2. Instruction prompt tuning is a cheap, effective alignment lever. A 100-token, ~1.84M-parameter soft prefix trained on 40 exemplars lifted scientific-consensus agreement 61.9% → 92.6% and cut potentially-harmful answers 29.7% → 5.9% — without touching the 540B base.
  3. Scale wins exams; it doesn't win the clinic. Flan-PaLM's 67.6% on MedQA leans on scale + self-consistency over public, possibly-contaminated questions. The human study shows Med-PaLM still trails clinicians and even raised incorrect content — scale alone is insufficient for safety-critical use.
