MoReBench: Evaluating Procedural and Pluralistic Moral Reasoning in Language Models, More than Outcomes

Most evals grade the answer. MoReBench grades the argument — scoring how a model reasons through 1,000 moral dilemmas against 23,018 criteria written by 53 moral philosophers. The headline: moral-reasoning skill is almost uncorrelated with the math, code, and scaling signals we use to rank models.

Yu Ying Chiu*, Michael S. Lee*, Rachel Calcott, Brandon Handoko, … Mantas Mazeika, Bing Liu, Yejin Choi, Mitchell Gordon△, Sydney Levine△ — UW · NYU · Scale AI · Harvard · MIT · Oxford · Stanford

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

We increasingly ask models to make decisions with us and for us — to advise on a personal dilemma, or to act autonomously as an agent inside human institutions. For those uses, knowing what a model decided is not enough; we need to know how it got there. But almost every eval we have grades the outcome.

That works when there's a verifiable answer — AIME has a number, LiveCodeBench has a passing test. Moral dilemmas don't. A well-reasoned response can land on either side of "should I report the deception?" The thing worth grading isn't the conclusion, it's whether the reasoning surfaced the right considerations, weighed the trade-offs honestly, and respected pluralistic values on the way to a defensible answer.

Prior moral-AI work — ETHICS, Delphi, the Moral Machine, surveys of "moral beliefs" — almost all measures what systems decide. The closest attempts at process evaluation (hand-built deontology/consequentialism taxonomies for self-driving rationales, philosopher-vs-model comparisons, bespoke reasoning classifiers) are narrow and don't scale. Meanwhile a new ingredient has arrived: reasoning models that emit a thinking trace, partially exposing their process. MoReBench's bet is that you can grade that trace at scale — if experts write down, in advance, the considerations any good answer must contain.

// the reframing

Moral dilemmas aren't a weakness of process-based evaluation — they're the ideal testbed for it. Because multiple conclusions are defensible, you're forced to score the reasoning rather than the answer.

// how it works

The method

MoReBench is 1,000 contextualized moral scenarios, each paired with a hand-written rubric. In total there are 23,018 criteria (mean 23.0 per scenario, σ = 4.2, range 20–49). Scenarios run 44–393 words (mean 194.9) and split into two roles MoReBench expects models to play in the wild: Moral Advisor (58.6%, guiding a human — sourced from DailyDilemmas) and Moral Agent (41.4%, acting autonomously under AI-safety-flavored constraints — sourced from AIRiskDilemmas), plus expert-written cases drawn from ethics literature, debate cases, and applied-ethics news.

The rubrics are the product. 53 moral-philosophy experts (64.2% with a doctorate; mean age 39.3) wrote them, each criterion required to be objective, scenario-specific, and atomic — grading exactly one aspect. Every rubric is then reviewed by a second expert (and the research team), so each scenario captures at least two philosophers' views of what good reasoning must contain. Criteria are sorted into five dimensions and carry signed weights from −3 (critically detrimental) to +3 (critically important), excluding 0.

Scoring a reasoning trace

For sample i, with criterion j carrying expert weight pij ∈ [−3, 3] (≠ 0) and a fulfillment label rij ∈ {−1, 1}, the score is a signed-weighted fraction of the achievable total. An ideal trace fulfills every positively-weighted criterion and none of the negative ones → 100; the inverse → 0.

si = Σj sgn(pij) · rij · pijΣj |pij| // Eq. 1 — weighted criterion-fulfillment, normalized to [0, 100]

Because a chattier trace gets more chances to tick criteria — the same length bias HealthBench flagged — they also report a length-corrected score that scales by a 1,000-character reference length. The plain mean s̄ is MoReBench-Regular; the length-corrected s̄LC is MoReBench-Hard, which rewards reasoning that is efficient as well as thorough.

s̄LC = s̄ · lrefl ,  lref = 1000 // Eq. 2 — length control; l = avg response length in characters

Who grades, and can the rubric be trusted?

The grader is an LLM judge, so they meta-evaluate it first. Two experts independently labeled criterion-fulfillment on 7,176 response–criteria pairs (Cohen's κ = 0.75, excellent agreement) across responses from GPT-5, Claude Opus 4.1, and DeepSeek-R1-0528. They score each candidate judge by the lowest macro-F1 across five slices (the three models × the two roles) — a deliberate lower bound that punishes any model- or role-specific bias. GPT-5-high tops it at 77.46% F1, but at $156.12 it is 80× the cost of GPT-oss-120b (76.29% F1, $1.91) — so GPT-oss-120b becomes the judge for everything else.

Two more checks defend the rubrics themselves. Discriminatory power: experts wrote low/medium/high-quality traces for 30 cases; the rubric separates them (high X̄ = 0.53 vs low X̄ = 0.39, ANOVA F(2,87) = 6.34, p = 0.003; Spearman rs = 0.35). Robustness: a second group wrote high-quality traces arguing the opposite conclusion — and scored statistically identically (X̄ = 0.55 vs 0.53, t(58) = −0.59, p = 0.56), confirming the rubric rewards reasoning quality, not a preferred answer. Of the 1,000 scenarios, only 500 are public; the rest are held out as a private test set to blunt contamination.

Alongside the main set, MoReBench-Theory adds 150 scenarios (30 per framework) with rubrics written to score reasoning within one of five normative theories: Kantian Deontology, Benthamite Act Utilitarianism, Aristotelian Virtue Ethics, Scanlonian Contractualism, and Gauthierian Contractarianism — testing whether a model can reason in a framework when explicitly told to.

// what they found

The results

They graded the thinking traces of 14 frontier reasoning models. The single most important finding is a negative one: the predictors we normally use to rank models don't work here.

Scaling laws break

On MoReBench-Regular, the biggest model in a family is usually not the best. The mid-size model wins in the GPT-5-high and Gemini-2.5 families; the smallest wins in the Claude 4, GPT-oss, and Qwen3-Thinking families. The authors read this as inverse scaling: larger models can reason implicitly and emit terser visible traces, so fewer gradeable steps surface. The trend partly reverses under length-corrected MoReBench-Hard (where the largest GPT-5/Claude/GPT-oss/Qwen3 models lead — Gemini stays the exception), which supports the "implicit vs. explicit reasoning" reading.

Other benchmarks don't predict it either

Plot MoReBench against Chatbot Arena (user preference), Humanity's Last Exam (general reasoning), AIME 25 (math), and LiveCodeBench (code) and the relationships vanish — Pearson's r lands between −0.245 and 0.216, i.e. negligible in every case. Moral reasoning is effectively its own axis of capability, orthogonal to the STEM-and-preference signals the field optimizes.

Where the reasoning fails

Broken down by dimension, the pattern is consistent: models are dependable at the safety floor and weak at the hard part. Harmlessness is the one dimension they reliably satisfy (72.0–85.5%) — a clear fingerprint of content-safety training. Logical process — integrating competing considerations into a justified trade-off — is the weakest. Gemini-2.5-Pro satisfies just 26.9% of logical-process criteria; Qwen3-235B reaches 65.1%. Tellingly, Gemini's fame in code and math competitions doesn't carry over: in one case it missed the commonsense link that over-reliance on an AI chess tutor could hurt a student's near-term performance, while GPT-5-mini caught it.

The split between families is character, not just skill: Claude Opus 4.1 and Sonnet 4 score low on helpful outcome (32.3% / 39.2%) because they tend to offer a "balanced analysis" and a "nuanced compromise" rather than concrete next steps, while Qwen3 and DeepSeek hand over actual options and consequences.

Models are partial to particular ethics

On MoReBench-Theory, models reason best within Deontology (M = 65.9) and Act Utilitarianism (M = 64.8) and significantly worse within Virtue Ethics (M = 58.0) and Contractarianism (M = 56.7) — all differences p < 0.001 (mixed-effects F(4,52) = 19.71). The spread across models is enormous for the less-favored frameworks: on Contractarianism, Gemini-2.5-Flash-Lite manages 27.3% while Qwen3-235B hits 72.2%, a 44.9-point gap. The authors suggest the Utilitarian/Deontological tilt may be a side-effect of RLHF, whose preference-and-rationale collection implicitly leans on those frameworks — meaning users who try to steer a model into a specific ethical framework will find it can't comply equally well across theories.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

MoReBench is a genuinely careful piece of benchmark engineering — the κ = 0.75 inter-annotator agreement, the explicit discriminatory-power and conclusion-robustness checks, the private test split, and the lowest-of-five-slices judge metric are all rigor most rubric papers skip. But the headline claims rest on two load-bearing choices that deserve scrutiny.

The judge is the eval — and it disagrees with humans ~1 in 4 times on its worst slice. Everything is graded by GPT-oss-120b, chosen for cost, whose lower-bound F1 is 76.29%. Spread that disagreement across ~23 criteria per scenario and a 3–5 point gap between two models is well inside the noise floor. The min-over-slices metric guards against gross model-family favoritism, but it doesn't tell you the variance of a single score — and since "logical process" is exactly the dimension that's hardest to grade, the finding that "models are weakest at logical reasoning" is partly entangled with "the judge is weakest at scoring logical criteria."

The cross-vendor comparison isn't apples-to-apples. The paper grades "thinking traces," but for closed models (GPT-5, Claude, Gemini) those are provider-generated summaries of hidden CoT, while for open models (Qwen3, DeepSeek, GPT-oss) they're the raw trace. The authors flag this in a footnote — yet the main bar chart, the "scaling laws break," and the "Qwen3 leads" narratives all treat the two as comparable. A summary is shorter and pre-digested, which interacts directly with both the length correction and the count of gradeable intermediate steps. That open models win the visible-reasoning game while closed models are graded on a sanitized abstract is a confound large enough to plausibly drive a chunk of the result.

The "orthogonal axis" claim leans on n ≈ 14. Pearson's r ∈ [−0.245, 0.216] is computed over roughly fourteen models; correlations that small with that few points have wide confidence intervals and shouldn't be read as "zero relationship" so much as "no relationship detectable at this sample size." The conclusion is directionally believable, but the strength of the claim outruns the evidence.

baselines

Strong and current model set (GPT-5, Opus 4.1, Gemini-2.5, DeepSeek/Qwen3/GPT-oss). But the closed-vs-open "thinking trace" is summary-vs-raw — not a fair like-for-like.

contamination

Well handled: 500 scenarios held private; meta-eval cites contamination explicitly. Caveat — the public half draws on pre-existing datasets (DailyDilemmas, AIRiskDilemmas) that may already be in training corpora.

what's missed

No human ceiling per dimension and no judge-variance bands, so it's unclear how much of a model gap is real vs. judge noise — most acute on the hardest-to-grade "logical process" dimension.

would raise confidence

Re-grade closed models on raw CoT (or exclude summary models from cross-vendor claims); report judge agreement per dimension; widen the model set before asserting orthogonality.

// bottom line

The reframing — grade the reasoning, not the answer; use moral dilemmas because the answer is contestable — is the real contribution, and it's a good one. The specific rankings should be read with a wide error bar, especially across the closed/open summary divide. But the core message survives the caveats: a model that aces math, code, and Arena tells you almost nothing about whether it reasons well under moral uncertainty.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. Grade the process, not the outcome. 1,000 dilemmas × 23,018 expert-written criteria turn "is the answer right?" (undefined for moral questions) into "did the reasoning do the right things?" — scored at scale by an LLM judge against atomic, signed-weight criteria.
  2. Moral reasoning is its own capability axis. Scaling laws don't predict it (the biggest model often isn't best), and neither do Arena, HLE, AIME, or LiveCodeBench (r ∈ [−0.245, 0.216]). You can't infer it from the benchmarks the field already runs.
  3. Models are safe but shallow, and ethically partial. They reliably avoid harm (72–85%) but are weakest at integrating competing considerations into a justified trade-off, and they reason markedly better in Utilitarian/Deontological frames than in Virtue Ethics or Contractarianism — likely an RLHF fingerprint.
  4. Read the rankings with a wide error bar. A cost-chosen judge (76% worst-slice F1) and a closed-vs-open "summary vs. raw trace" mismatch mean specific model gaps are softer than the bar charts suggest — but the qualitative story holds.
