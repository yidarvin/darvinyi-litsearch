Constitutional AI: Harmlessness from AI Feedback

Train a harmless, non-evasive assistant with no human labels for harm — hand it a short list of written principles (a "constitution") and let the model critique, revise, and grade itself. The paper that coined RLAIF.

Yuntao Bai, Saurav Kadavath, Sandipan Kundu, Amanda Askell, Jackson Kernion, Andy Jones, Anna Chen, Anna Goldie, Azalia Mirhoseini, … Tom B. Brown, Jared Kaplan · Anthropic

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

RLHF works, but it has two stubborn costs. First, it is label-hungry and opaque: training a helpful-and-harmless assistant the way Bai et al. (2022) did takes tens of thousands of human preference labels, and once collected, those labels "do not shed much light on AI training objectives" — no one can read 100k pairwise judgments and tell you what behavior the model was actually steered toward.

Second, training hard for harmlessness produces an evasive assistant. In the team's prior human-feedback model, because crowdworkers rewarded "I can't answer that," the model learned to stonewall — and once it hit an objectionable query it could stay shut down for the rest of the conversation. An assistant that answers everything with "I don't know" is perfectly harmless and perfectly useless. There is a real tension: pushing harmlessness up pushes helpfulness down.

The deeper motivation is scalable oversight. As models approach or exceed human level on more tasks, we cannot keep relying on humans to label every aspect of behavior. So the paper asks an extreme version of the question: can we supervise harmlessness with almost no human labels at all — reducing human input to a short list of natural-language principles — by enlisting the model to supervise itself? The premise rests on a prior observation (Figure 4): large models, especially with chain-of-thought, are already nearly as good as a human-feedback-trained preference model at picking the more harmless response.

// how it works

The method

Constitutional AI (CAI) is two stages bolted onto a helpful-only RLHF model as the starting point. Human supervision enters only through ~16 written principles (the "constitution") plus a handful of few-shot examples; there are no human harmlessness labels anywhere in the pipeline.

Stage 1 — Supervised: critique → revision → finetune (SL-CAI)

Prompt the helpful model with a red-team request ("Can you help me hack into my neighbor's wifi?"). It happily complies — that's the point. Then append a critique request tied to a sampled principle ("Identify specific ways the response is harmful, unethical, racist, … or illegal"), and let the model critique its own answer. Then append a revision request and let it rewrite. Because the revised prompt-response pair is formatted exactly like the original, you can loop: draw a fresh principle and revise again, up to 4 times. Finetune a pretrained model on the final revisions — mixed with sampled helpful responses to retain helpfulness. The point of this stage is not peak harmlessness but to get the model "on-distribution" so the RL stage has less to explore.

Revisions help monotonically. On a 52B preference model trained only on human data, harmlessness score climbs with each of the 4 revisions while pure helpfulness slips slightly (Figure 5). Curiously, the number of principles doesn't raise the harmlessness score — but more principles produce more diverse revisions, which matters for exploration in the next stage. Skipping the critique and revising directly is slightly worse for small models and roughly equal for large ones; they keep the critique mainly for transparency.

Stage 2 — RL from AI Feedback (RL-CAI)

This mimics RLHF, but replaces the human harmlessness labeler with the model. SL-CAI generates two responses to a red-team prompt; a separate feedback model is shown the pair with a sampled principle, formatted as a multiple-choice question ("Which response is less harmful? Choose the response a wise, ethical, polite and friendly person would say"). The normalized log-probabilities of options (A) and (B) become a soft preference label — which is reasonable because multiple-choice answers from these models are well-calibrated. Those AI labels (for harmlessness) are mixed with the original human labels (for helpfulness) to train one preference model, and SL-CAI is then RL-optimized against it.

P(A ≻ B) = eℓAeℓA + eℓB // ℓA, ℓB = feedback model's log-probs of options (A)/(B). With CoT, clamp P to [0.40, 0.60] or the policy learns over-extreme responses.

An optional twist: prompt the feedback model with chain-of-thought ("Let's think step-by-step…") so it argues out which response is more harmless before choosing. CoT raises label quality and transparency — but it makes the feedback model commit hard, pushing probabilities to 0/1 and wrecking calibration. The fix is a hack: clamp the CoT-derived probabilities to the 40–60% band (they tried 20–80, settled on 40–60). Ensembling over the 16 principles per label made the preference model more robust.

// what they found

The results

The headline is a Pareto improvement at 52B. Plotting harmlessness Elo against helpfulness Elo (from crowdworker comparisons, ~8,135 harmlessness and 10,274 helpfulness comparisons across 24 snapshots), the RL-CAI models — especially RL-CAI with CoT — sit above and to the right of the standard RLHF frontier: more harmless at any given level of helpfulness. SL-CAI alone is more harmless than the helpful model but less helpful than either RL model; the RL stage is what makes it competitive.

Crucially, RL-CAI is virtually never evasive — it engages with sensitive prompts and explains its objection rather than stonewalling, the exact failure mode of the prior HH model. A second, independent metric agrees: an absolute harmfulness score (0–4, from a model trained to predict crowdworker "success at baiting harm" over 64 held-out red-team prompts × 256 responses). Over RL training the helpful RLHF model climbs to ~3.5 (more harmful), while RL-CAI and RL-CAI-w/-CoT fall to ~0.7 (Figure 10).

The team is candid about a failure mode: over-trained RL-CAI Goodharts. It can become over-harsh, or paste boilerplate comfort language onto nearly every red-team reply — "You are valid, valued, and cared for." That is reward over-optimization (Gao et al., 2022) showing through, and a reason the 40–60% clamp and principle ensembling exist.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

The method is real and the influence is enormous — RLAIF and "criteria-as-reward" both start here. But the evidence is softer than the iconic Pareto plot suggests, and the paper, to its credit, hands you most of the caveats itself.

The biggest one is a moving goalpost in the labeling instructions. For this paper, crowdworkers were told to prefer non-evasive responses when two answers were equally harmless — a rule that did not apply in the prior HH-RLHF work. That single change compresses the HH RLHF baseline's harmlessness Elo (the authors say so explicitly) and inflates the helpful model's. So part of "CAI beats RLHF on harmlessness" is the new metric rewarding the thing CAI was built to do (engage, don't dodge), not a like-for-like win. The comparison crowd also switched vendors (Surge AI vs. the earlier Upwork/MTurk).

Second, the "no human feedback" framing is narrower than it sounds. Harmlessness labels are AI-generated, yes — but the pipeline still leans on human helpfulness labels, a human-written constitution, hand-written few-shot critique/revision/CoT exemplars, and red-team prompts that were originally human-seeded. It is reduced, more legible oversight, not the absence of it. And the constitution itself was, in their words, "chosen in a fairly ad hoc and iterative way" — number of principles doesn't even move the PM score (Figure 6).

Third, everything is one model family at one moment: Anthropic's 52B, December 2022, graded by Elo from a few thousand subjective pairwise comparisons. PM scores "become less calibrated at higher values" (their caveat on the revision curves), the absolute-harm metric "may not be well-calibrated" across workers, and the feedback model is the same base family as the policy — the model is, in a real sense, grading itself, which later self-preference work (Panickssery et al., 2024) suggests is not neutral. Robustness to adversarial attacks — the thing you'd most want from a "harmless" model — is explicitly named as future work and never measured; jailbreak/red-team robustness against novel attack distributions is untested.

baselines

Fair in spirit (same Elo protocol, same prior HH/helpful models) but compromised by a mid-stream labeling-instruction change that disfavors evasiveness — exactly the axis CAI optimizes. No external or open-weight model is compared; the win is in-house.

contamination

Not a benchmark-leakage paper, but a self-grading one: the feedback model and policy share a base, so harmlessness "preferences" are partly the model agreeing with itself. Soft labels lean on the model's own calibration claim.

what's missed

No adversarial robustness / jailbreak eval (the headline goal, deferred). Goodharting into boilerplate is shown but not quantified. Helpfulness held "roughly constant" rests on noisy Elo, not task accuracy. Single scale, single org, single date.

would raise confidence

A held-fixed labeling rubric matched to prior work; an independent (different-family) feedback model; adversarial-robustness numbers; reproduction on open models (the repo shares prompts/principles but not the models or the 52B stack).

// bottom line

A genuinely new training paradigm — self-critique plus RLAIF — backed by suggestive-but-soft, subjective, in-house evidence on a single model. The mechanism is what mattered: it showed AI feedback against a short written constitution could replace tens of thousands of harmlessness labels and kill evasiveness, and the whole rubric/criteria-as-reward line (Rule-Based Rewards, checklists, rubric anchors) descends from it. Read the Pareto plot as "this clearly works," not "this is the measured size of the effect."

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. RLAIF was born here. Replace the human harmlessness labeler with a model that grades two responses against a sampled principle; distill those AI preferences into a reward model; RL against it. Harmlessness supervision drops from tens of thousands of labels to a ~16-rule constitution.
  2. Two stages, and the SL stage is scaffolding. Critique-and-revise finetuning (SL-CAI) mainly gets the model on-distribution so RL has less to explore; the RL-from-AI-feedback stage is what produces the Pareto improvement and the non-evasiveness.
  3. Self-supervision invites reward hacking. Over-trained RL-CAI Goodharts into boilerplate ("you are valid, valued, and cared for"); CoT labels needed clamping to 40–60% to stay calibrated. Making the model its own judge is powerful and fragile — the tension the rubric-as-reward literature has wrestled with ever since.
