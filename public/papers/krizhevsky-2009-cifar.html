Learning Multiple Layers of Features from Tiny Images

Alex Krizhevsky set out to train deep generative models — RBMs and Deep Belief Networks — on 80 million unlabeled 32×32 web images. The generative results are a historical footnote. What survived is the byproduct: two small, hand-cleaned labeled subsets, CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100, that became the default first benchmark for essentially every image-classification idea published for the next decade and a half.

Alex Krizhevsky

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

By 2009, colleagues at MIT and NYU had assembled something genuinely new: a corpus of 80 million tiny colour images, each downscaled to 32×32, gathered by searching the web for every non-abstract English noun in WordNet. In principle it was an ideal substrate for training deep generative models of natural images at a scale nobody had attempted — Restricted Boltzmann Machines (RBMs) and Deep Belief Networks (DBNs) were the leading unsupervised feature-learning tools of the moment, and here, finally, was enough raw visual data to feed them. In practice, two things stood in the way. First, prior groups who had tried to learn generative models from this exact dataset had failed — the report opens by noting the attempt "has been attempted by several groups but without success." Second, the dataset's only labels were the noisy search terms used to retrieve each image, which is not a reliable signal for object identity — a search for "cat" returns plenty of images that are not photographs of cats — so there was no way to check whether any learned features were actually useful for object recognition.

There was also a systems problem hiding underneath the modeling problem: training an RBM with thousands of hidden units on millions of images, with the standard single-machine contrastive-divergence algorithm, was not tractable in reasonable time. Any credible attempt needed both a working training recipe for this kind of data and a way to parallelize it across machines.

// how it works

The method

The report's core experiment is a fairly classical unsupervised-pretraining pipeline, applied at unusual scale. Images are first ZCA-whitened — a linear transform that removes second-order (pairwise pixel) correlations so the model is forced to discover higher-order structure rather than simply re-learn that nearby pixels are similar. On top of whitened (and, separately, unwhitened) pixels, Krizhevsky trains Gaussian-Bernoulli RBMs: first on small 8×8 patches (merging 26 patch-local RBMs into one model of the full 32×32 image), then directly on whole 32×32 images with up to 10,000 hidden units, using roughly 2 million of the 80 million tiny images. A second RBM is then stacked on top of the first to form a 2-layer Deep Belief Network. The learned RBM weights are subsequently used to initialize a feed-forward neural net, which is fine-tuned with backpropagation and a logistic-regression output layer — the paper's best-performing recipe throughout.

Because training RBMs this large on this much data was itself the bottleneck, chapter 4 of the report is devoted to a distributed contrastive-divergence algorithm: the visible and hidden layers are partitioned into n equal chunks, one per machine, with synchronization points inserted after each of the three CD-1 sub-steps so every machine proceeds in lockstep. Total communication scales linearly with the number of machines while communication per machine stays constant — the engineering contribution that made training 10,000-hidden-unit RBMs on millions of images practical at all.

The labeling story — the part of this report that mattered most in hindsight — is in chapter 3, and it is not the Mechanical Turk pipeline sometimes assumed. Krizhevsky paid students to manually clean a labeled subset out of the noisy search-term labels. Each labeler was assigned one class and asked to examine every image retrieved under that class's search term (plus WordNet hyponyms of it — e.g. "cargo ship," "ocean liner," and "frigate" for the class ship), rejecting anything that didn't meet a written criterion sheet (reproduced in the report's Appendix C): the image had to be photo-realistic (no line drawings), contain one prominent instance of the class, and have that class be a top answer to "what is in this picture?" Labelers were paid by the hour rather than per image, "so there was no incentive to rush," and — critically — every submitted label was personally verified by the author. Near-duplicates (including re-saved JPEGs) were removed via an L2-distance threshold. The result is CIFAR-10: 10 mutually exclusive classes — airplane, automobile (excluding truck/pickup truck), bird, cat, deer, dog, frog, horse, ship, and truck (excluding pickup truck) — with 6,000 images each (5,000 train / 1,000 test per class). The same methodology, applied afterward, produced CIFAR-100: 100 classes of 600 images each, mutually exclusive with the CIFAR-10 classes and grouped into 20 superclasses of 5 classes apiece (e.g. the superclass reptile contains crocodile, dinosaur, lizard, turtle, and snake). A footnote credits Vinod Nair for "his substantial contribution to the labeling project."

// what they found

The results

All of the report's classification experiments run on CIFAR-10 only — CIFAR-100 is described as "only very recently completed" at time of writing and isn't benchmarked. Every method funnels into the same multinomial logistic-regression (or backprop) output layer; what varies is the input representation. Raw, unwhitened pixels reach 41.13% test accuracy; raw whitened pixels do worse, at 37.32%. Features from a 10,000-unit RBM trained on unwhitened data push logistic regression to 63.78% (vs. 59.61% for the whitened-data RBM) — confirming the paper's point that whitening, useful for the generative model, is not obviously useful for the downstream classifier. The best result in the report, 64.84%, comes from fine-tuning a backprop net whose hidden layer was initialized from those same unwhitened-RBM features. A second RBM/DBN layer does not help: the two-hidden-layer nets top out at 62.20% and 60.34%, both below the single-hidden-layer 64.84% — "two hidden layers did not give us any improvement over one," as the text puts it.

A confusion matrix over the RBM-feature logistic-regression model's predictions shows a pattern that would recur in CIFAR-10 discourse for years afterward: the ten classes split cleanly into an "animal" cluster and a "vehicle" cluster, with almost no cross-cluster confusion ("seldom is an animal mistaken for a non-animal, save for the occasional misidentification of a bird as a plane"), while within the animal cluster, cat and dog are confused far more often than any other pair.

None of these numbers is why this report has 43,266 citations. Almost no one cites it for its RBM results — it is cited because CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 became the field's default small-image sandbox: cheap enough to iterate on for a single grad student, big enough to separate a real idea from noise, and standardized enough that thousands of subsequent papers could report a directly comparable number on it. Every classic CIFAR-era architecture and training trick — Network-in-Network, Maxout Networks, Wide ResNets, and the vast literature on regularization, augmentation, and architecture search that followed — measured itself against exactly the two datasets defined in chapter 3 of this report.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

Judged as a 2009 machine-learning paper, this report holds up fine on its own terms — it is honest that its RBM/DBN pipeline is an internal ablation study, not a claim of state-of-the-art object recognition, and it says so plainly ("all of our experiments were with the CIFAR-10 dataset because the CIFAR-100 was only very recently completed"). Judged as the origin document of the two most-reused small-image benchmarks in deep learning, the more interesting question is how well the dataset construction — not the modeling — has aged, and the honest answer is "well enough to last fifteen-plus years, with real, well-documented cracks."

baselines

Not really applicable to the dataset's later life: this report never compares its RBM/DBN pipeline to a contemporaneous supervised baseline, and once CIFAR-10/100 became a community benchmark, "baselines" meant thousands of other papers' numbers on the same fixed split — not anything this report itself established.

contamination

Train and test are a random split carved from one pool that was deduplicated only globally (an L2-distance near-duplicate filter), not specifically checked for train↔test leakage. A decade of the same fixed 10,000-image test set being reported against by essentially every published CV architecture is itself a form of community-scale adaptive overfitting risk, distinct from classic train-time contamination.

what's missed

Labeling quality control was one person per class doing the filtering, with the author personally re-checking every submitted label — there is no inter-annotator agreement statistic, no multi-rater consensus step, and no published error-rate audit of the released labels, unlike later crowd-verified benchmarks (e.g. ImageNet's multi-annotator pipeline).

would raise confidence

An explicit train/test near-duplicate check (not just global dedup); a documented label-error rate; and — since this already happened after publication — the kind of independent, same-methodology resample that Recht et al.'s CIFAR-10.1 test set provided, which is exactly the check a benchmark this heavily reused needed.

// bottom line

The dataset construction in chapter 3 — student labelers, a written criteria sheet, one hyperparameter-free split — was good enough to become the default benchmark for a generation of computer vision, but it was never designed to survive fifteen years of the exact same fixed test set being optimized against by the entire field. Follow-up work built independent CIFAR-10-style test sets from the same source distribution and found consistent accuracy drops across the models checked against them, and a separate manual audit of the original CIFAR-10 labels turned up genuine labeling mistakes on re-inspection. Neither finding invalidates CIFAR-10/100 as a fast, cheap, still-useful sanity-check benchmark; both are reasons to treat any single model's CIFAR-10 number as noisier and more overfit-to-the-split than the two-decimal-place precision it's usually reported with.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. CIFAR-10 (10 classes × 6,000 images) and CIFAR-100 (100 classes × 600 images, in 20 superclasses) were carved out of the 80-million-image "Tiny Images" corpus by paying students to manually filter noisy web-search labels against a written criteria sheet — not a Mechanical Turk crowd-sourcing pipeline, and with just one author personally verifying every submitted label.
  2. The report's actual headline result — an RBM/DBN feature-learning pipeline, trained with a novel distributed contrastive-divergence algorithm, reaching 64.84% CIFAR-10 accuracy — is a historical footnote today. CIFAR's staying power comes entirely from the two labeled datasets it introduced as a side effect of that pipeline's evaluation.
  3. A benchmark that stays fixed for over a decade while the entire field optimizes against it is exactly the setup that produces adaptive-overfitting risk; independent resamples of CIFAR-10 built from the same source distribution later found real, consistent accuracy drops, and a separate audit found genuine errors in the original labels — worth remembering every time a paper reports a CIFAR-10 number to two decimal places.
