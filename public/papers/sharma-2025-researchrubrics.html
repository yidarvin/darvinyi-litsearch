ResearchRubrics: A Benchmark of Prompts and Rubrics For Evaluating Deep Research Agents

Scale AI built a 101-prompt, 2,593-criterion benchmark for Deep Research agents — every rubric hand-written by experts over 2,800+ hours of labor. Even the strongest agents, Gemini's and OpenAI's Deep Research, clear under 68% of the criteria, failing mostly on implicit context and multi-document synthesis rather than factual retrieval.

Manasi Sharma, Chen Bo Calvin Zhang, Chaithanya Bandi, … Brad Kenstler, Bing Liu · Scale AI

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

Deep Research (DR) agents — OpenAI's Deep Research, Google's Gemini Deep Research, Perplexity's Deep Research — are autonomous LLM systems that run multi-step web exploration, targeted retrieval, and cross-document synthesis to answer open-ended queries. Their outputs are 10–100× longer than a normal chat reply (5,000–50,000+ tokens), admit many valid forms, and depend on a live web that changes between runs. That makes them genuinely hard to grade.

Existing evals fall short in two opposite ways. QA benchmarks — general ones like GAIA, FRAMES, HotpotQA, and Humanity's Last Exam, and DR-specific ones like DeepResearchGym and LiveDRBench — reward short, verifiable factual answers ("Which material has band gap 0.9 eV and dislocation density 4×10⁸ cm⁻²?" → "Gallium nitride"). They never test the long-form, multi-source synthesis that is the deep-research task.

The DR benchmarks that try to grade long answers have their own problem: many lean on LLM-generated rubrics or LLM-generated reference reports (e.g. DeepResearch Bench), which raises circularity and oversight concerns, or they are narrow — scoring only a "Related Works" section, or only AI topics, so an Agent-as-a-Judge can cope. In practice users point DR systems at everything from business reports to consumer questions. The gap ResearchRubrics targets: domain-diverse, realistic prompts paired with fine-grained rubrics that are written and reviewed by humans from the outset, not auto-generated and merely human-checked (which the authors argue introduces an anchoring bias).

// how it works

The method

ResearchRubrics is 101 single-turn prompts, each paired with 20–43 prompt-specific rubric criteria — 2,593 unique criteria in total (mean ≈ 26 per task). The prompts span nine domains chosen for diversity: AI & ML (16.8%), Historical Analysis (12.9%), Business Planning & Research (11.9%), Technical Documentation (11.9%), General Consumer Research (10.9%), Hypotheticals & Philosophy (10.9%), STEM (7.9%), Creative Writing (5.9%), Current Events (5.0%), and Other. Crucially, no prompt or criterion was seeded or generated by an LLM.

Each task is built by a three-stage expert pipeline: Expert 1 drafts the prompt and an initial rubric; Expert 2 independently reviews and iterates with Expert 1 until they agree; Expert 3 makes a final independent pass. Here an "expert" means someone with a strong STEM background skilled in task design and evaluation — they only worked on domains they were familiar with. Initial prompt ideas were drawn from user forums, Q&A sites, and brainstorming, then sharpened into research-grade questions.

A tri-axial complexity framework

To keep the benchmark from collapsing into "easy lookups," every task is annotated along three orthogonal axes: Conceptual Breadth (how many distinct topics/sources — Simple / Moderate / High), Logical Nesting Depth (how many dependent reasoning steps — Shallow / Intermediate / Deep), and Exploration (how underspecified the goal is — Low / Medium / High). The mix is deliberately weighted toward the middle and hard tiers: 35.6% of tasks need Deep (4+ step) reasoning, and 51.5% have Moderate breadth.

Weighted, signed rubrics

Each criterion gets an integer weight in [−5, +5]. Weights of ±4 or ±5 mark mandatory criteria (core requirements without which a response is fundamentally flawed); weights in [−3, +3] are optional "nice-to-haves." Negative criteria penalize specific failure modes — uncited blanket claims, off-topic tangents, factual errors. The weights map onto a six-level human preference scale from Critically Detrimental to Critically Important, and the criteria sort into six evaluation axes: Explicit Requirements, Implicit Requirements, Synthesis of Information, Use of References, Communication Quality, and Instruction Following. Tellingly, implicit criteria (39.4%) and explicit criteria (27.7%) dominate the benchmark — it is built to reward inferring unstated context, not just answering what was literally asked.

Scoring

An LLM judge issues a ternary verdict per criterion — Satisfied (1), Partially Satisfied (0.5), Not Satisfied (0) — including for negative criteria, whose negative weight is subtracted when met. A task's score is the weighted sum of all verdicts, normalized by the total positive weight available (the maximum achievable):

Sk = Σrᵢ∈C wrᵢ · mrᵢΣrᵢ∈C, wrᵢ>0 wrᵢ   ,  mrᵢ ∈ { 1, 0.5, 0 } // wrᵢ ∈ [−5,+5] is the criterion's weight; mrᵢ is the judge's ternary satisfaction. Binary grading collapses 0.5 → 0.

The authors evaluate three commercial DR systems with three state-of-the-art judges — GPT-5, Claude Sonnet 4.5, and Gemini 2.5 Pro — under both grading schemes, and validate the automated grader against 9 expert annotators over 303 responses using Macro-F1 over the three verdict classes.

// what they found

The results

The headline: no system exceeds 70% rubric compliance. Under ternary grading, Gemini DR leads at 0.677, with OpenAI DR at 0.664 and Perplexity DR at 0.566; under stricter binary grading the same order holds at 0.615 / 0.597 / 0.487. For context, the authors note this matches LiveResearchBench (best systems below 74%) and DeepResearch Bench (below 50%) — a consistency they read as a fundamental ceiling rather than a benchmark quirk.

Where the failures land is the real story. Implicit reasoning and synthesis jointly account for 45–50% of all failures. Agents are good at explicit factual retrieval and at writing clean prose (failure rates below 20%) but consistently miss the unstated requirements a knowledgeable reader would expect, and they struggle to weave multi-document evidence into a coherent argument.

Splitting criteria by importance sharpens this. Mandatory criteria fail more often on most axes — but implicit reasoning inverts: there, optional criteria drive the failures (60.4% vs 46.3% under ternary). In other words, current systems clear the basic implicit requirements but miss the nuanced quality signals that separate excellent from merely-adequate research.

Two more patterns. Performance degrades monotonically with logical nesting depth — shallow one- or two-step queries are handled well, but multi-step analytical problems drop sharply, and tasks exceeding ~4 sequential steps see near-universal collapse; breadth hurts too, but less steeply (systems synthesize across domains better than they chain inferences). And there is a breadth–accuracy trade-off in citations: Gemini produces 111 citations at 81% accuracy, while Perplexity hits 90% accuracy with only 31 citations — no system manages both coverage and precision. Response length correlates weakly-positively with score (r ≈ 0.245–0.280 for Gemini/ChatGPT), but because grading is criterion-based, that mostly reflects genuine information density, not a verbosity bias.

Methodology findings worth keeping

Validating the grader, binary grading reaches 0.72–0.76 Macro-F1 with human experts (Gemini 2.5 Pro is the best judge at 0.76, beating HealthBench's 0.709), while ternary lands at just 0.53–0.57 — partial credit introduces ambiguity without improving discriminative power, a ~20-point alignment gap. And on rubric design: adding brief inline examples to a criterion improves human–model agreement by 3–4% (binary), but using an LLM to "augment" or rephrase the rubrics catastrophically degrades alignment by 15–20% (Gemini's binary agreement collapses from 0.760 to 0.564). Expert curation cannot be replaced by automated expansion.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

The core contribution is solid and the failure-mode map is the most valuable thing here: grading long-form research output against 2,593 hand-written, weighted criteria — and finding that today's agents clear under ~68% of them, missing on implicit reasoning and multi-document synthesis rather than factual retrieval — is a credible, actionable result that answer-matching benchmarks simply can't produce. The trouble is in the inferences stacked on top of a fairly thin experimental base.

Start with the base: just 101 prompts and only three commercial DR products (OpenAI, Gemini, Perplexity), all closed boxes whose retrieval stacks and orchestration are unknown. The paper's repeated conclusion that failures reflect "fundamental architectural limitations" is drawn from three black-box products with no open-weight or controlled-model variants to isolate what is architectural versus a product/retrieval-config difference. Worse, the judges (GPT-5, Claude Sonnet 4.5, Gemini 2.5 Pro) overlap in family with the systems they grade — the top-scoring Gemini DR is graded partly by Gemini 2.5 Pro, which is also the highest-agreement judge — and the paper never isolates self-preference.

Then the headline metric. Table 5's ranking is reported under ternary grading, yet the paper's own ablation shows ternary aligns with humans ~20 points worse than binary (0.53–0.57 vs 0.72–0.76) and concludes partial credit shouldn't be trusted. So the marquee numbers come from the grading scheme the authors argue against — and Gemini's 0.677 vs OpenAI's 0.664 is a 1.3-point gap reported with no confidence intervals, on a grader that is at best ~76% aligned with humans. The top two are very likely a statistical tie. There is also some over-reach in the discussion ("universal performance collapse" past 4 steps, a "2ⁿ−1 ... theoretical saturation point") that reads as strong generalization from a small set, and an "expert" definition (strong STEM generalists, not per-domain specialists) that is weaker than sibling PRBench's credentialed JD/CFA authors.

baselines

Only 3 commercial DR products (OpenAI / Gemini / Perplexity DR), all closed-box, no open-weight or controlled-model variants; the three LLM judges overlap in model family with the systems they grade.

contamination

Prompts adapted from public forums / Q&A so some topic overlap with training data is likely; rubric grading (vs answer-matching) limits the leakage payoff, but there's no private held-out set, and live-web retrieval makes runs non-reproducible — scores are a snapshot.

what's missed

The headline ranking uses ternary grading, which the paper itself shows is ~20 pts less human-aligned than binary; no confidence intervals, so Gemini 0.677 vs OpenAI 0.664 is plausibly a tie; "expert" authors are STEM generalists, not domain specialists.

would raise confidence

Report CIs and run-to-run web variance; lead with the binary numbers the κ-study actually validates; diversify judges off the evaluated model families; add open-weight / controlled-model coverage; retain a private held-out split.

// bottom line

ResearchRubrics is a genuinely useful, human-grounded answer to a real evaluation gap, and its central finding — DR agents clear under ~68% of expert criteria, failing on implicit reasoning and synthesis, not retrieval — is believable and worth acting on. The softness is everything layered above it: a 1.3-point lead with no error bars, reported on the grading mode the paper's own data says to distrust; three closed systems judged partly by their own families; and "architectural" verdicts inferred from 101 prompts. Trust the failure-mode map; hold the leaderboard order loosely.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. Even the best Deep Research agents clear under 68% of expert criteria — Gemini DR 0.677, OpenAI DR 0.664, Perplexity DR 0.566 (ternary) — with no system topping 70%.
  2. Failures concentrate in implicit reasoning + multi-document synthesis (45–50% of all failures), not explicit retrieval or prose quality — and the pattern is near-identical across all three systems, pointing to architecture over prompt tuning.
  3. It's a fully human-authored benchmark — 101 prompts × 2,593 weighted criteria over 2,800+ hours, plus a tri-axial complexity framework (breadth / nesting depth / exploration); performance degrades monotonically with reasoning depth.
  4. Two rubric-eval lessons: binary grading aligns with humans ~20 points better than ternary partial credit (0.76 vs 0.57 Macro-F1), and LLM-augmenting expert rubrics degrades alignment 15–20% — expert curation can't be auto-expanded.
