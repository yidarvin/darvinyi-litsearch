GPTScore: Evaluate as You Desire

GPTScore turns text evaluation into a likelihood question: instead of training a scorer, it measures how probable a pre-trained language model finds a high-quality output under a natural-language task-and-aspect instruction — tested with 19 backbones from 80M to 175B parameters across 37 datasets and 22 evaluation aspects.

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

By early 2023, automatic text-quality metrics had a rigidity problem. Reference-based metrics like ROUGE and BLEU score one narrow notion of overlap; learned metrics like BERTScore or BARTScore are trained (or fine-tuned) against a fixed target and can't be re-purposed without new annotation. A handful of multi-aspect evaluators — Yuan et al.'s BARTScore, Zhong et al.'s UniEval — go further by scoring several dimensions of quality at once, but the paper argues they still under-specify what each aspect actually means and how aspects relate to each other, and they either bind evaluation to specific metric variants or require supervised training signal to learn a new aspect. Practically, that makes it expensive to stand up an evaluator for a new criterion: someone has to write the labels, then train the model, every time a product or research team needs a new axis of judgment (e.g., "is this response engaging" vs. "is it factually consistent").

The authors' framing is that generative pre-trained models had, by this point, developed exactly the capability needed to sidestep this: zero-shot instruction-following and in-context learning. If a model can be instructed in natural language to generate a high-quality summary, the same instruction ought to let it recognize — without any additional training — how likely a given candidate text is to be a high-quality generation under that instruction. That reframing is the paper's core bet: stop building bespoke scorers and instead read quality off the pre-trained model's own conditional-generation probabilities.

// how it works

The method

GPTScore evaluates a hypothesis text h given a task description d, an aspect definition a, and optional context S (e.g. the source document being summarized) as the summed, per-token log-probability that a generative pre-trained model would have produced h, conditioned on a prompt template T that packages d, a, and S together:

GPTScore(h|d,a,S) = Σt=1m wt log p(ht | h<t, T(d,a,S), θ) // per-token generation probability of the evaluated text, weighted (equal weights in this work) and summed over its m tokens

Concretely, an "evaluation protocol" is assembled from a task specification ("Generate a summary for the following text"), an aspect definition in plain English ("The details provided by the generated text are consistent with the details in the source text" for relevance), and — optionally — a handful of demonstrated input/output pairs for in-context learning. The evaluated sample is slotted into the same template, and the score is simply how likely the model finds the candidate output under that instruction. No gradient step, no labeled training set: the entire "training" is prompt engineering.

The framework is instantiated across four model families spanning nearly three orders of magnitude in scale: GPT2 (355M–1.5B, plus GPT-J-6B), OPT (350M–66B), FLAN-T5 (80M–11B), and GPT3 (the five OpenAI API variants from text-ada-001 at 350M up to text-davinci-001 and text-davinci-003, both 175B). Three settings are compared for each backbone: vanilla (VAL) — no instruction, no demonstration; instruction (IST) — instruction only; and instruction+demonstration (IDM) — instruction plus a handful of few-shot examples appended to the prompt.

Evaluation spans four generation tasks — dialogue response generation (the FED dataset), text summarization (SummEval, RealSumm, Newsroom, QAGS-XSUM), data-to-text generation (BAGEL, SFRES), and machine translation (MQM-2020, the Chinese→English subset of the Multidimensional Quality Metrics collection) — covering 37 datasets and 22 evaluation aspects in total, meta-evaluated against human judgments via Spearman and Pearson correlation.

// what they found

The results

The headline pattern holds across nearly every setting: adding a natural-language instruction reliably improves correlation with human judgment over the vanilla (no-instruction) prompt. On SummEval, averaged across all 19 GPTScore backbone variants, instruction lifts Spearman correlation on every aspect — coherence from 38.0 to 40.2, consistency from 40.4 to 41.4, fluency from 35.8 to 37.2, and relevance from 31.3 to 32.2 — and on the companion RealSumm coverage aspect, from 37.4 to 39.8. Adding few-shot demonstrations on top of instruction (IDM) improves things further on machine translation and data-to-text tasks, though the paper notes diminishing returns past about four demonstrations.

The clearest win over prior automatic metrics is on dialogue. On the FED dataset, the strongest GPTScore variant (GPT3-d01, i.e. text-davinci-001, with instruction) reaches an average Spearman correlation of 54.3 at the dialogue level and 38.3 at the turn level — well above the best prior baseline tested, DynaEval, at 39.8 and 25.6 respectively, and far above reference-based BARTScore variants, some of which score negative correlation on several aspects (e.g. BARTScore+CNN+Para scores −18.9 on coherence at the dialogue level). On individual aspects the gap is larger still: GPT3-d01 hits 56.9 on coherence and 66.9 on dependability versus DynaEval's 43.7 and 49.8.

A striking secondary finding involves the two 175B GPT3 variants. text-davinci-001 (d01) is not RLHF-tuned; text-davinci-003 (d03) is. On FED, d01 outperforms d03 by 40.8 Spearman points at the dialogue level and 5.5 points at the turn level (the paper's own prose actually swaps these two labels when stating the finding, but its Table 6 data — reproduced above — is unambiguous about the direction and magnitude) — the RLHF-tuned model is a noticeably worse evaluator on dialogue quality despite being the more capable generator. The relationship flips on SummEval: there, d03 significantly outperforms d01 on several aspects (e.g. 43.7 vs. 40.0 on coherence, VAL setting). The paper surfaces this inconsistency but does not resolve it, flagging it as a direction needing "deep explorations on the working mechanism of human feedback-based instruction learning."

An ablation over aspect-definition composition on FED's "interesting" (INT) aspect shows that combining correlated aspect definitions helps: scoring with the INT definition alone reaches Spearman 30.8, combining it with "engaging" and "specific" reaches 48.6, and combining five correlated aspects (INT, ENG, SPE, COR, REL) into one prompt reaches 51.4 — already above DynaEval's 41.6 baseline on that dataset. This is presented as evidence that GPTScore's aspect definitions are not independent and that hand-tuning which aspects to combine is itself a lever on performance.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

The dialogue results are genuinely strong and the training-free framing is a real methodological contribution — but the summarization results complicate the "GPTScore just wins" narrative the abstract implies, and the paper's own experimental design leaves some open questions about robustness.

On SummEval/RealSumm, the fine-tuned reference-based baseline BARTScore+CNN+Para — which needs labeled fine-tuning data and is not training-free — still matches or beats GPTScore's best zero/few-shot variants on several aspects: 42.5 on coherence versus GPTScore's IST-setting values, which cluster mostly in the high-30s to mid-40s across backbones (40.1 for GPT3-d01, 47.0 for FT5-XL, the highest of any variant), and 40.9 on the RealSumm coverage aspect versus GPT3-d01's 33.9. The paper's own Table 3 shows this plainly, but the prose foregrounds the dialogue wins and undersells that on summarization specifically, the "no training needed" evaluator is trading blows with — not uniformly beating — a fine-tuned one. A reader skimming the abstract could reasonably conclude GPTScore dominates across the board; it doesn't.

baselines

Mostly fair and contemporaneous (ROUGE, BERTScore, MoverScore, PRISM, BARTScore family, DynaEval) — but no comparison against learned multi-aspect evaluators like UniEval, which the intro explicitly critiques and which was concurrently available; the closest learned competitor tested is BARTScore, a weaker multi-aspect baseline.

contamination

Not discussed at all. GPTScore's score is literally the pre-trained model's log-probability of the reference/candidate text; if any evaluation text (SummEval references, CNN/DailyMail summaries, MQM translations) leaked into GPT3/GPT2/OPT pretraining — plausible for widely mirrored web corpora like CNN/DailyMail — the "likelihood" signal is partly memorization, not quality judgment. The paper never checks this.

what's missed

Cost: the paper admits it "randomly extract[s] and construct[s] sub-datasets for meta-evaluation" specifically "due to the expensive API cost of GPT3" — meaning the headline GPT3 numbers are not even computed on the full evaluation sets, and the practical cost of using 175B-parameter API calls as a routine eval metric is never quantified.

would raise confidence

Full (non-subsampled) evaluation at the dataset level; an explicit contamination check for the reference/generated texts against each backbone's pretraining cut-off; and a mechanistic account of why RLHF (d03) helps on summarization but hurts on dialogue evaluation, rather than flagging it as future work.

// bottom line

The dialogue-evaluation result (54.3 vs. 39.8 Spearman against the strongest baseline) is a real, well-documented win for training-free generative scoring. But the paper's implicit claim of general superiority is weaker than it reads: on summarization the fine-tuned baseline is competitive, the RLHF-vs-non-RLHF finding is left unexplained, and the cost/contamination caveats that would matter for anyone adopting this as a production metric are absent.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. GPTScore reframes evaluation as a likelihood question — score = how probable a frozen pre-trained model finds the candidate text under a natural-language task + aspect instruction — turning "define a new eval criterion" into "write a new sentence" instead of "collect labels and fine-tune."
  2. The dialogue-evaluation numbers are the strongest evidence (54.3 vs. 39.8 average Spearman against the best prior baseline on FED), but on summarization a fine-tuned reference-based baseline (BARTScore+CNN+Para) remains competitive with or ahead of GPTScore on several aspects — the training-free win is task-dependent, not universal.
  3. The non-RLHF GPT3 variant (text-davinci-001) is a markedly better dialogue evaluator than the RLHF-tuned text-davinci-003, while the reverse holds on summarization — an unresolved finding worth watching for anyone using RLHF-tuned models as judges.
