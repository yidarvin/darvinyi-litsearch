LIMA: Less Is More for Alignment

A 65B LLaMA fine-tuned with plain supervised loss on just 1,000 hand-curated prompt-response pairs — no RLHF, no preference modeling — testing whether alignment mostly teaches output style rather than new capabilities.

Chunting Zhou, Pengfei Liu, Puxin Xu, Srini Iyer, Jiao Sun, Yuning Mao, Xuezhe Ma, Avia Efrat, Ping Yu, Lili Yu, Susan Zhang, Gargi Ghosh, Mike Lewis, Luke Zettlemoyer, Omer Levy

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

By mid-2023 the standard alignment recipe had two expensive stages after pretraining: large-scale instruction tuning over multi-million-example datasets (FLAN, T0-style corpora), and then reinforcement learning from human feedback collected over millions of human interactions (InstructGPT, Constitutional AI). Both stages are treated as necessary machinery for turning a raw pretrained model into something that reliably follows instructions and matches user preferences — and both are costly to run at the scale the leading labs run them at.

LIMA asks a sharper question than "how do we make alignment better": does alignment need to teach the model anything new at all, or is a pretrained model's knowledge already sufficient, with alignment just teaching it which subdistribution of output formats to use when talking to a user? If the latter is true — the paper calls this the Superficial Alignment Hypothesis — then a small, carefully curated set of examples should suffice to unlock strong instruction-following, without any RLHF or large-scale tuning data at all. That's a testable, falsifiable claim, and the paper's entire design is built to test it directly rather than just report a leaderboard number.

// how it works

The method

The entire training set is 1,000 examples, split across community-sourced and manually authored data (Table 1 in the paper). 750 come from online Q&A communities: 200 from Stack Exchange's STEM exchanges and 200 from its non-STEM exchanges (sampled with a temperature-3 softening across the 179 exchanges to get more uniform topic coverage, then filtered for length, tone, and self-containedness — no first-person "I"/"my" language, no "as mentioned above" references to other answers), 200 from wikiHow ("How to..." articles used verbatim as prompt/response pairs), and 150 from the r/WritingPrompts subreddit. A further 50 come from Super-Natural Instructions, one random example drawn from each of 50 NLG tasks. The remaining 200 training examples are written from scratch by the paper's own authors (Group A), deliberately optimizing for prompt diversity and a single consistent "helpful AI assistant" response tone. A held-out 50-example dev set (also Group A) is used only for manual checkpoint selection — the authors found validation perplexity doesn't track generation quality. The 300-prompt test set combines 230 prompts from a second author group (Group B) with 70 self-contained r/AskReddit prompts.

Training itself is unremarkable by design — that's the point. A pretrained 65B LLaMA is fine-tuned for 15 epochs with AdamW (β₁=0.9, β₂=0.95, weight decay 0.1), an initial learning rate of 1e-5 linearly decaying to 1e-6 with no warmup, batch size 32, and a 2048-token context limit. The one deliberate deviation from standard practice is residual dropout, linearly increased from p=0.0 at the bottom transformer layer to p=0.3 at the top layer, following Ouyang et al.'s InstructGPT recipe. A special end-of-turn (EOT) token is introduced to separate speaker turns without overloading the pretrained EOS token's existing meaning. No RLHF, no reward model, no preference pairs — just next-token supervised loss on 1,000 conversations.

A footnote easy to miss on first read (footnote 1, Section 2.2): "Despite our efforts to prevent leakage, there was significant contact between the groups before the annotation process, which resulted in certain shared priors that can be observed in the data." Group A wrote the 200 training + 50 dev prompts; Group B wrote 230 of the 300 test prompts. The authors are flagging, in their own words, that the train/test split isn't as independent as the Group A/Group B labels suggest.

// what they found

The results

The core evidence is a pairwise human preference study: for each of 300 test prompts, crowd annotators compare a single LIMA response against a single response from each of five baselines (Alpaca 65B — the same base LLaMA fine-tuned on 52,000 examples; DaVinci003, RLHF-trained; Bard; Claude; GPT-4), all sampled in April 2023, and label a winner or a tie. As the abstract states it: "responses from LIMA are either equivalent or strictly preferred to GPT-4 in 43% of cases; this statistic is as high as 58% when compared to Bard and 65% versus DaVinci003." Despite being trained on 52× less data than Alpaca 65B with the identical base model, LIMA wins or ties Alpaca 53%+21%=74% of the time. GPT-4 itself, used as a parallel annotator with the same instructions, reproduces the same ranking of baselines (Figure 1), and inter-annotator agreement is comparable across the board: crowd-crowd 82%, crowd-author 81%, author-author 78%, and crowd-vs-GPT-4 78%.

On an absolute scale — annotators grading 50 sampled LIMA responses as Fail / Pass / Excellent, independent of any baseline — 50% are rated excellent and LIMA fails to meet the prompt's requirements on only 12% (6 of 50). Of the original 50, only 7 lack a related training example; the authors add 13 more explicitly out-of-distribution prompts to reach 20 total, and on that 20-example OOD set, 45% are still rated excellent and 20% fail — the paper reads this as evidence the model generalizes rather than pattern-matching its 1,000 examples. On 30 safety-adjacent test prompts, LIMA responds safely 80% of the time, including 6 of 10 with explicit malicious intent.

The ablation section is arguably the paper's sharpest result. Holding the base model and hyperparameters fixed, training on 2,000 diverse filtered Stack Exchange examples scores 3.83 on a 6-point ChatGPT-graded helpfulness scale, versus 3.49 for homogeneous wikiHow prompts and 3.33 for unfiltered (lower-quality) Stack Exchange — diversity and quality both move the number, each independently. Scale alone does not: exponentially increasing the Stack Exchange training set from 2K to 32K examples leaves quality essentially flat (~3.8 throughout). Multi-turn dialogue ability, despite zero dialogue examples in the base 1,000, is already present but fragile (6 of 10 live conversations fail within 3 turns); adding just 30 hand-authored dialogue chains (1,030 examples total) raises the excellent-response rate from 45.2% to 76.1% and cuts the failure rate from 15-per-42-turns to 1-per-46.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

Start with the footnote the paper itself surfaces: "there was significant contact between the groups before the annotation process, which resulted in certain shared priors that can be observed in the data." Group A wrote the training and dev prompts; Group B wrote most of the test prompts. If the two groups of authors shared context, jokes, or topical priors before writing supposedly independent prompt sets, the test set is not a clean held-out sample from the same distribution as an arbitrary user's queries — it's biased toward whatever a small, self-selected group of NLP researchers who talk to each other find worth asking. That's a genuinely unusual thing for a paper to admit in a footnote, and it undercuts exactly the claim the eval is built to support: that 1,000 examples generalize to "real user prompts."

Second, the pairwise comparison protocol never mentions randomizing which response is shown first/left versus second/right, or otherwise controlling for response-position bias. This isn't a hypothetical concern for this exact setup — a paper already in this graph, Wang et al.'s "Large Language Models are not Fair Evaluators", shows GPT-4 and ChatGPT judges systematically favor whichever response is shown in a particular position, enough to flip which model "wins" a majority of close pairs. LIMA's GPT-4-as-annotator arm (Figure 2) reproduces the same rankings as the human arm, which the paper reads as corroboration — but if both arms share an un-controlled-for position bias (e.g., baseline responses consistently shown in the same slot), agreement between them doesn't rule that out; it's also consistent with both being biased the same way.

Third, the headline percentages fold "tie" into LIMA's side of the ledger — "equivalent or strictly preferred." That's a defensible modeling choice for a "does LIMA hold its own" framing, but it means the eye-catching "43% vs. GPT-4" number is roughly half ties (18% strict wins + 25% ties = 43%): read narrowly, LIMA outright beats GPT-4 on only 18% of prompts, loses on 57%, and the rest are judged too close to call. Both framings are honest, but only one made the abstract.

baselines

All baseline responses are single-sample, single-decode snapshots from April 2023 — a fair comparison at the time, but the specific proprietary-model win rates (vs. GPT-4, Claude, Bard) are now a stale, one-time snapshot that cannot be reproduced against current models. The same-base-model comparison against Alpaca 65B is the strongest baseline in the paper precisely because it holds architecture and pretraining fixed.

contamination

Not benchmark contamination in the usual sense, but a structurally similar leakage the paper itself flags: "significant contact between the [training-prompt-writing and test-prompt-writing] groups before the annotation process, which resulted in certain shared priors" — an admitted train/test independence violation in the very data the headline preference numbers are computed on.

what's missed

No mention of randomizing or controlling response position/order in the pairwise comparisons — precisely the axis that Wang et al. (also in this graph) later showed can materially swing both human and GPT-4 pairwise judgments. Single-sample generation per prompt per model also means none of the reported win rates carry a variance estimate over decoding stochasticity.

would raise confidence

A fully author-disjoint train/test split (or a quantified estimate of how much the shared-prior contamination affects the win rate); documented position randomization with a reported bias check; and multiple-sample preference judgments to get a variance estimate rather than one generation per model per prompt.

// bottom line

The controlled comparisons that hold the base model fixed — LIMA vs. Alpaca 65B (same LLaMA backbone, 52× less data), and the diversity/quality/quantity ablations — are the paper's most credible evidence for the Superficial Alignment Hypothesis. The showcase numbers against GPT-4/Claude/Bard are weaker: they rest on an eval protocol with an author-admitted train/test leakage footnote and no visible control for position bias, on a set of proprietary baselines that were already stale within a year of publication.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. 1,000 carefully curated examples, no RLHF, produce a 65B model that ties-or-beats Alpaca 65B (52,000 examples, same base LLaMA) 74% of the time — the paper's strongest evidence that prompt diversity and response quality matter more than raw instruction-tuning volume.
  2. The ablations back this up directly: 2,000 diverse, quality-filtered examples score 3.83/6 vs. 3.33 for unfiltered and 3.49 for homogeneous wikiHow prompts, while scaling the same source from 2K to 32K examples leaves quality essentially flat — quantity alone plateaus, diversity and quality don't.
  3. The GPT-4/Claude/Bard head-to-head numbers rest on a footnoted admission of train/test prompt-writer contact ("shared priors that can be observed in the data") and a pairwise protocol with no visible control for response-position bias — a mechanism the FairEval paper (also in this graph) later showed can materially move both human and GPT-4 pairwise judgments.
