Teaching Machines to Read and Comprehend

A recipe for turning a million CNN and Daily Mail news articles into cloze-style reading-comprehension questions — entity-anonymized so a model can't cheat with world knowledge — plus the Attentive and Impatient Reader architectures built to solve them.

What problem is this solving?

By 2015, machine reading comprehension — answering questions about a document's content — had been stuck for years on data scarcity. The only labeled datasets for testing reading comprehension, such as MCTest, ran to a few hundred hand-written question–answer pairs, enough to evaluate a system but not to train one from scratch. Two workarounds had emerged, and both had ceilings. One was to hand-engineer symbolic pipelines — grammars, or information-extraction systems that parsed text into predicate–argument triples and queried them like a relational database — which scale with engineering effort, not data, and cap out on the linguistic phenomena their rules anticipated. The other was to generate synthetic narratives and queries (as in Memory Networks' bAbI tasks), which produce unlimited training data but, as decades of computational-linguistics work on toy grammars had shown, close worlds don't transfer to the noise and richness of real natural language. What was missing was a way to harvest real documents paired with real questions at a scale — hundreds of thousands to millions of examples — large enough to train a statistical or neural model end-to-end, without either the labor of hand-annotation or the unreality of synthetic text.

// how it works

The method

The paper's real contribution is a recipe for manufacturing supervised reading-comprehension data automatically, at the scale of online journalism. CNN and Daily Mail articles are typically accompanied by a handful of human-written bullet-point highlights that abstractively summarize the story — crucially, these highlights paraphrase rather than copy sentences verbatim. The authors turn each highlight into a Cloze-style (fill-in-the-blank) query by picking one named entity in it and replacing it with a placeholder, pairing that query with the full article as context and the removed entity as the answer. Scraping 93k CNN and 220k Daily Mail articles this way yields a combined corpus of roughly one million document–query–answer triples (Table 1: 380,298/3,924/3,198 CNN train/valid/test queries and 879,450/64,835/53,182 for Daily Mail, split by publication date so test articles postdate training articles).

A cloze query alone would let a model cheat: a query like "the hi-tech bra that helps you beat breast X" is answerable as X=cancer from language-model statistics alone, without reading the document at all. To close that shortcut, the authors run a coreference resolver over each document, replace every mention of every entity with an abstract marker (e.g. @entity193), and then randomly re-permute those markers every time an example is loaded during training or testing — so a marker's identity carries no information across examples, and the only way to answer a query is to actually resolve which anonymized entity in this specific document the query refers to. Table 2 in the paper quantifies just how hard this makes the task even for a frequency heuristic: picking the single most-frequent entity marker in a document is correct only 30.5% (CNN) / 25.6% (Daily Mail) of the time.

Having built the data, the authors introduce three neural architectures to solve it, all estimating p(a|d,q) ∝ exp(W(a)·g(d,q)) for a joint document–query embedding g. The Deep LSTM Reader is the simplest: concatenate document and query into one long token sequence (separated by a delimiter) and feed it through a multi-layer LSTM with skip connections, reading off the answer from the final state. The Attentive Reader instead encodes document and query with separate bidirectional LSTMs, then computes a soft attention distribution over document tokens conditioned on the query encoding — the document representation is a weighted sum of token states rather than a single fixed final vector, which lets the model revisit the exact span of the document relevant to the question instead of squeezing 700+ tokens through one bottleneck. The Impatient Reader goes further: instead of attending once after seeing the whole query, it re-reads and updates its running document representation at every query token as the query is consumed left to right, accumulating evidence incrementally.

The results

The neural attention readers beat every symbolic and heuristic baseline the authors tried, on both datasets (Table 5, test-set accuracy). The two hand-engineered NLP baselines — frame-semantic parsing (extracting predicate–argument triples and matching them between query and document) and a bag-of-frequency heuristic — top out at 40.2% (CNN) / 35.5% (Daily Mail). A surprisingly strong word-distance benchmark, which simply aligns the query's placeholder with the nearest lexically-matching context words, reaches 50.9% / 55.5% — evidence that a fair amount of headline-summary lexical overlap survives anonymization. The plain Deep LSTM Reader, with no attention mechanism at all, already clears both of those at 57.0% / 62.2%, and the two attention-based readers open a further gap: the Attentive Reader reaches 63.0% CNN / 69.0% Daily Mail, and the Impatient Reader 63.8% / 68.0% — roughly 23 points above the symbolic frame-semantic baseline on CNN and 33 points above it on Daily Mail. An ablation "Uniform Reader," identical to the Attentive Reader but with its attention weights forced equal, collapses back to 39.4% / 34.4%, isolating attention itself (not just the bidirectional encoder) as the source of the gain.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

The paper's own Table 2 is the first hint that this benchmark measures something narrower than "reading comprehension": simply guessing the single most frequently-mentioned entity in a document is right 30.5% of the time on CNN and 25.6% on Daily Mail, and picking among the top-10 most frequent entities is right 85%+ of the time on both. A model doesn't need to parse the query's semantics to do well here — it needs to find the article's salient entity and check it's plausible given the query, which is a much easier problem than open-ended question answering. That suspicion was confirmed a year later: Chen, Bolton & Manning's "A Thorough Examination of the CNN/Daily Mail Reading Comprehension Task" (2016) hand-annotated a random sample of the CNN test set and found roughly one in four questions were unanswerable or required outside information — many due to coreference-resolution errors baked in during the automatic anonymization pipeline itself, not genuine reading difficulty. Once they excluded those broken examples, a carefully-tuned single-layer attention model (their "Stanford AR") reached an accuracy within a few points of what they estimated as the effective human/data ceiling, meaning the headline gap between symbolic and neural methods this paper reports is real, but the remaining "unsolved" fraction of the task the field kept chasing was mostly noise, not unsolved reasoning.

baselines

The symbolic baselines (frame-semantic parsing, frequency heuristics) are the authors' own hand-tuned rule sets rather than a contemporary state-of-the-art QA pipeline, so the "neural beats symbolic" story is somewhat stacked; the word-distance benchmark is the more informative baseline and it already gets over half the questions right by lexical alignment alone.

contamination

No cross-split leakage in the ordinary sense — training articles run through March 2015 and test articles are drawn only from April 2015 — but the entity-anonymization and permutation scheme introduces its own artifact: correctness partly reflects the quality of the automatic coreference resolver used to build the corpus, not just the reader model being evaluated.

what's missed

A single aggregate accuracy number hides that most of the task is solvable via entity salience alone (Table 2's 85%+ top-10 coverage) and that, per Chen et al. (2016), roughly a quarter of CNN test questions are corrupted by coreference errors or genuinely require information outside the document — neither of which the headline metric distinguishes from a model's actual reading ability.

would raise confidence

A human-annotated audit of a sample of the test set (which the paper doesn't itself provide, but which Chen et al. supplied the following year), reporting accuracy stratified by whether coreference resolution succeeded, and a comparison against an un-anonymized extractive-span task less dependent on a Cloze/entity-marker format.

// bottom line

The entity-anonymized cloze construction was the real invention here — it manufactured a million-scale supervised reading-comprehension corpus overnight and made attention-based neural readers look decisively better than symbolic NLP pipelines. But the anonymization pipeline's own coreference errors and the task's heavy reliance on entity salience meant the ceiling was lower, and reached faster, than the paper implied; within a year Chen et al. (2016) showed a tuned attention model was already near that ceiling, and the field moved to SQuAD's un-anonymized, human-authored span-extraction format as the benchmark that better isolated genuine comprehension from corpus artifacts.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. Turned CNN and Daily Mail articles paired with their bullet-point summaries into roughly one million Cloze-style document–query–answer triples — the first supervised reading-comprehension corpus at a scale large enough to train, not just test, a model.
  2. Introduced coreference-driven entity anonymization plus per-example random permutation specifically to prevent a model from answering via world knowledge or cross-example entity memorization, forcing it to ground every answer in the document at hand.
  3. Introduced the Attentive and Impatient Reader — early neural attention mechanisms conditioned on a query and applied over a document — which beat hand-built symbolic NLP pipelines by roughly 23–33 accuracy points and set the template later QA and machine-reading architectures followed.
  4. The dataset's low effective ceiling, exposed a year later by Chen et al. (2016)'s error analysis, was part of what motivated SQuAD's shift to un-anonymized, human-authored questions over real Wikipedia text.
