Dense Passage Retrieval for Open-Domain Question Answering

Two ordinary BERT encoders, fine-tuned with an "in-batch negatives" loss on only a few thousand question–passage pairs, beat a well-tuned BM25 keyword baseline by 9–19 points on open-domain QA retrieval — and showed dense retrieval needs no exotic pretraining. The result became the default first stage of open-domain QA and seeded the RAG era.

What problem is this solving?

Open-domain question answering answers a factoid question — "Who first voiced Meg on Family Guy?" — using a corpus too large to read end to end. The standard recipe is two stages: a retriever narrows millions of passages down to a handful, then a reader extracts the answer span from that handful. The retriever is the bottleneck: if the answer-bearing passage never makes the shortlist, no reader can recover it. The paper notes that exact-match on SQuAD drops from above 80% to under 40% once you remove the gold paragraph and force the system to find context itself.

For decades that retriever was a sparse term-matching model — TF-IDF or BM25 over an inverted index. It is fast, untrained, and strong, but it matches words, not meaning. Ask "Who is the bad guy in Lord of the Rings?" and a keyword index struggles to surface "Sala Baker is best known for portraying the villain Sauron" — "bad guy" and "villain" share no tokens. Dense retrieval, which maps questions and passages to learned vectors and matches them by inner product, should fix this vocabulary-mismatch problem by design.

The catch: dense retrieval had never beaten BM25 for open-domain QA until ORQA (Lee et al., 2019), and ORQA needed a heavy, specially designed pretraining task — the Inverse Cloze Task — plus a frozen context encoder. The prevailing belief was that learning a good dense representation requires an enormous number of labeled query–passage pairs or exotic pretraining. This paper asks a deliberately simple question: can you train a better dense retriever using only the question–passage pairs already sitting in existing QA datasets, with no extra pretraining at all?

// how it works

The method

DPR is a dual encoder. Two independent BERT-base (uncased) networks map text to 768-dim vectors via the [CLS] token: EQ for questions and EP for passages. Relevance is just the dot product of the two vectors:

sim(q, p) = EQ(q)⊤ EP(p) // decomposable on purpose — so all M passage vectors can be encoded once, offline

The decomposability is the whole point. A richer scorer (cross-attention between question and passage) would be more accurate but couldn't be precomputed — you'd have to re-run the model against all 21 million passages per query. With a dot product, every passage vector is computed once, stored in a FAISS index, and queried at run time with maximum-inner-product search. (Ablations find L2 distance performs comparably and both beat cosine, so they keep the simplest option.)

Training: a contrastive loss

Each training instance is a question q, one relevant positive passage p⁺, and a set of negative passages. The objective is the negative log-likelihood of the positive — pull the positive close, push the negatives away:

L = −log e sim(q, p⁺) e sim(q, p⁺) + Σj e sim(q, p⁻j) // softmax over one positive against its negatives

The trick: in-batch negatives

Where do negatives come from cheaply? DPR reuses the other examples in the mini-batch. Take a batch of B question–passage pairs, stack the question vectors into a B×d matrix Q and the passages into P, and compute one matrix product S = Q P⊤. Now S is a B×B grid of scores: the diagonal entries are the true (question, positive) pairs, and every off-diagonal entry is a "free" negative — passage j paired with question i≠j. One matmul yields B positives and B(B−1) negatives, so a larger batch directly means more negatives, and accuracy rises with batch size.

The best model adds one hard negative per question on top of the in-batch ones: the top passage returned by BM25 that matches the question's words but does not contain the answer. This single BM25 negative — a passage that looks right lexically but isn't — gives the largest single jump in the ablation. Training is otherwise unremarkable: batch size 128, Adam at 1e-5, up to 40 epochs.

The corpus and the reader

The knowledge source is the Dec. 20, 2018 English Wikipedia, cleaned with DrQA's pipeline and split into 21,015,324 disjoint 100-word passages, each prefixed with its article title. Retrieval and the reader are trained separately — a pipeline, not joint training. The reader is a third BERT that, given the top-k retrieved passages, scores each passage and extracts a start/end answer span; the highest-scoring span from the highest-scoring passage is the answer. An appendix ablation shows joint training does not beat this simple pipeline (39.8 EM either way on NQ).

// what they found

The results

On retrieval, DPR beats BM25 across the board except SQuAD. On Natural Questions, top-20 accuracy (the share of questions whose top-20 passages contain the answer) is 78.4% for DPR vs 59.1% for BM25 — a 19.3-point gap — and the abstract reports 9–19 points absolute across datasets. The gap is largest at small k, exactly where it matters for feeding a reader.

Sample efficiency is the headline surprise. A DPR trained on just 1,000 question–passage pairs already outperforms BM25 on Natural Questions, and accuracy keeps climbing from 1k to the full 59k examples (Figure 1). This is what overturns the "dense retrieval needs huge labeled sets or ICT pretraining" assumption.

Better retrieval flows through to better answers. End-to-end exact-match accuracy reaches 41.5 on NQ, beating ORQA (33.3) and the concurrent REALM (39.2 / 40.4) — both of which rely on expensive extra pretraining — and sets a new state of the art on 4 of 5 datasets, by 1 to 12 EM points.

Two more practical findings. Speed: with a FAISS HNSW index, DPR answers 995 questions/second vs BM25/Lucene's 23.7 — roughly 42× faster at query time (though building the dense index is far more expensive; see below). Generalization: a DPR trained only on NQ, applied directly to WebQuestions and CuratedTREC, loses just 3–5 points of top-20 accuracy and still comfortably beats BM25. The one persistent loss is SQuAD, where BM25 wins (68.8 vs 63.2 top-20) — the authors attribute this to SQuAD's high question/passage lexical overlap (annotators wrote questions while looking at the passage) and its narrow base of ~500 articles.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

As an engineering result DPR is rock-solid and reproducible — but the headline "9–19 points better than BM25" deserves three asterisks, all of them about how retrieval quality is measured rather than whether dense retrieval works.

First, the retrieval metric is generous. "Top-k accuracy" counts a hit whenever one of the top-k passages contains the answer string — not whether the passage actually answers the question. A passage can contain "Sauron" or "1991" by coincidence and never be about the question, so the metric overstates true relevance for both retrievers, and possibly asymmetrically. The paper never reports a relevance-judged metric.

Second, SQuAD — the one dataset where DPR loses to BM25 — is explicitly excluded from the combined "multi-dataset" training set as "biased." The explanation (lexical overlap, ~500 source articles) is honest and plausible, but the single benchmark that exposes dense retrieval's weakness on pure keyword-overlap questions is quarantined out of the flagship number. Worse, DPR's positive passages are partly chosen by BM25: for TREC, WebQuestions, and TriviaQA the gold positive is the top BM25 passage that contains the answer, and any question BM25 can't surface in its top 100 is discarded — removing exactly the hardest cases from both training and evaluation.

Third, every test set is English Wikipedia factoid QA, and "cross-dataset generalization" stays inside that family. The out-of-domain story is weaker than it looks: BEIR (Thakur et al., 2021), now a node in this atlas, later showed that DPR-style dense retrievers underperform BM25 zero-shot across heterogeneous IR tasks. And Lewis et al. (2021) found that 60–70% of test answers in NQ/TriviaQA/WebQuestions also appear in the training set, so the end-to-end EM partly measures memorization, not retrieve-then-read — a confound that inflates everyone's numbers and caps what the EM gains say about retrieval.

baselines

BM25 is properly tuned (Lucene, b=0.4, k1=0.9 on dev sets) — a fair, strong sparse baseline, not a strawman. But the "gold" positives are themselves selected by BM25, and questions BM25 can't answer in its top 100 are dropped, quietly favoring the contrast.

contamination

Not examined in the paper. Lewis et al. (2021) later measured 60–70% test/train answer overlap in these open-QA sets, inflating end-to-end EM for both DPR and the baselines.

what's missed

Top-k "accuracy" only checks the answer string appears, not that the passage supports the answer; SQuAD (where BM25 wins) is removed from multi-dataset training; all evaluation is in-domain Wikipedia QA, so zero-shot IR generalization is untested (and BEIR later found it weak).

would raise confidence

A relevance-judged retrieval metric, an out-of-domain retrieval benchmark (later supplied by BEIR), de-duplication of train/test answers, and significance tests on the 1–12 EM gaps.

// bottom line

DPR is a genuinely load-bearing result: a plain dual encoder, an in-batch-negative loss, and a few thousand pairs were enough to retire the "you need ICT-style pretraining" assumption and make dense retrieval the default first stage for open-domain QA and, soon after, RAG. The caveats are about how far to trust the size of the gap, not whether the method works — the answer-string metric and the in-domain-only evals flatter it, and BEIR later showed the zero-shot generalization is weaker than implied. As a recipe it held up completely; as a measurement of "how much better is dense than sparse," read it alongside the SQuAD result and BEIR.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. A simple two-BERT dual encoder, fine-tuned on a few thousand question–passage pairs, beats a well-tuned BM25 by 9–19 points top-20 retrieval accuracy — with no special pretraining, overturning the ORQA/REALM assumption that dense retrieval needs an expensive auxiliary objective.
  2. In-batch negatives are the workhorse: one B×B matmul turns a batch into B positives and B(B−1) free negatives, so bigger batches mean more negatives and higher accuracy. Adding a single BM25 "hard" negative — a lexically plausible passage with no answer — gives the biggest single boost.
  3. Better retrieval translated into better answers: higher top-k passage accuracy yielded new end-to-end exact-match SOTA on 4 of 5 datasets (41.5 EM on NQ), validating the retrieve-then-read pipeline and seeding the RAG era.
  4. The caveats are measurement, not method: the "answer-string-in-top-k" metric is generous, SQuAD (where BM25 still wins) is dropped from the flagship training mix, and BEIR later showed DPR-style retrievers generalize poorly zero-shot out of domain.
