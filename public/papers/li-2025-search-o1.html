Search-o1: Agentic Search-Enhanced Large Reasoning Models

A training-free framework that lets o1-style reasoning models search the web mid-thought — firing a retrieval query whenever the chain of thought hits a knowledge gap, then distilling the noisy pages it gets back into a clean reasoning step before splicing them back in.

Xiaoxi Li, Guanting Dong, Jiajie Jin, Yuyao Zhang, Yujia Zhou, Yutao Zhu, Peitian Zhang, Zhicheng Dou

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

Large reasoning models (LRMs) like OpenAI-o1, QwQ-32B-Preview, and DeepSeek-R1 solve hard problems by generating a long, stepwise internal chain of thought before answering. But that chain of thought only draws on whatever the model memorized during pretraining — and the authors show this runs out fast. Sampling QwQ-32B-Preview on the GPQA diamond set, they count how often the model hedges with words like "perhaps," "alternatively," "wait," and "likely" mid-reasoning: "perhaps" alone shows up an average of 30.4 times per output under direct reasoning. Those hedges are the visible symptom of a knowledge gap the model can't fill on its own, and a wrong guess early in a multi-step chain propagates errors through everything that follows.

The obvious fix — bolt on retrieval-augmented generation — doesn't actually close the gap. Standard, problem-oriented RAG retrieves a fixed batch of documents once, up front, for the whole question. The paper's own numbers show this barely helps and sometimes hurts: on the same uncertainty count, Standard RAG only brings "perhaps" down to 26.4 occurrences, worse than what an agentic approach achieves. The reason is structural — a single upfront retrieval can't anticipate the different, evolving knowledge needs of each individual reasoning step in a long chain, and dumping long, redundant retrieved documents straight into the reasoning stream disrupts the coherence of the chain of thought itself. What's missing is a mechanism that lets the model decide, on its own and repeatedly, exactly when in its reasoning it needs to look something up — and a way to hand back only the distilled fact it asked for, not a pile of raw web pages.

// how it works

The method

Search-o1 requires no additional training — it is a purely inference-time framework wrapped around an existing LRM backbone (QwQ-32B-Preview in all the paper's experiments). It adds two pieces on top of ordinary chain-of-thought decoding: an agentic RAG mechanism that lets the model trigger retrieval whenever it wants, and a Reason-in-Documents module that cleans up what comes back before it re-enters the chain.

Agentic retrieval

While generating its reasoning chain, the model can emit a query wrapped in special tokens, <|begin_search_query|> … <|end_search_query|>. The moment that closing tag appears, generation pauses, the query is extracted and sent to the Bing Web Search API (top-10 results, US-EN region), and full page content is fetched via the Jina Reader API. This can fire — and does, in practice — multiple times within a single reasoning session, so different steps of a long chain can each pull in different knowledge as they need it, unlike a single upfront RAG call.

Reason-in-Documents

Retrieved web pages are long and full of irrelevant text, and the authors find LRMs specifically struggle to fold that raw material into an ongoing reasoning chain without breaking its coherence. So instead of injecting the documents directly, a separate generation pass — using the same backbone model — reads the current search query, the reasoning steps so far, and the retrieved documents, and produces a short, refined statement of just the relevant fact. Only that distilled statement is spliced back into the chain, inside <|begin_search_result|> … <|end_search_result|> tags, and reasoning resumes from there. Formally, the final answer distribution factors as reasoning conditioned on all the refined knowledge injected up to each point:

P(R, a | I, q) = Trt=1Π P(Rₜ | R<ₜ, I, q, {rfinal})  ·  Tat=1Π P(aₜ | a<ₜ, R, I, q) // reasoning tokens Rₜ condition on every refined knowledge snippet {r_final} injected up to that point; the answer aₜ then conditions on the finished chain R

To keep this practical at scale, Search-o1 also uses a batch inference mechanism: many questions run in parallel, and whenever any sequence in the batch needs a search, the queries across the whole batch are extracted and refined together before the batch resumes generating — this is what makes running the framework across thousands of eval questions tractable.

// what they found

The results

On five complex-reasoning domains — GPQA physics/chemistry/biology, MATH500, AMC2023, AIME2024, and LiveCodeBench — Search-o1 beats every baseline built on the same QwQ-32B backbone. GPQA overall Pass@1 goes from 58.1 (direct QwQ-32B, no retrieval) to 63.6 (Search-o1), with MATH500 at 86.4, AMC2023 at 85.0, AIME2024 at 56.7, and LiveCodeBench overall at 33.0. Averaged across all five reasoning datasets, the paper reports Search-o1 exceeding the naive agentic-RAG baseline (RAgent-QwQ-32B) by 4.7% and plain direct reasoning (QwQ-32B) by 3.1%, and beating non-reasoning models Qwen2.5-32B and Llama3.3-70B by 44.7% and 39.3% respectively.

On the GPQA extended set (546 questions), Search-o1's overall score of 57.9 beats the overall accuracy of every human-expert group tested — physicists (39.9 overall), chemists (48.9), and biologists (37.2) — and Search-o1 even beats physicists and biologists in their own subject (68.7 vs. 57.9 physics; 69.5 vs. 68.9 biology). The one domain where it doesn't win is chemistry, where Search-o1 scores 40.7 against chemists' own 72.6.

On open-domain QA, the gains concentrate almost entirely in multi-hop tasks, which is exactly what the method targets. On single-hop QA (NQ, TriviaQA), agentic retrieval barely moves the needle over standard RAG (47.8 vs. 47.6 average EM on QwQ) — a single-hop question only ever needs one lookup, so there's little for the agentic loop to do. On multi-hop QA, Search-o1 reaches EM/F1 of 45.2/57.3 on HotpotQA, 58.0/71.4 on 2WikiMultihopQA, 16.6/28.2 on MuSiQue, and 56.0/67.8 on Bamboogle, exceeding standard RAG-QwQ-32B and naive agentic RAgent-QwQ-32B by 29.6% and 5.3% respectively on average multi-hop EM.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

The comparisons that matter most — Search-o1 vs. Direct Reasoning, Standard RAG, and RAgent, all on the QwQ-32B backbone — are apples-to-apples, since all four share the same base model and the authors ran them all themselves. That isolates the contribution of the agentic loop and the Reason-in-Documents module cleanly. The closed-source rows (GPT-4o, o1-preview, DeepSeek-R1-Lite) are a different story: the paper states plainly that "closed-source model results are sourced from their official releases" — not reproduced under the paper's own harness, so they're context, not a controlled comparison.

The retrieval setup is the part that most needs scrutiny. Search-o1 hits live Bing Web Search at evaluation time — not a frozen, snapshot corpus — for benchmarks built from Wikipedia (NQ, TriviaQA, HotpotQA, 2WikiMultihopQA) that have been public online for years. Nothing in the paper filters search results for pages that discuss the benchmark itself (leaderboards, blog posts, cached dataset dumps), so part of the multi-hop QA gain could in principle come from the live web surfacing more direct answer text than a static corpus would, rather than purely from better agentic reasoning. LiveCodeBench sidesteps this by restricting to problems from August–November 2024 specifically to avoid contamination — a deliberate freshness guard the QA benchmarks don't get.

One number worth flagging on its own: standard RAG-QwQ-32B actually beats Search-o1 on TriviaQA specifically (65.6/77.6 EM/F1 vs. Search-o1's 63.4/74.1) — a reminder that the multi-hop story doesn't generalize to every single-hop case, even though the paper's framing emphasizes the wins.

baselines

Fair and controlled where it counts (same-backbone ablation chain); the closed-model rows are borrowed numbers from other papers' harnesses, not reproduced.

contamination

Live Bing search at eval time against Wikipedia-derived QA sets with no filtering for benchmark-discussing pages; LiveCodeBench gets an explicit freshness cutoff, the QA sets don't.

what's missed

No report of added latency/API cost from the extra search + Reason-in-Documents round trips, and no measurement of how often the paper's own "back-off strategy" — falling back to the result from direct reasoning whenever a final answer isn't produced — actually fires; that backoff could be silently masking search-loop failures.

would raise confidence

Reproducing the closed-model baselines under the same live-retrieval harness; reporting backoff-trigger rates; and testing on QA sets curated to postdate the search index snapshot to rule out search-time leakage.

// bottom line

Search-o1 is a clean, training-free demonstration that an o1-style model deciding for itself when to search — and getting back a distilled fact instead of a raw document — beats both no-retrieval and naive single-shot RAG, especially on multi-step science, math, and code reasoning. But the live-web retrieval setup means part of its open-domain QA edge is hard to fully disentangle from search-time leakage, and the paper doesn't quantify how often its own search loop fails silently into the fallback.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. Search-o1 needs no fine-tuning — it's a prompting/inference framework wrapped around an off-the-shelf LRM (QwQ-32B-Preview), triggered purely by special search tokens the model is instructed to emit.
  2. The Reason-in-Documents module — a second generation pass that condenses retrieved pages before they re-enter the chain — is what separates Search-o1 from naive agentic RAG (RAgent), and it accounts for real headroom beyond agentic retrieval alone (63.6 vs. 61.6 GPQA overall).
  3. Gains concentrate in multi-step reasoning and multi-hop QA, not single-hop QA — a sign the framework's real edge is repeated, step-specific retrieval, not retrieval itself.
  4. On the GPQA extended set, Search-o1 (57.9 overall) beats every human-expert group's overall accuracy, and even beats physicists and biologists within their own subject — but it still trails chemists specifically in chemistry (40.7 vs. 72.6), so reasoning-plus-search isn't uniformly superhuman.
