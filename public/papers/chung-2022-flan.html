Scaling Instruction-Finetuned Language Models

Take a raw pretrained model, finetune it on 1,836 tasks phrased as instructions — scaling the task count, the model size, and crucially mixing in chain-of-thought data — and you get the "Flan" recipe: +9.4% average on held-out benchmarks, a new 75.2% on MMLU, and a model that actually answers questions instead of completing them. All for 0.2% of pretraining compute.

Hyung Won Chung, Le Hou, Shayne Longpre, Barret Zoph, Yi Tay, William Fedus, … Quoc V. Le, Jason Wei · Google

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

A model trained only to predict the next token is a strange artifact. PaLM 540B "knows" an enormous amount, yet ask it a plain question zero-shot and it will often continue your prompt instead of answering it, repeat the input with minor edits, or never stop generating — it was never given an end-of-sequence signal or a reason to treat your text as a request. Closing that gap usually meant prompt engineering: hand-crafting few-shot exemplars until the model fell into the right behavior.

Instruction finetuning — finetuning on many datasets reworded as natural-language instructions (FLAN, T0, InstructGPT) — was the known fix, but by late 2022 it had only been explored at the edges. Prior work had scaled the number of templates, or the number of tasks (to ~1.6K, but on a 3B model), or the model (to 137B, but on only 62 tasks) — never all at once. And a separate, awkward finding lurked underneath: instruction finetuning as practiced only ever evaluated held-out NLP tasks in the same answer-directly format it trained on. Nobody had asked what it does to multi-step reasoning, the capability chain-of-thought prompting had just shown large models possess.

The paper's bet is that pushing instruction finetuning on three axes simultaneously — (1) number of tasks, (2) model size, and (3) including chain-of-thought (CoT) data in the mixture — is not three incremental tweaks but a single, cheap, general recipe that improves performance, reasoning, and usability across model families at once.

// how it works

The method

Flan ("Finetuning language models") is a finetuning procedure, not a single model — you prepend "Flan-" to whatever base you apply it to (Flan-PaLM, Flan-T5, Flan-U-PaLM). The recipe has three moving parts.

1 · A very large task mixture

They combine four mixtures from prior work into 1,836 finetuning tasks spanning 473 datasets and 146 task categories: Muffin (80 tasks, incl. new dialog and program-synthesis data), T0-SF (193 tasks from T0, "sans Flan"), NIV2 (1,554 tasks from Super-NaturalInstructions), and a small but pivotal CoT mixture of just nine reasoning datasets with hand-written chain-of-thought annotations. (They scrub 44 MMLU-related tasks out of NIV2 so the held-out benchmark stays held-out.)

2 · Four prompting formats, mixed

Every task is rendered with and without exemplars (few-shot vs. zero-shot) and, for the CoT data, with and without a chain-of-thought. Training on this Cartesian product of formats is what lets a single checkpoint later do zero-shot, few-shot, and CoT prompting — rather than being good at only the format it saw.

3 · Apply it broadly, and cheaply

The same procedure is run across model families and sizes: T5 (encoder-decoder, span corruption) from 80M to 11B, PaLM (decoder-only, causal LM) at 8B/62B/540B, and U-PaLM. The striking part is the cost. Finetuning is a rounding error next to pretraining — Flan-PaLM 540B uses only 0.2% of the pretraining compute (≈512 v4 TPU chips for 37 hours; 1.4B finetuning tokens against 780B pretraining tokens). Instruction finetuning buys a large capability jump without touching inference cost or training a new model from scratch.

Every figure in the paper reports a single aggregate, the normalized average across six held-out scores (MMLU-Direct, MMLU-CoT, BBH-Direct, BBH-CoT, TyDiQA, MGSM-CoT), where each raw score is rescaled against its random-guess floor — so a multiple-choice task at chance reads as 0, not 25:

norm = raw − random100 − random × 100 // a 55% raw score on a 4-way MC task (random = 25) → norm ≈ 40; a below-chance score goes negative
// what they found

The results

Both knobs help, monotonically. Flan-PaLM 540B beats PaLM 540B by +9.4% normalized average on the held-out suite; the smaller models gain even more in absolute terms (+10.4% at 62B, +15.5% at 8B). But the task-scaling curve flattens fast: most of the gain arrives by ~282 tasks, and the jump from 282 to 1,836 tasks is small. The authors' read is that instruction finetuning mostly teaches the model to express knowledge it already has from pretraining, rather than installing new knowledge — consistent with finetuning touching only 0.2% as many tokens as pretraining.

The chain-of-thought twist

This is the paper's most consequential finding. Instruction finetuning on non-CoT data alone degrades reasoning — a finetuned model gets worse at chain-of-thought than the base model. Adding just nine CoT datasets to the mixture reverses the damage and lifts every evaluation, CoT and non-CoT alike. The interpretation: instruction finetuning only improves unseen tasks that share the prompting paradigm of the finetuning data, so you need both formats in the mix to keep a single checkpoint strong everywhere.

It also unlocks zero-shot reasoning. After CoT finetuning, Flan-PaLM responds to a bare "let's think step-by-step" on the 23 challenging BBH tasks; PaLM without finetuning does not.

Combine CoT with self-consistency (SC) and Flan-PaLM sets new state of the art on several benchmarks — headlined by 75.2% on five-shot MMLU (vs. PaLM 69.3%, code-davinci-002 68.3%, Chinchilla 67.6%). On the multilingual math benchmark MGSM it reaches 72.0% with CoT+SC, even hitting 69.6% on Bengali, a low-resource language. The apples-to-apples gap over PaLM is real at every cell:

Generality and usability

The recipe transfers across architectures, sizes, and pretraining objectives. Flan-T5-XL is only 3B parameters yet scores 52.4% on MMLU — above GPT-3 175B's 43.9%; Flan-T5 models (encoder-decoder) gain the most of any family (+26.6 normalized at 11B). Flan-PaLM 62B beats the un-finetuned PaLM 540B on TyDiQA (58.7 vs. 52.9 EM). And instruction finetuning stacks with other adaptation tricks: Flan-U-PaLM (Flan + UL2 continued pretraining) is the single strongest model in the paper at 59.1 normalized average.

Beyond benchmarks, a human evaluation on 190 open-ended prompts found Flan-PaLM's answers preferred over PaLM's 79% of the time, with the margin widening another ~10% on prompts using a CoT trigger and no regression on few-shot. On the Responsible-AI side, Flan models produce toxic continuations less often than PaLM at every scale on RealToxicityPrompts (though Flan-T5-XXL behaves worse at high prompt toxicity).

// does it hold up?

The eval read

The core claims are unusually robust because the comparisons are clean: Flan-X vs. X on benchmarks held out of finetuning, run across many model families. The "instruction finetuning works, scales, and is cheap" story is hard to argue with. But the headline framing rewards a careful read.

First, the SOTA numbers lean on chain-of-thought + self-consistency, which samples and majority-votes many reasoning chains — a far heavier inference budget than a single forward pass. The paper is honest enough to print the matched comparison (PaLM also with CoT+SC), and the gap survives: 75.2 vs. 69.5 on MMLU is +5.7, not the "75.2 vs. 43.9 GPT-3" gulf a casual reader might infer. The bigger asterisk is GSM8K: the much-quoted 83.9% "new SOTA" is on a dataset whose training split is inside the finetuning mixture — held-in, not a generalization result. The authors flag this in a sentence; it deserves more weight.

Second, contamination is handled by deferral, not analysis. They remove 44 MMLU-related NIV2 tasks and otherwise cite the PaLM paper's contamination study rather than re-checking the 1,836-task mixture against the held-out benchmarks themselves — reasonable, but a fresh check would close the loop. Third, the marquee usability result rests on a thin human eval: 190 in-house prompts, each scored by one rater, with no inter-annotator agreement reported — a 79% preference is suggestive, not statistically nailed down. Finally, the custom normalized-average aggregate, while sensible, can blur which benchmarks actually move; and the "most gains by 282 tasks" curve quietly undercuts the paper's own title — beyond a few hundred tasks, more data is teaching format, not capability.

baselines

Strong and matched — every result is Flan-X vs. the same base X, plus contemporaneous SOTA (Chinchilla, code-davinci-002). No head-to-head against RLHF-tuned models (InstructGPT) on the same prompts, though.

contamination

Held-out benchmarks are kept out of finetuning (44 MMLU tasks scrubbed), but pretraining-contamination is inherited from the PaLM paper, not re-run. GSM8K's 83.9% is held-in by their own admission.

what's missed

SC inference cost isn't accounted in the headline numbers; the human eval uses one rater per pair with no agreement stats; no factuality/forgetting audit beyond the CoT-degradation result.

would raise confidence

Multi-rater blind preference study; a fresh contamination scan of the 1.8K mixture; reporting single-pass (no-SC) numbers alongside the SC headlines; an RLHF baseline on the open-ended set.

// bottom line

The findings hold — and the CoT-data result (finetuning on non-CoT alone breaks reasoning; nine CoT datasets fix it) is the durable, non-obvious contribution. Just read the SOTA headlines with their inference budget and their one held-in benchmark in mind.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. Flan is the canonical SFT recipe. Instruction-finetune on a large, multi-format task mixture and a raw pretrained model becomes usable — for ~0.2% of pretraining compute. This is the "instruction tuning" half of the SFT→RLHF pipeline every later model assumes.
  2. Mix in chain-of-thought or you lose reasoning. Finetuning on non-CoT data alone actively degrades CoT ability; adding just nine CoT datasets restores it and lifts everything — a single checkpoint that does zero-shot, few-shot, and step-by-step reasoning.
  3. Task scaling saturates; model scaling doesn't. Most of the benefit lands by ~282 tasks — instruction finetuning largely surfaces knowledge already in the weights rather than adding new knowledge, which is why a 3B Flan-T5 can out-score GPT-3 175B on MMLU.
