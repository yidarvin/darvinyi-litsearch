MuJoCo: A Physics Engine for Model-Based Control

A physics simulator built for control research, not graphics. Instead of the penalty-based spring-damper contacts games use, or the brittle, non-convex complementarity solvers robotics used, MuJoCo represents multi-body systems in generalized (joint) coordinates and resolves contact through a convex, soft constraint model — fast enough to run thousands of rollouts per second, and it became the physics substrate underneath a decade of deep-RL continuous-control research, from TRPO and PPO to Soft Actor-Critic and TD3.

Todorov, Erez, Tassa

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

By 2012, general-purpose physics engines were mature — ODE, Bullet, PhysX, Havok — but every one of them had been built for games and graphics, where the standard is "looks plausible at 60Hz," not "is numerically trustworthy enough to differentiate through or trajectory-optimize against." That design target shows up in the contact model. Most engines use penalty-based (spring-damper) contacts: a stiff virtual spring pushes interpenetrating bodies apart. Stiff springs need very small integration time steps to stay stable, which is fine for a real-time game loop but expensive when a control algorithm needs to run the simulator thousands of times per optimization step. The alternative — hard-contact solvers based on the linear complementarity problem (LCP) — model contact more physically, but the resulting problem is non-convex for systems with friction, hard to solve robustly and efficiently at scale, and its solutions aren't smooth, which is exactly what breaks gradient-based and trajectory-optimization methods.

Emo Todorov's group at the University of Washington was not doing graphics — it was doing model-based control: trajectory optimization, differential dynamic programming, optimal control of humanoid and robotic-hand models with dozens of degrees of freedom and frequent, changing contact. That line of work needed something none of the existing engines were built to give it: a simulator fast enough for thousands of rollouts, expressive enough for general articulated systems, and numerically well-behaved enough that a controller could actually rely on — and even invert — its dynamics. Handcrafting per-model contact stiffness parameters to make a game-physics engine behave was, at the scale their research needed, not viable.

// how it works

The method

MuJoCo — Multi-Joint dynamics with Contact — makes two coupled design choices, and the paper's real contribution is making them work together.

First, generalized coordinates. Instead of representing every rigid body independently in Cartesian ("maximal") coordinates and then imposing joint constraints to hold an articulated structure together — the approach most game-physics engines take — MuJoCo represents a mechanism directly in its own n joint degrees of freedom. A humanoid with 30 hinge/ball joints is a 30-dimensional system, not a 6×(number of bodies)-dimensional one with dozens of constraint equations bolted on. This is the classical articulated-body approach (Featherstone's recursive O(n) algorithms, among the paper's own references), and it buys smaller state, faster unconstrained dynamics, and no constraint drift between joints — but it makes contact harder, because a contact between two links doesn't align with either body's joint axes.

M(q) q̈ + c(q, q̇) = τ + J(q)Tf // equations of motion in generalized coordinates: joint-space inertia M, bias forces c (Coriolis/centrifugal/gravity), applied torque τ, and constraint/contact force f mapped back into joint space through the constraint Jacobian J

Second — and this is the paper's central idea — contact and constraint forces f are found by solving a convex optimization problem at every timestep, rather than the classical linear complementarity problem (LCP). Hard-contact LCP formulations are physically cleaner but, once Coulomb friction is included, non-convex: there's no guarantee of a unique solution, and the numerics are fragile. The alternative most engines fall back on — soft penalty springs at each contact — sidesteps the non-convexity but forces very small integration steps to stay stable. MuJoCo's contact model, built on the "modern velocity-stepping" line of work (Stewart & Trinkle's implicit time-stepping scheme and Todorov's own prior work on implicit nonlinear complementarity are both in its reference list), reformulates the problem as convex, soft, and analytically invertible: soft enough to remain well-conditioned at large timesteps without hand-tuned stiffness, convex enough to solve reliably and fast, yet still respecting a genuine friction cone rather than treating friction as an afterthought.

The payoff of convexity is a capability most contact simulators don't have at all: inverse dynamics that stay well-defined in the presence of contact. Given a desired trajectory, MuJoCo can compute the generalized forces that would produce it — including the contact forces — because the convex formulation pins down a unique, differentiable answer where a hard-contact LCP model generally cannot. That is exactly the primitive that trajectory-optimization and differential-dynamic-programming controllers need, and it's the reason a control lab, rather than a graphics lab, is the one that built this.

Models are authored in an XML format (MJCF) or a C++ API, then run through a built-in compiler that lowers the description into an optimized runtime data structure — separating "describe the mechanism" from "simulate it fast," so the same model definition can drive both forward simulation and the inverse-dynamics computation above.

// what they found

The results

This is an 8-page systems paper, not a benchmark paper, and its real evidence is what got built on it in the same year and every year since. The companion paper from the same lab, Tassa, Erez & Todorov's "Synthesis and stabilization of complex behaviors through online trajectory optimization" — also IROS 2012 — used MuJoCo to run online, real-time trajectory optimization (receding-horizon differential dynamic programming) on a simulated humanoid, closing the control loop fast enough to reject pushes and disturbances live. That demonstration only works if the simulator is fast and numerically well-behaved enough to be re-solved inside the control loop itself — the exact property the convex contact model was built to deliver, and direct evidence it delivered it.

The longer-run evidence is adoption. MuJoCo went on to become the default physics substrate for continuous-control deep reinforcement learning: it underlies Schulman et al.'s TRPO and Generalized Advantage Estimation, Duan et al.'s "Benchmarking Deep Reinforcement Learning for Continuous Control" (which established the HalfCheetah / Hopper / Walker2d / Ant / Humanoid task family still used today), PPO, Christiano et al.'s Deep RL from Human Preferences, Soft Actor-Critic, and TD3 — and its own authors later built OpenAI Gym's continuous-control tasks and the DeepMind Control Suite directly on top of it, standardizing the benchmark family the rest of the field measures against. As of this writing the paper itself sits at 7,527 citations (Semantic Scholar) — a number that understates its footprint, since most papers that use MuJoCo through Gym or the Control Suite cite those wrapper papers rather than the underlying engine.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

MuJoCo isn't a benchmark or a claimed result — it's infrastructure that an entire evaluation regime was built on top of, which makes the right critique a different one: what does it mean that "continuous control" in deep RL has meant, almost without exception, "control inside one specific engine's contact model"?

The field's own reproducibility literature answered part of this directly. Henderson et al.'s widely-cited "Deep Reinforcement Learning that Matters" (2018) showed that headline continuous-control results were highly sensitive to implementation details, random seeds, and — pointedly — which version of MuJoCo and which wrapper library an experiment used, sometimes changing which algorithm "won." That's not a flaw in MuJoCo's contact model so much as a consequence of monoculture: when nearly every continuous-control paper for a decade ran on the same engine with the same handful of task definitions, engine-specific numerical quirks (contact timestep, joint damping defaults, reward-shaping choices baked into the task wrappers) became indistinguishable from genuine algorithmic progress. A policy that exploits a MuJoCo Hopper's exact contact restitution isn't demonstrating a generalizable control competence — and because so few alternative engines were in wide use, that distinction was hard to see from inside the field.

Reproducibility also had a mundane, non-scientific obstacle: for its first nine years MuJoCo was closed-source and commercially licensed (free for students/personal use, paid otherwise, distributed by Todorov's own Roboti LLC), which meant license keys and per-machine activation sat between a paper and someone else re-running it. DeepMind acquired the software and made it free and open-source under Apache 2.0 in 2021, removing that friction — but by then a decade of published continuous-control results had already been produced under license-gated access, with all the reproducibility drag that implies. Separately, since MuJoCo policies are trained entirely against a simulated contact model, the well-known sim-to-real gap — a policy that's optimal against MuJoCo's specific friction/restitution parameters can fail on a physical robot whose true contact dynamics differ — is a limitation of any simulator-first control pipeline, not specific to this one, but MuJoCo's ubiquity means it's the gap nearly the whole continuous-control RL literature inherited.

baselines

Not applicable in the usual sense — this is a systems/infrastructure paper with no learned baseline to beat. The closest thing to a baseline comparison is implicit: existing engines (ODE, Bullet, PhysX, Havok) built for graphics rather than control, which the paper argues are unsuited to the target use case rather than benchmarked head-to-head on a shared task.

contamination

Not a meaningful category for a physics engine. The adjacent risk is monoculture, not contamination: because so much continuous-control RL ran on the same engine and the same handful of MuJoCo task definitions, "beats the state of the art on Hopper/Walker2d" can conflate genuine control progress with exploiting that one engine's specific numerics.

what's missed

The paper doesn't (and couldn't) anticipate that it would become the near-universal substrate for an entire subfield's empirical claims — with the reproducibility consequences (engine-version sensitivity, license-gated access) that later surfaced in the field's own self-critique.

would raise confidence

For the engine itself: independent validation against real robot hardware on the exact contact-rich tasks it's used for. For the field built on top of it: the sim-to-sim and sim-to-real generalization checks (does a policy trained in MuJoCo transfer to a different engine, or to hardware?) that most continuous-control RL papers still don't run.

// bottom line

The core engineering claim — a convex, generalized-coordinates contact model fast and stable enough for real-time trajectory optimization and thousands of rollouts — held up and then some: it's still, in substance, the same approach the open-source MuJoCo ships today. The less flattering legacy is what the field did with that ubiquity: a decade of "continuous control" results measured almost exclusively inside one engine's numerics, a monoculture the field's own reproducibility crisis eventually had to reckon with.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. Two ideas, one goal. Generalized (joint) coordinates keep an articulated system's state small and constraint-free between joints; a convex, soft-but-friction-respecting contact model keeps the resulting dynamics fast, stable at large timesteps, and — uniquely — invertible even with contact. Neither idea is new alone; combining them for control (not graphics) is the contribution.
  2. It's the reason "continuous control" means what it means today. TRPO, GAE, PPO, Soft Actor-Critic, TD3, OpenAI Gym's control tasks, and the DeepMind Control Suite all sit downstream of this engine — a systems paper that became load-bearing infrastructure for an entire subfield's evaluation practice.
  3. Ubiquity has a cost. A decade of results measured almost exclusively inside one engine's specific numerics fed directly into the field's own reproducibility crisis (implementation- and version-sensitive "wins"), and the software stayed commercially licensed for nine years before DeepMind open-sourced it in 2021 — two separate frictions on top of an otherwise durable technical contribution.
