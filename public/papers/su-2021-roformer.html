RoFormer: Enhanced Transformer with Rotary Position Embedding

A rotation matrix that encodes a token's absolute position while making the query–key inner product depend only on relative position — a multiplicative alternative to additive position embeddings that turned out to be the positional scheme nearly every subsequent open LLM would adopt.

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

By 2021 there were two families of position encoding for transformers, and both had the same structural flaw. Absolute encodings — the sinusoidal table from the original Attention Is All You Need, or the learned position vectors in BERT, GPT-2, and ALBERT — add a position vector pᵢ directly to the token's word embedding before it is projected into queries and keys. Relative encodings, from Shaw et al.'s clipped relative embeddings through Transformer-XL, XLNet, T5, and DeBERTa, instead inject a relative-distance term into the expanded query–key product. Both strategies work, and the paper's related-work section walks through nine of them, but the authors' point is narrower than "relative is better than absolute": every one of these schemes adds position information into the context representation, which means the position term has to be carried through the same additive expansion as the content term.

That additive structure is fine for standard softmax attention, but it breaks down for linear attention (Performer, linear transformers), whose whole efficiency argument rests on being able to factor the attention computation via the associativity of matrix multiplication. An additive position bias doesn't factor the same way, so none of the existing relative-position schemes could be dropped into a linear-attention model without giving up its linear-time property. The gap RoFormer targets is a position encoding that (a) captures relative position the way Shaw et al. and T5 do, (b) falls out of a well-motivated absolute encoding rather than being bolted on as a correction term, and (c) is multiplicative rather than additive, so it survives the algebra that makes linear attention linear.

// how it works

The method

The derivation starts from a single constraint: find functions f_q(x_m, m) and f_k(x_n, n) such that the inner product ⟨f_q(x_m, m), f_k(x_n, n)⟩ depends on the token contents x_m, x_n and on the positions only through their difference m − n. In the 2D case, the authors show (Section 3.4.1, via the complex-number form of a 2D vector) that the solution is to encode position by multiplying the query/key vector by e^{imθ} — i.e., rotating it by an angle proportional to its position index:

fq(xm, m) = (Wqxm)eimθ    fk(xn, n) = (Wkxn)einθ // rotating each vector by its own absolute position; the inner product's phase term becomes e^{i(m−n)θ} — relative position, for free

Because rotating two vectors by mθ and nθ and then taking their inner product leaves only the difference in rotation angle (m − n)θ, relative-position dependence emerges automatically from what is mechanically an absolute-position operation — no separate relative-position term needs to be added anywhere. To generalize past d = 2, the paper splits the d-dimensional embedding into d/2 independent 2D sub-spaces and rotates each sub-space by its own frequency θᵢ = 10000⁻²⁽ⁱ⁻¹⁾ᐟᵈ — the same geometric frequency schedule Vaswani et al. used for the sinusoidal table, just applied as a rotation of the vector rather than as an additive offset. The resulting R^d_{Θ,m} is a block-diagonal orthogonal matrix (Eq. 15), so the transform is norm-preserving and numerically stable by construction.

Two properties fall out of this construction that the paper spends its theory section on. First, long-term decay: with θᵢ set to the same geometric schedule as the sinusoidal table, the upper bound on the inner product's magnitude provably shrinks as the relative distance |m − n| grows (Section 3.4.3), so token pairs far apart contribute less to attention — a soft recency bias that isn't imposed by any explicit windowing or clipping, unlike Shaw et al.'s hard-clipped relative distance. Second, compatibility with linear attention: because the rotation matrix is norm-preserving, it can be applied directly to the non-negative feature maps φ(q), φ(k) that linear-attention methods substitute for softmax, without disturbing the associativity that makes those methods linear-time (Eq. 19). This is the property additive schemes structurally cannot offer, and it's the paper's central argument for why RoPE is the right primitive rather than merely another relative-position variant.

// what they found

The results

The paper runs four separate experiments, each swapping RoPE into a different baseline architecture. On WMT 2014 English→German machine translation, a fairseq Transformer-base with RoPE in place of the sinusoidal table scores 27.5 BLEU vs. 27.3 for the vanilla baseline — a real but modest 0.2-point gain, from a single run with no reported variance across seeds.

The more informative ablation swaps RoPE into BERT's pretraining pipeline, holding data (BookCorpus + Wikipedia), batch size (64), sequence length (512), and steps (100k) fixed and changing only the position embedding. RoPE converges to a lower masked-language-modeling loss throughout training (Figure 3, left), and the same pattern holds when RoPE is added to Performer's linear attention on the Enwik8 language-modeling task (Figure 3, right) — RoPE reaches lower loss than Performer without it, under identical hyperparameters.

The same pretrained RoFormer is then fine-tuned across six GLUE tasks against a same-recipe BERT baseline:

RoFormer wins on MRPC, STS-B, and QQP (the QQP jump — 71.2 → 86.4 — is the largest margin in the table) but loses on SST-2, QNLI, and MNLI. The paper's own framing is "RoFormer can significantly outperform BERT in three out of six datasets, and the improvements are considerable" — true as stated, but it is exactly half the benchmark, and the abstract's broader claim that RoFormer "consistently overcomes its alternatives" doesn't fully square with a 3-3 split with real losses on the other side (SST-2 drops 2.8 points, MNLI-m drops 4.4).

The long-text result — the one most cited by later work — comes from Chinese data. RoFormer, WoBERT, BERT, and NEZHA are pretrained on ~34GB of Chinese Wikipedia/news/forum text and evaluated on CAIL2019-SCM, a legal similar-case-matching task built from 8,964 triplets of Supreme People's Court case descriptions, most of them longer than 512 characters:

// does it hold up?

The eval read

The cleanest experiment in the paper is also the least flattering to the abstract's framing. The BERT-vs-RoFormer GLUE ablation is genuinely controlled — same pretraining corpus, same steps, same fine-tuning budget, position embedding as the only changed variable — which makes it the paper's most trustworthy result. And what it shows is a 3-3 split, not the "consistently overcomes its alternatives" the abstract promises. RoFormer's biggest single win, QQP (71.2 → 86.4), is large enough to be suspicious on its own — a 15-point jump from changing only the position embedding, on a paraphrase-detection task with no obvious dependence on long-range position modeling, is the kind of outlier that would normally prompt a re-run or an explanation, and the paper offers neither.

The Chinese long-text result is the one that mattered most for RoFormer's adoption (it's the empirical basis for "RoPE helps with long context"), and it's also the least clean. Table 3 shows RoFormer shares word-level tokenization with WoBERT but not with BERT or NEZHA, which are character-level — so the RoFormer-vs-BERT comparison on CAIL2019-SCM confounds the position-embedding change with a tokenization-granularity change, and only the RoFormer-vs-WoBERT comparison actually isolates RoPE. At the matched 512-length cutoff that comparison shows RoFormer barely ahead of WoBERT (68.29% vs. 68.10% test accuracy) — not the decisive win the paper's narrative implies. The move to 1024 length does help, but there's no WoBERT-1024 or BERT-1024 row in Table 5, so the claimed "absolute improvement of 1.5%" over WoBERT has no matched baseline in the paper to verify it against, and no single-position-embedding-length ablation isolates whether the gain comes from RoPE specifically or just from more context regardless of encoding.

The authors' own two-bullet "Limitations of the work" section (4.5.5) is unusually candid for a paper this influential: on why RoFormer converges faster during pretraining, they write "there lacks of thorough explanations on why it converges faster than baseline models"; on why it outperforms peer models on long text, they go further and say "we have not come up with a faithful explanation". Those are the paper's two headline empirical claims, and the authors are explicit, in their own words, that the mechanism behind both is unexplained. That's a genuine gap between what's demonstrated (RoPE helps, empirically, in these specific ablations) and what's understood (why it helps is still open, by the authors' own account).

baselines

Fair for 2021 (vanilla Transformer, BERT, WoBERT, NEZHA, Performer are the era-appropriate comparisons) but thin: every result is a single run with no reported variance, and the WMT gain (27.5 vs. 27.3 BLEU) is well within typical seed-to-seed noise for a from-scratch MT run.

contamination

Not applicable in the modern train/test-leakage sense — BookCorpus/Wikipedia pretraining and standard GLUE/WMT/CAIL2019-SCM splits were the norm at the time — but the paper also doesn't check it, since contamination auditing wasn't yet a standard practice.

what's missed

No seed variance anywhere; the GLUE ablation's 3-3 split isn't reconciled with the abstract's "consistently" claim; the Chinese long-text result conflates tokenization scheme with position embedding for the BERT/NEZHA comparisons and lacks a same-length WoBERT baseline at 1024 chars to check the headline 1.5% number.

would raise confidence

Multiple seeds with variance bars; a controlled ablation that holds tokenization fixed and varies only max sequence length; and a WoBERT-1024 (or BERT-1024) row so the reported long-context gain has an apples-to-apples baseline in the same table.

// bottom line

RoFormer's own benchmark numbers are a mixed bag — a marginal MT gain, a 3-3 GLUE split the abstract oversells, and a long-text result with a tokenization confound and an unverified headline number. None of that is why the paper matters. What survived from RoFormer wasn't its 2021 evaluation suite; it was the mechanism — an absolute-position rotation that yields relative-position behavior and composes cleanly with linear attention. That mechanism is why LLaMA, PaLM, the Qwen line, the DeepSeek line, and StarCoder2 all cite this exact paper for their position embedding, making it one of the highest-fan-in nodes in the modern LLM-architecture citation graph even though its own reported results wouldn't have predicted that outcome.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. RoPE encodes absolute position m by rotating the query/key vector by angle mθ; because rotation composes, the inner product of two rotated vectors depends only on their relative rotation (m − n)θ — relative-position behavior emerges from a purely absolute, multiplicative operation, with no separate relative term added anywhere.
  2. That multiplicative structure is what additive schemes (sinusoidal, learned, Shaw et al., T5, DeBERTa) can't offer: it's norm-preserving and survives the associativity linear attention depends on, so RoPE is one of the only relative-position schemes that plugs into linear-time attention without breaking it.
  3. The paper's own reported results are more modest than the abstract implies — a 0.2 BLEU MT gain, a 3-3 GLUE split, a long-text result confounded with tokenization — and the authors explicitly say they don't have a faithful explanation for their two headline findings. RoFormer's outsized influence came from the mechanism's fit with later decoder-only, long-context LLMs, not from this paper's own evaluation suite.
