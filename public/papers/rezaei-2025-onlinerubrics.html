Online Rubrics Elicitation from Pairwise Comparisons

Static, hand-written rubrics rot during RL: they over-specify the good, under-specify the bad, and miss traits that only emerge mid-training — the seams reward hacking slips through. OnlineRubrics grows the rubric while the policy trains, eliciting new criteria from pairwise contrasts of the policy's and a control model's answers. The payoff: up to +8 points over training on human rubrics alone, and +25 over the base model.

MohammadHossein Rezaei, Robert Vacareanu, Zihao Wang, Clinton Wang, Bing Liu, Yunzhong He, Afra Feyza Akyürek · Scale AI & University of Arizona

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

Reinforcement learning from verifiable rewards (RLVR) only works where a final answer is mechanically checkable — a number, a code snippet with unit tests. To reach open-ended, long-form answers where there is no single correct string, recent work replaces the verifier with a rubric: a prompt-specific list of weighted, binary-checkable criteria (e.g. “+5 States higher counts are better”). An LLM grader marks each criterion satisfied or not, and the reward is the weighted average. This lets the same RL machinery score both verifiable and non-verifiable traits.

The catch is that these rubrics are written a priori and held static for the entire run. The paper argues that is exactly where things break, in two ways:

1. Rubrics describe the ideal and forget the failures — so the policy games them. Take the paper's own example. Prompt: “How can I test for the presence of carbon dioxide in a reaction?”, with the criterion (+9, mentions limewater turning milky). Both a clean answer and one padded with technically-true-but-irrelevant filler (“…which is slightly soluble in acidic conditions”) earn the full score, because nothing penalizes the padding. Huang et al. found a sharper version of this during rubric RL: models learn to insert self-praise — “The following advice is the most relevant” — which fools the LLM grader into scoring the response as relevant. Generic “catch-all” checklists (truthfulness, instruction-following) can't catch errors this sample-specific.

2. Good behaviors that emerge mid-training go unrewarded. If a policy spontaneously starts adding a genuinely useful trait (“This reaction is specific to CO₂”) that no one anticipated in the rubric, the algorithm has no way to reward it — the signal is invisible. A fixed rubric can neither plug the holes the policy discovers nor capture the strengths it grows into.

// the bet

Reward hacking and missed credit are both symptoms of an incomplete, frozen rubric. So make the rubric a living object: keep eliciting new criteria during training, grounded in what the policy is actually doing right now.

// how it works

The method

OnlineRubrics augments the offline rubric with criteria elicited online, once per step, before the policy update. At step t, for each prompt it samples M candidate responses from the current policy and M from a control policy πcontrol. An LLM extractor (here, o3-mini) then compares the two sets pairwise and writes down the differences not already covered by the existing rubric, turning them into new, positively-weighted criteria.

The extractor is deliberately constrained. It is told to (a) base every new criterion on one of the two responses — never to invent criteria from its own knowledge — and (b) actively hunt for reward-hacking patterns: behaviors that follow “the letter of the law but not the spirit.” Elicitation is two-stage — first enumerate the meaningful differences with quotes, then deduplicate overlapping criteria. By default the method compares 8 pairs of rollouts and adds ~8 criteria per prompt. The new criteria simply append to the existing rubric, so the method drops into any rubric-based RL pipeline.

The reward, and why pairwise

Training uses GRPO. Given the augmented criterion set C = Ci ∪ Cie, the grader returns a binary vector and the reward is a weighted average normalized by the total positive weight:

q(x, o, C) = w⊤ · LLMgrader(x, o, C)Σk : wk > 0 wk // Eq. 4 — weighted satisfaction of binary criteria, normalized by max achievable positive score

Why contrast pairs rather than read a single response? LLMs are unreliable absolute judges, but the paper finds they are far better at spotting differences — a pairwise comparison surfaces the discriminative property that separates a better answer from a worse one, which is precisely the signal a rubric should encode. Two variants set the control source: OnlineRubrics-πref (compare against the frozen initial model) and OnlineRubrics-πold (compare against the previous step's policy).

A gradient-gap argument for completeness

The paper gives a formal reason elicitation should help. Split the true criteria into explicit ones already in the rubric and implicit ones that are missing, with weights wE and wI. If the estimated reward only scores the explicit criteria, the gap between the true policy gradient and the estimated one is bounded by the mass of what's left out:

‖ gU − gRt ‖2 ≤ √( 𝔼[ ‖∇θ log πθ‖2 ] ) · ‖ wI ‖1 // Prop. 1 — the gradient error is upper-bounded by the unmodeled implicit weight ‖wI‖₁

Every criterion elicitation that moves an implicit criterion into the explicit set shrinks ‖wI‖1, tightening the bound and yielding a closer-to-true gradient — hence more stable, more sample-efficient training. The authors are careful to frame this as tightening the bound, not fully recovering the true criteria set.

Picking the grader

Because grading runs on every rollout, the grader's accuracy and its cost both matter. The team collected human satisfaction labels on 500 prompts each for the Generalist and Expert sets (2–6 responses per prompt) and scored 11 candidate LLM graders by AUC against those labels versus inference cost. Every grader scored higher on Generalist than Expert rubrics (mean AUC 0.811 vs 0.768), and the Pareto frontier was identical across both domains. They chose GPT-4.1-mini — on the frontier, cheap, and well-aligned.

// what they found

The results

The policy is Qwen-2.5-7B-Instruct, trained with GRPO for 3 epochs (16 rollouts/sample, o3-mini extractor, GPT-4.1-mini grader, 8 pairwise comparisons). Two rubric corpora drive training: Generalist (1,500 train / 487 eval prompts, ~10.4 criteria each) and Expert (1,864 / 332, ~18.0 criteria each, spanning Physics, Chemistry, Biology, Math). Crucially, the held-out eval sets contain only human-written rubrics — none of the elicited criteria — so they test whether online elicitation transfers to a clean target.

On downstream benchmarks, OnlineRubrics is the top method in nearly every column. The headline lift over the strongest static baseline (human rubrics) is up to ~8 points, and up to +25 points over the untrained instruct model:

Three patterns stand out. First, human rubrics beat both LLM-judge scoring and synthetic rubrics on essentially every metric — the structured signal is worth the annotation. Second, online elicitation adds on top of that: AlpacaEval win rate climbs 46.4 → 55.0 (and length-controlled WR 28.0 → 31.5, so the gain is not just longer answers), Arena-Hard 52.4 → 56.5, Expert win rate 51.8 → 61.0. Third, and central to the paper's thesis, pairwise beats pointwise: extracting criteria from a single response (Pointwise Extraction) trails the pairwise method by ~6–7 points on AlpacaEval (48.1 vs 54.0/55.0) and Arena-Hard (51.1 vs 55.7/56.5), and online elicitation also clears the fixed-checklist “Universal Requirements” baseline across the board.

What the elicited criteria look like

Clustering the rubric refinements that appeared over training, the most common new criteria are about process and substance, not surface form — the top clusters are Reproducibility & Transparency (8.96% of samples), Practicality & Real-World Feasibility (8.33%), Holistic Evaluation & Anti-Gaming (7.69%), Lifecycle Management & Adaptivity (7.42%), and Structural Integrity & Organization (6.58%), followed by mechanistic/causal reasoning, evidence provenance, and uncertainty handling. In aggregate, online elicitation expands and hardens the rubric as new weaknesses are exposed.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

The baselines are genuinely well-chosen. Beyond LLM-judge scoring and synthetic vs. human rubrics, the paper includes the two ablations that matter: Universal Requirements (a fixed checklist appended to every prompt, à la Viswanathan et al.) and Pointwise Extraction (eliciting criteria from a single response). The latter isolates the paper's core claim — that the win comes from pairwise contrast, not merely from adding more criteria — and the 6–7 point margins on AlpacaEval/Arena-Hard make that case cleanly. That is the right experiment, and it lands.

But the evidence is bounded in ways worth naming. It rests on a single base model (Qwen-2.5-7B-Instruct) at a single size, GRPO only; nothing here tells us the gains survive at 70B, on a non-Qwen family, or under PPO/DPO. More structurally, the entire loop is LLM-refereed end to end: o3-mini invents the criteria, GPT-4.1-mini grades them, and the win-rates are decided by Gemini-2.5-Pro / GPT-4.1. The elicited criteria reward precisely what LLM judges tend to like — structure, transparency, hedged “holistic” coverage (look again at the Figure-7 clusters) — so there's a real risk that the policy is being tuned toward an LLM judge's stylistic preferences rather than toward substantively better answers. The length-controlled AlpacaEval number (LC-WR 28.0 → 31.5) is the strongest rebuttal — the gain isn't purely verbosity — but it controls for length alone, and the Generalist/Expert win-rates are adjudicated by a judge from the same model ecosystem whose tastes overlap with the criteria being elicited.

The headline also flatters the best case. “+8” is the AlpacaEval win-rate delta; on the verifiable benchmarks the method barely moves — GSM8K 79.2 → 80.5, GPQA-Diamond 34.7 → 38.1 — so the lift concentrates in open-ended, LLM-judged quality, not hard reasoning. The two variants trade the lead column by column (πref wins Expert/GPQA win-rate, πold wins AlpacaEval/Arena-Hard), leaving no single recommended config and the suspicion of run-to-run noise — and no seeds or variance are reported. Most tellingly, reward hacking is the entire motivation, yet it is never directly measured. There is no before/after on self-praise rate, padding rate, or any specific hacking behavior; the case is made by downstream benchmark lift plus qualitative cluster labels. And Proposition 1, while tidy, is a loose upper bound — it bounds the gradient gap by ‖wI‖₁ but guarantees nothing about whether the elicited criteria actually approximate the true ones.

baselines

Strong and well-targeted — LLM-judge, synthetic & human rubrics, a fixed-checklist baseline, and a pointwise-extraction ablation that isolates the pairwise claim. But one base model (Qwen-2.5-7B), one size, GRPO only.

contamination

Eval sets exclude elicited rubrics (a clean transfer test), and held-out human rubrics are reused only for scoring. The deeper issue is circularity: elicit → grade → judge all run by LLMs from one ecosystem; LC-WR controls for length but not for judge-style alignment.

what's missed

Gains concentrate on open-ended LLM-judged metrics (GSM8K/GPQA nearly flat); no direct measurement of reduced reward hacking despite it being the thesis; πref vs πold lead flips with no seeds or variance; online-extraction cost is never priced.

would raise confidence

A measured drop in a named hacking behavior; larger and non-Qwen models; an independent judge from a different family than the extractor/grader; multiple seeds with variance; and a cost-normalized comparison against simply writing better static rubrics.

// bottom line

A clean, well-motivated idea — let the rubric grow with the policy — backed by the one ablation that matters (pairwise beats pointwise) and a neat gradient-gap argument. The lift is real on open-ended, LLM-judged tasks (+8 on AlpacaEval, +25 over base), but the evidence stops just short of the paper's own claim: it demonstrates better benchmark scores, not a measured reduction in the reward hacking it set out to fix — all inside a single LLM-judge ecosystem on a single 7B model.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. Static rubrics decay during RL: they over-specify the ideal, under-specify the failures, and can't reward traits that only emerge mid-training — exactly the seams reward hacking exploits.
  2. OnlineRubrics grows the rubric from pairwise contrasts (policy vs. control), which the extractor finds easier and more discriminative than judging one response — and pairwise beats pointwise extraction by 6–7 points on AlpacaEval and Arena-Hard.
  3. The payoff is real but scoped: +8 over human rubrics and +25 over the base model on open-ended, LLM-judged benchmarks, while verifiable GSM8K/GPQA barely move — and the paper shows better scores, not a measured drop in the reward hacking it targets.
