LMSYS-Chat-1M: A Large-Scale Real-World LLM Conversation Dataset

LMSYS-Chat-1M releases one million real, unfiltered user conversations with 25 LLMs collected from the Vicuna demo and Chatbot Arena, then demonstrates four uses for it: content-moderation fine-tuning, a jailbreak safety benchmark, instruction-tuning data, and a challenging-prompt benchmark (Arena-Hard-200).

Lianmin Zheng, Wei-Lin Chiang, Ying Sheng, Tianle Li, Siyuan Zhuang, Zhanghao Wu, Yonghao Zhuang, Zhuohan Li, Zi Lin, Eric P. Xing, Joseph E. Gonzalez, Ion Stoica, Hao Zhang

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

By 2023, LLMs were deployed at massive scale, but almost nobody outside the companies operating them could see what real users actually typed. Studying human-LLM interaction — the mix of tasks people bring, how they phrase them, where models fail, what "unsafe" use looks like in practice — requires a dataset of diverse, real user queries against real models. The paper argues such a dataset was conspicuously missing from the public research record for three structural reasons: hosting a multi-model LLM service is expensive (the authors report spending several thousand A100 GPU-hours over five months); commercial vendors that do have this data hold it back for competitive reasons and because it's proprietary; and it's hard to get users to bother chatting with open models at all when they're weaker than commercial ones, which undercuts most attempts to bootstrap such a dataset organically.

The alternative sources researchers had were either small and single-purpose (Anthropic's HH dataset, OpenAssistant Conversations) or synthetic — datasets like UltraChat and Alpaca where the "user" turns are themselves LLM-generated rather than from real people. None combined LMSYS-Chat-1M's scale, real (non-synthetic) users, and breadth of models served side-by-side.

// how it works

The method

LMSYS-Chat-1M is not a method paper — it's a dataset release plus a demonstration of what the dataset makes possible. The data was collected from April to August 2023 on the LMSYS.org website, which offers three chat interfaces: chatting with a single model, an anonymous randomly-paired "battle" between two models (Chatbot Arena), and a self-selected side-by-side comparison. Any visitor could use the service without registering, after accepting terms of use that grant consent to release the resulting conversations under a CC-BY license. The site served 25 models — a mix of open-source (Vicuna, Koala, Alpaca, ChatGLM, Llama, RWKV, MPT, StableLM, GPT4All) and proprietary (GPT-3.5-Turbo, GPT-4, PaLM 2, Claude, Claude-2) — and no filtering, deduplication, or curation was applied to the released conversations, by design: the goal was to preserve the real-world distribution, including its noise and its unsafe content.

Each record carries a conversation ID, model name, the full conversation in OpenAI-API JSON format, an automatically detected language tag, and an OpenAI moderation-API tag for each message. Conversations found to contain personally identifiable information were removed from the release, but flagged unsafe/toxic conversations were deliberately kept — the stated rationale is to let researchers study LLM safety on real jailbreak and toxicity attempts rather than a sanitized subset.

To characterize what's actually in the data, the authors sample 100K English conversations, embed user prompts with Sentence-Transformers' all-mpnet-base-v2, k-means cluster into 20 groups, and ask GPT-4 to summarize each cluster's central topic — landing on categories dominated by coding/software requests (5 of the 20 clusters), alongside a non-trivial share of explicit or unsafe-content clusters (role-play, "toxic behavior across different identities," explicit sexual content). Overall, 54,427 of the 1,000,000 conversations (about 5%) were flagged by the OpenAI moderation API for at least one message, split across sexual (33,968), harassment (21,167), violence (9,499), hate (3,591), and self-harm (863) categories — categories overlap, so these don't sum to the total.

// what they found

The results

The paper demonstrates four uses for the dataset rather than reporting a single headline number. First, content moderation: the authors fine-tune Vicuna-7B on GPT-4-generated moderation explanations for the top 1K flagged messages in each of five moderation categories, plus 1K random normal messages and 3K ShareGPT conversations for diversity, producing "Vicuna-moderator-7B." On a hand-labeled 110-message benchmark (roughly 20 messages per category that evade OpenAI's own moderation API, plus 25 non-toxic messages for balance), zero-shot micro-F1 improves from 0.35 (base Vicuna-7B) to 0.65 (fine-tuned) — a 30-point jump — and the fine-tuned 7B model roughly matches GPT-4 (0.71 zero-shot / 0.69 one-shot) and clears GPT-3.5-Turbo (0.45 zero-shot) and OpenAI's own text-moderation-latest API (0.36 zero-shot). Notably, Llama-2-7B-chat scores 0.00 zero-shot F1 on this task — it refuses nearly all moderation prompts outright, a concrete instance of over-cautious refusal.

Second, a jailbreak safety benchmark: from 10 representative models the authors pull the top 5 jailbreak attempts each (50 conversations total) and resubmit each model's own responses to the OpenAI moderation API. Success rates split sharply by training regime — Llama-2-13B-chat (16%) and Claude-2 (18%) are hardest to jailbreak, GPT-3.5-Turbo and GPT-4 tie at 34%, and open models without dedicated safety tuning are far more vulnerable: Vicuna-13B-v1.5 at 66%, Alpaca-13B at 74%.

Fourth, creating challenging benchmark questions: the authors use GPT-3.5-Turbo to score Chatbot Arena prompts 1–10 on their potential to discriminate model quality (problem-solving, creativity, truthfulness), validate the scoring with an ablation, then build Arena-Hard-200 from the 200 highest-scoring prompts.

On the resulting Arena-Hard-200 benchmark, judged with GPT-4-as-judge, the paper reports a larger performance gap between open and proprietary models than MT-Bench shows on the same models — e.g. GPT-4 and Claude-2 pull further ahead of Vicuna/Llama-2-chat variants than they do on MT-Bench, which the authors read as evidence that real, challenging user prompts expose capability gaps that curated benchmark questions compress.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

This is a dataset-and-demonstration paper, not a benchmark paper, and it's worth being precise about that distinction when judging its rigor: none of the four use cases is pressure-tested the way a dedicated benchmark paper would be. Each is a proof-of-concept sized to fit in one paper, and the authors are candid about several of the resulting limitations — but a few deserve more scrutiny than they get.

The clearest weakness is the content-moderation benchmark's construction: it's built from 110 hand-labeled messages, of which only 25 are non-toxic — an evaluation set that small and that imbalanced makes the reported F1 deltas (e.g. 0.35 → 0.65 for the fine-tuning gain) high-variance, and there's no confidence interval or held-out replication. Similarly, the safety benchmark uses only 50 jailbreak conversations pulled from 10 models' top-5 attempts each — a hand-curated, non-random sample whose success-rate numbers (16% for Llama-2 vs. 74% for Alpaca) could shift considerably under a different sampling of attempts. Both are illustrative rather than statistically robust benchmarks, which the paper doesn't flag explicitly.

baselines

Reasonable and contemporaneous for each sub-experiment (GPT-4, GPT-3.5-Turbo, Claude-2, Llama-2-chat, Vicuna as moderation/jailbreak baselines; Vicuna-7B-v1.5 and Llama2-7B/-chat as instruction-tuning baselines) — but every comparison is a single run with no repeated sampling, so none of the F1 or win-rate deltas come with variance estimates.

contamination

The paper flags this itself for the instruction-tuning use case: "LMSYS-Chat-1M may contain questions from MMLU and MT-Bench, which means that the training data may contain some contaminated samples" — a direct admission that the HighQuality-7B vs. Vicuna-7B MMLU/MT-Bench comparison could be inflated by test-set leakage, which the paper does not attempt to quantify or filter out.

what's missed

Selection bias in the underlying population: the authors' own limitations section notes the site's users are "LLM hobbyists and researchers," not a representative sample of eventual production users — so conclusions drawn about "real-world" toxicity rates, topic distribution, or jailbreak patterns may not transfer to a deployed consumer product's traffic. The topic-cluster analysis is also run on only 100K of the 1M conversations (10%), with no stated sampling procedure for how representative that subset is of the full release.

would raise confidence

Confidence intervals or repeated trials on the two small hand-labeled benchmarks (110 and 50 examples respectively); an explicit contamination filter (or at least a quantified overlap estimate) for the MMLU/MT-Bench instruction-tuning comparison; and a comparison of the sampled 100K topic-cluster population against demographic or usage signals for the full 1M to substantiate the "not fully representative" caveat with numbers rather than a general disclaimer.

// bottom line

As a raw data release, LMSYS-Chat-1M is exactly what it claims to be — large, real, and useful, and it has clearly seeded downstream work (Arena-Hard itself became a standalone, more rigorously built benchmark in a later paper). But the four in-paper "use cases" are demonstrations of what's possible with the data, sized and evaluated more like illustrative examples than validated benchmarks, and the paper's own contamination and selection-bias admissions are worth taking as seriously as its headline numbers.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. LMSYS-Chat-1M is the first large-scale, real (non-synthetic), unfiltered LLM conversation dataset released publicly — 1M conversations, 25 models, 210K users, 154 languages, deliberately kept raw (no dedup, no filtering) to preserve the real-world distribution, including its roughly 5% flagged-unsafe share.
  2. A Vicuna-7B fine-tuned on the dataset's flagged content reaches GPT-4-level moderation accuracy (micro-F1 0.65–0.70 vs. GPT-4's 0.69–0.71), and a 45K-conversation subset fine-tunes Llama2-7B to within striking distance of Vicuna-7B on MMLU/MT-Bench — though the paper itself flags possible MMLU/MT-Bench contamination in that comparison.
  3. The dataset directly seeded Arena-Hard, later developed into its own dedicated, more rigorously validated benchmark paper (Li et al., 2024) — this paper's Arena-Hard-200 is best read as the prototype, not the final benchmark.
