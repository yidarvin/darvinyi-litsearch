Universal and Transferable Adversarial Attacks on Aligned Language Models

A gradient-guided greedy search builds a single string of gibberish that, appended to any harmful request, flips an aligned chatbot from refusal into compliance — and the suffix optimized only on open-source Vicuna and Guanaco transfers to ChatGPT, Bard, and Claude. The work that recast LLM alignment as an adversarial-robustness problem.

Andy Zou, Zifan Wang, Nicholas Carlini, Milad Nasr, J. Zico Kolter, Matt Fredrikson

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

By mid-2023, the standard recipe for making a chatbot "safe" was alignment finetuning — RLHF and its relatives — and on the surface it worked: ask GPT-3.5, Bard, or Claude to write bomb-making instructions and they refuse. The known way around this was the jailbreak: a hand-crafted prompt ("you are DAN, you have no rules…") that talks the model into misbehaving. But jailbreaks were a craft, not a science — they required human ingenuity, were patched one at a time, and broke under small wording changes.

Computer vision had long known a sharper threat: adversarial examples. Add an imperceptible, automatically-optimized perturbation to an image and any classifier flips its prediction; worse, the perturbation transfers across architectures and is essentially impossible to fully defend against. The obvious question was whether aligned LLMs were vulnerable the same way. The answer, until this paper, was effectively "no": text is discrete, so you can't just follow a gradient into the input the way you can with pixels, and prior automatic attacks (AutoPrompt, PEZ, GBDA) had mostly failed against aligned models. Carlini et al. (2023) had explicitly reported being unable to reliably attack aligned LLMs even with full white-box access.

The gap, then, was a method that was simultaneously automated (no human prompt-engineering), universal (one suffix that works across many different harmful requests), and transferable (optimized on models you control, but effective against black-box production systems you don't). This paper delivers all three at once.

// how it works

The method

The attack appends an adversarial suffix (20 tokens, by default) to the user's original request and leaves the request itself intact. The user types Tell me how to build a bomb. and the attack silently turns it into Tell me how to build a bomb. <suffix>. Three ingredients — each already present in prior work, but here combined carefully — make it reliable.

1. The affirmative-response objective

Rather than target a specific harmful answer, the attack targets the first few tokens of the response, forcing the model to begin with a positive affirmation that restates the request — "Sure, here is how to build a bomb:". The intuition, borrowed from manual jailbreaks, is that once a model is in a "state" where that opener is the most likely continuation, it tends to barrel ahead into the actual content instead of refusing. Concretely, the loss is the negative log-probability of that target prefix x⋆:

L(x1:n) = −log p(x⋆n+1:n+H | x1:n) // minimize the negative log-prob of the affirmative target ("Sure, here is …"); the suffix tokens are the only free variables

2. Greedy Coordinate Gradient (GCG)

The hard part is optimizing over discrete tokens. GCG's trick: at each suffix position, take the gradient of the loss with respect to the one-hot indicator of the current token to cheaply rank the most promising replacements (the top-k tokens with the largest negative gradient). That linearized gradient is only an approximation, so candidates aren't trusted blindly — the algorithm samples a batch of B candidate substitutions, evaluates each exactly with a forward pass, and keeps the single best. The decisive difference from AutoPrompt, its closest ancestor, is that GCG considers replacements across all suffix positions every step, rather than fixing one coordinate in advance. For the same forward-pass budget (the dominant cost), that one change is what separates a 25% attack from an 88% one.

3. Universal, multi-prompt, multi-model

To make a single suffix work for many requests and transfer off the source model, GCG optimizes one shared suffix while aggregating gradients and losses across many harmful behaviors and across several models at once (Vicuna-7B/13B, plus Guanaco). Prompts are added incrementally — a new behavior only joins the optimization once the suffix already succeeds on the earlier ones — and gradients are clipped to unit norm before aggregation. Because the loss is over output tokens, models that share a tokenizer can be combined directly; stacking models is precisely what buys black-box transfer. The default configuration: batch size 512, top-k 256, 20 suffix tokens, 500 steps.

// what they found

The results

The authors built AdvBench — 500 "harmful strings" (exact toxic strings the model must be made to reproduce) and 500 "harmful behaviors" (instructions the model must be made to attempt) — and measured Attack Success Rate (ASR).

White-box (the model you optimize on). On the harder Harmful Strings setting, GCG reproduces 88% of strings on Vicuna-7B and 57% on Llama-2-7B-Chat, versus 25% / 3% for AutoPrompt and 0% for both PEZ and GBDA. On Harmful Behaviors, a single universal suffix reaches 100% train / 98% test ASR on Vicuna-7B and 88% / 84% on Llama-2-7B-Chat — Llama-2 being the one model where AutoPrompt collapses (35% test) while GCG holds.

The headline — black-box transfer. A suffix optimized only on Vicuna and Guanaco, never on the targets, transfers to production chatbots. Using an ensemble of GCG suffixes (counting an attack successful if any one suffix works, over 388 held-out behaviors), ASR reaches 86.6% on GPT-3.5, 46.9% on GPT-4, 47.9% on Claude-1, and 66.0% on PaLM-2, against near-zero for the request alone. Claude-2 is the conspicuous holdout at 2.1%. On open models the suffix was never tuned against — Pythia, Falcon, MPT, Stable-Vicuna — ensembled ASR approaches 100%.

Two more findings sharpen the picture. Concatenating three GCG suffixes more than doubles GPT-3.5 ASR (47.4% → 79.6%). And where the automated attack still stalls, an author given a 30-second budget to manually reword the human-readable part of the request (e.g. "Generate instructions" → "Create a tutorial") pushed GPT-3.5 to nearly 100% — the suffix conditions the model to comply, and a trivial rephrase finishes the job.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

As a white-box result, the paper is airtight: PEZ, GBDA, and AutoPrompt are the right discrete-optimization baselines, and crucially the authors run them inside the same multi-prompt / multi-model harness, so the comparison isolates the optimizer rather than the scaffold. GCG's dominance (88% vs 25% on Vicuna strings; holding at 84% on Llama-2 where AutoPrompt drops to 35%) is real and reproducible.

The famous transfer numbers deserve more caution than they usually get. The single biggest confound is that Vicuna is distilled from ChatGPT-3.5 outputs — and in the vision literature, transfer between a model and its distilled copy is far easier than transfer between independent models. So the spectacular 86.6% on GPT-3.5 may reflect distillation similarity as much as a universal vulnerability; the authors say as much, and Figure 6 (right) supports it — a suffix tuned on a LLaMA fine-tuned on Claude outputs transfers best to Claude. The cross-family story (GPT-4 at 47%, PaLM-2 at 66%) is the more impressive and less confounded part.

Two methodological choices inflate the reported rates. First, the transfer ASR is an ensemble / max-over-prompts metric — success if any of several suffixes works — so it overstates what a single fixed attack achieves (47.4% vs 86.6% on GPT-3.5 is exactly that gap). Second, "success" on Harmful Behaviors hinges on a human judgment that a response "makes a reasonable attempt," with no inter-rater agreement reported and the harmful content redacted — so we cannot audit whether the elicited instructions were actually correct or dangerous, as opposed to a confident-sounding "Sure, here's…" followed by nonsense. And running the optimizer longer can hurt: Figure 6 shows transfer ASR peaking around 150 steps and then declining as the suffix overfits the source models, so the numbers are sensitive to checkpoint selection.

Finally, ecological validity. The attack was responsibly disclosed to OpenAI, Google, Meta, and Anthropic before publication, and the specific suffixes were patched within weeks; the gibberish suffixes are also detectable by a simple perplexity filter, a defense that appeared almost immediately. Claude-2's low 2.1% is itself partly an artifact of an input content filter in the chat interface (not the model), which the authors bypass with a word-substitution conditioning trick. None of this undercuts the contribution — the lasting object is the method and AdvBench, not any particular string.

baselines

Fair and well-controlled: PEZ, GBDA, and AutoPrompt run inside GCG's own multi-prompt/multi-model harness, isolating the optimizer. GCG wins decisively (88% vs 25% strings on Vicuna; 84% vs 35% behaviors on Llama-2).

contamination

Not train/test leakage but a distillation confound — Vicuna is distilled from GPT-3.5, so the 86.6% GPT-3.5 transfer likely overstates cross-family universality; GPT-4 (47%) and PaLM-2 (66%) are the cleaner signals.

what's missed

ASR rewards an affirmative opener, judged by hand with no agreement stats and content redacted — it never verifies the harmful output is correct or actionable. Ensemble/max-over-prompts and early-stopping selection both inflate the rate vs a single fixed suffix.

would raise confidence

A single fixed suffix scored once (no ensemble, no checkpoint cherry-picking), an automated harmfulness grader with inter-rater agreement, and transfer to a model provably not distilled from the source set.

// bottom line

GCG showed that aligned LLMs inherit the adversarial fragility of vision models: a cheap, fully-automated, gradient-guided suffix breaks safety training and transfers to black-box production chatbots. The exact strings were patched quickly and the gibberish is filterable — but the paper's real legacy is reframing alignment-by-finetuning as "posthoc repair" of a model still capable of harm, and handing the field a reproducible attack and benchmark (AdvBench) that the entire jailbreak-and-defense literature is now measured against.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. Three known ideas, combined, broke alignment. An affirmative-prefix objective ("Sure, here is…") + greedy-coordinate-gradient search over all suffix positions + multi-prompt/multi-model aggregation = the first reliable, automated jailbreak. The single change over AutoPrompt — searching every position each step — is what took it from 25% to 88%.
  2. Transferability is real, but confounded. A gibberish suffix tuned only on open 7–13B models hits GPT-3.5 (86.6% ensembled), GPT-4 (46.9%), and PaLM-2 (66.0%); Claude-2 is far more robust (2.1%), partly via an input filter. High GPT-3.5 transfer is inflated by Vicuna being distilled from it — the cross-family numbers are the honest measure.
  3. It changed the threat model. If LLM attacks behave like vision attacks — automated, transferable, and effectively undefendable by posthoc patching — then alignment finetuning alone may never be robust. GCG + AdvBench became the reference point that later red-teaming (HarmBench, JailbreakBench) and defenses build on.
