2020 · NeurIPS · Retrieval & RAG

Retrieval-Augmented Generation for Knowledge-Intensive NLP Tasks

Stop cramming the world's facts into a model's weights. Keep them in a swappable Wikipedia index, retrieve the relevant passages, treat which passage as a hidden variable, and fine-tune the retriever and the generator together. That recipe — RAG — became the default way to give language models an open book.

Patrick Lewis, Ethan Perez, Aleksandra Piktus, Fabio Petroni, Vladimir Karpukhin, Naman Goyal, Heinrich Küttler, Mike Lewis, Wen-tau Yih, Tim Rocktäschel, Sebastian Riedel, Douwe Kiela · FAIR / UCL / NYU

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

By 2020 it was clear that large pre-trained language models are, in effect, parametric knowledge bases: they soak up an enormous amount of factual knowledge during pre-training and recall it straight from their weights, with no external memory. That implicit store powered state-of-the-art results across NLP — but it has three structural flaws the paper names directly.

  • You can't revise or expand it. The world changes; the weights don't. Updating what a parametric model "knows" means retraining or finetuning, not editing a record.
  • You can't inspect it. There's no provenance — a model emits an answer with no pointer to the source it came from, so you can't audit why it said what it said.
  • It hallucinates. When the parametric memory is fuzzy, the model confidently confabulates.

Hybrid models that bolt a retriever onto a language model — REALM and ORQA, both 2020 — had started to address this by giving a masked language model a differentiable way to pull in Wikipedia passages. But they had only been shown to work on extractive open-domain QA: the answer is a span copied verbatim out of a retrieved document. That leaves the whole "workhorse of NLP" — sequence-to-sequence generation — untouched. Could you take a general-purpose pre-trained generator and give it the same swappable, inspectable, non-parametric memory, then fine-tune the combination end-to-end on any task? That is the gap RAG fills.

// how it works

The method

RAG combines two memories. The parametric memory is BART-large (400M parameters), a pre-trained seq2seq transformer. The non-parametric memory is a dense vector index of Wikipedia — the December 2018 dump, chopped into 21 million 100-word passages, each embedded by a BERT document encoder. At inference, the input query is encoded and a Maximum Inner Product Search (MIPS, run over a FAISS index with HNSW approximation) pulls the top-K passages. The retriever is the off-the-shelf Dense Passage Retriever (DPR) bi-encoder; the generator is BART. You concatenate each retrieved passage with the query, let BART generate, and combine.

The conceptual move is to treat the retrieved document z as a latent variable and marginalize over the top-K. RAG comes in two flavours that differ only in when they marginalize.

RAG-Sequence — one document for the whole answer

Pick the top-K passages, generate the entire output sequence conditioned on each one, and average the per-document sequence probabilities, weighted by how relevant the retriever thought each passage was:

pRAG-Seq(y|x) ≈ Σz ∈ top-K  pη(z|x) · ∏i pθ(yi | x, z, y1:i−1) // the same document z conditions every token of the output

RAG-Token — a fresh document per token

Marginalize inside the product instead: at every output position the model can lean on a different passage, so a single answer can stitch together facts from several documents.

pRAG-Tok(y|x) ≈ ∏i  Σz ∈ top-K pη(z|x) · pθ(yi | x, z, y1:i−1) // the document distribution is re-marginalized at each token i

The retriever itself is a dot-product bi-encoder, pη(z|x) ∝ exp(d(z)⊤q(x)), with BERTBASE encoders for documents and queries. (For a classification task the target is a length-one sequence, and the two formulations coincide.)

// the key training trick

RAG is fine-tuned end-to-end by minimizing the negative marginal log-likelihood of the target — with no supervision on which document should have been retrieved. The retriever learns what to fetch purely from the downstream signal. Crucially, only the query encoder and BART are updated; the document encoder and the 21M-passage index are kept frozen, which sidesteps REALM's costly periodic re-indexing during training and makes the recipe cheap to run.

// what they found

The results

On open-domain QA, RAG set a new state of the art on Natural Questions, WebQuestions and CuratedTrec — and did it with a 400M-parameter generator, beating both the retrieval-based REALM/DPR systems and the 11-billion-parameter closed-book T5+SSM, without the expensive "salient span masking" pre-training those models relied on. RAG-Sequence reached 44.5 Exact Match on NQ vs T5+SSM's 36.6 and REALM's 40.4.

A striking detail: because RAG generates rather than copies a span, it answers correctly even when none of the retrieved documents contains the answer string — 11.8% of NQ questions, where a purely extractive reader scores exactly 0%.

On generation, RAG's grounding shows up as factuality. On open MS-MARCO abstractive QA, RAG-Sequence beat the BART baseline by +2.6 BLEU and +2.6 Rouge-L. On the harder task of generating Jeopardy questions, human raters judged RAG more factual in 42.7% of head-to-head pairs versus BART's 7.1%, and more specific in 37.4% vs 16.8%. RAG's outputs were also far more lexically diverse — a distinct-trigram ratio of 53.8% on Jeopardy vs BART's 32.4% — with no diversity-promoting decoding tricks. On FEVER fact verification, RAG landed within 4.3% of complex SOTA pipelines on 3-way classification and within 2.7% on 2-way, despite using no retrieval supervision; its top retrieved article matched the gold-annotated evidence article 71% of the time (90% within the top 10).

Two ablations pin down what matters. Learned retrieval beats fixed retrieval on every task, and beats a word-overlap BM25 retriever everywhere except FEVER (whose entity-centric claims are well-suited to exact lexical match). And the headline demonstration of the whole non-parametric premise: index hot-swapping. Querying with a 2016 Wikipedia index answered 70% of "who is {leader}?" questions correctly for 2016 office-holders; swapping in a 2018 index answered 68% correctly for 2018 office-holders — and accuracy collapsed to 4–12% with a mismatched index. The model's world knowledge was updated by editing a database, not by touching a single weight.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

RAG is one of the most consequential architecture papers of the era, and the core idea is sound. But the eval is worth reading with a sharp eye, because two of its headline framings — "state of the art" and "no retrieval supervision" — lean on setup details that the topline numbers quietly inherit.

The retriever is warm-started with task-matched supervision. RAG's retriever is initialized from DPR, which was trained with retrieval supervision on Natural Questions and TriviaQA — the very benchmarks RAG then reports SOTA on. So "we use no supervision on which document to retrieve" is true of the fine-tuning objective but not of the components: a meaningful chunk of the retrieval signal is baked in before RAG ever starts. The paper is upfront about this, but the topline "unsupervised retrieval" reading overstates it; the BM25 ablation (which strips the DPR warm-start) is the honest lower bound, and it's much weaker on QA.

The benchmark answers live in the index. NQ, WebQuestions and CuratedTrec are Wikipedia-derived, and RAG's non-parametric memory is Wikipedia. Some of the "open-book" advantage is structural — the target answers are literally retrievable text. Open-domain QA also has well-known train/test question overlap (a point the same FAIR group quantified soon after in their "Question and Answer Test-Train Overlap" study), so raw Exact Match can reward memorized question–answer pairs as much as genuine retrieval-and-reasoning. The comparison to an 11B closed-book T5 is rhetorically powerful but isn't a parameter-matched contest: RAG is a 400M generator plus a 21M-passage index plus a BERT retriever.

The generation metrics are thin where it counts. Factuality is the whole pitch, yet it's measured relatively ("more factual than BART") on a single task (Jeopardy), over 452 pairs, with a large "no majority" bucket. There's no absolute hallucination rate — how often RAG itself is simply wrong — and BLEU/Rouge/Q-BLEU are weak proxies for faithfulness. Retrieval is also single-hop (one query → top-K), so genuinely multi-hop questions are out of scope, and everything rides on a single, clean Wikipedia corpus; there's no test of robustness to noisy, contradictory, or adversarial sources — the regime real-world RAG deployments actually live in.

baselines

Strong and current for 2020 (REALM, DPR, T5-11B+SSM, BART) — but the closed-book T5 is 27× larger, and RAG's own retriever inherits DPR's NQ/TQA supervision, so the comparison flatters the "tiny + unsupervised" framing.

contamination

Knowledge source (Wikipedia) overlaps the QA benchmarks' provenance, and open-domain QA carries known question/answer train-test overlap; Exact Match can credit memorization. No de-overlapped subset reported.

what's missed

No absolute factuality/hallucination rate (only pairwise vs BART, one task, 452 pairs); single-hop retrieval only; a single clean corpus with no noisy/contradictory-source stress test.

would raise confidence

Ablate the DPR warm-start (train the retriever from scratch); report EM on the non-overlapping question subset; measure absolute faithfulness; add multi-hop and non-Wikipedia / noisy corpora; widen the human eval beyond Jeopardy.

// bottom line

The idea has aged extraordinarily well — separate knowledge (a swappable index) from reasoning (the generator), make retrieval a latent variable, and train end-to-end. The numbers are real but should be read with the warm-started retriever and Wikipedia-provenance caveats in mind: the raw Exact-Match deltas overstate how much of the win comes from RAG's unsupervised fine-tuning rather than from supervision and corpus overlap it inherited. As a launch point for an entire subfield, though, it's hard to overstate: nearly every "give the model an open book" system since traces back to this recipe.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. Knowledge in a database, reasoning in the weights. RAG's enduring contribution is the separation: facts live in a non-parametric index you can read, edit, and hot-swap; the generator just learns to use them. Hot-swapping a 2016 index for a 2018 one updated answers about world leaders with zero retraining.
  2. Retrieval as a latent variable, marginalized end-to-end. Treat "which document" as hidden, marginalize over the top-K, and train the retriever from the downstream loss alone. RAG-Sequence fixes one document per answer; RAG-Token can switch document per token to fuse facts from several sources.
  3. Grounding buys factuality and provenance. A 400M generator beat an 11B closed-book model on open QA, answered correctly even when the answer wasn't in any retrieved span (11.8% of NQ), and humans found its generations more factual (42.7% vs 7.1%) and more diverse — while every answer now comes with a citable source.
