Fair Prediction with Disparate Impact: A Study of Bias in Recidivism Prediction Instruments

A single algebraic identity shows why the ProPublica/COMPAS controversy had two technically correct, mutually incompatible sides: any recidivism instrument that is calibrated across race will generically have unequal false-positive and false-negative rates whenever recidivism prevalence itself differs by group.

Alexandra Chouldechova

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

In May 2016, Julia Angwin, Jeff Larson, Surya Mattu, and Lauren Kirchner published Machine Bias, a ProPublica investigation of the COMPAS recidivism-risk instrument used in Broward County, Florida pretrial decisions. Their headline finding: a non-recidivating Black defendant was nearly twice as likely to be misclassified as high-risk as a non-recidivating White defendant. COMPAS's vendor, Northpointe, and a team led by Anthony Flores countered with their own statistical defense — that COMPAS was well-calibrated, meaning a given risk score corresponded to roughly the same likelihood of reoffending regardless of race. Both sides marshaled real numbers from the same Broward County dataset, and both seemed to be describing the same instrument in irreconcilable terms: was COMPAS biased, or was it fair?

What the public debate lacked was a formal account of how "calibrated" and "equal error rates" relate to each other at all. Chouldechova, writing from the Heinz College psychometrics tradition, supplies exactly that: a short, general derivation showing that calibration and error-rate balance are generically incompatible whenever the base rate of the outcome differs across groups — which it does for COMPAS, since documented recidivism prevalence is higher among Black defendants (51%) than White defendants (39%) in this data. The paper doesn't adjudicate who was "right" in the ProPublica fight; it proves that, under those base rates, both camps' numbers were mathematically compatible with a single calibrated instrument, and that no amount of re-analysis of COMPAS alone could have reconciled them, because the two fairness properties they were each measuring cannot both hold when the underlying recidivism rates diverge.

// how it works

The method

The setup is deliberately minimal. Let S = S(x) be a risk score, R ∈ {b, w} the defendant's race, and Y ∈ {0, 1} whether they go on to recidivate. Definition 2.1 formalizes the psychometric notion the Northpointe/Flores defense was implicitly invoking, calling a score test-fair (well-calibrated) if it reflects the same likelihood of recidivism regardless of group: P(Y=1 | S=s, R=b) = P(Y=1 | S=s, R=w) for every score value s. Figure 1 plots exactly this — observed recidivism rate against COMPAS decile score, split by race — and the two curves track each other closely across the ten deciles, which is the empirical basis for Flores et al.'s calibration claim.

To connect calibration to the false-positive/false-negative rates that ProPublica's investigation actually reported, the paper coarsens the score into a binary high-risk/low-risk classifier Sc by thresholding at a cutoff sHR, then reads off a confusion matrix. Test fairness implies the coarsened classifier's positive predictive value — P(Y=1 | Sc=HR, R=r) — is constant across groups (a necessary condition, since calibration at every score value forces calibration after any thresholding). That single constraint, combined with each group's own recidivism prevalence pr = P(Y=1 | R=r), is enough to derive an exact identity relating the false positive rate, false negative rate, PPV, and prevalence:

FPR  =  p1 − p  ·  1 − PPVPPV  · (1 − FNR) // eq. 2.4 — for a fixed, group-constant PPV (i.e. calibration), FPR is pinned to a function of the group's own prevalence p and FNR

The consequence falls out immediately: if PPV is equalized across groups (calibration) but prevalence p differs, then FPR and FNR cannot also be equal across groups — the equation forces them apart. Since Black defendants in the Broward data recidivate at 51% versus 39% for White defendants, a calibrated COMPAS is mathematically guaranteed to produce unequal error rates between the two groups; the paper notes the same algebraic observation was reached independently and concurrently by Kleinberg, Mullainathan, and Raghavan. This is the paper's whole engine: one identity that turns "the ProPublica numbers and the Northpointe numbers can't both be true" into "the ProPublica numbers and the Northpointe numbers are exactly what you'd expect from each other."

The results

Applied to the Broward County COMPAS data, the identity reproduces — and explains — ProPublica's own headline numbers:

Black defendants51%45%28%
White defendants39%23%48%

Section 3 then turns the error-rate gap into a concrete harm: under a "MinMax" policy where high-risk defendants receive a stricter penalty tH and low-risk defendants a lighter one tL, Proposition 3.1 shows the expected penalty gap between groups decomposes cleanly by outcome. Among non-recidivators specifically, the gap in average penalty is exactly (tH − tL)(FPRb − FPRw) (Corollary 3.1); among recidivators, it's (tH − tL)(FNRw − FNRb) (Corollary 3.2). In the special case where the only stakes are "incarcerated or not" (tL=0, tH=1), a non-recidivating Black defendant is literally FPRb/FPRw times more likely to be incarcerated than a non-recidivating White defendant under an otherwise "fair," calibrated instrument.

A natural objection is that the FPR gap just reflects Black defendants having worse average criminal histories. Figure 2 rules that out directly: restricting to only misdemeanor defendants and stratifying by number of prior arrests, the FPR gap between Black and White defendants persists at every prior-count level, from defendants with zero priors (≈22% vs ≈10%) up through defendants with more than ten priors (≈91% vs ≈60%).

Section 3.1 connects the size of this disparity to a familiar effect-size measure. The COMPAS decile-score distributions for Black and White defendants (Figure 3) differ by a Cohen's d of 0.60, equivalent to a total-variation distance of 24.5% — and Proposition 3.2 shows this quantity sharply upper-bounds the disparate-impact measure Δ from the MinMax analysis, giving a single number that connects "how differently are the two groups scored" to "how much worse off is one group under a penalty policy built on those scores."

// does it hold up?

The eval read

This is a theory paper wearing an applied-statistics dataset, so the usual "baselines and benchmarks" critique doesn't map cleanly — the real question is whether the impossibility result is as general as it's presented, and whether the single COMPAS case study is doing more work than it should. On both counts the paper is mostly careful, with real gaps left unaddressed.

The algebra itself (eq. 2.4) is exact and general — it's a statement about any binary classifier and any two groups with differing base rates, not something specific to COMPAS or criminal justice. That generality is the paper's real contribution, and it's why the result reproduces so cleanly across the two other 2016 fairness-impossibility papers (Kleinberg, Mullainathan & Raghavan; Hardt, Price & Srebro) despite each using a different framing and dataset. But the applied half of the paper — the claim that COMPAS specifically triggers this trap — leans on a single empirical premise that isn't independently re-verified here: that COMPAS is in fact well-calibrated. Figure 1's own confidence intervals show visible separation between the Black and White curves at deciles 6–10, and the paper takes Flores et al.'s logistic-regression validation as sufficient evidence of calibration rather than re-deriving it from the Broward data directly. If COMPAS is only approximately, not exactly, calibrated, the clean "calibration forces the error-rate gap" story is muddier than the identity alone suggests.

A second gap concerns the label itself. Every quantity in the paper — prevalence, FPR, FNR, calibration — is defined relative to Y, the observed two-year re-arrest outcome in the ProPublica release. The whole argument is agnostic to whether Y is itself a race-neutral measurement of "reoffending": if Black defendants are re-arrested at higher rates than White defendants for the same underlying behavior (a live empirical question in criminal-justice measurement research, not one this paper engages), the base-rate gap that drives the impossibility result could itself be partly a policing artifact rather than a fact about the population. The paper's math is correct conditional on Y being a trustworthy target; it doesn't audit that assumption.

baselines

Not a benchmarks paper — there's no comparison across instruments or methods. The empirical claims rest on one instrument (COMPAS) and one dataset (ProPublica's Broward County release), with calibration itself taken from a third party's (Flores et al.'s) analysis rather than independently re-derived.

contamination

Not applicable in the train/test sense, but the analogous risk is measurement bias in the label: the two-year re-arrest outcome Y is treated as ground truth for "recidivism," with no discussion of whether differential policing inflates Black defendants' observed base rate relative to White defendants' at the same true reoffense rate.

what's missed

The result proves calibration and error-rate balance are incompatible under differing base rates — it does not say which criterion a specific deployment (bail, parole, sentencing) should prioritize, or engage with whether the recidivism label itself is a biased proxy for the underlying construct.

would raise confidence

An independent re-derivation of COMPAS's calibration at finer subgroup granularity (race × prior count × offense severity, given Figure 2 already shows a gap surviving one such stratification); a sensitivity analysis to label bias in Y; and an explicit decision-theoretic argument — spelled out more fully in the companion Kleinberg et al. paper — for which fairness criterion should bind given the asymmetric costs of false positives vs. false negatives in a specific legal setting.

// bottom line

A short paper doing exactly one thing, cleanly: it proves that ProPublica and Northpointe were both reading real numbers off the same calibrated instrument, and that no amount of further argument about COMPAS specifically could have made calibration and equal error rates agree once recidivism prevalence differs by race. Published within about six weeks of Kleinberg et al. and Hardt et al., it's the empirically-grounded leg of 2016's fairness-impossibility trio — the one that shows the abstract incompatibility result actually explains a real, high-stakes public controversy. What it doesn't do is tell a practitioner which of the incompatible criteria to pick, or interrogate whether the recidivism label the whole analysis is calibrated against is itself race-neutral.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. One identity resolves the COMPAS controversy. Eq. 2.4 shows that a calibrated score's false positive rate is pinned to a function of the group's own recidivism prevalence and false negative rate — so if prevalence differs across groups (51% vs. 39% for Black vs. White defendants here), a calibrated instrument is mathematically guaranteed to show unequal FPR/FNR. ProPublica and Northpointe were both right; they were measuring different, generically incompatible properties.
  2. The disparity survives the obvious objection. Figure 2 shows the FPR gap between Black and White defendants persists across every prior-arrest stratum among misdemeanor-only defendants — it isn't an artifact of Black defendants having worse average criminal histories.
  3. The third leg of the 2016 fairness-impossibility trio. Published within about six weeks of Kleinberg, Mullainathan & Raghavan's Inherent Trade-Offs and Hardt, Price & Srebro's Equality of Opportunity, this paper independently derives the same core incompatibility and grounds it in a real, high-profile deployment — establishing that calibration and error-rate balance cannot generally be reconciled, a constraint every risk-assessment fairness debate since has had to work around rather than solve.
