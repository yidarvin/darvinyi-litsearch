Do Better ImageNet Models Transfer Better?

Retrain 16 CNN architectures — from MobileNet to NASNet-A Large — under one matched protocol, then test them as fixed feature extractors and as fine-tuning starting points across 12 downstream datasets. ImageNet top-1 accuracy turns out to be a near-perfect predictor of transfer accuracy (r = 0.99 fixed, r = 0.96 fine-tuned) — but the paper's own causal control shows a real share of that is just architecture capacity, not anything ImageNet specifically teaches.

What problem is this solving?

Computer vision research spent the 2010s treating ImageNet top-1 accuracy as a universal scoreboard: whichever architecture won ILSVRC that year became the default backbone for object detection, segmentation, perceptual losses, and — above all — transfer learning to smaller downstream datasets. The implicit assumption underneath that default is strong: that an architecture which scores higher on ImageNet classification necessarily learns better, more general features, and will therefore also perform better once fine-tuned or fixed-feature-extracted onto a completely different dataset. It is a load-bearing assumption — it justifies picking a backbone by a single leaderboard number rather than by actually testing it on the target task — and, as the authors point out, it "has never been systematically tested."

Earlier evidence was thin and inconsistent. Chatfield et al. found that, out of three AlexNet-era networks, the two more computationally expensive ones did better on PASCAL VOC; another study concluded deeper networks transfer better but wider ones transfer worse. Nobody had run a controlled study across the actual modern zoo — Inception variants, ResNets, DenseNets, MobileNets, NASNets — spanning a real range of ImageNet accuracy, evaluated identically across a broad set of downstream tasks. This paper is also a sibling validity check to Recht et al.'s "Do ImageNet Classifiers Generalize to ImageNet?", but it interrogates a different question: Recht asks whether the ImageNet metric generalizes to a fresh draw from the same distribution; this paper asks whether that metric predicts anything about performance on different distributions altogether — the question underneath every transfer-learning backbone choice ever made.

// how it works

The method

The authors assemble 16 modern CNN architectures spanning ImageNet (ILSVRC 2012 validation) top-1 accuracy from 71.6% to 80.8%: the Inception family (v1, v3, v4, BN-Inception, Inception-ResNet v2), ResNets (50/101/152 v1), DenseNets (121/169/201), MobileNets (v1, v2, v2×1.4), and NASNets (A-Mobile, A-Large). Crucially, every network is retrained by the authors themselves under one fixed recipe — batch-norm scale parameters enabled, label smoothing off, dropout off, no auxiliary classifier head — rather than pulled from public checkpoints. This control turns out to matter enormously: when the authors first tried public checkpoints, ResNets and DenseNets scored suspiciously high on transfer regardless of ImageNet accuracy, and the whole correlation degraded to statistical insignificance. The culprit was inconsistent regularization across checkpoints, not architecture — a confound that would have invalidated the entire study had it gone unnoticed.

Each of the 16 networks is evaluated on 12 image classification datasets (Food-101, CIFAR-10, CIFAR-100, Birdsnap, SUN397, Stanford Cars, FGVC Aircraft, PASCAL VOC 2007 classification, Describable Textures, Oxford-IIIT Pets, Caltech-101, Oxford 102 Flowers), ranging from 2,040 to 75,750 training images and spanning superordinate classification, fine-grained classification, texture recognition, and scene recognition — under three transfer regimes: (1) an L2-regularized logistic regression classifier trained on frozen penultimate-layer features via L-BFGS, no augmentation; (2) full fine-tuning from ImageNet initialization for 20,000 steps with Nesterov momentum and cosine learning-rate decay, with learning rate and weight decay grid-searched per dataset; and (3) training the identical architecture from random initialization directly on the target dataset — the causal control that decouples "this architecture" from "this architecture pretrained on ImageNet."

Because raw accuracy compresses non-linearly near the ceiling (a jump from 98% to 99% is a much bigger improvement than 60% to 61%), all correlations are computed on a logit (log-odds) transform of accuracy rather than raw percentage:

logit(p) = p1 − p // accuracy p is log-odds transformed before averaging across datasets or computing r, so gains near 100% aren't washed out relative to gains near 50%
// what they found

The results

The headline result holds under the matched protocol: ImageNet top-1 accuracy is highly correlated with transfer accuracy for both fixed features (r = 0.99) and fine-tuning (r = 0.96), across all 16 architectures averaged over the 12 datasets (Figure 1). Inception-ResNet v2 and NASNet Large — the top two models by ImageNet accuracy — were statistically tied for first place on fixed-feature transfer; Inception v4 led fine-tuning. As noted above, this relationship is fragile to protocol: with heterogeneous public checkpoints, the fixed-feature correlation collapsed to a level that was "low and not statistically significant."

Regularization choices matter a great deal for fixed features but much less for fine-tuning. Disabling the batch-norm scale parameter, and enabling label smoothing / dropout / an auxiliary head, each degrade the quality of frozen penultimate-layer features for transfer (visible directly in t-SNE embeddings of the feature space) even though they barely move ImageNet accuracy itself — but once the whole network is fine-tuned, those same settings have only a minor effect, since fine-tuning can partially undo a suboptimal frozen representation.

The paper's sharpest methodological move is its causal control: train the same 16 architectures from random initialization directly on each target dataset, with ImageNet removed from the picture entirely. Here the correlation between ImageNet top-1 accuracy and transfer accuracy is markedly weaker and more variable — r = 0.55 overall — and it is not uniform across dataset sizes: for the 7 datasets with fewer than 10,000 training examples, r = 0.29 and does not reach statistical significance; for the larger datasets, r = 0.86. In other words, part of what looks like "ImageNet teaches transferable features" is actually "bigger/better-designed architectures are just better at any sufficiently large image task," independent of ImageNet pretraining.

On raw performance, fine-tuning the better ImageNet backbones matches or exceeds prior published state-of-the-art on 6 of the 12 datasets at matched image resolutions, beating specialized transfer-learning methods from the literature (kernel pooling, deep filter banks, and others) simply by using a better pretrained CNN. Pretraining's biggest unambiguous win is convergence speed: fine-tuned models reach 90% of their maximum log-odds accuracy in an average of 26 epochs / 1,151 steps, versus 444 epochs / 19,531 steps training from scratch — a 17-fold speedup — even on the two datasets where final accuracy from scratch nearly catches up.

Those two datasets are the paper's most informative failure case. On Stanford Cars and FGVC Aircraft, ImageNet pretraining provides minimal benefit, and training from random initialization reaches parity with fine-tuning on FGVC Aircraft at just 1,600 total examples (16 per class). The reason isn't a lack of relevant ImageNet data — ImageNet's training set actually contains more car images than Stanford Cars itself (12,455 vs. 8,144) — it's class granularity: ImageNet has only 10 coarse car classes (e.g. "sports car"), while Stanford Cars distinguishes 196 classes by make, model, and year. Pretraining transfers well when the target task's classes are well-represented in ImageNet's own taxonomy (cat/dog breeds, bird species, food dishes); it transfers poorly when the target task requires finer distinctions than ImageNet ever had to make.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

The paper's most valuable asset is the one it doesn't lead with: the random-initialization control. If "better ImageNet architectures transfer better" is read as evidence that ImageNet pretraining teaches better features, the r = 0.55 correlation between ImageNet accuracy and accuracy trained from scratch on the target task (rising to r = 0.86 on the larger datasets) is a direct rebuttal of the strong version of that claim. Part of what looks like "ImageNet-learned features transfer" is simply "bigger, better-designed architectures do better on any sufficiently large image classification task" — a fact about architecture capacity and inductive bias that has nothing to do with ImageNet's labels. The paper reports this cleanly, but never computes the more useful single statistic: a partial correlation of ImageNet accuracy against fine-tuning accuracy, controlling for random-init accuracy, would say directly how much of r = 0.96 survives once architecture capacity is priced out. A reader has to do that arithmetic themselves, and the paper's abstract and title lead with the uncontrolled number.

This is also squarely a result about the 2012–2018 supervised-CNN era: 16 convolutional architectures, all trained on the same 1.28M-image, 1,000-class corpus, under one supervised cross-entropy objective. Whether "ImageNet top-1 accuracy predicts transfer performance" still holds for today's foundation-scale pretraining — self-supervised or contrastive ViTs trained on web-scale image-text corpora, at resolutions and objectives ImageNet accuracy was never designed to summarize — is a question this paper cannot answer, and the paper's own near-miss is a warning about extrapolating it: the correlation nearly vanished the moment training protocol became heterogeneous across the public checkpoints they first tried. Modern pretrained backbones vary enormously in data scale, objective, and resolution — exactly the kind of heterogeneity that broke this relationship once already.

It's worth reading this paper directly against Recht et al.'s "Do ImageNet Classifiers Generalize to ImageNet?", published within months of each other and both framed as "accuracy on the line" validity checks on the same benchmark. Recht asks whether ImageNet top-1 accuracy generalizes to a freshly drawn i.i.d. test set from the same distribution, and the answer is reassuring for benchmark validity even though absolute accuracy drops 11–14 points: model rankings and per-point gains transfer almost perfectly (slope > 1), so a decade of ImageNet leaderboard competition produced real, not adaptively-overfit, progress. This paper asks a different question — whether that same metric predicts performance on different tasks entirely — and the answer is a more qualified yes: strong under a matched training protocol, fragile under protocol heterogeneity, and partially confounded with raw architecture capacity rather than exclusively "ImageNet-taught features." Put together, they say ImageNet top-1 is a highly reliable ranking device for "how good is this classifier at ImageNet-like images," but a considerably more fragile and confounded proxy for "how good is this architecture in general" — the second claim is the one the field actually leaned on for backbone selection, and it's the one this paper shows needs the most care.

baselines

Fair against literature-reported numbers for specialized transfer-learning methods (kernel pooling, deep filter banks, and others), but not independently re-run under one shared harness — some "beats SOTA" claims (6 of 12 datasets) depend on matching each baseline's own image resolution rather than a fully controlled bake-off.

contamination

Not directly applicable pre-LLM, but worth naming: all 12 downstream datasets are long-standing academic sets whose classes substantially overlap ImageNet's own taxonomy (pet breeds, bird species, food categories), so most of what's measured is near-domain transfer. Stanford Cars and FGVC Aircraft are the informative exception precisely because their class granularity diverges from ImageNet's.

what's missed

No confidence interval on the headline r values themselves — each is computed over only 16 architecture points — and no partial-correlation decomposition isolating "architecture capacity" (measurable via the random-init control the paper already ran) from "ImageNet-derived features" in a single number.

would raise confidence

Bootstrap CIs on the r = 0.99 / 0.96 headline correlations; a partial correlation controlling for random-init performance; and a rerun of the identical three-regime protocol on post-2019 architectures and pretraining paradigms (ViTs, ConvNeXt, self-supervised/contrastive pretraining) to test whether the relationship survives outside the supervised-CNN regime it was measured in.

// bottom line

A genuinely field-shaping validity check: "ImageNet accuracy predicts transfer accuracy" (r = 0.99 fixed-feature, r = 0.96 fine-tuned) became a standard justification for backbone selection across computer vision. But the paper's own causal control — training the same architectures from scratch, with no ImageNet in the loop at all, still correlates at r = 0.55 (r = 0.86 on larger datasets) — shows a real share of that headline number is just "better architectures are better," not "ImageNet teaches transferable features." And because using inconsistent public checkpoints nearly erased the whole effect, this is a protocol-sensitive relationship measured entirely within the 2012–2018 supervised-CNN era — not a law that necessarily extends to the foundation-model backbones that replaced these architectures.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. Under a matched training protocol, ImageNet top-1 accuracy is a near-perfect predictor of transfer accuracy across 16 architectures and 12 datasets — r = 0.99 for fixed-feature logistic regression, r = 0.96 for fine-tuning — but this collapses to statistical insignificance when compared across heterogeneous public checkpoints with inconsistent regularization.
  2. The paper's own random-initialization control shows the correlation is partially confounded with raw architecture capacity: training the same architectures from scratch, with no ImageNet pretraining at all, still correlates with ImageNet accuracy at r = 0.55 (rising to r = 0.86 on larger datasets) — so a real chunk of "better ImageNet models transfer better" is really "better architectures perform better," independent of what ImageNet specifically teaches.
  3. ImageNet pretraining's clearest unconditional win is convergence speed (17× fewer training steps to reach 90% of peak accuracy), while its accuracy benefit is uneven — it nearly vanishes on fine-grained datasets whose class taxonomy diverges from ImageNet's own (Stanford Cars, FGVC Aircraft), where training from scratch catches up with as few as 1,600 labeled examples.
