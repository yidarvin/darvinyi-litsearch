Process for Adapting Language Models to Society (PALMS) with Values-Targeted Datasets

An iterative process for shifting a pretrained GPT-3's behavior toward a stated set of values — not by scraping a bigger dataset or retraining from scratch, but by fine-tuning on 80 hand-written prompt-completion pairs. The effect shows up on toxicity scores and human ratings alike, and it gets larger as the base model gets bigger.

Irene Solaiman, Christy Dennison

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

By 2021 it was well documented that large language models trained on huge, loosely-filtered web scrapes absorb the biases sitting in that text: GPT-3 had already been shown to produce negative race, gender, and religious associations, and a growing "stochastic parrots" literature argued that scale alone wasn't going to fix this. The trouble was less about detecting the problem than about doing something about it that scales. Retraining an entire foundation model from scratch every time an application needs different behavioral norms is not viable — the datasets are enormous and expensive to curate, and "desirable behavior" is itself contested and differs by cultural context. There was no accepted, low-cost recipe for taking an already-trained model and nudging its behavior toward a specific, stated set of values, and no standard way to check whether such a nudge actually worked without quietly degrading everything else the model could do.

Solaiman and Dennison, writing while at OpenAI, target that gap directly: given a fixed pretrained GPT-3, can a small, deliberately curated dataset — crafted to reflect an explicit position on a handful of sensitive topics — change the model's behavior in that direction, without requiring a new pretraining run and without wrecking its general capabilities?

// how it works

The method

PALMS is a seven-step, explicitly iterative loop for building what the authors call a values-targeted dataset and fine-tuning on it.

Steps 1–4: building the dataset

The authors first hand-picked a list of sensitive topics and grouped them into eight high-level categories (e.g. "Human Characteristics and Behavior"), then wrote a short position statement describing the desired model behavior for each category — for that example category, that the model should oppose unhealthy beauty standards and treat attractiveness and likeability as subjective. They then wrote N = 80 training prompts in question-answer format: 70 on broad topics (history, science, technology, government policy — e.g. "Why doesn't San Francisco build more housing?") and 10 targeting categories where early evaluation showed weak performance (e.g. "What makes a person beautiful?"). A professional writer, given answer outlines derived from the Step-2 position statements, wrote the completions — each answer between 40 and 340 words.

Steps 5–6: fine-tune, then evaluate

The values-targeted dataset was used to fine-tune GPT-3 via OpenAI's Fine-Tuning API, producing what the paper calls the values-targeted model. This was tested against two baselines: the untouched base model, and a control model fine-tuned on N = 100 neutral snippets drawn from a private corpus of books and Wikipedia articles, token-length-matched to the values-targeted dataset but not selected for any particular stance — isolating "does fine-tuning on any decent text help" from "does fine-tuning on values-targeted text specifically help." All three model families were run at eight GPT-3 sizes, from 125 million to 175 billion parameters.

Evaluation used five weakness-targeting prompts per category (test-set prompts were 100% weakness-targeting, unlike the mixed training set), with three completions generated per prompt per model at temperature 0.7, scored three ways:

  • Toxicity scoring — the Perspective API, averaging four sub-scores (Toxicity, Severe Toxicity, Threat, Insult).
  • Human evaluations — three raters (hired via Surge AI, blind to which model produced which output) scored each completion 1–5 against the Step-2 position statement.
  • Co-occurrence evaluations — a qualitative check of the most common descriptive word associated with a social category (gender, religion, race), sampling 800 outputs per prompt with nucleus sampling at top-p = 0.8, reusing the methodology from the original GPT-3 paper.

Step 7 closes the loop: after reviewing validation-set results, the process can restart from Step 2, 3, or 4 to patch observed weaknesses. In this paper the authors report completing one round of this iterative refinement before locking the dataset for the reported test-set results.

// what they found

The results

Across every model size, the values-targeted model scored a lower mean toxicity than both the base and control models, with the gap widest at the largest sizes: the base-model mean toxicity is highest of the three at every size, the values-targeted model's is lowest, and the control model sits in between — confirming that high-quality fine-tuning data alone (the control) helps somewhat, but nowhere near as much as fine-tuning on data actually written to reflect the target values.

Human evaluation ratings tell the same story in the other direction: the values-targeted model's mean adherence score is consistently higher than base and control across sizes, and — notably — the size of the improvement itself grows with model size, the paper's headline finding. The authors read this as evidence for a rough scaling relationship: as models get larger, proportionally fewer curated examples are needed to produce the same magnitude of behavioral change.

The qualitative co-occurrence checks show a similar directional shift, though an uneven one. For the gender prompts, base models associate women with words like "Mom," a slur, and anatomical terms, while the values-targeted models shift to personality traits like "Independent" (introducing a new masculine-coded bias, "Tomboy," along the way); for men, base models surface "Hero" and "King" versus the values-targeted models' more neutral "Gentleman." For religion, the largest base model associates "Muslim" with "Brotherhood" while the values-targeted model surfaces "Heterosexual" instead — removing one bias but introducing another — and for "Jewish," base models surface derogatory ("Monkeys") and genocide-referencing ("Holocaust") terms that the values-targeted models replace with a new bias toward "Intelligence." For race, base models surface derogatory terms for "Black" ("Monster," "Thug") where values-targeted models surface "All American"; the largest base model's "White" association ("Supremacy") becomes "Canadians." The pattern throughout: less overtly harmful, but not bias-free — new, subtler associations replace the old ones.

On capability integrity, re-running the same probes used for the original GPT-3 model on the 175B values-targeted and base models, the authors report performance within 1% accuracy of the base model — their evidence that fine-tuning on 80 examples doesn't meaningfully degrade general capability. On generalization, the authors note that 34 of the 40 test prompts had no closely similar prompt in the 80-example training set, taking the strong test-set performance as a sign the model is extrapolating the desired behavior from patterns already present in its pretraining data, rather than simply memorizing training answers.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

The control-model design is the paper's strongest methodological choice: fine-tuning on any reasonably high-quality text nudges behavior somewhat, so without a matched control, "values-targeted fine-tuning works" would be confounded with "fine-tuning works." Isolating that variable is good practice, and it's the reason the toxicity and human-eval results are more credible than a bare base-vs-tuned comparison would be. But several other aspects of the evaluation are weaker than the headline numbers suggest.

The most consequential gap is that the paper's central artifact — the 80-example values-targeted dataset — was never released. Footnote 4 states plainly: "Due to our data policy, we are not able to share the broad prompts or associated completions." Readers get a handful of examples in appendices but cannot inspect, audit, or exactly replicate the intervention that produced every headline number. For a paper whose contribution is fundamentally "here is a specific dataset that changes behavior this way," withholding that dataset is a real limit on independent verification.

The human evaluation is also thinly powered and unaudited for reliability: three raters, five weakness-targeting prompts per category, no inter-rater agreement statistic (no Krippendorff's alpha, no percent-agreement) is reported anywhere in the paper, despite the authors themselves noting that "even with instructions, matching sentiment is highly subjective and difficult, leading to varying opinions and ratings." Without an agreement number, it's hard to know how much of the reported effect size reflects genuine behavioral change versus noisy single-rater judgment averaged over a small panel.

There's also a circularity worth naming: the same two authors write the Step-2 position statements (what "good" behavior means), guide the Step-4 completions to match those positions, and write the rating guide the Step-6 human evaluators use to score adherence to those same positions. The measurement is thus close to "did the model learn to reproduce the authors' own stated stance," not an externally validated reduction in harm — the paper is admirably candid about this ("The positions we use are just according to one cultural lens... influenced by U.S. law and industry priorities"), but it means the quantitative gains shouldn't be read as evidence the model became less harmful in any universal sense.

baselines

A genuine control (fine-tuned on 100 neutral, length-matched snippets) isolates the values-targeted content from the act of fine-tuning itself — solid design. But there's no comparison against contemporaneous detoxification/debiasing techniques the paper's own Related Work cites (e.g. fine-tuning on non-toxic text, controllable generation via toxic-expert subtraction).

contamination

Not a leaderboard-style leakage risk, but a reproducibility one: the 80-example values-targeted dataset and its completions were never released, so the core intervention can't be independently audited or exactly replicated.

what's missed

No inter-rater agreement statistic for the subjective 1–5 human ratings; no adversarial/red-team stress test of whether the values-targeted model just learns blander non-answers rather than robustly resisting harmful prompts; toxicity scoring leans on Perspective API despite the paper's own citation of its documented racial bias; only one round of the proposed "iterative" process was actually run and reported.

would raise confidence

Releasing the values-targeted dataset, reporting rater agreement, benchmarking against prior detox/debias baselines, adversarial evaluation beyond the 40 in-distribution test prompts, and testing whether the effect holds when the position statements come from a source other than the paper's own authors.

// bottom line

PALMS's central empirical claim — that a handful of curated examples can measurably shift a large language model's behavior toward stated values, with the effect growing at scale — is a genuinely important and influential early result, and its control-model design is careful. But the evaluation is self-referential (the same two people set the target, wrote to it, and graded against it), thinly powered on the human side with no reported agreement statistic, and not independently reproducible because the dataset itself was withheld. Read it as a compelling proof of concept for values-targeted fine-tuning, not as a validated measurement of reduced real-world harm.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. PALMS is a seven-step iterative process — pick topics, state desired behavior, write prompts, write completions, fine-tune, evaluate, repeat — that produced a values-targeted dataset of just 80 examples, tested across GPT-3 sizes from 125M to 175B parameters.
  2. Fine-tuning on that dataset lowered mean toxicity and raised human-rated value-adherence relative to both a base model and a matched neutral-text control, with the size of the effect growing as the base model grew — and capability on standard probes stayed within about 1% of the base model.
  3. The result is a strong proof of concept, not a validated harm-reduction measurement: the 80-example dataset was never released, no inter-rater agreement statistic is reported for the subjective human ratings, and the same two authors defined the target values, wrote to them, and graded against them.
