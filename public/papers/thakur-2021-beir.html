BEIR: A Heterogeneous Benchmark for Zero-shot Evaluation of Information Retrieval Models

Neural retrievers were being judged on the same dataset they trained on. BEIR bundles 18 datasets across 9 retrieval tasks and asks a harder question — how do they do out of domain, with no training data? The uncomfortable answer: a 1990s lexical baseline, BM25, is brutally hard to beat.

Nandan Thakur, Nils Reimers, Andreas Rücklé, Abhishek Srivastava, Iryna Gurevych · UKP Lab, TU Darmstadt

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

By 2021, dense neural retrievers — bi-encoders like DPR and ANCE that map a query and a document to vectors and match by cosine similarity — were the exciting story in information retrieval. They were beating the decades-old lexical baseline BM25 by wide margins, and the field was ready to declare the keyword era over.

But almost every one of those wins was reported in-domain: train on MS MARCO (533k labeled query–passage pairs) or Natural Questions (133k), then test on the held-out split of that same dataset. That is not how retrieval is usually deployed. In practice you point a retriever at a new corpus — a biomedical archive, a legal collection, a Twitter stream, a company's docs — where you have no training data at all. Building a labeled retrieval corpus is expensive, so most systems run zero-shot. Nobody had a clean, broad way to measure whether the neural gains transferred to that setting.

Prior "multi-dataset" efforts didn't fill the gap. MultiReQA covered eight QA datasets but tested a single task (sentence-level answer retrieval), five of its eight sources were Wikipedia, and six had corpora under 100k candidates — small pools that structurally favor dense retrieval. KILT spanned five knowledge-intensive tasks but retrieved only from Wikipedia, and retrieval wasn't even its primary objective. There was no benchmark built to stress out-of-distribution generalization across genuinely different tasks, domains, query lengths, and document lengths.

// the core bet

In-domain accuracy is a vanity metric for retrieval. The number that matters is how a model does on a corpus it has never seen — and you can only measure that if your benchmark is heterogeneous by construction.

// how it works

The method

BEIR ("Benchmarking-IR") is a curated collection, not a new model. The authors assembled 18 publicly available English datasets spanning 9 distinct retrieval tasks — fact checking, citation prediction, duplicate-question retrieval, argument retrieval, news retrieval, tweet retrieval, question answering, bio-medical IR, and entity retrieval — and wrapped them in a single standardized data format and open-source evaluation package.

Datasets were chosen against four explicit criteria: diverse tasks (queries range from a one-word keyword to a full news article; documents from a 14-word tweet to a 635-word article), diverse domains (Wikipedia and news at one end, specialized scientific, financial, and biomedical corpora at the other), sufficient difficulty (nothing already solved), and diverse annotation strategies (some labeled by crowd-workers, some by domain experts, some derived from online-community feedback) — deliberately mixing annotation methods to reduce any single labeling bias.

On those 18 corpora they evaluate 10 retrieval systems spanning five architecture families, all in zero-shot mode (most are trained on MS MARCO, then applied untouched):

  • Lexical — BM25, the bag-of-words term-matching baseline.
  • Sparse / expansion — docT5query (a T5 seq2seq model invents queries a document should answer and appends them before indexing), DeepCT (BERT re-weights terms), and SPARTA (learned token-level sparse representations).
  • Dense bi-encoders — DPR, ANCE, and TAS-B, which embed query and document independently into one vector each. GenQ adds unsupervised domain adaptation: it fine-tunes TAS-B on synthetic queries generated for the target corpus.
  • Late interaction — ColBERT, which keeps per-token embeddings and scores a pair with a MaxSim operation.
  • Re-ranking — BM25+CE, a cross-encoder that re-scores BM25's top-100 by reading query and document jointly with full cross-attention.

The headline metric is nDCG@10 — normalized discounted cumulative gain over the top 10 results, which rewards putting graded-relevant documents near the top and is comparable across datasets with different relevance scales:

nDCG@k = DCG@kIDCG@k,    DCG@k = Σi=1..k 2reli − 1log2(i + 1) // gain from each hit, discounted by rank, normalized by the ideal ordering (IDCG). k = 10.

Crucially, BEIR is model-agnostic and extensible: any retriever that can score (query, document) pairs plugs in, and new tasks and datasets can be added. That design — a shared harness over many corpora — is exactly what let it become the field's default retrieval benchmark.

// what they found

The results

The single most-cited takeaway: in-domain performance does not predict zero-shot generalization. Models that crush MS MARCO in-domain — DPR, ANCE — fall apart on BEIR, under-performing BM25 on nearly every dataset. The 25-year-old lexical baseline turns out to be a remarkably robust generalist.

Reading the ranking from the figure and Table 2:

  • Re-ranking and late interaction generalize best. The BM25+CE cross-encoder is the top system, beating BM25 on 16 of 18 datasets (it only loses on ArguAna and Touché-2020) and sitting +11% above BM25 on average. ColBERT is next, ahead on 9/18. The lesson: cross-attention between query and document — reading them together — is what transfers.
  • Document expansion is the cheap winner. docT5query beats BM25 on 12/18 by injecting paraphrased keywords into documents before indexing — it stays in the lexical regime but widens it.
  • Dense bi-encoders struggle out of domain. They embed query and document separately, and that independence hurts under domain shift (e.g. BioASQ) or task shift (e.g. Touché-2020 argument retrieval). DPR — the only model not trained on MS MARCO — generalizes worst of all, roughly −47.7% below BM25 on average and ahead on a single dataset.
  • Training signal matters more than architecture, among dense models. TAS-B is the strongest bi-encoder (it beats ANCE on 14/18 and DPR on 17/18), which the authors attribute to its Margin-MSE knowledge-distillation training rather than anything architectural. GenQ's synthetic-query domain adaptation helps on specialized corpora (science, finance, StackExchange) but slightly hurts on generic Wikipedia-style data.

The catch is cost. The best-generalizing models are the slowest. Re-ranking 100 BM25 hits with a cross-encoder, or running ColBERT's late interaction, costs >350 ms per query; dense bi-encoders answer in <20 ms — 20–30× faster. And index size explodes for late interaction: ColBERT stores per-token vectors, needing roughly 900 GB to index the 15M-document BioASQ corpus where BM25 needs only 18 GB.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

BEIR's lasting contribution is real: it reframed retrieval evaluation around zero-shot generalization and made "does it transfer?" a first-class question. But the famous headline — BM25 beats neural retrieval — deserves an asterisk that the authors themselves supply, and it is the most important thing to understand about this benchmark.

The benchmark is biased toward lexical retrieval, by construction

Retrieval test sets are built by pooling: you can't judge every (query, document) pair, so you take the top hits from some retrieval systems, have annotators label those, and assume everything unjudged is irrelevant. Many BEIR datasets were pooled with lexical systems (BM25, TF-IDF). So when a dense retriever surfaces a genuinely relevant document that no lexical system ever retrieved, it was never judged — and is silently scored as wrong. The paper measures this directly with Hole@10 (the fraction of a system's top-10 that annotators never saw) on TREC-COVID, then hand-annotates 980 missing pairs and re-scores:

The pattern is stark. Lexical systems have tiny holes (BM25 6.4%, docT5query 2.8%) because the pools were built from lexical retrieval; dense systems have huge ones (TAS-B 31.8%, DPR 30.6%). After filling the holes, ANCE jumps from 0.654 — just below BM25 — to 0.735, which is 6.7 nDCG points above BM25; ColBERT gains 5.8. Lexical models barely move. In other words, part of "BM25 wins" is an artifact of who judged the test sets, not of which retriever is actually better. The honest reading is not "dense retrieval can't generalize" but "this benchmark, as judged in 2021, under-counts dense retrievers' correct hits."

It became a training target

BEIR was so influential that the next generation of retrievers — Contriever, GTR, E5, instruction-tuned embedders — was explicitly built and tuned to climb the BEIR leaderboard. A zero-shot benchmark only stays zero-shot until everyone optimizes against it; the "out-of-distribution" framing erodes once BEIR's datasets are, in effect, part of the development loop. The 2021 verdict aged into a starting line.

baselines

Strong and fair for 2021: 10 systems across five architecture families, all in honest zero-shot mode. But the dense entrants (DPR, ANCE, single-vector MS MARCO models) are now dated, and the benchmark's own annotation bias handicaps them.

contamination

Inverted from the usual worry — BEIR is zero-shot by design. The real validity threat is annotation selection bias: lexical pooling means dense retrievers' novel hits are scored as irrelevant (Hole@10 up to 31.8%), depressing their numbers.

what's missed

English-only. Averaged nDCG@10 over 18 very different datasets hides large per-task variance and a few datasets dominate the mean. The "BM25 robust" story is true on average but obscures decisive dense-retriever wins on individual tasks.

would raise confidence

Re-pool every dataset with diverse systems (dense + late-interaction, not just lexical), not just TREC-COVID; report per-dataset distributions and significance, not just means; rotate in fresh held-out corpora to keep the benchmark from becoming a training set.

// bottom line

BEIR is the field-defining zero-shot IR benchmark, and rightly so — it killed the habit of reporting only in-domain numbers. Read its leaderboard as a generalization stress test, not a verdict: the "BM25 is hard to beat" result was real in 2021 but is partly downstream of a lexical annotation bias the authors transparently quantified, and the gap has closed substantially since.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. In-domain ≠ out-of-domain. BEIR's enduring lesson: a retriever's score on its own training distribution tells you almost nothing about how it generalizes to a new corpus. Always evaluate zero-shot.
  2. BM25 is a robust generalist; cross-attention is what transfers. The lexical baseline beats most neural retrievers out of domain. The models that reliably beat it — BM25+CE (16/18), ColBERT — read query and document together, at 20–30× the latency of a dense bi-encoder.
  3. Mind the annotation bias. BEIR's own Hole@10 study shows lexical pooling under-counts dense retrievers' hits (ANCE rises from below to 6.7 points above BM25 once holes are filled). The "BM25 wins" headline is real but inflated by how the test sets were judged.
