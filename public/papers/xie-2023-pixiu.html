PIXIU: A Large Language Model, Instruction Data and Evaluation Benchmark for Finance

The first open financial-LLM stack, bundled as three named, first-of-a-kind contributions: FIT, a 136K-sample financial instruction-tuning dataset; FinMA, LLaMA fine-tuned on it; and FLARE, an evaluation benchmark pairing financial NLP with financial prediction. FinMA beats GPT-4 and BloombergGPT on sentiment and headline classification, but every model tested — FinMA included — is close to chance at the one task meant to test real-world usefulness: predicting whether a stock goes up or down.

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

Before PIXIU, financial NLP had pretrained language models but not open, instruction-following LLMs. The paper's own comparison table lists five prior financial PLMs — finBERT, FinBERT, Mengzi-fin, FLANG, BBT-FinT5 — all under a billion parameters and none instruction-tuned. The one large financial LLM that existed, BloombergGPT (50B parameters), changes the picture on scale but not on openness: "BloombergGPT and its training data are not openly released," and it "is also not instruction-following like other LLMs such as ChatGPT and GPT-4." That leaves a research community with no open financial LLM to build on, no open instruction-tuning data to fine-tune one, and no benchmark built to test one.

The evaluation side had a narrower gap too. The existing FLUE benchmark covered financial NLP tasks — sentiment, headline classification, NER, QA — but, as the paper notes, benchmarks like FLUE "only consider financial NLP tasks and don't include financial prediction tasks, such as stock movement prediction... that are critical for evaluating the model's performance applied to real-world scenarios." Predicting language understanding is not the same as predicting markets, and no open benchmark tested both together.

// how it works

The method

PIXIU is a three-piece framework, and all three pieces are named, headline contributions rather than one model with incidental evaluation. FIT (Financial Instruction Tuning dataset) assembles 136,609 instruction-formatted samples across five task families — financial sentiment analysis (FPB, FiQA-SA), news headline classification, named entity recognition, question answering (FinQA, ConvFinQA), and stock movement prediction (BigData22, ACL18, CIKM18) — built from existing expert-annotated datasets rather than self-instruct generation, and deliberately multi-modal: it mixes plain text, tables from financial reports, and historical price time-series in a single instruction format. FinMA is LLaMA fine-tuned on FIT, in three variants: FinMA-7B and FinMA-30B (tuned on the four NLP tasks only) and FinMA-7B-full (tuned on all five, including stock prediction). FLARE (Financial Language Understanding And PRediction Evaluation benchmark) is the evaluation side: 4 financial NLP tasks over 6 datasets plus 1 financial prediction task over 3 datasets — 5 tasks, 9 datasets in total in this version of the paper — with test-set sizes and distributions deliberately matched to BloombergGPT's reported test sets so its published numbers can be compared against, even though BloombergGPT's own test data was never released.

Evaluation on FLARE uses task-appropriate metrics: F1/accuracy for sentiment, weighted-average F1 across nine categories for headline classification, entity-level F1 for NER, exact-match accuracy for the two QA datasets, and accuracy plus Matthews correlation coefficient (MCC) for the three stock-movement datasets — MCC specifically because stock direction is a noisy, near-balanced binary label where raw accuracy alone can be misleading. FinMA's own results are all zero-shot on FLARE and auto-parsed since it was fine-tuned to emit exactly the instruction-following output format the parser expects; several general LLM baselines, by contrast, are graded on human evaluation "since LLMs without fine-tuning will fail to generate answers pre-defined in the given instruction."

// what they found

The results

On the financial NLP tasks FinMA was fine-tuned for, it wins clearly. On FPB sentiment, "FinMA-30B outperforms GPT-4 by 10% F1 score, and BloombergGPT by 37% F1 score" (0.88 vs. 0.78 vs. 0.51 F1). On headline classification FinMA-7B reaches 0.98 average F1 against ChatGPT's 0.77 and GPT-4's 0.86. On NER, FinMA-7B's 0.75 entity-F1 clears BloombergGPT's 0.61, though it still trails GPT-4's 0.83. The story flips on the task requiring numeric reasoning: on FinQA exact-match accuracy, FinMA-7B and FinMA-30B score just 0.06 and 0.11 versus ChatGPT's 0.58 and GPT-4's 0.63 — a gap the paper attributes to LLaMA's backbone having "no mathematical datasets for pre-training."

Stock movement prediction is the outlier across the board: on BigData22 and CIKM18, MCC scores cluster near zero for every model — ChatGPT −0.025 to 0.01, GPT-4 0.02–0.03, FinMA-7B 0.04 and −0.02 — indistinguishable from chance. The one exception is FinMA-7B-full (fine-tuned on the prediction task itself) on ACL18: accuracy 0.56 and MCC 0.10, clearing ChatGPT (0.005) and GPT-4 (0.02) but still a weak signal in absolute terms. The paper's own read: "all LLMs, including FinMA, ChatGPT and GPT-4, still present limited performance on stock movement prediction, indicating room for further improvement" — a rare case of a benchmark paper stating plainly that its headline model doesn't solve the hardest task it introduces.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

PIXIU's "FinMA beats GPT-4" framing needs one important qualifier the paper doesn't spell out as a limitation: FinMA is directly fine-tuned on FIT's training splits of the same task families it's then scored on in FLARE, while GPT-4, ChatGPT, and BloombergGPT are evaluated zero- or few-shot with no financial fine-tuning at all. That's not an invalid comparison — it's the standard "does domain fine-tuning help" question, and the paper is honest that its own contribution is exactly that fine-tuning — but the "FinMA outperforms GPT-4" headline reads more like "a fine-tuned 7B/30B model beats a zero-shot general model on tasks it was directly trained for" than like evidence of superior financial reasoning, and the FinQA/ConvFinQA results (where FinMA trails badly) show that gap clearly once the task requires reasoning FIT's supervised examples don't teach.

There's also a grading asymmetry baked into the comparison: FinMA's scores are "conducted on zero-shot and can be automatically evaluated" precisely because it was fine-tuned to emit the parser's expected output format, while "the results of some baselines are based on human evaluations, since LLMs without fine-tuning will fail to generate answers pre-defined in the given instruction." Different models are being scored by different graders (an automatic string parser vs. a human), which is a real methodological wrinkle in a benchmark whose whole purpose is head-to-head comparison. And the BloombergGPT comparison specifically rests on distribution-matched, not identical, test sets — "BloombergGPT doesn't release their test datasets. Test datasets were built to have the same data distribution with that of BloombergGPT" — a reasonable workaround given BloombergGPT's closed test data, but it means the "beats BloombergGPT by 37 F1" claim is a same-distribution, not same-examples, comparison.

One more thing worth flagging for anyone citing this specific paper: the version on arXiv (the only version posted, still marked "Preprint. Under review.") reports FIT at 136K samples across 5 tasks and 9 datasets, but the peer-reviewed NeurIPS 2023 Datasets and Benchmarks Track abstract (proceedings.neurips.cc) describes a broader final scope — 128K instruction samples across 8 tasks (six financial NLP, two financial prediction) and 15 datasets. The camera-ready benchmark that was actually accepted and evaluated is larger than what this arXiv preprint documents.

baselines

A reasonable, contemporary field for June 2023 (GPT-4, ChatGPT, BLOOM, GPT-NeoX, OPT-66B, Vicuna-13B, BloombergGPT) — but FinMA is fine-tuned on the exact task families it's tested on while every baseline is zero/few-shot, and grading methods differ (auto-parse for FinMA, human eval for several baselines).

contamination

Not leakage in the usual sense, but FinMA is trained and tested on splits of the same datasets (train/test split, not held-out unseen tasks), so its NLP-task wins reflect supervised in-domain fine-tuning more than generalized financial understanding.

what's missed

No genuinely unseen financial task to isolate "domain adaptation" from "task memorization"; the BloombergGPT comparison uses distribution-matched, not identical, test items; the NeurIPS camera-ready benchmark (8 tasks/15 datasets/128K samples) is larger than this arXiv preprint's scope (5 tasks/9 datasets/136K samples).

would raise confidence

A matched grading protocol across all models, a held-out financial task FinMA never saw during fine-tuning, and — given FinQA/ConvFinQA and stock prediction are both near-failure — a harder look at whether instruction-tuning a math-weak backbone (LLaMA) is the right lever for the tasks that actually require reasoning.

// bottom line

PIXIU's open-source contribution is real and its influence shows in what followed it (FinBen explicitly extends FLARE): first open financial instruction data, first open instruction-tuned financial LLM, first benchmark to pair financial NLP with financial prediction. But its headline wins are a fine-tuned model beating zero-shot general models on its own training distribution under an auto-parser tuned to its output format, not evidence FinMA reasons better about finance — and on the task the paper itself calls out as most realistic, stock movement prediction, FinMA is barely distinguishable from GPT-4, ChatGPT, or chance.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. PIXIU bundles three first-of-their-kind, equally headline contributions: FIT (136K financial instructions, 9 datasets/5 tasks), FinMA (LLaMA fine-tuned on it), and FLARE (the benchmark) — FLARE is the piece later work like FinBen explicitly extends.
  2. FinMA's wins over GPT-4/BloombergGPT (FPB F1 +10 and +37 points) are a fine-tuned-vs-zero-shot, in-distribution comparison graded by an auto-parser tuned to FinMA's own output format — not proof of general financial reasoning, and FinMA still badly trails on FinQA (EM 0.06–0.11 vs. GPT-4's 0.63), the task that actually requires numeric reasoning.
  3. On stock movement prediction — the task meant to test real-world usefulness — every model tested, FinMA included, is close to chance (MCC mostly 0.00–0.04, some negative); the paper says so plainly rather than papering over it.
