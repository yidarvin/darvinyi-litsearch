Big Bird: Transformers for Longer Sequences

A sparse attention pattern — random keys, a sliding window, and a handful of global tokens — that cuts self-attention from O(n²) to O(n) while provably keeping full attention's theoretical guarantees: BigBird is still a universal approximator of sequence functions and still Turing complete. The payoff is sequences up to 8x longer on the same hardware, and new state-of-the-art results on QA leaderboards, long-document summarization, and a new genomics application.

Manzil Zaheer, Guru Guruganesh, Avinava Dubey, Joshua Ainslie, Chris Alberti, Santiago Ontañón, Philip Pham, Anirudh Ravula, Qifan Wang, Li Yang, Amr Ahmed · Google Research

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

Full self-attention is what makes BERT-style Transformers so versatile — every token attends to every other token — but that versatility has a quadratic price: memory and compute both scale as O(n²) in sequence length. The paper is blunt about what that means in practice: "using commonly available current hardware and model sizes, this requirement translates to roughly being able to handle input sequences of length 512 tokens." Anything that needs more context than that — question answering over long evidence documents, classifying an entire patent or arXiv paper rather than its first 512 tokens, summarizing a document whose median length is in the thousands, or modeling a DNA sequence — either gets truncated or requires an expensive, heuristic pipeline to first retrieve a smaller relevant slice.

By 2020 there were already several ways to relax full attention — autoregressive models with limited lookback (Transformer-XL, Sparse Transformer at O(n√n), Reformer's LSH attention at O(n log n), Longformer's local-plus-global window) — but the paper argues these were largely heuristic: "most of the aforementioned methods are heuristic based and empirically are not as versatile and robust as the original transformer, i.e. the same architecture do not attain SoTA on multiple standard benchmarks," with Longformer as the one exception the authors keep in every comparison. Just as importantly, nobody had shown that a sparse attention pattern could keep the two theoretical properties known to hold for full attention: that Transformers are universal approximators of sequence-to-sequence functions, and that a full Transformer encoder-decoder is Turing complete. Without that, adopting a sparse pattern was a bet that you weren't quietly giving up expressive power for speed.

// how it works

The method

BigBird treats the attention pattern as a directed graph on the token sequence and asks a graph-sparsification question: which sparse graph keeps the useful properties of the complete graph (full attention) while using only O(n) edges instead of O(n²)? The paper motivates three building blocks from two intuitions borrowed from random-graph theory. First, an Erdős–Rényi-style random graph — each query attends to r random keys — gives a short average path length between any two nodes, so information can mix quickly across the whole sequence with only Θ̃(n) edges. Second, drawing on Watts–Strogatz small-world graphs and on Clark et al.'s finding that "neighboring inner-products are extremely important" in BERT's own attention, a window/local component has each query attend to w neighboring tokens on either side, capturing the strong locality of language (and of DNA). Neither piece alone is enough — an ablation at the same 512-token length as BERT-base shows random-only and window-only attention both underperforming full attention, and combining them still falls short (Table 1 below) — so the third piece, global tokens that attend to and are attended by every token, is added and is the piece the paper's theory says is critical for closing the gap.

Formally, for a directed graph D with out-neighborhood N(i), the generalized attention output for token i is

ATTND(X)i  =  xi + Σh=1..H σ( Qh(xi) Kh(XN(i))T ) · Vh(XN(i)) // D = complete digraph recovers ordinary full attention; BigBird's D is the union of random, window, and global edges

Global tokens come in two flavors: BigBird-ITC ("internal transformer construction") promotes a subset of existing tokens — e.g. CLS — to global status, so no extra sequence length is spent; BigBird-ETC ("extended transformer construction") instead appends new dedicated global tokens on top of the existing sequence, spending a small amount of extra length for extra representational capacity. On the theory side, the headline results are Theorem 1 — any graph containing a star graph (i.e. one global token that everyone attends through) makes the resulting sparse-attention Transformer a universal approximator of continuous sequence-to-sequence functions, matching full attention — and Theorem 3, that a sparse encoder-decoder built this way is Turing complete under the same arbitrary-precision assumption used for full attention. The paper also proves this isn't free: Proposition 1 shows a natural task (finding, for each vector in a set, its furthest counterpart) that full attention solves in O(1) layers but that any Õ(n)-edge sparse attention graph — not just BigBird's — needs Ω̃(n1−o(1)) layers to solve, via a reduction from the Orthogonal Vector Conjecture. Practically, BigBird is only applied on the encoder side of encoder-decoder tasks like summarization, since output sequences are typically much shorter than the input and the decoder's own attention cost is comparatively small; and the implementation "blockifies" the random/window lookups (Appendix D) because naively gathering scattered individual keys is inefficient on GPU/TPU hardware that rewards coalesced, contiguous memory access.

The results

On question answering, BigBird-ETC (base size) beats RoBERTa and Longformer on the development sets of HotpotQA, Natural Questions, TriviaQA, and WikiHop (Table 2), so the authors scale it to a large model and submit to the public leaderboards. The paper's own summary of the test-set leaderboard results: "For Natural Questions Long Answer (LA), TriviaQA, and WikiHop, BIGBIRD-ETC is the new state-of-the-art. On HotpotQA we are third in the leaderboard by F1 and second by Exact Match (EM)." They're also careful to flag a fairness wrinkle in their own favor: "it is worth noting that BIGBIRD submission is a single model, whereas the other top-3 entries for Natural Questions are ensembles, which might explain the slightly lower accuracy in exact answer phrase selection."

On long-document classification, BigBird "improves state-of-the-art for Arxiv dataset by about 5% points," while gains on the much larger Patents dataset are smaller and the small IMDb dataset (short documents, ample training data) shows no gain at all — the pattern the paper predicts: longer context helps most when documents are long and training data is scarce. On abstractive summarization of long documents (Table 4), pretraining a sparse encoder plus full decoder produces the largest jump on BigPatent, whose median input is far longer than Arxiv or PubMed:

The genomics extension pretrains BigBird with masked-language-modeling on the human reference genome (byte-pair-encoded DNA tokens) and fine-tunes on two downstream tasks. On promoter-region prediction the paper reports BigBird "achieve[s] nearly perfect accuracy with a 5% jump from the previous best reported accuracy": F1 of 99.9 versus DeePromoter's 95.6. On chromatin-profile prediction (predicting 919 functional annotations from non-coding DNA), BigBird improves AUC over DeepSea on transcription-factor binding (96.1 vs. 95.8) and, more notably, on histone-mark profiles (88.7 vs. 85.6) — the sub-task the paper notes is "known to have longer-range correlations" and so should benefit most from extra context — while landing essentially tied, and very slightly behind, on DNase-sensitivity profiles (92.1 vs. 92.3). The ablation in Figure 8 (masked-LM accuracy vs. training steps, at three context lengths) is the paper's direct evidence that the context-length increase itself is doing the work: longer context wins throughout training, not just asymptotically.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

The theory is the strongest part of this paper — Theorem 1 and Theorem 3 are genuine proofs, not empirical claims, and Proposition 1's matching lower bound is an unusually honest touch: most efficient-attention papers don't go looking for a task their own method provably can't do efficiently. The empirical side is broad (QA, classification, summarization, two genomics tasks) and mostly wins, but two things are worth separating from the paper's own framing.

First, the central practical claim — sequences "up to 8x" longer "using similar hardware" — is a memory argument (O(n²) → O(n) attention cost, plus blockified sparse ops that map efficiently onto GPU/TPU memory access patterns), not a measured wall-clock speed or throughput number. The paper reports the base MLM models were trained "on a reasonable 16GB memory/chip with batch size of 32-64," which supports the capacity claim, but nowhere in the main results is there a tokens-per-second or steps-per-hour comparison against full attention at matched sequence lengths. Readers get accuracy-vs-context-length (Figure 8) but not speed-vs-context-length — the two are related but not the same measurement, and the paper's title promises "longer sequences," which is delivered, more directly than it promises a wall-clock speedup, which is only implied.

Second, the sparse-attention comparison set is thin: the paper itself notes "there is one exception of Longformer which we include in all our comparisons," and the related-work section discusses Sparse Transformer, Reformer, and other O(n√n)/O(n log n) methods without ever putting their numbers next to BigBird's on the same benchmark. That leaves open whether BigBird's gains come specifically from its random+window+global recipe, or would show up for any reasonably-designed O(n) sparse pattern at these sequence lengths.

baselines

Full-attention baselines (BERT, RoBERTa) are fair and contemporary; the sparse-attention baseline set is essentially one model (Longformer) despite several other O(n√n)/O(n log n) methods being discussed but never benchmarked head-to-head.

contamination

Not the typical leakage concern for an architecture paper, but base models warm-start from a public RoBERTa checkpoint, so gains over RoBERTa baselines partially inherit RoBERTa's own pretraining-data provenance rather than starting from scratch.

what's missed

No wall-clock throughput/latency benchmark anywhere in the main text — the "8x longer, similar hardware" claim is argued via memory complexity and a 16GB/chip training footprint, not a measured speed comparison at matched context lengths.

would raise confidence

A tokens-per-second or steps-per-hour table across context lengths versus full attention; numeric head-to-head results against Sparse Transformer/Reformer, not just Longformer; and a comment on the DHS chromatin task, the one result where longer context (92.1) is a hair behind the DeepSea baseline (92.3).

// bottom line

BigBird earns its citations on the theory: a sparse attention pattern that's provably as expressive as full attention, with a matching proof that sparsity does cost something on at least one task. The empirical wins are real and span QA, summarization, classification, and a genuinely new genomics application — but the paper's efficiency story is a memory-capacity argument dressed as a speed argument, and its only apples-to-apples sparse-attention competitor throughout is Longformer.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. BigBird = random attention + a sliding window + global tokens, cutting attention from O(n²) to O(n) while Theorem 1 (universal approximation) and Theorem 3 (Turing completeness) prove this doesn't sacrifice full attention's expressive power.
  2. The global-token piece is empirically load-bearing, not decorative: random+window alone underperforms full-attention BERT-base even at the same 512-token length (Table 1) — sparsity alone doesn't recover BERT's quality, only the full random+window+global recipe does.
  3. Longer context wins on QA leaderboards (new SOTA on Natural Questions LA, TriviaQA, WikiHop), summarization (+8.35 ROUGE-1 over Pegasus on BigPatent), and a new genomics application (99.9 F1 on promoter-region prediction) — but the paper never directly measures the wall-clock speedup implied by its "8x longer sequences" headline.
