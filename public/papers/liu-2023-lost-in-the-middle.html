Lost in the Middle: How Language Models Use Long Contexts

A 16K-token context window does not mean a model reads 16K tokens evenly. In controlled experiments, accuracy traces a U — highest when the answer sits at the very start or end of the input, sagging in the middle, sometimes below answering with no documents at all. The effect survives in models explicitly built for long contexts.

Nelson F. Liu, Kevin Lin, John Hewitt, Ashwin Paranjape, Michele Bevilacqua, Fabio Petroni, Percy Liang · Stanford / UC Berkeley / Samaya AI

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

By mid-2023 the headline number on a language model had become its context window: 4K gave way to 16K, 32K, even Anthropic's 100K. The marketing implied a capability — feed the model a long legal brief, a codebase, or fifty retrieved passages and it will use all of it. But almost nothing was known about whether models actually do. Most long-context work measured perplexity on a web corpus as a proxy for "handling" long inputs, which rewards smooth next-token prediction over distant but mostly-redundant text. It says nothing about whether a model can reach in and pull a single relevant fact out of position 50 of 100.

This matters because the dominant way models touch long text is retrieval-augmented generation: a search system dumps the top-k documents into the prompt, and the model is supposed to find the answer among them. If the model only reliably reads the ends of its context, then retrieval recall — getting the right document somewhere in the prompt — stops predicting task success, and "just retrieve more documents" can quietly make things worse.

The paper's framing is a clean falsifiable test. If a model genuinely uses its whole context, then moving the relevant information around inside the prompt should not change the answer — performance must be roughly invariant to position. So instead of inventing a new leaderboard, the authors hold the task fixed and vary only two things: where the answer sits and how long the context is. Any sensitivity to position is direct evidence that the model is not reading robustly.

// how it works

The method

Two controlled tasks, each with exactly two knobs — position of the relevant content and total context length — so that any change in accuracy can only be attributed to those two factors.

1 · Multi-document question answering

The model receives a question plus k Wikipedia passages (chunks of ≤100 tokens), of which exactly one contains the answer and k−1 are distractors. Questions and gold answers come from NaturalQuestions-Open — 2,655 real Google queries whose annotated long answer is a paragraph. The distractors are not random: they are the most relevant non-answer chunks returned by a Contriever retriever (fine-tuned on MS-MARCO), so the task mimics a real RAG prompt where every document looks on-topic. To probe position, the authors slide the answer-bearing document to rank 1, the middle, or the end; to probe length, they use 10, 20, or 30 total documents (~2K / 4K / 6K tokens). Accuracy is whether any gold answer string appears in the output.

2 · Synthetic key–value retrieval

To strip away semantics and language confounds, a second task gives the model a JSON object of k key–value pairs — every key and value a random 128-bit UUID — and asks it to return the value for one specified key. This is the most generous possible test of "can you find a token in your context": no reasoning, no paraphrase, just exact-match lookup. They run 75, 140, and 300 pairs (~4K / 8K / 16K tokens), 500 examples each, sliding the target key across positions.

The models span the open/closed and standard/extended-context grid of the era: MPT-30B-Instruct (8K, ALiBi positions), LongChat-13B (16K) (LLaMA-13B stretched with condensed rotary embeddings), GPT-3.5-Turbo (4K) and its 16K sibling, and Claude-1.3 (8K) and Claude-1.3 (100K). Decoding is greedy. The whole design rests on one diagnostic:

Δ = maxp Acc(p) − minp Acc(p) // a robust long-context model should have Δ ≈ 0 — accuracy invariant to where the relevant token sits. Large Δ = the context is being read unevenly.

To contextualize the numbers there are two anchors: a closed-book setting (no documents — pure parametric memory) and an oracle setting (only the single answer document). A model that uses context well should sit near oracle regardless of position; one that is "lost in the middle" can dip toward — or below — closed-book.

// what they found

The results

A U-shaped curve, not a flat line

Across context lengths, accuracy is highest when the answer document is at the very beginning (primacy bias) or the very end (recency bias) and falls in the middle. For GPT-3.5-Turbo the gap between best and worst position exceeds 20 points, and in the 20- and 30-document settings the mid-context accuracy drops below its closed-book score of 56.1% — the model does worse with the right document buried in the prompt than with no documents at all. The curve steepens as the context gets longer: a wider middle is a deeper trough.

The closed-book / oracle anchors make the size of the effect concrete: the gap between "answer is present and findable" (oracle) and "no documents" (closed-book) is enormous, and the U-shape says position alone can erase a large fraction of it.

A bigger window is not better use of it

When a context fits inside both a model and its extended-context counterpart, their position curves are nearly superimposed — GPT-3.5-Turbo and GPT-3.5-Turbo (16K), Claude-1.3 and Claude-1.3 (100K). Buying a longer maximum context length did not, on its own, buy better use of context. The window grew; the reading habit did not.

Even pure retrieval breaks in the middle

The synthetic key–value task removes every excuse — it is exact-match lookup of a UUID. Claude-1.3 and Claude-1.3 (100K) solve it nearly perfectly at all lengths, proving the task is doable. But GPT-3.5-Turbo, its 16K sibling, and MPT-30B-Instruct still sag in the middle once there are 140 or 300 pairs, with worst-case accuracy as low as 45.6%. (LongChat-13B sometimes degenerates into writing code to look up the key instead of returning the value.) If a model can't reliably copy a token from the middle of its context, it certainly can't reason over one.

Why does it happen?

Three preliminary probes into the cause:

  • Architecture. Encoder–decoder models (Flan-T5-XXL, Flan-UL2) are robust to position — Flan-UL2 shows just a 1.9-point best-vs-worst gap — but only when evaluated within their training-time sequence length (2048 tokens). Push them past it and the U returns. The hypothesis: a bidirectional encoder lets each document be contextualized by the documents that come after it, improving relative-importance estimation.
  • Query-aware contextualization. Placing the query both before and after the data lets a decoder-only model condition the documents on the question. This makes key–value retrieval near-perfect (GPT-3.5-Turbo-16K hits 100% even at 300 pairs) — but barely moves the multi-document QA curve.
  • It is not instruction tuning. The base MPT-30B is U-shaped before any instruction tuning; tuning only narrows the best–worst gap from ~10 points to ~4. And the curve is a function of scale: Llama-2 7B is only recency-biased, while 13B and 70B develop the full U.

Is more context always better? A RAG case study

Finally, an open-domain QA test with a real retriever–reader pipeline: reader accuracy saturates long before retriever recall does. Going from 20 to 50 retrieved documents lifts GPT-3.5-Turbo by only ~1.5% and Claude-1.3 by ~1%, while multiplying latency and cost. The practical implications the authors draw: rerank so the most relevant document lands near the start, and truncate the ranked list rather than stuffing the window. The connection they name is psychological — the serial-position effect (Ebbinghaus, 1913; Murdock, 1962), where humans best recall the first and last items of a list — which is striking to see in a self-attention mechanism that is, in principle, equally able to reach any token.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

This is one of the most-cited empirical findings of the long-context era, and the design is genuinely clean — controlling position while holding the task fixed neutralizes most confounds at once. But it is a 2023 snapshot, and the parts that aged need to be separated from the parts that didn't.

The model roster is the obvious dated edge: GPT-3.5-Turbo, Claude-1.3, MPT-30B, LongChat-13B. GPT-4 was run only on a subset of the experiments — the authors note a full sweep would have cost upward of $6,000 — so the strongest 2023 model is undersampled, and it already showed higher absolute accuracy. Since then, changes to positional encodings, attention, and explicit "needle-in-a-haystack" training have flattened the curve substantially in frontier models. The absolute numbers here do not transfer; the protocol and the qualitative warning do.

On leakage: NaturalQuestions-Open is squarely in pretraining data, which is exactly why closed-book scores are so high (56.1% for GPT-3.5 with no documents). That contamination doesn't confound the headline, though — the manipulation is position, and the same items are simply reordered, so memorization is held constant across the curve. It does muddy the secondary "worse than closed-book" comparison, since closed-book is buoyed by memorized answers. (NQ's known answer-ambiguity — a stray distractor occasionally containing a valid answer — is addressed: re-running on an unambiguous subset gives the same trends.)

What the headline can oversell: the multi-document QA U is real but modest in absolute terms (single-to-low-double-digit points for most models); the dramatic mid-context collapses live mostly in the synthetic key–value task, which by construction (random UUIDs, exact match) is the cleanest possible retrieval probe but the least representative of real RAG, where semantics and reasoning interact. And the "why" section is explicitly preliminary — the encoder–decoder comparison pits Flan models against GPT/Claude/MPT, so model family, scale, and training length all vary at once; the bidirectional-encoder story is suggestive, not a controlled ablation.

baselines

Era-appropriate but dated: GPT-3.5-Turbo, Claude-1.3, MPT-30B, LongChat-13B; GPT-4 only on a subset for cost (~$6K for a full run). Newer models have partly closed the gap, so absolute numbers don't carry forward.

contamination

NaturalQuestions-Open is in pretraining data (hence 56.1% closed-book), but the position manipulation holds memorization constant, so leakage doesn't drive the U. It does inflate the closed-book anchor used in the "worse than no documents" claim.

what's missed

Greedy decoding only; lenient string-match accuracy; the most extreme failures are on a deliberately artificial UUID task. The mechanism probes vary family/scale/training-length together, so the architecture claim isn't fully isolated.

would raise confidence

Matched-architecture enc-dec vs dec-only ablations; attention/mechanistic analysis of why the middle is dropped; full GPT-4-class sweep; genuinely long (100K+) contexts where the middle is vast; a downstream test that reranking/truncation actually fixes RAG.

// bottom line

A controlled, reproducible diagnosis that a longer context window is not the same as reading the context — it named the U-shaped "lost-in-the-middle" effect and handed the field a position-invariance evaluation protocol. The specific models are obsolete and the cleanest collapses come from a synthetic probe, but the phenomenon and the protocol have held up, and the practical fixes it motivated — reranking relevant content to the edges, truncating ranked lists, context compression — became standard RAG hygiene.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. Position is a first-class variable. Accuracy traces a U — strong at the start (primacy) and end (recency), weak in the middle. Burying the answer mid-context can cost GPT-3.5-Turbo over 20 points, dropping it below its 56.1% closed-book score.
  2. A bigger context window ≠ better use of context. Extended-context variants nearly match their base siblings on the same prompt, and reader accuracy saturates long before retriever recall — 20→50 documents buys only ~1–1.5%. More context is a trade-off, not a free lunch.
  3. The cause is structural, and the legacy is the protocol. The U appears even in base (non-instruction-tuned) models, emerges with scale, and is resisted by encoder–decoder models within their training length. The lasting contribution is the rule: to claim robust long-context use, show that performance is roughly invariant to where the relevant information sits.
