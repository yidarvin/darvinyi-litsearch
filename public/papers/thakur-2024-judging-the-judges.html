Judging the Judges: Evaluating Alignment and Vulnerabilities in LLMs-as-Judges

If you grade language models with another language model, how trustworthy is the grader? This paper rigs the deck in the judge's favour — a clean, factual task where humans agree 98.5% of the time — and finds that even there, only the largest judges come close to human alignment, while the field's favourite metric quietly flatters all of them.

Aman Singh Thakur, Kartik Choudhary, Venkat Srinik Ramayapally, Sankaran Vaidyanathan, Dieuwke Hupkes · UMass Amherst & Meta

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

Human evaluation is the gold standard for judging free-form LLM outputs, but it is slow and expensive, so the field has stampeded toward LLM-as-a-judge: prompt a strong model to grade another model's answers. The paradigm now underpins leaderboards, RLHF reward signals, and most rubric-based evals. The trouble is that its reliability is mostly asserted rather than carefully measured.

The usual validation — show that GPT-4's verdicts correlate with humans on open-ended tasks (MT-Bench, Chatbot Arena, story or RAG evaluation) — has a confound baked in: on subjective, open-ended prompts humans themselves disagree. When a judge diverges from a human label, you cannot tell whether the judge is wrong or the task is simply ambiguous. And almost every study reports a single headline number (percent agreement, or correlation with a reference judge) that tends to look reassuringly high.

This paper removes the confound by deliberately choosing the cleanest possible task: short-answer factual question answering on TriviaQA, where there is a reference answer and a binary correct/incorrect verdict. Here three human annotators agree 98.52% ± 0.42% of the time (Scott's π of 96.2 ± 1.07). With the task's own ambiguity driven nearly to zero, any remaining gap between a judge and a human is the judge's fault — letting the authors isolate, for the first time in such a sterile setting, how good LLM judges actually are and where they break.

// how it works

The method

The setup is a matrix of judges grading exam-takers. Thirteen judge models span sizes and families: instruction-tuned Llama-2 (7B/13B/70B), Llama-3 (8B/70B), Llama-3.1 (8B/70B), Mistral 7B, Gemma 2B, the purpose-built JudgeLM 7B, and GPT-4 Turbo — plus two lexical baselines, exact match (EM) and contains (the reference answer appears as a case-insensitive substring of the response). These judges grade answers from nine exam-taker configurations: Llama-2 7B/13B/70B and Mistral 7B in both base and chat versions, plus GPT-4 Turbo.

The testbed is a random sample of 400 TriviaQA questions (a bootstrap test in the appendix confirms this sample is low-variance for the main result). Crucially, judging is reference-based, not pairwise: each judge sees the question, the model's answer, and the gold short answer, and must reply with a single word — "correct" or "incorrect". Ground truth comes from human majority vote, validated by having three annotators label 1,200 questions (the near-perfect 96.2 π above), after which a single annotator per sample is used to keep costs down.

Alignment between a judge and the humans is measured two ways. The naïve way is percent agreement — the fraction of samples where they give the same verdict. The better way is Scott's π, which corrects for the agreement you would expect by chance given how often each side says "correct." (The authors explicitly switched from Cohen's κ to Scott's π between versions, citing κ's well-documented pathologies.) Chance-correction matters: on a task where most answers are correct, two annotators who both mostly say "correct" rack up high raw agreement for free.

π = Po − Pe1 − Pe // Po = observed agreement;  Pe = Σ pi² = chance agreement from the pooled category proportions

To rank exam-takers (rather than score them), they also compute Spearman's ρ between each judge's model ordering and the human ordering. Finally, a battery of stress tests probes why judges fail: precision/recall and error-type breakdowns, sensitivity to prompt length and specificity, responses to planted dummy answers, and an estimate of each judge's leniency bias.

// what they found

The results

Only the biggest judges align — and even they fall short. Of the thirteen judges, only GPT-4 Turbo, Llama-3 70B, and Llama-3.1 70B reach Scott's π in the high 80s (88, 88, and 87). The best judge, Llama-3 70B at π = 88, still sits 8 points below the 96 human–human coefficient, and even these top judges' assigned scores can differ from human scores by up to 5 points. At the bottom, Gemma 2B (π = 26) and exact match (π = 34) are essentially noise.

Percent agreement is deceptive; Scott's π separates judges. Raw percent agreement is high for almost every judge, hiding the real spread. Llama-3 8B, for instance, posts a Scott's π of only 59 while its percent agreement is still well above 80%. For EM, the gap to humans is 26 points on percent agreement but a far more telling 64 points on Scott's π. And judges scoring above 90% percent agreement can still be more than 10 points off in the scores they assign — a discrepancy invisible to anyone reporting only raw agreement.

Ranking ≠ scoring. If you only need to order models on a leaderboard, you do not need an expensive judge at all. The dumb contains substring check ranks the nine exam-takers at Spearman ρ = 0.99, and Mistral 7B at ρ = 0.98 — matching or beating GPT-4 Turbo and the large Llamas, despite much worse alignment scores. Most judges clear ρ = 0.7. A judge with a consistent bias is like a strict-but-fair teacher: wrong on absolute scores, right on the ordering. Getting the right ranking is simply not the same problem as assigning the right score.

Where the errors come from. Both precision and recall rise with alignment (R² = 0.31 for recall, 0.21 for precision), and the gains are driven mainly by better recall — fewer false negatives. As judge quality drops, false positives climb. A manual error analysis of 900 outputs shows GPT-4 Turbo and Llama-3 70B reliably catch answers that name the wrong entity or list too many entities, but both struggle badly with under-specified answers that give only part of the correct response.

Prompt fragility and gullibility. Top judges are robust to prompt length (tested at 45, 58, 245, and 301 tokens) but, the authors note, are correspondingly hard to steer; weaker judges actually lose alignment as instructions get longer and more elaborate. A simple planted-answer probe is damning: when a dummy exam-taker just replies "Yes" or "Sure" to a factual question, several judges — Llama-2 70B most notably — mark it correct, despite that same judge looking well-aligned on the real benchmark. And even when the answer is a verbatim copy of the gold reference, judges do not always return "correct."

Underlying much of this is a leniency bias: the authors estimate each judge's tendency to default to "correct" when its criteria are not perfectly aligned, and find that probability (P+) is significantly above 0.5 for most judges. When in doubt, LLM judges give the benefit of the doubt.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

The clean-task design is simultaneously the paper's greatest strength and its hard ceiling. By choosing short-answer factual QA with a gold reference and a binary verdict, the authors get a setting where humans agree 98.5% — but that is the easiest judging task imaginable, and almost the opposite of where LLM-judges are actually deployed: open-ended helpfulness, writing quality, multi-turn dialogue, pairwise preference, and no-reference grading (exactly the MT-Bench / Chatbot Arena / AlpacaFarm regimes). The authors' own logic says judges will be worse in the wild, which makes these results an optimistic upper bound — useful, but unable to say by how much real-world reliability degrades. The paper's most quotable finding, that the contains substring matcher ranks models as well as GPT-4 Turbo (ρ = 0.99), is itself an artifact of short factual answers being substring-checkable; it would collapse on free-form generation.

The model roster is also a 2024 snapshot. Judges top out at GPT-4 Turbo and Llama-3.1 — no Claude, Gemini, GPT-4o, and crucially no reasoning models, which are precisely the kind of slow, deliberate grader that might close the 8-point gap. "Only the biggest align, and they're still short of humans" is true as of this roster, but the human–judge gap is being measured against a single, now-superseded frontier model. Two more caveats temper the precision: the main results are scored against a single human annotator per sample (the 3-annotator validation was done separately on a different 1,200-question set), and TriviaQA (2017) almost certainly sits in every model's pretraining — which confounds the exam-takers' raw scores and lets judges lean on memorized facts rather than the reference-comparison skill being measured.

baselines

Strong and wide: two lexical baselines (EM, contains) plus 13 LLM judges across five families and sizes. But the panel is capped at GPT-4 Turbo / Llama-3.1 — no Claude, Gemini, GPT-4o, or reasoning judges — so the "frontier" ceiling is already dated.

contamination

TriviaQA (2017) predates every model and is near-certainly in pretraining. It confounds exam-taker scores and lets judges fall back on parametric knowledge instead of comparing to the reference — the very skill under test.

what's missed

Everything outside short-answer factual QA: open-ended generation, pairwise preference, multi-turn, and reference-free grading — i.e. where LLM-judges are actually used. The headline "contains ≈ GPT-4" ranking result is task-specific and will not transfer.

would raise confidence

Re-run on open-ended and pairwise tasks; add frontier and reasoning judges; use multiple human annotators throughout the main results; and validate on a contamination-controlled or post-cutoff benchmark.

// bottom line

A rigorous, honest negative result on the easy end of the difficulty curve. It proves that even under ideal conditions LLM-judges trail humans, and that a single agreement number flatters all of them. But precisely because the setup is so sterile, it is best read as a floor on the problem — a demonstration that the paradigm is imperfect at its best — rather than a measurement of judge reliability in the messy, reference-free settings where these judges really run.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. Even in the cleanest possible setting — short-answer TriviaQA where humans agree 98.5% — only the largest judges (GPT-4 Turbo, Llama-3 / 3.1 70B) reach Scott's π in the high 80s, still ~8 points below human–human agreement and up to 5 points off on the scores they assign. Small judges like Gemma 2B (π = 26) are noise.
  2. Report Scott's π, not just percent agreement. Raw agreement clusters every judge near the top and hides 10–20-point scoring errors; chance-corrected agreement actually separates good judges from bad. And note that a substring contains check ranks models at ρ = 0.99 — getting the ranking right is a different, easier problem than getting the score right.
  3. LLM judges have systematic, exploitable biases: leniency (defaulting to "correct" when unsure, P+ > 0.5), fragility to prompt length on weaker models, and gullibility to throwaway answers like "Yes" or "Sure." High benchmark alignment does not mean a judge is robust — audit it qualitatively before trusting it.
