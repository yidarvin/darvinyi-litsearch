LLaMA: Open and Efficient Foundation Language Models

A 13B model trained on a trillion public tokens beats GPT-3 (175B) on most benchmarks, and a 65B trained on 1.4T rivals Chinchilla-70B and PaLM-540B — built entirely from data anyone can download, and released to the research community. The paper that re-aimed scaling at inference cost, and lit the fuse on the open-weights era.

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

By early 2023, the recipe for a frontier language model was a closed one. The strongest systems — GPT-3, Gopher, Chinchilla, PaLM — were trained on data that was either proprietary, undocumented, or both ("Books – 2TB", "social media conversations"), and the weights stayed inside the labs that made them. Academic study of large models had become spectating: you could read a paper but not run, probe, or fine-tune the artifact it described.

There was also a deeper modelling assumption worth questioning. The dominant scaling result, Chinchilla (Hoffmann et al., 2022), answered the question "for a fixed training compute budget, how big should the model be and how many tokens should it see?" — and concluded that most models were oversized and undertrained. But that objective optimizes the wrong thing for anyone who actually deploys a model. As the authors put it: that objective "disregards the inference budget, which becomes critical when serving a language model at scale." Once a model is in production, you pay for its parameter count on every single token of every request, forever — training is a one-time cost.

LLaMA reframes the goal: not the model that is cheapest to train to a target quality, but the one that is cheapest to serve at that quality. That points the other way from Chinchilla — toward smaller models trained well past their compute-optimal token count — and it sets up the paper's two-part bet: (1) you can reach the frontier with public data only, and (2) a deliberately "overtrained" small model is the better artifact to ship.

// how it works

The method

LLaMA is a family of four decoder-only Transformers — 6.7B, 13B, 32.5B, and 65.2B parameters — trained only on data with public provenance. The 7B and 13B models see 1.0T tokens; the 33B and 65B see 1.4T. That token count is the whole point: a Chinchilla-style rule would have trained a 7B on a fraction of that, but LLaMA keeps going, because the curves were still dropping (Figure 1) and the resulting model is cheap to run.

The inference-cost argument, in one line

Training compute scales with both model size N and tokens D; inference cost scales with N alone. So for a fixed quality target you should push D up and N down — accept a bigger training bill to win on the bill that never stops.

Ctrain  ≈  6 · N · D     |     Cinference  ∝  N   (per token) // Chinchilla minimizes Ctrain for fixed compute; LLaMA minimizes N for fixed quality, paying more D to get there

The data — public only (~1.4T tokens)

The entire mixture is reproducible from open sources, weighted as: CommonCrawl 67%, C4 15%, GitHub 4.5% (Apache/BSD/MIT only), Wikipedia 4.5% (20 languages), books (Gutenberg + the Books3 slice of The Pile) 4.5%, ArXiv 2.5%, and Stack Exchange 2%. Tokenized with byte-pair encoding (SentencePiece), falling back to bytes for unknown characters. The authors flag the academic-text share as small — ArXiv + Gutenberg + Books3 sum to only 177 GB — a detail that returns to bite them on MMLU.

Architecture — the now-standard stack

LLaMA collected the improvements that had accumulated since GPT-3 into one widely-copied recipe:

  • Pre-normalization with RMSNorm (Zhang & Sennrich, 2019) — normalize each sub-layer's input rather than its output, for training stability.
  • SwiGLU activation (Shazeer, 2020, from PaLM) replacing ReLU, with a hidden dimension of ⅔·4d.
  • Rotary positional embeddings (RoPE) (Su et al., 2021, from GPTNeo) at every layer, instead of absolute position embeddings.

Optimization: AdamW (β₁=0.9, β₂=0.95), a cosine schedule decaying to 10% of the peak learning rate, weight decay 0.1, gradient clipping 1.0, 2,000 warmup steps, 4M-token batches. The efficiency engineering is as load-bearing as the architecture: a memory-efficient causal attention from the xformers library (it never materializes the attention matrix and skips the masked scores), plus manual activation checkpointing and overlapped compute/communication. The payoff: ~380 tokens/sec/GPU on 2,048 A100-80GB GPUs, so 1.4T tokens trains in roughly 21 days.

// what they found

The results

The headline lands: LLaMA-13B outperforms GPT-3 (175B) on most benchmarks despite being >10× smaller and running on a single GPU, and LLaMA-65B is competitive with Chinchilla-70B and PaLM-540B across a 20-benchmark zero-/few-shot suite.

On the eight common-sense reasoning tasks (zero-shot), LLaMA-65B beats Chinchilla-70B on every benchmark but BoolQ, and PaLM-540B on every one but BoolQ and WinoGrande. On closed-book NaturalQuestions (exact match), LLaMA-65B scores 23.8 zero-shot — ahead of GPT-3 (14.6), Chinchilla-70B (16.6) and PaLM-540B (21.2) — rising to 39.9 at 64-shot. On math, LLaMA-65B reaches 50.9 on GSM8k (69.7 with majority voting over 100 samples) and 10.6 on MATH, edging out Minerva-62B on GSM8k despite no mathematical fine-tuning. On code (HumanEval / MBPP pass@1), LLaMA-13B (15.8 / 22.0) already beats LaMDA-137B (14.0 / 14.8), and LLaMA-65B (23.7 / 37.7) clears PaLM-62B.

The one clear loss is MMLU: LLaMA-65B averages 63.4 (5-shot), trailing Chinchilla-70B (67.5) and PaLM-540B (69.3) "by a few percent … across most domains." The authors' own diagnosis is the data mix — too few books and academic papers (that 177 GB). A single instruction-tuning experiment (LLaMA-I) lifts MMLU to 68.9, a strong hint that the base model was not the finished product.

On safety/bias probes the picture is mixed and honest: on TruthfulQA, LLaMA-65B scores 0.57 truthful / 0.53 truthful-and-informative — well above GPT-3 (0.28 / 0.25) but, the authors note, "still low, showing that our model is likely to hallucinate." CrowS-Pairs bias averages 66.6, marginally below GPT-3 (67.2) and OPT-175B (69.5), and toxicity on RealToxicityPrompts rises with model size. The full development run is costed at ~2,638 MWh and 1,015 tCO₂eq — disclosed, with the argument that releasing the weights spares the community from re-training.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

As an evaluation, LLaMA is more a demonstration than a controlled study, and it leans on a comparison that flatters it. The marquee claim — "beats GPT-3" — is measured against the 2020 GPT-3 numbers, a three-year-old, un-instruction-tuned baseline that no one was actually deploying by 2023. The fairer bar is the contemporary frontier (Chinchilla-70B, PaLM-540B), and against those LLaMA-65B is "competitive" but loses on MMLU. None of the baseline numbers were re-run by the authors: they are quoted from the original papers, with some values explicitly "read from figures in Chowdhery et al." — meaning prompt formats, few-shot example selection, and decoding differ across every row, so the head-to-head columns are not strictly commensurable.

The benchmark suite itself is almost entirely multiple-choice and exact-match academic tasks. That measures what a base model knows, not whether it can follow an instruction, hold a conversation, or do useful work — exactly the axes on which a raw pretrained model is weakest and on which the field had already moved (InstructGPT, ChatGPT). The single LLaMA-I instruction experiment (MMLU 63.4 → 68.9) quietly concedes this: the released base weights are a starting point, not the product. And there is essentially no contamination analysis — 1.4T tokens of CommonCrawl/C4/GitHub plausibly overlap web-sourced evals like TriviaQA, NaturalQuestions, and the common-sense sets, yet the paper offers no train-test overlap audit, so absolute scores should be read with that caveat.

None of this dents the paper's actual importance, which the academic tables undersell. LLaMA's impact was the release: open, runnable, fine-tunable weights that — after leaking weeks later — seeded Alpaca, Vicuna, and the entire open-weights ecosystem this atlas is full of. Its legacy is sociological as much as scientific, and "13B > GPT-3" is the line that made that release impossible to ignore.

baselines

Numbers quoted from other papers (some read off figures), never re-run under a shared harness; the hero comparison is the stale 2020 GPT-3, while the live frontier (Chinchilla, PaLM) is only matched, and beaten on MMLU.

contamination

No train-test overlap study despite 1.4T tokens of web/code data and web-derived evals (TriviaQA, NaturalQuestions, HellaSwag) — absolute scores may be inflated by unknown leakage.

what's missed

All multiple-choice / exact-match; nothing measures instruction-following, dialogue, or real tasks. No red-teaming or jailbreak testing. Truthfulness (0.57) and bias remain non-trivial; the base model is not the usable artifact.

would raise confidence

A single re-evaluation harness across all baselines with fixed prompts/decoding; an explicit contamination audit; and instruction-tuned + human-preference evals against same-size tuned competitors.

// bottom line

A genuinely important result wrapped in a generous benchmark framing. The scientific claim that holds is the inference-aware scaling thesis — small models trained long are the artifact you want to ship — demonstrated convincingly by Figures 1–2. The "beats GPT-3" headline is true but cherry-picks a dated baseline, and the evaluation skips the contamination check and the instruction-following dimension that would tell you whether the model is actually good rather than merely efficient. The release, not the leaderboard, is why it mattered.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. Optimize for inference, not training. Chinchilla minimizes training compute for a quality target; LLaMA minimizes the model you have to serve, by training a smaller model on far more tokens (1.0–1.4T). For anything you deploy at scale, that's the right objective.
  2. Public data is enough to reach the frontier. No proprietary corpora — CommonCrawl, C4, GitHub, Wikipedia, ArXiv, Stack Exchange — and LLaMA-13B still beat GPT-3 (175B) on most benchmarks while running on a single GPU.
  3. The architecture became the default. Pre-norm + RMSNorm, SwiGLU, and RoPE — assembled here — are now the standard open-LLM stack, and the released (then leaked) weights launched Alpaca/Vicuna and the open-weights era. Caveats: the MMLU gap traces to a thin academic-data mix, and the eval is base-model multiple-choice with no contamination audit or instruction-following test.
