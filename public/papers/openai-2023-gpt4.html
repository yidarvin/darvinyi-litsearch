GPT-4 Technical Report

A large multimodal model that scores at human level on the bar exam, the SAT and 57-subject MMLU — paired with a genuine predictable-scaling result, and a report that, citing "the competitive landscape," discloses nothing about its architecture, data, or training.

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

By early 2023, scaling laws (Kaplan, Chinchilla) had established that a language model's pre-training loss falls predictably as a power law in compute. But no one had shown you could forecast a frontier model's downstream capabilities — its actual scores on tasks — before spending the money to train it. With a flagship run costing tens of millions of dollars, a model that disappoints only after training is an enormously expensive surprise. The first gap GPT-4 targets is de-risking the run: build infrastructure whose behaviour is predictable across three-to-four orders of magnitude of scale (runs with 1,000×–10,000× less compute), so a cheap small run tells you what the giant one will do.

The second gap is communication. LM evaluation had fragmented into dozens of NLP benchmarks that strong models were already saturating, and none of them answered the question a non-specialist actually asks: how capable is this thing? GPT-4's answer is to evaluate the model the way you'd evaluate a person — on professional and academic exams written for humans (the Uniform Bar Exam, LSAT, SAT, GRE, AP suite, USABO, and more), reported as human percentile ranks.

And there is a third, quieter shift the report enacts rather than solves. The GPT-2 and GPT-3 papers described their architecture and training data. This one does not — it is the moment a frontier-model "technical report" stops being reproducible.

// how it works

The method

The report's entire architectural disclosure is essentially one sentence: GPT-4 is "a Transformer-based model pre-trained to predict the next token in a document," then aligned with reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF), the same recipe as InstructGPT. No model size, no dataset, no compute, no training method. What it does describe in detail is the one thing that is genuinely novel here: predictable scaling.

OpenAI built optimization methods and infrastructure that "behave predictably across a wide range of scales." Final loss of a properly-trained LM is well-approximated by a power law in training compute, so they fit that law on a ladder of small models and extrapolated it to the full run. They predicted GPT-4's final loss on an internal codebase next-word-prediction dataset from models trained with as little as 1/1,000th to 1/10,000th the compute — and the actual GPT-4 point landed right on the extrapolation (Figure 1).

L(C) = a · Cb + c // final loss L approximated as a power law in training compute C, fit on small runs and extrapolated to GPT-4

Loss is one thing; an interpretable capability is another. They went further and predicted mean log pass rate on a 23-problem subset of HumanEval (Python coding) by extrapolating from runs with up to 1,000× less compute (Figure 2). The honest caveat, in their own words: this worked on "the easiest bucket," and "certain capabilities remain hard to predict" — on the Inverse Scaling Prize's Hindsight Neglect task, GPT-4 actually reverses the expected decline rather than continuing it.

Finally, on alignment: RLHF here changes behaviour, not raw ability. OpenAI's evaluations "suggest RLHF does not significantly affect the base GPT-4 model's capability" — averaged across the exam suite, the base model scores 73.7% and the post-RLHF model 74.0%. The lift you feel when chatting with GPT-4 is the model being made helpful and steerable, not smarter than its pre-trained self.

// what they found

The results

The headline is human-level exam performance. GPT-4 scores 298/400 (~90th percentile) on a simulated Uniform Bar Exam — where GPT-3.5 sits at 213/400, the bottom 10% — plus 163 (~88th) on the LSAT, 710/800 (~93rd) on SAT Reading & Writing and 700/800 (~89th) on SAT Math. On traditional academic benchmarks (few-shot), it posts 86.4% on MMLU (57 subjects), 95.3% on HellaSwag, 96.3% on ARC-Challenge, 87.5% on WinoGrande and 67.0% on HumanEval — clearing GPT-3.5 and the prior published SOTA on each.

GPT-4 is also the first of the line to be multimodal: it accepts arbitrarily interleaved image-and-text input and produces text (the report shows it reading charts, memes and exam diagrams), though the vision capability was not broadly released at launch. And it is strikingly multilingual — on machine-translated MMLU it reaches 85.5% in English and beats the prior English-language SOTA in 24 of 26 languages; its scores in Welsh, Swahili and Bengali all clear GPT-3.5's 70.1% English mark (Figure 3).

On factuality, GPT-4 scores 19 percentage points above the latest GPT-3.5 on OpenAI's internal, adversarially-designed factuality evals (Figure 4), reaches ~60% on TruthfulQA after RLHF, and at launch was 19 / 29 points better at avoiding open- / closed-domain hallucinations. On safety, post-mitigation GPT-4 reduced its tendency to respond to requests for disallowed content by 82% versus GPT-3.5, while complying with genuinely sensitive requests (e.g. medical, self-harm) in line with policy 29% more often.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

The defining feature of this report is what it refuses to say. "Given both the competitive landscape and the safety implications of large-scale models like GPT-4, this report contains no further details about the architecture (including model size), hardware, training compute, dataset construction, training method, or similar." Every number below is therefore unreproducible by anyone outside OpenAI — you cannot rerun it, cannot inspect the data, cannot verify that baselines were measured under matched protocols. That is a real break with the contract the phrase "technical report" usually implies.

The sharpest evaluation risk is contamination, and it cuts straight at the marquee claim. GPT-4's pitch is human-exam performance — but the bar exam, the SAT, the AP suite and MMLU are all over the public web that the model was trained on. OpenAI's disclosed check is a substring match: strip spaces and symbols from each eval example, randomly sample three 50-character substrings, and flag the example as contaminated if any one is a substring of a processed training document, then discard and rerun for an "uncontaminated" score. By their own admission this "can result in false negatives" — paraphrases, reformatting and translated copies sail through. They also discovered that BIG-bench had been "inadvertently mixed into the training set" (and excluded it), and that part of the GSM-8K training set was deliberately in the pre-training mix. If the filter is porous enough to miss those, the exam percentiles inherit genuine uncertainty.

Even taken at face value, the exam framing flatters. Percentile ranks are relative to a self-selected human population — people who sit the bar already finished law school — and static, public, largely multiple-choice instruments measure test-taking under leakage, not the professional competence the "top 10%" headline invites you to infer. And one cost is quantified in the report itself: RLHF degrades calibration by roughly 10×. The pre-trained model is almost perfectly calibrated (ECE 0.007); the same model after RLHF no longer has its confidence track its accuracy (ECE 0.074, Figure 5). The deployed model "knows what it knows" markedly less well than the base model — alignment buys behaviour at the price of honest uncertainty.

baselines

Mostly OpenAI's own GPT-3.5, plus best-reported numbers lifted from other papers (Flan-PaLM, PaLM, Chinchilla, LLaMA) — no controlled, matched-protocol head-to-head, and few-shot counts vary by benchmark.

contamination

Self-reported substring match (three 50-char samples) with admitted false negatives; BIG-bench leaked into training (excluded) and part of GSM-8K was deliberately in the pre-training mix — so the exam scores carry real uncertainty.

what's missed

Percentiles measure test-taking on static, public exams against a self-selected human pool, not real-world competence; RLHF collapses calibration (ECE 0.007 → 0.074); image input is reported but was unreleased; nothing is reproducible.

would raise confidence

Held-out or freshly-authored post-cutoff exams; disclosed (even gated) data/compute for independent auditors; matched-protocol baselines; calibration reported on the deployed, not just the base, model.

// bottom line

GPT-4 is a real capability jump, and predictable scaling is a genuine methodological advance — being able to forecast a frontier model's loss (and a coding metric) from runs 1,000–10,000× cheaper is the most durable science in the report. But as a document it inverts the field's norms: its most quoted results rest on a contamination check OpenAI itself calls leaky, and there is nothing to reproduce. Read it as a product announcement with unusually good scaling science attached — not as a verifiable technical paper.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. Predictable scaling is the real contribution. Power-law fits on models 1,000–10,000× cheaper forecast GPT-4's final loss, and a capability metric (HumanEval pass rate) on the easiest subset — turning a multi-million-dollar run into something you can de-risk in advance.
  2. Human-exam framing, contamination-prone substrate. ~90th-percentile bar exam, 86.4% MMLU, 95.3% HellaSwag, 67% HumanEval — measured on static, public instruments against a substring contamination filter OpenAI admits yields false negatives (and which missed BIG-bench).
  3. The transparency rollback. Citing competition and safety, GPT-4 discloses no architecture, size, data, compute or training method. This is the report where frontier-model "technical reports" stopped being reproducible.
  4. Alignment is behaviour, not capability — and it has a cost. RLHF leaves base exam performance essentially unchanged (73.7% → 74.0%) but degrades calibration ~10× (ECE 0.007 → 0.074).
