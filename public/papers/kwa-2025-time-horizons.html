Measuring AI Ability to Complete Long Software Tasks

A benchmark score tells you almost nothing about what an AI can actually do. METR's answer: measure the 50%-task-completion time horizon — the length of task, in human time, that a model can finish half the time. On software and ML tasks it has doubled roughly every seven months since 2019, a trend that — taken at face value — puts month-long tasks within reach around 2029.

What problem is this solving?

AI capability has become impossible to read off a leaderboard. Scores climb, but what does "82% on MMLU" mean for whether a model can ship a feature, run an experiment, or cause harm? Three structural problems make benchmarks bad capability trackers. First, the tasks are often artificial rather than economically meaningful. Second, benchmarks are frequently adversarially filtered against current models — HellaSwag and Humanity's Last Exam were both built by discarding problems the best models of the day could already solve — which biases any comparison to humans. Third, and most damaging for forecasting: individual benchmarks saturate faster and faster, and there is no common, intuitive scale on which to compare a GPT-2 to a Claude 3.7. Each new benchmark is a fresh ruler with no marked units.

What's missing is a single axis that (a) is grounded in something humans understand, (b) spans many orders of magnitude of difficulty so it doesn't saturate, and (c) lets you put a 2019 model and a 2025 model on the same plot. METR's proposal is to borrow the obvious human unit: time. Instead of asking "what fraction of this fixed test can the model pass?", ask "how long a task — measured by how long it takes a skilled human — can the model complete?" That reframing turns a saturating accuracy number into an open-ended, human-relatable quantity, and makes the central question of AI forecasting answerable: not "is the score going up?" but "how fast is the length of automatable work growing, and where does the line point?"

// how it works

The method

The unit is the X%-task-completion time horizon: the human-time length of tasks a model can complete with X% success. To estimate it, METR assembles a task suite that deliberately spans five orders of magnitude in difficulty — 170 tasks built from three sources: HCAST (97 diverse software/agency tasks, 1 minute to 30 hours), RE-Bench (7 hard ML-research-engineering tasks, ~8 hours each), and SWAA — Software Atomic Actions — a new suite of 66 single-step tasks lasting 1–30 seconds, added specifically so that weak pre-2023 models (down to GPT-2) register a non-zero signal.

Every task gets two numbers. A human time rating comes from skilled baseliners — software, ML, and cybersecurity professionals with ~5 years of experience, most from top-100 universities — who attempt the task without task-specific context; the rating is the geometric mean time of the successful human runs. METR collected over 800 baselines totaling 2,529 hours (148 of 169 tasks have human baselines; 21 use researcher estimates). An agent success rate comes from running each of 12 frontier and 4 near-frontier models (2019–2025), inside a scaffold, 8 times per task and scoring each run as success/failure (continuous scores are binarized at the human target threshold).

Then, per model, a logistic regression maps human task length to agent success probability. The fitted location parameter hagent — the task length at which the model crosses 50% — is the time horizon. The method is deliberately analogous to Item Response Theory, except task "difficulty" is set directly by human baseline time rather than learned from model performance.

Psuccess(agent, task) = σ ( βagent · ( log hagent − log ttask ) ) // ttask = geometric-mean time of successful human baselines; hagent = the 50% time horizon (the model's location on the human-time axis); βagent = slope
// what they found

The results

The headline is a clean exponential. Plotting each model's 50% time horizon against its release date on a log axis, the horizon has doubled every 207 days — roughly seven months — from 2019 to 2025, with a tight R² of 0.97 and a 95% bootstrap CI on the doubling time of 166–240 days (about ±19%). The span is enormous: GPT-2 sits at a ~2-second horizon, while o3 reaches ~110 minutes and succeeds at several tasks that take humans over four hours. (The first version of this paper, in March 2025, put the frontier — Claude 3.7 Sonnet — at ≈50 minutes; the trend, not any single point, is the durable result.)

Crucially, the authors trust the slope far more than any single model's level: error bars on individual horizons are wide but highly correlated across models, because sampling an easy (or hard) set of tasks shifts every model's estimate together. The fit barely moves under changes to regularization, task weighting, or WLS-vs-OLS. There's also a hint of acceleration: o3 lies significantly above the long-run line (p = 0.006), and the 2023–2025 growth rate is about 20% faster than the 2019–2025 rate.

The metric is well-defined because difficulty for models tracks difficulty for humans: model success rate falls steadily with human completion time (R² ≈ 0.80), and the tasks different models can do are strongly correlated (≈ 0.73). Recent models clear ~50–60% average success on the diversity-weighted suite; GPT-2 manages ~10%.

Why the horizon is growing

A failure analysis of GPT-4 (1106) vs o1 runs locates the gains in reliability and error-recovery, not just raw reasoning. The single biggest change: o1 almost stopped repeating failed actions (12 such failures for GPT-4 → 2 for o1). It got better at tool use and logical reasoning. But it also abandoned tasks prematurely more often (8 → 16), and both models still struggle in "messy" environments without clear feedback loops.

Extrapolation

Taking the trend at face value, METR forecasts when AI reaches a 1-month horizon — 167 working hours, chosen because a system that can do a month of expert work could plausibly onboard like a human employee and acquire context, and because a "1-month AGI" would by definition exceed humans at writing large applications, founding startups, or making novel discoveries. Perturbing the data across bootstraps and methodology choices, the central estimate lands around mid-2029 (80% CI ≈ 2 years wide, roughly mid-2028 to mid-2031). If instead the faster 2024–2025 trend holds, it arrives much sooner — half the probability mass before 2027.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

This is one of the most carefully-caveated capability papers around — the authors litigate their own weaknesses harder than most reviewers would. The methodology is genuinely new and the ~7-month doubling is robust to a wall of ablations. The question is not whether the trend is real on this suite, but whether the suite is the right thing to extrapolate from. Two issues dominate.

First, 50% is a coin flip. A "110-minute model" succeeds at 110-minute tasks half the time — not a system you'd let run unsupervised. The 80% horizon doubles at a near-identical rate (204 days) but the horizons are 4–6× shorter, and the dataset can't even pin down a 95% horizon. So the deployable capability is years behind the headline number, and reliability — not the 50% length — is the real bottleneck the metric underweights.

Second, external validity. The tasks are self-contained, automatically scorable, with clear feedback loops; real software work rarely is. METR's own probes are honest about this. Scoring tasks against 16 hand-picked "messiness" factors, models do worse on messier tasks (though, reassuringly, the trend over time looks similar for messy and clean subsets — no visible plateau). And the choice of which human sets the clock swings the answer by an order of magnitude: on internal pull requests, repo maintainers were 5–18× faster than contractors, and agents matched contractor — not maintainer — time. The SWE-bench Verified replication shows the same fragility: it reproduces the exponential but with a much shorter ~70-day doubling, which the authors trace to annotation times that underestimate how long the easy tasks really take.

baselines

800+ skilled contractors (~5 yrs experience, 2,529 hours) — but timed "without task-specific context." The internal-PR probe shows repo maintainers are 5–18× faster, so the reference-human choice can move the whole horizon by an order of magnitude. Difficulty uses the geometric mean of successful human runs, discarding failed attempts.

contamination

Not the central threat — tasks are agentic and execution-scored, and the 66 new SWAA tasks specifically anchor pre-2023 models. But HCAST, RE-Bench, and SWE-bench Verified are public and could leak; the trend's slope is the load-bearing claim, not any single level.

what's missed

50% reliability is a coin flip; the 80% horizon is 4–6× shorter and 95% is unmeasurable here. Self-contained, auto-gradeable tasks omit the messiness of real work (other agents, resource limits, dynamic environments, no oracle).

would raise confidence

Replicating the doubling on a large, uncontaminated, deliberately messy real-world task set, with reliability measured at 80–95% and human times pinned to one consistent reference population — and longer to see whether the 2024–2025 acceleration is signal or noise.

// bottom line

A genuinely useful new yardstick: it converts opaque benchmark scores into "how long a task can the model do?" and shows a strikingly regular ~7-month doubling over six years. But it measures 50%-reliable performance on clean, gradeable software tasks. Whether the line bends on messy real-world work, and how far behind deployable reliability trails, are exactly the questions the authors can't yet answer — and they're where the 2029 forecast lives or dies.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. The 50%-task-completion time horizon — the human-time length of tasks a model can finish half the time — turns saturating benchmark scores into a single, human-relatable, non-saturating axis that puts GPT-2 and o3 on the same plot.
  2. On software and ML tasks the horizon has doubled every ~207 days (≈7 months) since 2019 (R² = 0.97), from ~2 seconds (GPT-2) to ~110 minutes (o3) — and the gains come mostly from reliability and error-recovery (o1 stopped repeating failed actions), not raw reasoning alone.
  3. Naive extrapolation puts month-long (167-hour) software tasks within reach around 2029 (mid-2028 to mid-2031) — but the dominant uncertainty isn't statistical noise, it's external validity and the gap between 50% and deployable reliability, both of which the paper foregrounds rather than hides.
