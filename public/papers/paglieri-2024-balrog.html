BALROG: Benchmarking Agentic LLM and VLM Reasoning On Games

Six long-horizon reinforcement-learning games — from a seconds-to-master BabyAI errand to a years-to-master NetHack run — behind one client, one fine-grained 0–100 progress metric, and one blunt finding: today's best model still averages 1.5% progress on the hardest game.

What problem is this solving?

Most agentic LLM benchmarks in 2024 — WebArena's web navigation, SWE-bench's GitHub issues, office-task suites like OfficeBench — cap an episode at "no more than a few dozen rounds of interaction," as the authors put it. That ceiling is convenient, but it doesn't test the thing that makes real-world autonomy hard: acting correctly for hundreds or hundreds of thousands of steps, recovering from mistakes, and reasoning about space and causality along the way. The paper's wager is that the next frontier for LLM/VLM capability is long-horizon decision-making, and that the field lacks a comprehensive, difficulty-graded way to measure it.

Games are the paper's answer, for a reason that predates LLMs entirely: reinforcement learning has used them as a yardstick for decades (Atari, Go, StarCraft II), precisely because they come with lightweight simulators, clear objectives, and a known difficulty ladder. BALROG stitches six existing RL environments — BabyAI, Crafter, TextWorld, Baba Is AI, MiniHack, and the NetHack Learning Environment — into a single agentic testbed spanning tasks a human clears in seconds (BabyAI) to one that takes human players years to master without a guide (NetHack). Crucially, every environment is procedurally generated: no fixed level layout repeats, so the benchmark can't be gamed by memorizing a walkthrough — a leakage problem the authors call out directly as endemic to "other benchmarks."

// how it works

The method

BALROG is a thin, deliberately decoupled harness: an env_wrapper.py standardizes the step/reset interface across all six environments regardless of their native APIs; a client.py abstracts away model providers (OpenAI, Gemini, Claude, or a local vLLM-served model); an agent.py couples a model with a pluggable "agentic strategy" (the paper only evaluates zero-shot prompting, but the interface is built for chain-of-thought, few-shot, or self-refinement submissions later); and an evaluator.py runs the episode loop and collects the metrics. Decoupling the model from the prompting strategy is the whole design point: swapping in a new inference-time method means editing agent.py only, and benchmarking a new model zero-shot means editing a config in client.py only.

Each timestep, the agent receives the interaction history (observation + prior action) in a chat-formatted prompt and must emit the next action as a natural-language string; an invalid or hallucinated action triggers a logged fallback (a "do-nothing" or default move) rather than crashing the episode. History length is fixed at 16 observations for every experiment in the paper. Every environment except TextWorld (which has no visual component) is evaluated in two modalities: language-only (a templated text description of the state) and vision-language (that same text plus a rendered image of the current frame — with the two hardest environments, MiniHack and NetHack, additionally getting an ASCII-rendered 2D map). Because of the cost of image tokens, only a single current-frame image is included per prompt, with the rest of the history staying text-only.

Scoring is deliberately fine-grained rather than binary-only:

progress = 100 if task solved, else 0 // MiniHack, BabyAI, Baba Is AI — discrete per-episode completion
progress = 100 · achievementsmax achievements // TextWorld, Crafter, and NetHack (via a novel data-informed progression metric, since NetHack's native score is a poor proxy for actual advancement)

Seven models are the main subjects: Gemini-1.5-Flash and -Pro, GPT-4o-mini and GPT-4o, Claude 3.5 Sonnet and Haiku, and Llama 3.1 (8B/70B) and 3.2 (1B/3B/11B/90B) instruct — plus OpenAI's o1-preview, tested only on NetHack "due to budget constraints." All evaluations in the paper are zero-shot; BALROG's toolkit supports chain-of-thought, few-shot, and in-context-RL submissions, but the authors explicitly leave those as open problems (a single NetHack demonstration alone can run past 700,000 input tokens, which they found infeasible to prompt with naively at the time of writing).

// what they found

The results

In the language-only setting, Claude 3.5 Sonnet is the best overall model at an average progression of 32.64% ± 1.93 across all six games, with GPT-4o a hair behind at 32.34% ± 1.49, then Llama 3.1 70B (27.88%) and Llama 3.2 90B (27.29%). Every model does reasonably on the easy end — BabyAI, Crafter, Baba Is AI — and then falls off a cliff: MiniHack's Quest and Boxoban tasks were never solved by any model, and every single model "flat lines" on NetHack. The best NetHack performance in the whole paper belongs to o1-preview at 1.5% average game progression — nearly three times its closest text-only competitor, Claude 3.5 Sonnet, and still, in the authors' words, "far from satisfactory."

Adding vision does not help, and often actively hurts. GPT-4o drops from 32.34% (text) to 22.56% (vision); Llama 3.2 90B drops from 27.29% to 20.99%. Claude 3.5 Sonnet and Gemini-1.5-Pro are the clear exceptions, both improving with an image added (Gemini-1.5-Flash ticks up too, but only marginally, 14.63% → 14.94%) — Claude 3.5 Sonnet is in fact the single best vision-language model overall at 35.48% ± 2.02, better than its own text-only score. Gemini models additionally failed every TextWorld task outright — Gemini's API flagged the (entirely benign) prompts as "unsafe," a quirk that zeroes out their TextWorld score regardless of underlying capability.

The qualitative read is the paper's most memorable contribution: a "knowing-doing" gap. Asked in a separate thread whether eating rotten food in NetHack is dangerous, or whether exiting the dungeon on level 1 causes instant death, models answer correctly. In actual play, they do it anyway — GPT-4o repeatedly dies from rotten food it can correctly identify as lethal, and models exit the dungeon early despite knowing it ends the game. The models have the relevant knowledge; they fail to apply it under the pressure of sequential decision-making.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

BALROG's procedurally-generated environments genuinely close off the most common leakage vector — no fixed level layout can be memorized from a walkthrough — and that's a real, checkable strength worth crediting. But the "how far are models from solving this" headline numbers rest on a narrower evidence base than the framing suggests.

baselines

Every reported number is naive zero-shot prompting. The toolkit supports chain-of-thought, few-shot, and in-context RL, but the paper explicitly leaves all of them as future work rather than baselines — so 32.64% (Claude 3.5 Sonnet, language-only) is a floor for "prompted with nothing extra," not a ceiling on what these models could do on BALROG with a ReAct-style scaffold or self-refinement loop.

contamination

Procedural generation defeats instance memorization, but NetHack in particular has decades of public wikis, spoiler guides, and forum strategy the models were plausibly pretrained on — which is exactly what the "knowing-doing gap" surfaces (models can recite the danger of rotten food, they just can't act on it in-context). That's a different, subtler form of contamination than test-set leakage: static declarative knowledge about the game's mechanics is likely in-distribution even though no specific dungeon layout is.

what's missed

o1-preview — the one model that meaningfully moves the NetHack needle — is tested on NetHack only, "due to budget constraints," so there's no o1 data point for the five easier games or for vision mode at all. The paper's most attention-grabbing claims (severe vision-based decision-making deficits) are also built on a single-image-per-observation protocol adopted for cost reasons; the authors themselves note video or multi-image history might change the picture, so "severe deficiencies in vision-based decision-making" is partly a statement about their specific (cheap) VLM protocol, not vision-based decision-making in general. No cost/compute-normalized comparison is reported either, despite runtime and inference cost being exactly the kind of practical constraint that shaped several of the paper's own methodological choices.

would raise confidence

Publishing at least one non-zero-shot baseline (few-shot or CoT) per model so the "difficulty" numbers have a known upper bound; running o1 (or any reasoning model) across all six games and both modalities rather than one game in one modality; reporting a cost/token budget alongside every score, the way the paper already does for one small side-experiment on turns and dollars; and treating the BabyAI/Baba-Is-AI "narrowly surpassing" claims in the main text more cautiously given that several rankings sit inside overlapping standard-error bars from a 25-seed evaluation.

// bottom line

BALROG's real contribution is the diagnosis — a leakage-resistant, difficulty-graded testbed that surfaces a genuine knowing-doing gap and a vision-hurts-not-helps result — not a definitive capability ceiling. Read the headline numbers as "how far zero-shot prompting gets you on six hard games," and treat the o1/vision asymmetry as a real gap in the evidence rather than a settled verdict on reasoning models or multimodal agents more broadly.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. BALROG aggregates six procedurally-generated RL games — BabyAI, Crafter, TextWorld, Baba Is AI, MiniHack, and NetHack — behind one harness and one 0–100 fine-grained progress metric, spanning seconds-to-master to years-to-master difficulty.
  2. Zero-shot Claude 3.5 Sonnet and GPT-4o lead language-only play at ~32% average progress, but every model flatlines on NetHack — o1-preview's best-in-class score is just 1.5% average progression, and MiniHack's Quest and Boxoban tasks were never solved by any model.
  3. Giving models an image of the game state usually makes them worse, not better, and the paper's qualitative analysis surfaces a genuine "knowing-doing" gap: models can correctly state that an action is fatal and still take it in play.
