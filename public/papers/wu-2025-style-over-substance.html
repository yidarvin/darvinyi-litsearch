Style Over Substance: Evaluation Biases for Large Language Models

GPT-4 rates "Several Minor Factual Errors" at 1206 Elo above a correct-but-short answer at 1096 Elo. A controlled bias audit of Elo-based LLM-as-judge evaluation across crowd workers, experts, GPT-4, and Claude-1 — and a multi-dimensional fix that only half-works.

Minghao Wu, Alham Fikri Aji · Mohamed bin Zayed University of Artificial Intelligence & Monash University

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

By mid-2023, Elo-style pairwise judging — either crowd workers or an LLM like GPT-4 comparing two model outputs and picking a winner — had become the default way to rank chatbots, from Chiang et al.'s Vicuna blog post to the emerging LMSYS Chatbot Arena. It's fast, it's cheap relative to expert review, and it produces a clean leaderboard number. Prior work had already flagged that these judges carry a position bias (Wang et al., 2023, "Large Language Models are not Fair Evaluators"), but nobody had isolated which surface properties an Elo judge actually rewards, independent of genuine answer quality.

The methodological difficulty is confounding: if you compare two different models' real outputs and a judge prefers one, you can't tell whether it preferred the better answer or just the longer, more confidently-worded one. This paper's contribution is the experimental design that removes that confound — instead of comparing different models, it takes a single verified-correct answer per question and has GPT-4 systematically inject one of five controlled defects (minor or major factual errors, or spelling/grammar mistakes typical of a non-native English learner) at two lengths, producing 12 "models" whose only real difference from the ground-truth-correct answer is the one deliberately introduced flaw. Any judge preference among them is then directly attributable to that flaw, not to a confound.

// how it works

The method

The authors start from 40 general-purpose questions drawn from Chiang et al.'s (2023) 80-question Vicuna evaluation set, keeping only the "generic," "knowledge," "common sense," and "counterfactual" categories and dropping "fermi," "coding," "math," "roleplay," and "writing" — categories that need specialized expertise or are inherently subjective to grade. For each question, GPT-4 (gpt-4-0613) is prompted to produce a correct baseline answer plus five deliberately flawed variants: one minor factual error, several minor factual errors, several major factual errors, an "advanced English learner" version (occasional spelling slips), and an "intermediate English learner" version (grammatical mistakes) — each rendered at both roughly 100 words and roughly 50 words, giving 12 total answer "models" per question. Every output is manually reviewed and edited to guarantee it contains exactly the intended defect and nothing else.

All 12 models are compared pairwise in both answer orders, yielding 5,280 unique pairwise comparisons across the 40 questions. Four judge types score every comparison: crowd-sourced Amazon Mechanical Turk workers (377 unique annotators, each required to have completed over 5,000 prior assignments at a 99%+ approval rate, capped at 20 annotations per worker, contributing 14 annotations on average, with a mandatory 20-second delay before submitting to discourage skimming); expert annotators (a dedicated team of 20, each holding at least a master's degree from an English-speaking country, who together annotate a 200-comparison sample of the 5,280 — justified by a US Chess Federation rule of thumb that 25 games are enough to rate a player, and every one of the 12 models appears in at least 28 of the 200 sampled games); and two LLM judges, GPT-4 (gpt-4-0613) and Claude-1 (Claude-1.3) — Claude-1 is added specifically because using GPT-4 alone to judge GPT-4-generated (then hand-edited) text risks a self-preference confound. Elo ratings are computed with the standard chess formula (K-factor 32), averaged over 10,000 random orderings of the games, following the protocol of Chiang et al. (2023) and Dettmers et al. (2023).

To address the bias this uncovers, the authors propose the Multi-Elo Rating System (MERS): instead of collapsing a comparison into one holistic win/lose/tie judgment, judges (crowd, expert, or GPT-4) score each answer independently on three axes — Accuracy (factual correctness and logical consistency), Helpfulness (relevance and depth), and Language (grammar, clarity, tone) — with a separate Elo computed per axis. The idea is borrowed from the Multidimensional Quality Metrics (MQM) framework used in machine-translation evaluation (Lommel et al., 2014), which likewise splits translation quality into fluency and adequacy rather than reporting one blended score.

// what they found

The results

The headline number is stark: on the single unified Elo scale, GPT-4 rates "Several Minor Factual Errors" at 1206 Elo, above "Correct + Short" at 1096 Elo — a longer answer with several fabricated facts beats a shorter, entirely correct one. Manually reviewing GPT-4's own justifications, the authors find it sometimes ranks "Several Major Factual Errors" above "Correct + Short" even after explicitly noting the factual errors in its reasoning.

Every judge type shows a length bias when forced to choose between comparably-scored answers of different lengths (Figure 2, left): the longer answer wins 66.8% of the time for Claude-1, 67.9% for GPT-4, 57.1% for crowd workers, and 51.9% (the least biased) for experts. A position/order bias shows up too (Figure 2, right): crowd workers, GPT-4, and Claude-1 all default to whichever answer is shown first — Assistant 1 wins 45.9% of crowd judgments (vs. 44.8% for Assistant 2, 9.3% ties), 50.4% of GPT-4's (44.4% / 5.2%), and 52.6% of Claude-1's (44.6% / 2.8%). Experts break the pattern, actually favoring Assistant 2 (35.5% vs. 44.5%) and calling a tie far more often (20% of the time, versus 2.8–9.3% for the others).

Humans, meanwhile, are strikingly indecisive: crowd-sourced Elo scores across all 12 models span only 926–1086, and expert scores span 832–1162 — both far narrower than GPT-4's and Claude-1's spreads, even though the underlying quality gap between "Correct" and "Several Major Factual Errors" is large and deliberate. Under MERS, splitting the score into Accuracy/Helpfulness/Language sharpens GPT-4's factual discrimination considerably — its Accuracy Elo for "Correct" (1200) is now clearly separated from "Several Major Factual Errors + Short" (746). But the fix doesn't rescue crowd-sourced judgment: on the same isolated Accuracy axis, crowd workers still rate "Several Major Factual Errors + Short" at 955 Elo, barely below "Correct + Short" at 963 — nearly indistinguishable, exactly the failure MERS was meant to fix.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

The experimental design here is genuinely clever — collapsing "different models" down to "one model, one controlled defect" is exactly the right move to isolate a bias signal from genuine quality variance, and the resulting numbers (GPT-4 preferring fabricated facts over a correct short answer) are a real, useful indictment of naive Elo-based LLM judging. But several things temper how far the result generalizes.

First, the "12 models" are one model wearing different costumes: every answer is GPT-4-written, then hand-edited by the authors to guarantee a specific defect. That's necessary for the controlled design, but it means the study measures "can a judge detect a defect the researchers planted in GPT-4 prose," not "how do judges behave when comparing genuinely different models' natural output distributions" — real models don't fail in this neat, single-axis way, and a judge's real-world bias profile against, say, a terse Llama output versus a verbose GPT-4 output could look different. Second, the question set is deliberately restricted to 40 "easy" questions from four low-expertise categories, explicitly to keep human annotation tractable — the paper's own Limitations section admits this "may not capture the full spectrum of question types," and it's plausible that harder, more technical questions would show either more or less fact-checking diligence from human judges. Third, the judge models themselves (gpt-4-0613, Claude-1.3) are now several generations behind current frontier judges; the paper offers no evidence on whether the same length/order bias magnitudes hold for newer models that have since been tuned partly in response to exactly this class of criticism.

The expert-annotation sample is also thin for the claims resting on it: only 200 of 5,280 comparisons (3.8%) get expert eyes, and the justification — a US Chess Federation heuristic that 25 games suffice to rate one player — is a loose stand-in for a real power calculation; no confidence intervals are reported around any Elo number or bias percentage in the paper, so it's hard to know how much of, e.g., the 57.1% vs. 51.9% crowd/expert length-bias gap is a genuine between-population effect versus sampling noise. Finally, the abstract's framing of MERS ("significantly enhances the quality of LLM-based evaluations, particularly in terms of factual accuracy") is true for GPT-4-as-judge but glosses over the paper's own Table 4 result that MERS does essentially nothing to fix the crowd-sourced Accuracy problem — a reader skimming only the abstract could walk away thinking the fix generalizes across judge types when the body shows it doesn't.

baselines

Not applicable in the usual sense — there's no competing bias-detection method to beat; the paper establishes the phenomenon against the status quo (single unified Elo) and evaluates its own fix (MERS) against that same status quo.

contamination

Not the usual train/test leakage, but the 40-question set is drawn from the already-public Vicuna 80-question set that Chatbot Arena and similar systems were built around — today's frontier judges may already be tuned against exactly this style of failure, so the bias magnitudes here are likely an upper bound for 2023-era judges specifically, not a timeless constant.

what's missed

Whether MERS holds up beyond GPT-4 (Claude-1 is never evaluated under the multi-dimensional protocol), and whether the same biases replicate on real, naturally-diverse model outputs rather than one model's hand-edited variants.

would raise confidence

Replication on a fresh, harder question set with genuinely different models' natural outputs; confidence intervals on every reported Elo gap and bias percentage; and a MERS evaluation against Claude-1 and a current-generation judge model.

// bottom line

A tightly controlled and genuinely important demonstration that Elo-based LLM evaluation rewards length and answer order over correctness — GPT-4 literally prefers several fabricated facts to a correct-but-short answer — but the "12 models" are one model in disguise, the question set is 40 deliberately easy items, and the paper's own numbers show its proposed fix barely moves crowd-sourced accuracy judgment at all, a caveat the abstract underplays.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. GPT-4-as-judge scores "Several Minor Factual Errors" at 1206 Elo above "Correct + Short" at 1096 Elo — a length-over-accuracy trade the paper's own Table 1 makes explicit.
  2. Every judge type favors the longer answer when comparing similarly-scored options: Claude-1 66.8%, GPT-4 67.9%, crowd 57.1%, expert 51.9% — and crowd/GPT-4/Claude-1 also default to whichever answer is shown first.
  3. Human judges reach only "slight" Cohen's-κ agreement with each other and with LLM judges (κ 0.08–0.14); only GPT-4 and Claude-1 agree at a "moderate" level (κ=0.51) — a sign that LLM judges may share correlated failure modes rather than being more correct.
  4. The proposed fix — separate Elo scores for Accuracy, Helpfulness, and Language (MERS) — sharpens GPT-4's factual discrimination, but the paper's own data shows crowd-sourced Accuracy Elo for "Several Major Factual Errors + Short" (955) and "Correct + Short" (963) remain nearly tied even under MERS.
