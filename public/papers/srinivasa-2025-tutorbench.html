TutorBench: A Benchmark To Assess Tutoring Capabilities Of Large Language Models

Scale AI built a 1,490-sample, 15,220-criterion benchmark of real high-school STEM tutoring — adaptive explanations, feedback on student work, and hints — where over half the samples are photos of handwritten work and every response is graded against an expert rubric. No frontier model clears 56%, and adaptive explanation, the most pedagogical task, is the weakest at 47%.

Rakshith S. Srinivasa, Zora Che, Chen Bo Calvin Zhang, Diego Mares, … Anisha Gunjal, Yunzhong He, Bing Liu, Chen Xing · Scale AI

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

LLMs are already de-facto tutors for millions of students. Providers lean into it — OpenAI ships a dedicated Study Mode, Google hands students free Gemini Pro — because personalized tutoring at near-zero marginal cost is one of the most economically attractive uses of the technology. Yet the evidence on whether it works is mixed: some field studies report learning gains, while a 2025 PNAS study found high-school students did worse on exams once the AI tutor was taken away. If we are going to deploy these systems as teachers, we need to measure how good they actually are at teaching.

Almost no benchmark does. The flagship evals — Humanity's Last Exam, FrontierMath, OlympiadBench — measure advanced domain knowledge and reasoning: can the model solve the problem? Tutoring is a different skill. A good tutor diagnoses the specific misconception behind a wrong answer, calibrates the explanation to what the student already knows, scaffolds with hints instead of handing over the solution, and reads frustration or confusion. A model can ace OlympiadBench and still be a terrible tutor.

The handful of tutoring benchmarks that exist are narrow. They are text-only, cover just one or two subjects (usually math or intro CS), and — by their authors' own reported numbers — models like GPT-4 already near-saturate them, so they no longer separate frontier systems. They also lean on slow, expensive human grading with no reliable automatic judge. TutorBench's bet: build something multimodal (students photograph their handwritten work), broad (six STEM subjects), hard (filtered to stay unsaturated), and auto-gradable via sample-specific expert rubrics.

// how it works

The method

TutorBench is 1,490 expert-authored conversations between a student persona and a tutor persona, drawn from high-school and AP-level curricula across six STEM subjects — Biology, Physics, Chemistry, Statistics, Calculus, and Computer Science. Questions and rubrics were written by subject experts holding at least a Bachelor's degree with tutoring or professional experience. Crucially, 828 of the samples include images of handwritten or printed student work, mirroring how students actually use these tools — by snapping a photo of a half-finished problem.

Every sample targets one of three tutoring use cases, each constructed to probe a distinct teaching skill rather than raw problem-solving:

  • Adaptive Explanation Generation. The model sees a question, a tutor's initial explanation, and a student's follow-up that reveals a specific point of confusion. It must address that gap — not re-explain everything — calibrated to the student's apparent level.
  • Assessment & Feedback. The model is shown an incorrect student solution and must analyze the work, classify the error (arithmetic, conceptual, factual…), and give corrective feedback.
  • Active Learning Support. Given a partial or stuck attempt, the model must produce a hint that moves the student forward without revealing the answer — the classic Socratic balance.

The grading is rubric-based, which is what makes an open-ended task auto-scorable. Each sample ships its own set of 3 to 39 criteria — 15,220 rubrics in total — written to be self-contained, mutually exclusive, and collectively comprehensive. Each criterion is graded pass/fail by an LLM judge (Claude Sonnet 4) and carries a weight: +5 for a critical requirement, +1 for a non-critical one, and −5 for a critical prohibition — for example, "must not reveal the final answer" in an active-learning hint. The model's score on a sample is the weighted pass rate, normalized to [0, 1]:

ARRwj = Σi wij · 𝟙[response passes criterion i]Σi wij · 𝟙[wij > 0] // weighted average rubric rating for sample j; w ∈ {−5, 1, 5}. Denominator = total positive weight available, so the score is "credit earned ÷ credit possible." A model's final score is the mean of ARRw across all samples.

To enrich the analysis, every criterion is also tagged four ways: by an evaluation dimension (instruction following, truthfulness, conciseness/relevance, style/tone, visual perception, visual reasoning, student-level calibration, emotional component), by a tutoring skill (e.g. identifying misconceptions, asking guiding questions, providing examples), as explicit vs. implicit, and as objective vs. subjective.

Two design choices keep the benchmark honest. To guarantee difficulty, the authors had five strong 2025 models (Gemini 2.5 Pro, Claude 3.7 Sonnet, Llama 4 Maverick, o3, DeepSeek-R1) each answer every candidate sample and kept only those where at least three of the five scored below 50% — which is why the eventual best score is just 55.65%. And to trust the auto-judge, they collected three human-expert ratings on 250 samples: the Claude Sonnet 4 judge agreed with the human majority 0.78 of the time, slightly better than the median human expert's 0.75, reaching an F1 of 0.82 on the critical (±5) criteria versus the single best human's 0.91.

// what they found

The results

Across 16 frontier models, tutoring is far from solved. Gemini 2.5 Pro tops out at 55.65%, with GPT-5 at 55.33% and o3 Pro at 54.62% — and nobody crosses 56%. The Claude family trails the leaders by a clear margin (Claude Opus 4.1 Thinking is the best Claude at 50.78%), and GPT-4o sits at the bottom at 36.12%. Notably, the open-weight gpt-oss-120b reaches 56.01% on the text-only split, essentially matching the closed frontier where it can compete.

The story sharpens by use case. Averaged over all models, Adaptive Explanation is the hardest at 47.16%, then Assessment & Feedback at 51.56%, then Active Learning Support at 54.07%. The most interesting split is by model family: Claude models lead on Active Learning — Claude Opus 4 Thinking hits 0.59 and Opus 4.1 Thinking 0.58, the highest of any model on that use case — even though they trail overall. Gemini 2.5 Pro, meanwhile, dominates Assessment at 0.66. Different model families are good at different parts of teaching.

Breaking the rubrics down by evaluation dimension and by Bloom's taxonomy exposes more texture. Gemini 2.5 Pro is markedly better than the field at the emotional component — recognizing confusion, frustration, curiosity — and at style/tone (headings, bullets, LaTeX). GPT-5 and o3 Pro win on truthfulness, student-level calibration, and instruction following. And performance does not track Bloom's difficulty order: Gemini scores lower on "remember" than on "evaluate," meaning models can perform advanced reasoning yet stumble at simply recalling and stating the relevant fact when not explicitly asked — exactly the kind of context-setting a tutor needs.

By tutoring skill, the pattern is consistent across the board: models are relatively good at identifying correct and incorrect student steps (53.7% and 53.0% on average) but weak at the generative, pedagogical moves — providing alternative solutions (41.9%) and, worst of all, including examples or analogies (32.8%). They can diagnose; they struggle to teach around the difficulty.

The sharpest finding is about purpose-built tutoring. The authors separately evaluated OpenAI's GPT-5 Study Mode — "powered by custom system instructions written in collaboration with teachers." It scored 46.94% overall, well below vanilla API GPT-5's 55.33%. Why? Study Mode often returns partial, intermediate responses that end in a counter-question ("Before we move on, do you want me to walk you through…?"), and even when complete it is more concise and addresses fewer rubric criteria. Pedagogically deliberate behavior, penalized by a benchmark that grades a single final response.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

The construction is genuinely careful. The difficulty filter — keep only samples that beat three of five strong models — is an effective anti-saturation move and explains the believable 55.65% ceiling. The multimodal coverage (828 image samples) reflects real student behavior that text-only tutoring benchmarks ignore. And the team did the work to validate the judge: 0.78 agreement with the human majority, edging the median human's 0.75. This is a serious benchmark.

But three structural choices bound what its headline number means. First, and most important, TutorBench grades only the final response to a frozen conversation. Real tutoring is interactive — the tutor probes, the student responds, the tutor adapts. By scoring one turn against a fixed history, the benchmark structurally rewards comprehensive single-shot answers and penalizes the Socratic, one-step-at-a-time, withhold-the-answer style that good human tutors actually use. The Study Mode result is the tell: a mode explicitly built for learning scores 46.94% precisely because it tutors that way. So the benchmark arguably measures "good tutoring answer" better than "good tutor," and the authors acknowledge this as a limitation.

Second, the judge is a Claude model grading Claude contestants. Claude Sonnet 4 was picked for best human alignment, which is defensible, but the family overlap is a clean design hole the paper never closes — there is no self-preference check across model families. (In TutorBench's favor, the Claude entrants actually trail overall, which cuts against a naive bias worry — but the test still isn't run.) Third, the headline metric is a weighted mean of pass/fail criteria, so it rewards breadth: a verbose response that hits many small +1 criteria can outscore a focused one, which is in tension with the conciseness/relevance dimension and the active-learning goal of restraint. There is no separately reported "did it trip a critical −5 criterion (e.g., revealed the answer)" pass rate in the top-line.

On leakage: a 30-sample subset is already on Hugging Face and "the full dataset will be released soon," with — unlike sibling benchmarks such as PRBench — no private held-out split mentioned. Once the full set and its golden responses are public, the score has a shelf life, and the difficulty filter is pinned to a fixed 2025 model panel, so the "unsaturated" guarantee weakens as models improve.

baselines

16 current models — Gemini 2.5 Pro, GPT-5, o3 Pro/medium/high, Claude Opus 4/4.1 (±thinking), 3.7 Sonnet, Llama 4 Maverick, GPT-4o, gpt-oss-120b/20b, DeepSeek-R1 — with ±~1% CIs. Strong and current for mid-2025.

contamination

A 30-sample subset is public; the full dataset and golden responses are slated for release with no private heldout, so the public score will erode. The difficulty filter is anchored to a frozen 2025 model set.

what's missed

Only the final response to a fixed conversation is graded — so true multi-turn adaptivity is unmeasured and Socratic/withholding tutoring is penalized. The weighted-mean rewards breadth over restraint; one expert's rubric encodes contested pedagogy (inter-human agreement 0.75).

would raise confidence

A retained private split, a judge drawn from outside the Claude family plus a self-preference check, an interactive multi-turn protocol, and a separate critical-criterion (e.g. "didn't reveal the answer") pass rate.

// bottom line

TutorBench is a useful, hard, multimodal measure of a capability the field badly under-tests, and its headline — no model above 56%, with adaptive explanation the weakest at 47% — is credible and motivating. Hold two caveats in mind: it scores a single response to a frozen dialogue, so it captures "good tutoring answer" more than "good tutor" (the Study Mode result proves the point), and its longevity rests on a Claude-family judge over soon-public data. Read the per-use-case and per-skill breakdowns, not just the leaderboard.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. Tutoring is a distinct, unsaturated capability. Models that ace domain-knowledge benchmarks cap at 55.65% here, and the most pedagogical task — adapting an explanation to a student's confusion — is the hardest at 47.16%.
  2. The skill profile is uneven and family-specific. Models are decent at spotting correct/incorrect steps (~53%) but weak at generative teaching — alternative solutions (41.9%) and examples/analogies (32.8%). Claude leads Active Learning (0.58–0.59) yet trails overall, while Gemini dominates Assessment (0.66) and emotional attunement.
  3. Pedagogy ≠ a benchmark answer. GPT-5 Study Mode scored 46.94%, below vanilla GPT-5, because it withholds and asks counter-questions — a reminder that single-final-response rubric scores and genuine interactive teaching are not the same measurement.
