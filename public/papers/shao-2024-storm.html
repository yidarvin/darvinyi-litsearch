Assisting in Writing Wikipedia-like Articles From Scratch with Large Language Models

STORM treats the hardest part of long-form grounded writing as a research problem, not a generation problem: before drafting, it discovers diverse perspectives on a topic, role-plays multi-turn interviews between a curious writer and a search-grounded expert, and curates an outline — then writes the article section by section with citations.

Yijia Shao, Yucheng Jiang, Theodore A. Kanell, Peter Xu, Omar Khattab, Monica S. Lam · Stanford University

What problem is this solving?

LLMs write fluently, but writing a full Wikipedia-style article from scratch is not a fluency problem — it is a research problem. The hard work happens in what writing theorists call the pre-writing stage: figuring out what aspects a topic even has, hunting down trustworthy sources, and organizing them into an outline before the first sentence is drafted.

Prior work on Wikipedia generation quietly skipped this stage. WikiSum (Liu et al., 2018) treats the task as multi-document summarization with the reference documents handed to it; Fan & Gardent (2022) assume the outline is already given and only expand sections. Those assumptions collapse in the real setting, where collecting references and crafting an outline is exactly the expensive, expert-level work. STORM's framing (their Table 1) is the only one that is simultaneously open-domain, full-article, and given neither an outline nor references.

The two obvious LLM shortcuts both fail. Direct generation from parametric knowledge hallucinates and thins out on long-tail topics. Naive RAG retrieves on the bare topic string — but most of what a good article needs cannot be surfaced by searching the title alone; it takes follow-up questions a layperson wouldn't think to ask. And when you simply prompt an LLM to "ask questions about this topic," it produces shallow What / When / Where queries that skim the surface (Figure 1, panel A).

// the bet

STORM rests on two hypotheses: (1) diverse perspectives produce diverse questions — an event planner asks about budgets and transportation logistics where a layperson asks for basic facts; and (2) in-depth questions require iterative research — good follow-ups only occur to you once you've read the answer to the previous one.

// how it works

The method

STORM — Synthesis of Topic Outlines through Retrieval and Multi-perspective question asking — automates the pre-writing stage in four moves, then drafts the article.

1. Discover perspectives. Given a topic, STORM asks the LLM to name related Wikipedia pages, pulls their tables of contents through the Wikipedia API, and uses that scaffold to identify N distinct editorial perspectives that would collectively cover the topic. A fixed perspective p₀ = "basic fact writer" is always prepended so the basics aren't lost.

2. Simulate grounded interviews. Each perspective becomes a Wikipedia Writer persona that interviews a topic Expert. The writer asks one question at a time, conditioned on the topic, its perspective, and the conversation so far — so it can ask genuine follow-ups. The expert doesn't hallucinate the answer: it breaks the question into search queries, runs them, sifts the results with a rule-based filter built on Wikipedia's reliable-sources guidelines, and only then synthesizes a grounded answer. Every source it touches is added to the reference set R. Conversations run up to M rounds, in parallel across perspectives.

3. Curate the outline. STORM first prompts the model for a draft outline from the topic alone (its parametric knowledge gives good high-level structure), then refines that draft using everything learned in the simulated conversations.

4. Write with citations. The article is composed section by section: for each heading, relevant documents are retrieved from R by Sentence-BERT similarity, and the section is generated with inline citations. A final pass deletes repeated content across sections and synthesizes a lead paragraph in Wikipedia's house style.

The whole system is built with zero-shot prompting in the DSPy framework — no per-domain prompt engineering. The reported runs set N = M = 5, use gpt-3.5-turbo for question asking and gpt-4 for drafting/refining the outline and for the final article (GPT-3.5 was not faithful when generating cited text), and ground the expert on the You.com search API — with the ground-truth Wikipedia page excluded from results.

To grade the outline against a human article without demanding exact heading matches, STORM introduces heading soft recall: headings are embedded with Sentence-BERT and matched by cosine similarity, so "Aftermath" and "Consequences" count as near-hits.

soft heading recall = card(G ∩ P)card(G) // G = human headings (ground truth), P = generated headings; cardinalities use soft, embedding-similarity-weighted counts (Fränti & Mariescu-Istodor, 2023)

The companion metric, heading entity recall, is the share of named entities in the human headings (extracted with FLAIR NER) that the generated outline covers. Both are evaluated on FreshWiki — a dataset the authors curate from the top-100 most-edited pages per month (Feb 2022–Sep 2023), filtered to B-class-or-better quality, specifically to dodge pre-training leakage. Articles average 8.4 sections, 15.8 headings, ~2,159 words, and 90.1 references apiece — a reminder of how much sourcing a real article demands.

The results

Better outlines. With GPT-4, STORM reaches 92.73 heading soft recall and 45.91 entity recall, edging out a strengthened "RAG-expand" baseline (91.36 / 43.53) and direct generation (87.66 / 34.78). With the weaker GPT-3.5 the gap widens sharply — STORM 86.26 vs RAG/oRAG 73.59 — and, tellingly, plain RAG actually hurts the weaker model, because dumping unorganized retrieved text into the context window makes outlining harder, not easier.

The mechanism is load-bearing. Ablations isolate where the gains come from. Removing the conversation simulation drops GPT-3.5 soft recall to 77.97; removing perspectives drops it to 84.49. The clearest signal is in sourcing: the full pipeline gathers 99.83 unique references on average, versus 54.36 without perspectives and just 39.56 without conversation — perspective-guided interviewing roughly doubles to triples the sources surfaced.

Better articles. On the full text, STORM beats the strongest baseline (outline-driven RAG, "oRAG") on ROUGE-1 (45.82 vs 44.26), entity recall (14.10 vs 12.57), and the LLM-rubric scores for Coverage (4.88), Relevance (4.45), and Interest (3.99) — all significant. Removing the outline stage entirely collapses ROUGE-1 from 45.82 to 26.77, confirming that "outline, then write" is doing real structural work and not just decoration. A separate citation check (Mistral-7B judging entailment) finds 84.83% citation recall and 85.18% precision.

Experts agree — mostly. Ten experienced Wikipedia editors compared 20 article pairs. STORM wins decisively on Organization (4.00 vs 3.25, p = 0.005) — 25% more of its articles were judged well-organized — and is preferred overall 26 to 14. It leads on Interest (4.03 vs 3.63) and Coverage (4.00 vs 3.58), though those fall short of significance (p = 0.077 and 0.084). In the closing survey, every editor agreed STORM would help their own pre-writing stage.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

STORM's headline wins are all on breadth and structure. The dimension that actually defines an encyclopedia — verifiable, with no original research — is exactly where it doesn't move. In the human study, editors rated STORM 3.80 on Verifiability versus 3.85 for the baseline (p = 0.843): statistically indistinguishable, and nominally worse. The paper is admirably candid that this is the price of breadth — gathering ~100 references per article gives the model far more raw material, and more chances to draw connections the sources don't support.

Those connections are the real failure mode. About 15% of generated sentences are unsupported by their citations, and the error breakdown names the dangerous categories: inaccurate paraphrasing (14%), improper inferential linking (7%), and citing irrelevant sources (4%). The memorable example — an article on the superconductor LK-99 comparing its "performance" to frame rates in the video game Battlefield 2042 — is a red-herring / over-association error, not a hallucination. Editors flagged this kind of fabricated connection 11 times. A second systemic issue: source-bias transfer. With no content-sifting module, promotional web prose flows straight into the article; 7 of 10 editors called STORM's output "emotional" or "unneutral" (the word "significant" appears 17 times in one piece).

The evaluation scaffolding has its own soft spots. The automatic article scores lean on LLM judges that may over-rate machine text — the authors say so explicitly. Prometheus-13B is run without the reference answer it was designed to use (it didn't fit the context window) and on articles trimmed to 2,000 words; the citation judge is a 7B model whose own 15% false-negative slice in Figure 4 shows how noisy it is. The outline metric reports recall only, against a single human article as ground truth — so a longer, more sprawling outline can inflate the score, and "the human Wikipedia structure is the one correct structure" is a strong assumption. The human study is thin (20 pairs, 2 raters each), and inter-annotator agreement is low across the board (Krippendorff's α 0.22–0.39), so even the Organization win rests on a noisy signal.

baselines

Fair within the LLM family (Direct Gen, RAG, oRAG, RAG-expand), and oRAG is a genuinely strong, strengthened comparison. But prior non-LLM Wikipedia-generation systems (Table 1) are described, not run head-to-head, and the toughest baseline is the authors' own construction.

contamination

FreshWiki is a thoughtful defense — recent, heavily-edited, post-cutoff pages. Still partial: the live You.com search can return Wikipedia mirrors/derivatives even with the target page excluded, and the underlying events may already sit in pre-training.

what's missed

Verifiability and neutrality — the two properties that matter most for an encyclopedia — don't improve. No precision metric on outlines; no neutrality/bias metric at all; no cost or latency accounting for what is a many-call pipeline ((N+1)×M interviews × query-splitting × search).

would raise confidence

Report outline precision alongside recall; a larger human study with more raters and higher agreement; a stronger or human-audited citation judge; a neutrality metric plus a content-sifting module; and a head-to-head against non-LLM systems with a compute budget reported.

// bottom line

STORM convincingly shows that how you research — perspective-guided question asking with grounded interviews — beats bolting retrieval onto generation, and nearly doubles the sources surfaced. Its lasting contribution is as much diagnostic as constructive: by pushing breadth, it makes the harder problems legible — coverage without neutrality, and richness without airtight attribution. That honest framing is why STORM became a foundational reference for the deep-research / report-generation wave that followed.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. The bottleneck in long-form grounded writing is the pre-writing stage — and it's learnable. Perspective-guided, conversational question asking surfaces 2–3× the sources of plain question generation (99.83 vs 39.56 references) and lifts outline coverage well past RAG.
  2. Outline-then-write is doing real work, not decoration: removing the outline stage collapses article ROUGE-1 from 45.82 to 26.77, even when the model still has all the gathered conversations.
  3. Breadth ≠ trustworthiness. STORM wins organization and coverage with expert editors but ties on verifiability (3.80 vs 3.85), inherits source bias (7/10 editors), and makes over-association / red-herring errors — the open problems STORM names for grounded generation, and the agenda later deep-research systems inherited.
