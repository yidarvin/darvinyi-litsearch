Automatic Evaluation of Summaries Using N-gram Co-occurrence Statistics

A year before ROUGE had a name, Lin & Hovy ported BLEU’s n-gram trick to summarization — flipped it from precision to recall to match DUC’s human protocol, and found plain unigram overlap beat BLEU’s own weighted n-gram formula outright.

Chin-Yew Lin, Eduard Hovy · USC Information Sciences Institute

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

By 2003, automatic text summarization had a growing research community — workshops (WAS 2000–2002), special sessions at ACL/COLING/SIGIR, and government-run bake-offs in the US (NIST's Document Understanding Conference, DUC) and Japan (NTCIR) — but no cheap way to score a system. DUC's evaluation used human judges to compare each system's summary against an "ideal" model summary, and Lin himself estimates NIST spent about 3,000 person-hours in DUC 2001 and again in DUC 2002 on topic/document selection, summary writing, and manual scoring. That is workable for one annual competition; it is unusable for the day-to-day cycle of building and tuning a system. Worse, the human signal itself was noisy — Lin & Hovy's own earlier study found 18% of the DUC 2001 single-document judgments were multiply scored by the same assessor, i.e. inherently variable.

Machine translation had just solved its own version of this problem: IBM's BLEU (Papineni et al. 2001) scored MT output by counting n-gram overlap with reference translations, and the field adopted it as a cheap stand-in for human judges. The obvious question — can the same trick just be dropped onto summarization? — is what this paper answers, and the honest answer is "not without changing it." A naive port of unmodified IBM BLEU onto DUC 2001 data got promising but shaky numbers (Spearman ρ of 0.66–0.82 depending on task and reference count), and the paper diagnoses why: BLEU is a precision metric (what fraction of the candidate's n-grams are found in the references), while DUC's own human protocol scores coverage — how much of the reference's content the candidate recovered, which is a recall notion. Using a precision-shaped tool to approximate a recall-shaped judgment was the mismatch to fix.

// the one-line gap

DUC's human evaluation was accurate but cost thousands of hours and was itself inconsistent across judges. BLEU showed n-gram overlap could substitute for humans in machine translation — but BLEU is precision-oriented, and DUC's own "coverage" protocol is recall-oriented. Nobody had yet reframed the n-gram trick to match summarization's own evaluation logic, or tested which n-gram length actually correlates with human judgment once that reframing is done.

// how it works

The method

DUC's own human protocol first has to be made precise before anything can be validated against it. Assessors used the Summary Evaluation Environment (SEE) — built by Lin himself — to step through each unit of a human "model" summary and mark which units of a system's "peer" summary express the same content, rating the match as all, most, some, hardly any, or none. That produces a coverage score C: the fraction of model units marked, weighted by how completely each was expressed (E = 1, 3/4, 1/2, 1/4, or 0 for the five levels).

C = (Number of MUs marked) · ETotal number of MUs in the model summary // DUC's own human "coverage" score — the target this paper tries to approximate automatically. Setting E=1 recovers plain sentence recall.

BLEU scores machine translation as precision: the count of candidate n-grams that are clipped to how many times they appear in any single reference, divided by the total n-grams in the candidate, combined across n=1–4 with a log-weighted average and a brevity penalty for short outputs. Lin & Hovy's move is to flip the ratio's denominator from the candidate side to the reference (model) side — turning the same n-gram-matching machinery into a recall statistic that lines up with DUC's own coverage notion:

Ngram(i,j) = BB · exp [ Σn=ij wn log Cn ] // BB is a brevity bonus (fixed to 1 in every experiment here — never actually validated); wn = 1/(j−i+1); Cn is the n-gram-level coverage score (matched reference n-grams over total reference n-grams, i.e. recall). Ngram(1,4) mirrors IBM BLEU's weighted multi-length average; Ngram(k,k) — i.e. i=j=k — is simply the plain k-gram coverage score.

That last case, Ngram(k,k), is the formula ROUGE-N would adopt verbatim a year later — this paper is where it is first written down and tested. The paper's actual experiment tests two hypotheses side by side on DUC 2001 data: does the BLEU-shaped weighted composite Ngram(1,4) correlate with human coverage rankings, and does a single fixed n-gram length (Ngram(1,1) through Ngram(4,4)) do better or worse? Correlation is measured with Spearman's rank order coefficient (plus, for the single/fixed-n runs, a linear-regression t-test, Pearson's ρ, and coefficient of determination), and a second, independent test asks whether a statistically significant gap between two systems under the automatic metric predicts a significant gap under human judges — scored as recall and precision over z-tests at five α levels (0.10, 0.05, 0.025, 0.01, 0.005).

// what they found

The results

All experiments run on DUC 2001 data — 11 systems on the single-document 100-word task, 12 systems (plus baselines and human summaries) on the multi-document task at 50/100/200/400 words. The first pass tests the BLEU-shaped composite directly: Ngram(1,4) scores, ranked against the human coverage ranking, give a Spearman ρ of 0.604–0.615 on single-document summaries and 0.529–0.875 across the multi-document sizes (Table 1) — "statistically significant" (α = 0.025) but, in the paper's own words, "not consistent across summary sizes and tasks."

The second pass is the paper's actual headline: instead of BLEU's weighted blend of n=1–4, score each n-gram length on its own (Ngram(k,k) for k=1…4) and compare. Plain unigram co-occurrence, Ngram(1,1), wins by a wide and consistent margin — outperforming the weighted Ngram(1,4) composite on every configuration tested:

Excluding stopwords helps modestly and consistently. The paper also runs a second, independent test: does a statistically significant score gap between two systems under an automatic metric (z-test at α = 0.10, 0.05, 0.025, 0.01, 0.005) predict a significant gap under human judges? Plotting the resulting recall/precision curves, Ngram(1,1) and Ngram(2,2) sit furthest into the upper-right corner — highest simultaneous recall and precision — with unigram again the single best performer, while the weighted Ngram(1,4) trails behind all four fixed-length variants.

The paper's own explanation is architectural, not incidental: BLEU's weighted long-n-gram average is tuned for machine translation, where longer matches also signal grammatical fluency — a real failure mode for MT, but not for the mostly sentence-extractive summarizers DUC was scoring in 2003. Rewarding long spans in a domain with no fluency problem to catch just adds noise; a candidate that trims "United States of America" to "United States" is arguably a better summary, yet the weighted score gives it strictly less credit for the dropped trigram/4-gram. Unigram overlap sidesteps that penalty entirely.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

Read on its own terms — not as "early ROUGE" but as the standalone 2003 paper it is — this work is careful about what it claims and where it stops. Its scope is deliberately narrow: one year of DUC data (2001), 11–12 systems, and two correlation-style tests (rank agreement, significance-prediction recall/precision). The paper is explicit that this is a first pass — its own conclusion promises to "run similar experiments on DUC 2002 data to see if unigram does as well," and only if that holds does it plan to release the scoring code. That follow-through, delivered a year later with DUC 2001–2003 and a packaged tool, is what became ROUGE. Here, the central finding — that unweighted unigram co-occurrence beats BLEU's own weighted multi-length formula — is a single-dataset result the authors themselves flag as unconfirmed.

That thin evidence base is also where the seed of every later ROUGE-family critique already sits. First, the target being approximated is itself a soft human judgment: DUC's "coverage" score folds a five-level completeness rating (all/most/some/hardly any/none) into one number, and Lin & Hovy's own prior work found 18% of the DUC 2001 single-document judgments were multiply scored by the same assessor — the automatic metric is being validated against a moving target, not ground truth. Second, an n-gram-overlap score — recall-oriented or not — cannot tell a summary that paraphrases the reference correctly from one that reuses its vocabulary while getting the content wrong; that gap is invisible on DUC's 2001 newswire systems (which were almost all extractive, so wording mismatches were rare) but becomes the central failure mode once abstractive and later LLM-based summarizers arrive. Third, the paper's own brevity-correction story is unfinished: it argues a summary that trims to a shorter, equivalent phrasing deserves a bonus rather than a penalty, but then sets "the brevity bonus (or penalty) to 1 for all our experiments in this paper" — i.e. the correction it motivates is never implemented or tested here.

baselines

The only competitor tested is unmodified IBM BLEU on the same DUC 2001 data (Spearman ρ 0.66–0.82 single-doc, 0.67–0.70 multi-doc) — a fair, contemporaneous comparison, but the only alternative family in scope is "other n-gram overlap metrics." No semantic or embedding baseline existed yet to check the recall-reframing against.

contamination

Not a 2003-era concern in the modern sense — DUC 2001 systems were mostly extractive, so surface overlap and "correct content" were rarely in tension. But that also means the study's own good results (unigram ρ up to 0.99) were earned in the one regime where overlap and quality are hardest to pull apart.

what's missed

Everything past ranking agreement on one human axis (coverage): no test of individual-summary accuracy, no coherence/grammaticality signal (DUC collected it; this paper doesn't use it), no second dataset at publication time, and a brevity correction that's proposed but fixed at a no-op value rather than evaluated.

would raise confidence

Exactly what the follow-up delivered: replication on DUC 2002/2003, more systems, and overlap variants beyond fixed-length n-grams (LCS, skip-bigrams) that don't collapse when a good summary just phrases things differently — i.e., ROUGE-L/W/S.

// bottom line

This is the moment n-gram co-occurrence scoring crossed over from machine translation into summarization, and the paper's real contribution is the reframing — precision (BLEU) to recall (Ngram/coverage) — plus the counterintuitive finding that plain unigram overlap beats BLEU's own weighted formula. But the finding rests on one year of DUC data, a dozen systems, and a brevity correction the authors propose and then don't test; the paper says so itself, and defers the multi-year validation, the LCS/skip-bigram generalization, and the packaged tool to the paper it directly seeds a year later: ROUGE.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. The precision-to-recall flip is the real idea. BLEU counts what fraction of a candidate's n-grams appear in the references (precision); Lin & Hovy invert the ratio to count what fraction of the reference's n-grams the candidate recovers (recall) — matching DUC's own "coverage" protocol. That single reframing is the direct conceptual ancestor of ROUGE's name and its ROUGE-N formula, a year before either existed.
  2. Simple beat sophisticated, on this data. Plain unigram co-occurrence (Ngram(1,1)) reached Spearman ρ up to 0.989–0.993 against human rankings, decisively beating BLEU's own weighted multi-length blend, Ngram(1,4) (ρ 0.604–0.875) — the opposite of what makes BLEU work for translation, because DUC's mostly-extractive 2001 systems had no grammaticality problem for longer n-grams to fix.
  3. It's a single-dataset pilot, not the finished tool. Validated on DUC 2001 alone, 11–12 systems, no LCS/skip-bigram variants, and a brevity correction proposed but never tested (fixed at 1). Everything the follow-up would add — multi-year validation, more overlap families, a packaged and distributed tool — is exactly what ROUGE (Lin, 2004) delivered.
