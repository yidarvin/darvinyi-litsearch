FinBen: A Holistic Financial Benchmark for Large Language Models

The first broad-coverage open financial benchmark — 36 datasets across 24 tasks and seven aspects of financial work, and the first to grade LLMs on stock trading, agents, and RAG. The profile it draws is lopsided: GPT-4 leads, yet every model collapses on complex extraction (FinRED F1 0.02), numeric QA (FinQA 0.00 for all but GPT-4), and forecasting (MCC ≈ 0, barely above a coin flip).

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

By early 2024 LLMs were being pitched into finance — sentiment desks, research summarization, even trading — but there was no honest way to ask how good are they, across the actual job? The financial-LLM evals that existed were narrow. PIXIU (the authors' own prior benchmark), FinanceBench, and BizBench all concentrated on a thin slice of financial NLP — sentiment, named-entity extraction, and question answering — and most covered only one dataset per category. PIXIU was the broadest at 15 datasets over 8 tasks, but still touched just a single evaluation task in most categories; FinanceBench was one dataset, one task.

That leaves the parts of finance that are actually hard and actually valuable untested. None of these benchmarks measured risk management (credit scoring, fraud detection, bankruptcy prediction), forecasting (will the stock move up or down?), or decision-making (run an actual trading strategy and see if it makes money). And none of them used the evaluation strategies that a deployed financial assistant would rely on — retrieving from a document store (RAG) or acting as an autonomous agent. A finance benchmark that grades sentiment classification and stops there is measuring the easy 20% of the job.

FinBen's bet is coverage: build a single taxonomy that spans the seven things financial LLMs are asked to do, populate it with 36 datasets (including three new ones), and — for the first time — put a model in the driver's seat of a trading agent and score it on profit and risk, not just text overlap.

// how it works

A seven-aspect taxonomy over 36 datasets

FinBen organizes 36 datasets spanning 24 tasks into a taxonomy of seven aspects, each targeting a different financial skill: Information Extraction (IE — entities, relations, causal spans, numeric labels), Textual Analysis (TA — sentiment, hawkish/dovish policy stance, ESG, argument mining), Question Answering (QA — numeric reasoning over tables, multi-turn, long-form regulatory), Text Generation (TG — earnings-call and news summarization), Risk Management (RM — credit scoring, fraud, distress, claims), Forecasting (FO — stock-movement prediction), and Decision-Making (DM — running a stock-trading strategy). The datasets are drawn from three sources: open datasets reformulated by domain experts into instruction–response pairs for zero-shot use; datasets already in instruction form (e.g. from PIXIU); and three datasets built for this paper.

The three novel datasets plug the gaps and make up 10.32% of the data: EDTSum (2,000 financial-news articles paired with their headlines as gold abstractive summaries), Regulations (254 long-form QA items mapping EU EMIR / OTC-derivatives questions to the relevant regulatory articles, spanning 2013–2021), and — the headline addition — FinTrade (3,384 trading days over 10 stocks, fusing historical prices, news, and sentiment so a model can be evaluated as a trading agent).

Grading: text metrics for NLP, money metrics for trading

Most tasks are graded with the usual suite — Entity-F1 and Exact-Match Accuracy for extraction and QA, ROUGE / BERTScore / BARTScore for summarization, and F1 plus the Matthews Correlation Coefficient (MCC) for the heavily class-imbalanced risk tasks (MCC, unlike F1, punishes a model that classifies everything into the majority class). The decision-making aspect is the genuinely new evaluation idea: FinBen drives the FinMem trading agent with each LLM as its reasoning core and scores the resulting strategy with finance metrics — Cumulative Return (CR), Sharpe Ratio (SR), daily and annualized volatility (DV / AV), and Maximum Drawdown (MD). The Sharpe Ratio is the load-bearing one: it rewards return earned per unit of risk, so a model can't win simply by gambling.

SR = Rp − Rfσp // Sharpe Ratio — excess return (portfolio Rp over risk-free Rf) divided by its volatility σp; FinBen's trading scorecard

The evaluation sweeps 15 representative LLMs — closed models (GPT-4, ChatGPT, Gemini Pro), general open models (LLaMA2-7B/70B-chat, LLaMA3-8B, Mixtral 8×7B, Falcon-7B, InternLM-7B, Baichuan2, ChatGLM3, Code-LLaMA), and finance-tuned open models (FinMA-7B, FinGPT, DISC-FinLLM, CFGPT). Scores are zero-shot from the authors' own runs, except for a handful of cells (marked *) carried over as few-shot results from prior papers. The full sweep cost ~600 GPU-hours on 16 A100s plus GPT-4 API fees — about $51,000.

// what they found

Strong at reading, near-blind at predicting

The capability profile is sharply bimodal. On the easy half — extraction of well-formed entities and sentiment — the models do real work: GPT-4 leads named-entity recognition (NER Entity-F1 0.83, FINER-ORD 0.77), and the finance-tuned FinMA-7B tops sentiment (FPB F1 0.88) and news-headline classification (AvgF1 0.97) — beating GPT-4 on exactly the tasks it was instruction-tuned for. GPT-4 also owns the numeric-reasoning QA tasks the others can't touch: ConvFinQA Exact-Match 0.76 and FinQA 0.63, versus essentially 0.00 for every open model.

On the hard half, scores fall off a cliff — and often to literal zero. Relation extraction (FinRED) tops out at F1 0.02; numeric-span labeling (FNXL) is 0.00 for all twelve models; causal detection (CD) maxes at 0.03. Extractive earnings-call summarization (ECTSum) scores 0.00 ROUGE-1 across the board — every model fails to emit the precise label sequences it demands. Gemini is the one bright spot in generation, leading abstractive news summarization (EDTSum ROUGE-1 0.39, BERTScore 0.72).

// the wall: forecasting

Across all three stock-movement datasets (BigData22, ACL18, CIKM18), every LLM hovers at chance — accuracy roughly 0.45–0.57 on a binary up/down call, with MCC pinned near 0.00 (best single cell ≈ 0.08). The authors are blunt: even GPT-4 and Gemini perform only slightly better than random guessing, and lag traditional methods. On the risk tasks, weak instruction-followers (LLaMA2-7B/70B-chat) collapse to a single predicted class, scoring MCC = 0 on imbalanced credit/fraud data.

The trading experiment — the one place LLMs shine

Decision-making is where FinBen's novelty pays off and where the most striking number lives. Run as the brain of the FinMem agent over 10 stocks, GPT-4 turns a profit and manages risk: Cumulative Return +28.19%, Sharpe Ratio 1.51 (the only model over 1.0), and the smallest Maximum Drawdown at 18.34% — well clear of a Buy-&-Hold baseline that lost 4.00% over the same window, and of reinforcement-learning trading baselines (DQN, PPO, A2C) that all post Sharpe Ratios at or below 0.01. Gemini is a credible second (CR +14.95%, SR 1.03). But the result is fragile: GPT-4o regresses to a negative Sharpe (−0.19), LLaMA3-70B loses money (CR −2.57%), and models under 70B can't reliably follow trading instructions at all (excluded from the table).

One more data point on headroom: when FinBen seeded the first financial-LLM shared task at the FinNLP-AgentScen workshop (IJCAI-2024), 12 teams built systems that beat GPT-4 — a sign the leaderboard is far from saturated and that targeted finance tuning still buys a lot.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

FinBen's lasting contribution is the taxonomy and the breadth — and especially the trading-agent eval, the first time a financial benchmark graded an LLM on realized return and drawdown rather than token overlap. As a map of where LLMs are strong (extraction, sentiment) and weak (numeric reasoning, forecasting), it is genuinely useful. But the leaderboard underneath that map deserves several asterisks, and the trading headline deserves the most.

First, the comparison is not apples-to-apples. The authors' runs are zero-shot, but several of the strongest cells for GPT-4 and ChatGPT (NER, FPB, FinQA, ConvFinQA) are carried over as few-shot numbers from prior papers, marked only with a small *. A reader comparing GPT-4's few-shot FinQA 0.63 against an open model's zero-shot 0.00 is partly comparing prompting regimes, not models.

Second, a benchmark whose modal score is 0.00 is not discriminating capability — it is largely measuring output formatting. FNXL is zero for everyone, FinRED ≈ 0.02, ECTSum ROUGE 0.00, FinQA 0.00 for every open model. Under a single fixed zero-shot prompt and strict exact-match / span parsing, much of what's being scored is whether the model emits the exact required string, not whether it understands the finance. Real capability is almost certainly higher than these floors suggest; the benchmark just can't see it.

Third, the trading result is one regime away from reversing. FinTrade is 10 stocks over a single ~6-month window (≈ Oct 2022 → Apr 2023, a market recovery). GPT-4's Sharpe of 1.51 carries a ±1.08 confidence interval that overlaps Gemini's 1.03 ± 1.24 — and the fact that GPT-4o flips to −0.19 on the same data signals the metric is noisy and prompt-bound, not a stable capability read. Worse, the prices are from 2022–2023, squarely inside GPT-4's pretraining window: a model that has "seen" AAPL's trajectory is not running an honest out-of-sample backtest.

Fourth, contamination cuts both ways. The static NLP datasets (NER, FPB, FinQA, ConvFinQA) long predate the models and are plausibly in pretraining corpora; the trading window postdates nothing the frontier models haven't read. And the whole sweep is a 2024 snapshot — GPT-4, ChatGPT, Gemini Pro 1.0 — so the absolute numbers are a watermark of that generation, not a standing fact.

baselines

Buy-&-Hold and three RL traders (DQN/PPO/A2C) anchor the trading task well — and GPT-4 clearly beats them. But the LLM leaderboard mixes the authors' zero-shot runs with few-shot (*) cells lifted from prior papers, so the closed-vs-open gaps are partly a prompting artifact.

contamination

High on both ends: the classic NLP datasets are likely in pretraining data, and the 2022–2023 FinTrade prices fall inside the frontier models' knowledge cutoff — a real look-ahead risk for a backtest. No contamination probe is reported.

what's missed

Pervasive 0.00 scores conflate "can't do the task" with "didn't match the exact output format" under one fixed prompt; no prompt-robustness or few-shot sweep for open models. Forecasting has near-zero signal (MCC ≈ 0), so it can't separate models regardless of skill.

would raise confidence

A uniform prompting regime across all models; prompt-sensitivity bands; many more stocks across multiple market regimes with a strict post-cutoff holdout for FinTrade; and either dropping forecasting or reframing it given the efficient-market noise floor.

// bottom line

Read FinBen as a breadth taxonomy and a 2024 capability map, not a settled ranking. Its enduring ideas — seven-aspect coverage and agent-graded trading — are right; its specific numbers are dominated by format-following, near-random forecasting, and a single-window, contamination-exposed trading backtest. The GPT-4 trading win is the most quotable result and the one to trust least.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. The most holistic open financial benchmark of its time: 36 datasets / 24 tasks / 7 aspects (IE, TA, QA, TG, RM, FO, DM), with three new datasets (EDTSum, FinTrade, Regulations) and the first agent-based stock-trading and RAG evaluation in finance.
  2. Lopsided capability profile: LLMs are strong at extraction and sentiment (GPT-4 NER 0.83; FinMA-7B FPB 0.88, Headlines 0.97) but collapse on complex extraction (FinRED 0.02, FNXL 0.00), numeric QA (FinQA 0.00 for every open model; GPT-4 0.63), extractive summarization (ECTSum 0.00 everywhere), and forecasting (MCC ≈ 0 — barely above a coin flip).
  3. Trading is the bright spot — and the shakiest claim: GPT-4 posts CR +28.19% and Sharpe 1.51 with the lowest drawdown (18.34%), beating Buy-&-Hold (−4.00%) and RL traders (Sharpe ≤ 0.01) — but on 10 stocks over one 2022–2023 window with wide CIs, in-window prices, and GPT-4o regressing to −0.19.
  4. Mind the methodology: closed models carry few-shot (*) numbers while open models are zero-shot, and many 0.00 scores measure format-following rather than finance skill. Twelve workshop teams later beat GPT-4 — the bar is real but far from saturated.
