Soft Actor-Critic: Off-Policy Maximum Entropy Deep Reinforcement Learning with a Stochastic Actor

An off-policy actor-critic algorithm that folds an entropy bonus straight into the reward objective — "succeed at the task while acting as randomly as possible" — turning exploration into training signal instead of injected noise, and becoming one of continuous control's most durable MuJoCo baselines in the process.

Tuomas Haarnoja, Aurick Zhou, Pieter Abbeel, Sergey Levine — UC Berkeley (BAIR)

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

By 2017, model-free deep RL for continuous control was stuck between two bad options. On-policy methods — TRPO, PPO, A3C — throw away every batch of data after one gradient step, so they are comparatively stable but burn through millions of environment interactions for even modest tasks. Off-policy methods can reuse a replay buffer and learn faster in principle, but the paper's own related-work section is blunt about the cost: DDPG, "regarded as one of the more efficient off-policy deep RL methods," is also "notoriously challenging to use due to its extreme brittleness and hyperparameter sensitivity" — a fragility the authors don't just assert but cite directly, pointing to Duan et al.'s rllab benchmarking study and Henderson et al.'s reproducibility critique Deep Reinforcement Learning that Matters. On the hardest tasks the gap becomes an outright failure mode: DDPG's deterministic-actor-plus-Q-critic design simply can't make progress on the 21-action-dimension rllab Humanoid at all. Prior maximum-entropy RL work — including the authors' own soft Q-learning (SQL) — had explored entropy bonuses for better exploration, but was either on-policy (poor sample efficiency, same as above) or required "complex approximate inference procedures in continuous action spaces" that don't scale cleanly. The gap SAC targets is therefore precise: an algorithm that is simultaneously off-policy (for sample efficiency) and stable across random seeds and hyperparameters (for practical usability) — a combination no single continuous-control algorithm had delivered together.

// how it works

The method

SAC's central move is to fold exploration directly into the optimization objective, rather than bolt it on as injected action noise the way DDPG does. Instead of maximizing expected return alone, the actor maximizes expected return plus the expected entropy of its own action distribution at every timestep — the policy is rewarded not just for succeeding, but for succeeding while remaining as stochastic as possible. A temperature parameter α sets how much entropy is worth relative to reward, and the paper notes the conventional RL objective is recovered exactly as α → 0. The authors first derive "soft policy iteration" in the tabular setting — alternating a modified Bellman backup (Eq. 2–3, using V(sₜ) = 𝔼[Q(sₜ,aₜ) − log π(aₜ|sₜ)] as the soft state value) with a KL-projection policy-improvement step — and prove two convergence lemmas plus a theorem showing this procedure converges to the optimal maximum-entropy policy within a policy class. That's the theoretical backbone; the practical algorithm approximates it with three function approximators trained jointly by stochastic gradient descent: a state-value network Vψ, a soft Q-network Qθ, and a reparameterized-Gaussian policy πφ (actions are drawn as at = fφ(εt; st), letting the Q-function's gradient backprop straight through the actor via the reparameterization trick rather than a higher-variance likelihood-ratio estimator). To fight the positive-bias problem that plagues Q-learning-style value estimation — identified by Hasselt's Double Q-learning and revisited concurrently by Fujimoto et al.'s TD3 — SAC parameterizes two independent Q-networks and takes their minimum for both the value-network target and the policy gradient, the same clipped-double-Q trick TD3 uses. In practice, the algorithm alternates one environment step with one (or several) gradient steps per network, tracking a slowly-updated target value network via an exponential moving average with smoothing constant τ.

// what they found

The results

The comparative evaluation runs on the OpenAI Gym continuous-control suite (Hopper-v1, Walker2d-v1, HalfCheetah-v1, Ant-v1, Humanoid-v1) plus the rllab implementation of the 21-action-dimension Humanoid task, training budgets ranging from 1 million steps (Hopper, Walker2d) to 3 million (HalfCheetah, Ant) to 10 million (both Humanoid variants), with five random seeds per algorithm and an evaluation rollout every 1,000 environment steps. The baselines are DDPG, PPO, and the authors' own soft Q-learning (SQL, reimplemented here with two Q-functions for a fairer comparison) — plus TD3, "using the author-provided implementation," explicitly flagged as "a concurrent work" rather than an established prior baseline. The paper's own summary: SAC "performs comparably to the baseline methods on the easier tasks and outperforms them on the harder tasks with a large margin, both in terms of learning speed and the final performance." The starkest gap is DDPG's outright failure to learn: it "fails to make any progress on Ant-v1, Humanoid-v1, and Humanoid (rllab)," a result the authors say is "corroborated by prior work" (Gu et al., Duan et al.). Reading directly off Figure 1: on HalfCheetah-v1, SAC's curve reaches roughly 15,000 average return by 3M steps, well ahead of TD3's roughly 9,500–10,000, SQL's noisier climb to about 7,000, and PPO's roughly 2,500; on the 21-dimensional rllab Humanoid, SAC is the only method that climbs cleanly above 5,000 average return within 10M steps while DDPG stays pinned near zero for the entire run.

A separate ablation (Figure 3) isolates two of SAC's operating knobs on Ant-v1. First, reward scale: the paper states plainly that it is "the only hyperparameter that requires tuning," and the sweep shows why — a scale of 1 leaves the policy "nearly uniform" and unable to exploit the reward at all, while a scale of 100 makes the policy "nearly deterministic," settling into a visibly worse asymptote than scales of 10 or 30. Second, the target-network smoothing constant τ: the paper reports using τ = 0.005 unchanged "across all of the tasks," and the Ant-v1 sweep over τ ∈ {0.0001, 0.001, 0.01, 0.1} shows a comparatively wide usable band before a large τ (0.1) visibly destabilizes training into negative-return excursions.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

The paper's introduction frames the goal as escaping "meticulous hyperparameter tuning," but its own ablation quietly walks that back: reward scale is, in the authors' words, "the only hyperparameter that requires tuning," and "the optimal reward scale varies between environments, and should be tuned for each task separately." That is a real per-environment hyperparameter search hiding behind a single scalar — a materially different claim than "hyperparameter-robust," even if it's a smaller search space than DDPG's brittleness. It's also worth being precise about what this specific paper does not contain: it fixes the temperature/reward-scale manually rather than adapting it automatically during training. The now-standard "automatic temperature tuning" that most modern SAC implementations use by default was introduced in a separate, later technical report (Haarnoja et al., Soft Actor-Critic Algorithms and Applications, 2018) — not in this ICML paper. A reader citing "SAC" for its auto-tuned-entropy behavior is often actually relying on that follow-up, a distinction worth keeping straight when comparing implementations.

On the comparison design itself: DDPG, PPO, and SQL are the authors' own implementations, while TD3 uses "the author-provided implementation" but is explicitly labeled "concurrent work" rather than a mature, independently-vetted baseline — TD3 and SAC were developed in parallel and each paper's own experiments necessarily reflect an early snapshot of the other. The paper cites Henderson et al.'s Deep Reinforcement Learning that Matters directly as related work, which makes the omission of its own methodology from that lens notable: five seeds per condition, min/max shading rather than confidence intervals or standard error, and no report of wall-clock or compute cost alongside the "million environment steps" x-axis — so despite SAC's clear data-efficiency edge, its actual training-time cost relative to PPO (which needs far fewer gradient updates per environment step) isn't visible from the figures alone.

baselines

DDPG/PPO/SQL are the authors' own implementations; TD3 uses the original authors' code but is explicitly flagged as concurrent work, not an independently mature baseline — an asymmetric, same-lab comparison design typical of the era.

contamination

Not applicable in the leakage sense (this is a control-task RL paper, not a held-out-eval benchmark) — the closer analogue is whether the reward-scale sweep was tuned on the same runs used for the reported curves; the paper doesn't disclose a separate tuning split.

what's missed

Wall-clock/compute cost per gradient step is never reported next to the "million steps" axis, so the practical training-time trade-off against PPO (fewer, cheaper updates) is invisible; also no statistical test on the seed-to-seed gaps, only min/max shading.

would raise confidence

More than five seeds with proper confidence intervals; an independent (non-author) TD3 baseline tuned to convergence rather than "concurrent"; wall-clock cost curves alongside sample curves; and testing the manual-reward-scale sensitivity against the later automatic-temperature variant on the same tasks.

// bottom line

The central empirical claim — that entropy-regularized, off-policy actor-critic training is both more sample-efficient and more stable than DDPG and PPO on hard continuous-control tasks — holds up well by the paper's own curves, especially on the 21-dimensional Humanoid where DDPG simply fails. But the "avoids meticulous hyperparameter tuning" framing oversells what this particular version of the algorithm delivers: reward scale is a real, per-task-tuned free parameter here, and the fix that removed that knob (automatic temperature tuning) is a different, later paper.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. SAC maximizes expected reward plus expected policy entropy (Eq. 1) — the same objective that drives exploration is the one the paper proves converges to an optimal fixed point via "soft policy iteration," derived first in the tabular case and then approximated with function approximators for continuous control.
  2. It borrows the clipped double-Q trick — training two independent Q-networks and taking their minimum — from concurrently-developed TD3 work, to counter the positive-bias problem that makes DDPG-style off-policy actor-critics unstable.
  3. Reward scale, not a learning rate or network width, is "the only hyperparameter that requires tuning" in this version of the algorithm; the automatic-temperature-tuning fix that later removed that per-task knob shipped in a separate 2018 follow-up paper, not this one.
