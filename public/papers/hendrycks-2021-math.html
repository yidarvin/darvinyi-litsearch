Measuring Mathematical Problem Solving With the MATH Dataset

12,500 competition-math problems, each with a full worked solution, graded by exact match on a single boxed answer. In 2021 the best models scored 3–7% and the authors forecast that scale alone wouldn't fix it — you'd need ~1035 parameters. Chain-of-thought and RL would overturn that prediction within two years.

Dan Hendrycks, Collin Burns, Saurav Kadavath, Akul Arora, Steven Basart, Eric Tang, Dawn Song, Jacob Steinhardt

What problem is this solving?

Before MATH, machine-learning benchmarks for mathematics fell into two camps, and both sidestepped genuine problem solving. Formal theorem-proving suites — HOList, HOLStep, the Metamath formalization GPT-f trained on — made models operate inside a proof-checking environment with a formal language. That gives automatic grading, but it is divorced from how mathematics is actually written and read, and it requires a heavyweight verifier. Plug-and-chug suites — the DeepMind Mathematics dataset, symbolic-integration tasks — were algorithmically generated calculations (arithmetic, sorting, differentiation) that Transformers could already do: Lample & Charton report >95% on symbolic integration.

The tell is in the leaderboard: on every one of those datasets, state-of-the-art was already 73–99%. None of them measured the thing that makes mathematics hard for a person — reading a novel word problem, picking good heuristics out of a large space, and chaining them into a derivation. There was also a tooling gap: free-form natural-language math resists automatic grading because the same answer can be written many ways, forcing prior work toward heuristic metrics like BLEU. MATH targets exactly this seam: competition problems written in LaTeX and natural language, hard enough that frontier models of the day score in the single digits, yet still exactly gradable.

// how it works

The method

The paper ships two datasets. MATH is the benchmark: 12,500 problems (7,500 train / 5,000 test) drawn from US high-school competitions — AMC 10, AMC 12, AIME and others, collected largely from AoPS contest archives. Every problem carries a full step-by-step solution and a single final answer wrapped in a \boxed{} command. Problems are tagged with one of seven subjects (Prealgebra, Algebra, Number Theory, Counting & Probability, Geometry, Intermediate Algebra, Precalculus) and a difficulty level 1–5, enabling fine-grained breakdowns. Geometry diagrams are encoded as Asymptote source, so even figure-bearing problems are fully expressible in text.

Exact-match grading is the key move

Because the final answer always sits inside \boxed{}, the grader can locate it unambiguously and compare it to ground truth under a set of normalization rules: fractions are canonicalized (\frac{x}{y} ≡ x/y ≡ \dfrac), probabilities are simplified fractions, multi-variable expressions are alphabetized, polynomials are ordered by decreasing degree, units may be present or omitted, spaces are ignored, 0.5 ≡ 1/2, and factor orderings are interchangeable. This dispenses with BLEU-style heuristics and yields a clean accuracy number over a combinatorially large output space.

AMPS: a pretraining corpus for the fundamentals

AMPS (Auxiliary Mathematics Problems and Solutions) is the second contribution — a corpus to teach models the basics before they ever see competition problems. It has two parts: a Khan Academy slice (693 exercise types, >100,000 problems with full solutions, from elementary arithmetic through multivariable calculus) and a Mathematica-generated slice (100 hand-written scripts spanning algebra, calculus, statistics, geometry, linear algebra and number theory; 37 of them emit step-by-step LaTeX; ~50,000 exercises per script ≈ 5 million problems, ~23 GB — larger than the 16 GB of text used to train BERT).

Models are autoregressive LMs: GPT-2 at 0.1B–1.5B (pretrained on AMPS, then fine-tuned on MATH) and GPT-3 Curie (~13B) and Davinci (~175B) through the OpenAI API, either fine-tuned where the API allowed or run few-shot with 8 exemplars at temperature 0, capped at 20 output tokens. T5 was tried but its tokenizer strips many LaTeX symbols, so after a two-week sweep it stayed uncompetitive.

// what they found

The results

The headline: 3.0%–6.9%. The AMPS-pretrained, fine-tuned GPT-2 1.5B topped the table at 6.9% average accuracy; few-shot GPT-3 175B managed only 5.2%, and few-shot GPT-3 13B just 3.0%. Accuracy climbed painfully slowly with scale — from 5.4% at 0.1B to 6.9% at 1.5B, a 28% relative gain for a 15× parameter increase. Extending that log-linear trend, 40% accuracy would require ~1035 parameters, the basis for the paper's claim that scaling alone is impractical here.

Humans, by contrast, clear it. On a 20-problem sample (one hour, no calculators), a computer-science PhD student who dislikes math scored 40% (8/20), another participant 13/20, and a three-time IMO gold medalist 90%.

AMPS beats raw scale — and real-world data

Pretraining on AMPS was worth roughly a 130× parameter increase: a 0.1B model with AMPS (5.4%) matched a 13B model without it (5.2%). AMPS even beat genuine, less-curated math text — a GPT-2 0.3B pretrained on AMPS plus ~3 GB of Math StackExchange reached 6.0%, below the 6.2% from AMPS alone.

Models couldn't use their own scratch space

Forcing GPT-2 1.5B to generate a full solution before its boxed answer lowered accuracy from 6.9% to 5.3% — a "snowballing" effect where an early mistake derails the rest of the generation. Yet supervision from solutions still helped: stripping step-by-step solutions from training cost 0.6% (down to 6.3%), and when a 0.7B model was handed 99% of the ground-truth solution as a hint it still reached only ~40%. Models were also badly miscalibrated — confidences clustered near 100% for both correct and incorrect answers.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

The central forecast was wrong, and fast. The paper's signature claim — that scaling "is not currently solving MATH," needing ~1035 parameters for 40% — was overtaken within roughly 18 months. Minerva (2022) hit 50.3%; GPT-4 (2023) landed in the 40s–50s; by 2024–25 DeepSeekMath-, o1- and R1-class systems report 90%+ on the MATH test set. The mechanism the extrapolation missed is the one the paper itself stumbled onto: chain-of-thought. Its sub-2B models got worse when they generated scratch-work, so the authors read scratch space as unhelpful — but that was a property of 2021-scale models, not of the task. Scale plus CoT plus RL on verifiable answers turned "impractical" into "saturated." Fitting a capability curve from models that couldn't yet do in-context reasoning is exactly how you mis-forecast the curve.

The GPT-3 numbers under-measured the model. The few-shot prompts deliberately omitted step-by-step solutions ("due to space"), used temperature 0, and capped output at 20 tokens — precisely the harness that suppresses reasoning. The resulting 3–5% figures say more about the evaluation setup than about Davinci's latent ability, and they reinforced the pessimistic read.

Contamination is now the dominant caveat. MATH is built from public AMC/AIME problems and scraped largely from AoPS, which is mirrored all over the web. Any model pretrained on a post-2021 crawl has plausibly seen the test problems and their solutions, so high modern scores partly reflect memorization. This is why later work pairs MATH-500 with held-out contest sets (AIME-2024/2025) and treats static MATH scores with caution.

Exact-match grades the answer, not the reasoning. The paper's own Figure 3 shows a model reaching the right boxed answer through an invalid derivation. Final-answer accuracy rewards lucky guesses and cannot tell sound work from unsound — the very thing a "problem-solving" benchmark claims to measure. The normalization rules are thorough but inevitably both over- and under-match in edge cases.

baselines

GPT-2 (0.1–1.5B) and GPT-3 Curie/Davinci, evaluated few-shot without chain-of-thought (solutions omitted, temp 0, ≤20 tokens) — a harness that suppresses the reasoning the task requires.

contamination

Test items are public AMC/AIME problems scraped from AoPS and widely mirrored; modern web-trained models have likely seen them, so today's MATH scores partly measure memorization.

what's missed

Exact-match grades only the final boxed answer — it credits right-answer/wrong-derivation cases (their Fig 3) and is blind to whether the reasoning is valid. The human baseline is also non-independent: of the six university-student participants, four are the paper's own authors (the study explicitly couldn't use crowdsourcing given the math background required), so the 40%–90% human range is self-reported, not blind third-party data.

would raise confidence

A contamination-controlled / refreshed test split, CoT-enabled evaluation of large models (impossible at the time), step-level (process) grading, and a larger human study with independent, non-author participants.

// bottom line

MATH the dataset is one of the most consequential reasoning benchmarks of the era — it created an automatically-gradable target that the entire CoT → Minerva → RLVR line optimized against, and its test set lives on as a default frontier eval. MATH the forecast — "scale won't solve this" — was decisively falsified, which is itself one of the most instructive results the paper produced.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. MATH turned competition mathematics into an automatically-gradable benchmark: 12,500 problems (5,000 test) with worked solutions, scored by exact match on a \boxed{} answer under careful normalization. That design is why the MATH(-500) test set is still a default frontier-reasoning eval.
  2. Its headline forecast — best models 3–7%, and scale alone would need ~1035 parameters for 40% — was wrong within two years. The extrapolation was drawn from sub-2B models that couldn't yet do in-context reasoning; chain-of-thought, scale, and RL on verifiable answers pushed MATH past 90%.
  3. Two findings aged better than the forecast: AMPS pretraining let a 0.1B model match a 130×-larger one, and 2021-era models could not use their own generated scratch-work (it hurt accuracy) — naming, in advance, the exact gap that chain-of-thought would close.
