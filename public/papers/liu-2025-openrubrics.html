OpenRubrics: Towards Scalable Synthetic Rubric Generation for Reward Modeling and LLM Alignment

Rubrics-as-rewards only work if someone writes good rubrics — and hand-authoring them doesn't scale. OpenRubrics auto-generates them by contrasting a prompt's chosen and rejected answers, filters out the unreliable ones, and trains a rubric-grounded reward model that beats same-size judges by 8.4 points.

Tianci Liu, Ran Xu, Tony Yu, Ilgee Hong, Carl Yang, Tuo Zhao, Haoyu Wang · Purdue · Emory · Georgia Tech · UAlbany

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

Reward models are the steering wheel of RLHF, but the usual ones hand back a single scalar or a bare A-beats-B preference. For subjective, open-ended tasks — long-form QA, general helpfulness, medical advice — that one number flattens a multi-dimensional judgment (is it factual? on-topic? does it honor the explicit constraints?) into a verdict you can't inspect.

Rubrics-as-rewards (RaR) is the fix the field landed on: decompose quality into a checklist of natural-language criteria and score against each. The catch is producing the rubrics. HealthBench and other expert benchmarks pay clinicians and domain experts to write them — accurate, but costly and impossible to scale. The cheaper route, directly prompting a frontier LLM to invent a rubric per query, has two failure modes the authors target head-on: weak quality control (the model writes vague or wrong criteria with nothing checking them) and runaway cost when every judgment calls a commercial API.

OpenRubrics' bet is that you don't need experts or a frontier API at inference time. You need a one-time, contrastively-built dataset of (prompt, rubric) pairs — 35.7k prompts and ~85.6k pairwise instances drawn from six public preference and instruction corpora (UltraFeedback, Magpie, Skywork-Preference, Synthetic-IF, MegaScience, Medical-o1) — that you distill into a small open model. That model then writes the rubrics for free.

// how it works

The method

The core idea is that rubrics come in two flavors, and a good generator needs both. Hard rules are explicit, objective constraints lifted straight from the prompt ("rewrite in the negative form", "fewer than two paragraphs"). Principles are the implicit qualities a strong answer shares — preserve meaning, stay grammatical, keep imagery vivid — that no prompt spells out. Hard rules are easy to extract; principles are subtle and need fine-grained reasoning.

Contrastive Rubric Generation (CRG)

This is the trick that surfaces principles. Instead of asking an LLM "what makes a good answer to x?" in a vacuum, CRG conditions the generator on the prompt plus a ranked list of responses — at minimum the chosen and rejected answers from an existing preference pair. By forcing the model to explain why the winner beats the loser, the contrast pulls out discriminative, ranking-aware criteria rather than generic platitudes. The generator emits a mixed set of hard rules and principles per prompt.

Filtering by preference-label consistency

Generated rubrics aren't trusted by default. Each rubric is concatenated back into the context and the same instruction-tuned model is asked to re-judge the induced pairs. A rubric is kept only if, used as the scoring guide, it reproduces the known human preference — a self-check that throws out criteria that sound plausible but don't actually separate good from bad answers:

Acci = 1|𝒫i| Σ(a,b)∈𝒫i 𝟙[ ℓ̂(a,b) = ℓ(a,b) ] ≥ τ // keep the rubric only if it reproduces the human label on the induced pairs (τ = 0.5), and keep each pair only when that pair is also judged correctly

Two models, two stages

The filtered data trains two components, both fine-tuned from Qwen-3-8B (a 4B variant is also reported). A rubric generator gθ learns to write rubrics from a prompt alone (standard cross-entropy SFT); a judge rϕ learns to output the pairwise verdict conditioned on the prompt, the two responses, and the generated rubric. Together they are Rubric-RM. At inference the generator writes (or reuses a cached) rubric, then the judge picks the winner — grounding every decision in explicit criteria. A voting@5 variant majority-votes over five sampled judge trajectories.

// what they found

The results

As a reward model, Rubric-RM-8B averages 70.1 across eight reward-modeling benchmarks (RewardBench, RM-Bench, RewardBench2, the IF suite, HelpSteer3), and even the 4B version hits 68.4 — both clearing every 7B-scale white-box judge, whose best is RM-R1-7B at 61.7. That is the headline +8.4-point margin over a size-matched baseline. With voting@5, the 8B model reaches 73.0, edging past the larger RM-R1-14B (71.7) and a GPT-4.1-mini + Gemini-2.5-Flash-Lite API rubric+judge pipeline (71.3).

The cleanest signal that the rubrics are doing the work: naively prompting the untuned Qwen-3-8B to generate-then-judge scores only 57.7; the same backbone, fine-tuned on OpenRubrics, scores 70.1 — a +12.4 jump. Gains concentrate where fine-grained constraints matter (FollowBench 81.5, InfoBench 83.8).

The gains transfer when Rubric-RM becomes the reward signal for policy training (offline DPO on Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct). On complex instruction-following, the Rubric-RM-trained policy reaches 30.3 on IFBench — above RLCF (28.2) and well past RLMT variants (22.4–22.8) — and lifts IFEval to 79.5 (vs. 76.0 with Skywork/ArmoRM) and InfoBench to 83.0. On general alignment it tops open-source reward models on Arena-Hard (53.1 SC), AlpacaEval (47.0), and WildBench (53.6 macro, +4.9 over the base model).

The biomedical study is the strongest case for domain-tuned rubrics. On HealthBench, Rubric-RM scores 68.3 as a judge (beating RRM-7B's 63.3 and approaching RM-R1-14B's 69.9), and voting@5 reaches 72.9. The naive Qwen-3-8B rubric+judge manages just 51.8 here — a +16.5 gap that the authors attribute to science-specific rubric SFT. Used to optimize a policy, Rubric-RM pushes HealthBench from 21.6 to 23.8, ahead of ArmoRM (22.5) and RM-R1-7B (22.7). And it's not slow: 130.8s per 100 samples, faster than RRM-7B (203.4s) and the RM-R1 family (170.8–382.0s), with rubrics cacheable across examples.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

The benchmark sweep is broad and the baselines are current — JudgeLRM, RRM, RM-R1 (all 2025), plus 14B and API references — so the top-line claim that a contrastively-built 8B judge beats same-size reasoning judges is well supported. The softer spots are about attribution and circularity, not whether the numbers are real.

The headline +8.4 (and the +12.4 over naive prompting) is offered as proof that rubrics are the active ingredient, but the only contrast is the untuned base model. There is no "fine-tune Qwen-3-8B on the exact same preference pairs without rubrics" control in the main table, and no ablation isolating CRG from the consistency filter. So the +12.4 bundles three things — domain SFT, contrastive generation, and filtering — and we can't see how the credit splits.

Distribution overlap also flatters the score. The training mix (UltraFeedback, Synthetic-IF, MegaScience, Medical-o1) lines up closely with the IF-heavy and science evals, and the FollowBench/InfoBench pairwise sets are built by sampling the same Qwen-3-8B/14B family used to construct preferences. Worse, the labels Rubric-RM learns from on MegaScience/Medical-o1 come from a reward-model ensemble (Athene-RM, Skywork-Reward-V2) — model-generated supervision judged, then filtered, by the same family of models. The consistency filter (τ = 0.5, barely better than chance, using the same generator as judge) can't catch blind spots the generator and judge share.

baselines

Strong and contemporary (JudgeLRM/RRM/RM-R1, plus 14B and GPT-4.1-mini/Gemini API refs). Missing the one that matters most: no same-data, no-rubric SFT control, and no CRG-vs-filter ablation, so the rubric contribution is asserted rather than isolated.

contamination

Training corpora overlap the eval domains; IF benchmarks are re-sampled from the same model family used to build preferences; cross-domain preference labels are themselves model-generated — a real circularity risk the paper doesn't quantify with a held-out, leakage-controlled set.

what's missed

Everything is pairwise preference accuracy and offline DPO. No absolute scoring, no multi-response ranking, and no online RLHF — all flagged in their own Limitations. Pairwise accuracy on benchmarks where one answer deliberately breaks a constraint can overstate real-world discrimination.

would raise confidence

A no-rubric SFT control on identical pairs; an ablation separating CRG from consistency filtering; human (not model) judgments of rubric quality; a contamination-controlled benchmark; and at least one online-RL result to back the "training signal" framing.

// bottom line

A genuinely useful, scalable recipe — auto-written rubrics from cheap contrast, self-filtered, distilled into a fast open model — with convincing breadth. But the paper proves "Rubric-RM > baselines" more firmly than it proves "rubrics are why." The standout result, the +16.5 on HealthBench, is also the one most exposed to in-distribution science-data overlap. Strong engineering; the causal story needs one more control.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. Contrast, don't just prompt. Generating rubrics by explaining why a chosen answer beats a rejected one (CRG) surfaces discriminative principles, not generic platitudes — the part direct prompting misses.
  2. Filter rubrics by whether they reproduce the preference. Feeding a rubric back to a judge and keeping it only if it recovers the human label is a cheap self-check that turns noisy synthetic criteria into reliable training signal.
  3. Rubric-aware fine-tuning buys a lot. Same Qwen-3-8B backbone: 57.7 naive → 70.1 tuned (+12.4), 73.0 with voting@5 — beating a 14B reasoning judge, at lower latency, with cacheable rubrics. The open question is how much credit goes to rubrics vs. the in-domain SFT they ride on.
