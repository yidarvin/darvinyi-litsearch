Benchmarking Neural Network Robustness to Common Corruptions and Perturbations

Introduces IMAGENET-C (15 algorithmic corruptions × 5 severities) and IMAGENET-P (perturbation sequences) as the field's first standardized benchmarks for common, non-adversarial robustness — and finds that a decade of ImageNet progress from AlexNet to ResNet-50 bought almost no relative corruption robustness once the accompanying clean-accuracy gains are normalized away.

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

By 2019, "robustness" in deep vision research had become nearly synonymous with adversarial robustness: worst-case, imperceptible pixel perturbations crafted by an optimizer specifically to fool a classifier. An entire cat-and-mouse literature of attacks and defenses had grown around it, and most defenses were quickly broken by a new attack. That framing, however useful, quietly excluded the failures a deployed vision system runs into constantly: a security camera goes grainy at night, a phone photo comes out of focus, a windshield fogs up, a JPEG gets recompressed one time too many. None of that is adversarial — nobody is optimizing those corruptions to break the network — yet prior fragility studies (Hosseini et al. breaking Google's Cloud Vision API with plain impulse noise; Geirhos et al.'s human-vs-network comparisons on noise and blur) suggested ordinary corruptions could be just as damaging, with no shared way to measure it.

Speech recognition had already solved this class of problem for its own modality: noisy-test benchmarks were standard practice, and the field explicitly separated "robust to common noise" from "robust to adversarial audio." Vision had no equivalent. Scattered corruption studies existed, but each used its own corruption types, severities, and architectures, so results were incomparable — inviting exactly the "moving goal posts and result cherry picking" the authors call out by name. Without a shared, standardized stress test, a claim like "our model is more robust" could not be checked, replicated, or ranked against a competing claim.

// how it works

The method

The fix is to build vision the benchmark speech recognition already had. IMAGENET-C takes the ImageNet validation set and applies 15 corruption types, drawn from four families — noise, blur, weather, digital — at five severity levels each, for 75 distinct corrupted test conditions per image: Gaussian, shot (Poisson), and impulse noise; defocus, frosted-glass, motion, and zoom blur; snow, frost, and fog (rendered procedurally — fog via a diamond-square algorithm); and brightness, contrast, elastic transform, pixelation, and JPEG compression as the "digital" family. Corruptions are applied only at test time — "networks should be trained on datasets such as ImageNet and not be trained on IMAGENET-C corruptions" — so the benchmark measures generalization to unseen corruption, not a model's ability to memorize a fixed augmentation recipe. To catch exactly that kind of overfitting to the visible 15 types, the authors also release four held-out validation corruptions (speckle noise, Gaussian blur, spatter, saturate), one per family, meant only for tuning — never for the headline number.

IMAGENET-P asks a different question: not "how much does accuracy drop under one corrupted snapshot" but "how stable are a model's predictions as an input drifts continuously" — the failure mode that erodes trust when, say, a video classifier's label for the same object flickers from frame to frame. Ten perturbation types spanning the same four families generate sequences of 30+ frames per validation image — tilt, translate, rotate, and scale/zoom ramps, brightness ramps, and small per-frame noise/blur increments — and the benchmark checks whether consecutive frames keep receiving the same prediction.

Both benchmarks need a metric that isn't raw accuracy, because a classifier with low clean error can look artificially "robust" simply by having less room left to fall. So every measure is normalized against a fixed reference classifier, AlexNet. The Corruption Error for corruption type c sums a classifier's error across the five severities and divides by AlexNet's error summed the same way:

CEcf = Σs=1..5 Es,cfΣs=1..5 Es,cAlexNet // averaged across all 15 corruption types → mean Corruption Error (mCE), scaled so AlexNet always scores exactly 100

A second variant, Relative CE, first subtracts each model's own clean error before normalizing, isolating how much a model degrades under corruption from how bad it already was on clean data; averaged into the Relative mCE, it answers a subtly different question — not "how correct is this model under corruption" but "how much extra damage does corruption do, beyond the model's own baseline error." For IMAGENET-P, the analogous quantities are the Flip Probability (how often consecutive frames in a sequence flip the top-1 prediction), normalized into a Flip Rate and averaged into the mean Flip Rate (mFR), plus a Top-5 Distance metric (mT5D) that penalizes churn anywhere in the ranked top-5 list, not just the top-1 label.

// what they found

The results

The headline table spans seven architectures across nearly a decade of progress: AlexNet (43.5% clean error, mCE 100.0 by definition), SqueezeNet 1.1 (41.8%, mCE 104.4), VGG-11 (31.0%, mCE 93.5), VGG-19 (27.6%, mCE 88.9), VGG-19+BN (25.8%, mCE 81.6), ResNet-18 (30.2%, mCE 84.7), and ResNet-50 (23.9%, mCE 76.7). Read at face value, this is good news: as architectures got more accurate, mCE fell in lockstep — exactly the trend you'd hope robustness research would show.

But the Relative mCE column — which isolates corruption-induced degradation from each model's own clean-error baseline — tells a different story: every architecture newer than AlexNet has a worse Relative mCE than AlexNet itself (105.0 for ResNet-50 vs. 100.0). None of a decade's worth of architectural progress bought dedicated corruption robustness — ResNet-50 is "more robust" only in the trivial sense that it starts from a lower clean-error floor; degrade it proportionally and it falls apart slightly more than AlexNet does. This is the finding behind the abstract's understated line that there are "negligible changes in relative corruption robustness from AlexNet classifiers to ResNet classifiers."

On IMAGENET-P the same architectures show a cleaner trend: ResNet-50 posts the best mFR (58.0, vs. AlexNet's 100.0 baseline) and mT5D (78.3), and larger feature-aggregating networks push further still — DenseNet-121 (mFR 56.4, mT5D 76.8), ResNeXt-50 (52.4, 74.2), DenseNet-161 (46.9, 69.5), and ResNeXt-101 (43.2, 65.9) — so perturbation stability, unlike corruption robustness, does scale cleanly with model size and feature aggregation. A grab-bag of robustness interventions gives mixed, sometimes counterintuitive results: CLAHE histogram-equalization preprocessing nudges a fine-tuned ResNet-50 from 76.7 to 74.5 mCE; training on Stylized ImageNet (Geirhos et al.'s texture-suppressing augmentation) drops it further to 69.3; and a bypassed adversarial defense, Adversarial Logit Pairing, still manages a 41% relative cut in Tiny-ImageNet-P's mean Flip Probability and 40% in mT5D — genuine common-perturbation robustness leaking out of a defense that fails against the adversarial threat it was built for. Set against these wins, several plausible fixes flatly don't work: five-epoch stability-training fine-tunes make mCE worse across every noise-strength and weight combination tried; 10-crop classification's mCE gains never outpace its accuracy gains; and smaller, pruned CondenseNets are less robust (26.3% error / 80.8 mCE, and with more pruning, 28.9% / 84.6) — directly refuting the intuition that simpler models generalize better.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

IMAGENET-C and IMAGENET-P earned their place as the default "common robustness" benchmark for half a decade of vision research, and the mCE/mFR vocabulary this paper coined is now cited reflexively in almost any robustness paper's evaluation section. But three issues are worth naming explicitly, because they're easy to lose once a paper gets reduced to a single "our mCE: 68.2" line in someone else's related-work table.

First, synthetic corruption and natural distribution shift are not the same threat, and this paper never claims they are — but the field routinely treats an mCE improvement as evidence of general-purpose robustness. A closely related follow-up by several of the same authors' collaborators, Taori, Dave, Shankar, Carlini, Recht & Schmidt's "Measuring Robustness to Natural Distribution Shifts in Image Classification" (NeurIPS 2020), evaluates 204 ImageNet models across 213 test conditions and finds "little to no transfer of robustness from current synthetic to natural distribution shift" — so an IMAGENET-C mCE gain is not a guarantee of robustness to the natural shifts (ObjectNet-style pose/background changes, replicated test sets in the spirit of Recht et al.'s ImageNetV2) a deployed model actually meets. Second, the "don't train on these corruptions" rule is an honor code, not an enforced protocol. The four held-out validation corruptions help catch obvious overfitting to the visible 15, but nothing stops a later method from training with augmentations that resemble — without exactly duplicating — a corruption family (heavy noise/blur augmentation is common practice for unrelated reasons) and reporting an inflated mCE gain; the authors' own later AugMix and DeepAugment work had to be careful to keep this distinction clean specifically because it is so easy to blur.

Third, the single-number "mCE" citation convention hides the paper's own most interesting result: absolute mCE improved (100.0 → 76.7, AlexNet → ResNet-50) purely because clean accuracy improved, while Relative mCE — which isolates the corruption-specific damage — got worse (100.0 → 105.0). None of a decade's architecture progress bought dedicated robustness; a paper that quotes only the absolute mCE trend reads as "architectures got more robust," which is close to backwards. Add to this: no confidence intervals or repeated seeds across the 75-plus-40 corruption/severity conditions tested per architecture, AlexNet as an aging and somewhat arbitrary normalizing reference, and a small baked-in confound the authors themselves flag — IMAGENET-C images are saved as lightly-compressed JPEGs, so every corruption carries a small uncontrolled dose of a 16th, unlisted corruption (JPEG artifacting).

baselines

Reasonable for 2019 — seven architectures spanning AlexNet to ResNet-50, VGG, and SqueezeNet, all trained identically on clean ImageNet only, so the internal comparison is fair. But every reference point (mCE = 100) is pinned to a single, aging architecture (AlexNet), and no confidence intervals or repeated seeds are reported across the full corruption/severity grid.

contamination

Explicitly self-policed, not enforced: "networks should not be trained on IMAGENET-C corruptions." Four held-out validation corruptions (speckle noise, Gaussian blur, spatter, saturate) catch overfitting to the visible 15, but a method trained on similar-but-not-identical augmentations can still report an inflated mCE with no way for a reader to detect it.

what's missed

That absolute mCE improved while Relative mCE got worse (100.0 → 105.0, AlexNet → ResNet-50) — architectures got more accurate, not more corruption-robust in any dedicated sense, a nuance the single-number "mCE" convention regularly drops. Also missed: whether synthetic-corruption gains transfer to the natural distribution shift models actually meet in deployment, and any quantified human-accuracy baseline on IMAGENET-C itself.

would raise confidence

Multiple seeds / confidence intervals per architecture; a rotating or larger private corruption battery to blunt years of community-wide implicit tuning pressure on a fixed public set; a direct, quantified human baseline on IMAGENET-C; and a reported correlation between IMAGENET-C mCE and natural-shift benchmarks (partially supplied afterward by Taori et al., 2020).

// bottom line

IMAGENET-C/P is a genuine landmark: it gave a scattered "robustness to common corruptions" literature a shared, standardized stress test and a normalized metric family (mCE, mFR) the field still reports by default years later. But its own most interesting result is also its most quietly buried one — none of AlexNet-to-ResNet-50's accuracy gains bought dedicated corruption robustness once you normalize for the baseline improvement — and whether these synthetic corruptions predict robustness to the natural shifts models actually face in deployment remains a separate, only partially answered question.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. Two new benchmarks, one new vocabulary. IMAGENET-C (15 corruption types × 5 severities = 75 conditions, plus 4 held-out validation types) and IMAGENET-P (10 perturbation types, 30+ frame sequences) gave vision robustness research a shared, non-adversarial stress test — and the mCE / mFR / mT5D metric family, all normalized against an AlexNet baseline, became the field's default reporting convention.
  2. Architectures got more accurate, not more corruption-robust. Absolute mCE fell from 100.0 (AlexNet) to 76.7 (ResNet-50) as clean accuracy rose — but Relative mCE, which isolates corruption-induced degradation from baseline accuracy, actually rose slightly (100.0 → 105.0). A decade of architecture progress bought essentially zero dedicated robustness to common corruptions.
  3. Some fixes transfer robustness from surprising places, others don't. Stylized-ImageNet training and even a bypassed adversarial defense (Adversarial Logit Pairing) meaningfully improved corruption/perturbation robustness, while intuitive fixes — stability training, image denoising, 10-crop, and smaller pruned CondenseNets — flatly failed or made things worse, undercutting easy assumptions about what "simpler" or "more careful" training should buy you.
