Long-form factuality in large language models

A GPT-4-generated, 38-topic prompt set (LongFact) paired with an LLM-agent grader (SAFE) that splits a response into individual facts and checks each one against live Google Search — and, on the same 16,011 facts humans rated by hand, SAFE agrees with them 72% of the time, wins 76% of the cases where they disagree, and costs 20× less.

Jerry Wei, Chengrun Yang, Xinying Song, Yifeng Lu, Nathan Hu, Jie Huang, Dustin Tran, Daiyi Peng, Ruibo Liu, Da Huang, Cosmo Du, Quoc V. Le · Google DeepMind

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

By early 2024, factuality benchmarking meant short-answer benchmarking. TruthfulQA, HaluEval, FreshQA, and HalluQA all probe a model with a question that has one definite answer — "How old is the world's oldest verified living person?" — and grade a single sentence. FELM mixes short and long responses. Only FActScore (Min et al., 2023) evaluates genuinely long-form output, but it does so on a single domain: Wikipedia-style biographies. None of the existing factuality datasets combine long-form responses (several paragraphs, dozens of individual claims) with broad topical coverage — which matters because that is exactly the shape of a typical open-ended LLM answer in the wild.

The second half of the gap is grading. Short-answer factuality can be scored by comparing a model's output to a preset reference answer. A long-form response has no such fixed target — it's "difficult to compile predetermined answers/knowledge that comprehensively and nonredundantly cover all facts that a model response may contain." Existing automated methods like BLEURT and ROUGE compare against a preset reference and don't transfer to open-ended long-form text, which leaves human annotation as the fallback — expensive, slow, and (as this paper goes on to show empirically) not even particularly accurate.

// how it works

The method

The paper ships three separable pieces. LongFact is the prompt set: GPT-4 (gpt-4-0613) is prompted to generate fact-seeking questions about a specific concept or object within each of 38 manually-selected topics, producing 30 unique prompts per topic per task. Two tasks — LongFact-Concepts and LongFact-Objects — give 1,140 prompts each, 2,280 total, each one designed to solicit a response spanning multiple paragraphs rather than a single sentence.

SAFE (Search-Augmented Factuality Evaluator) is the grader. Given a model's long-form response, an LLM first splits every sentence into individual self-contained facts (replacing pronouns and vague references with the entities they refer to — "it opened in the 20th century" becomes "The Eiffel Tower opened in the 20th century"), then checks whether each fact is even relevant to the prompt. For every relevant fact, the model acts as a search agent: it issues a Google Search query (via the Serper API), reads the results, and either issues another query or reasons to a verdict — supported or not supported — after up to five search steps, keeping three results per query. This is the same fact-level granularity FActScore introduced, but the reference material is live web search rather than a fixed knowledge source.

The third piece is the metric. Prior work scored only precision — the fraction of supported facts — which rewards a model for saying almost nothing (a one-sentence, fully-supported response scores perfectly). The paper's F1@K adds a recall term via a hyperparameter K: a user-chosen target number of supported facts a response should contain. Recall saturates at 1 once a response contains K supported facts, so piling on more true statements past that point no longer helps.

F1@K(y) = 2 · Prec(y) · RK(y)Prec(y) + RK(y)  where  Prec(y) = S(y)S(y) + N(y),  RK(y) = min(S(y)/K, 1) // S(y), N(y) = supported / not-supported fact counts from SAFE; F1@K = 0 when S(y) = 0
// what they found

The results

The headline claim is that SAFE beats the humans it's meant to replace. Against 16,011 individual facts from Min et al. (2023)'s crowdsourced annotations (496 prompt–response pairs), SAFE's verdicts agreed with the human labels 72.0% of the time. On the 28% where they disagreed, the authors manually re-adjudicated a random sample of 100 facts with full internet access as ground truth: SAFE was correct 76% of the time, the original human annotation only 19% (5% neither) — a 4-to-1 win ratio for the automated grader. And it's cheap: rating all 496 responses cost $96.31 in GPT-3.5-Turbo and Serper API calls ($0.19/response) against Min et al.'s reported $4.00/response for human annotation — more than 20× cheaper.

With SAFE validated, the paper benchmarks thirteen models across four families (Gemini, GPT, Claude, PaLM-2) on 250 random LongFact-Objects prompts, decoding up to 1,024 tokens at temperature 0, and reports F1@K at K=64 (the median number of relevant facts across all responses) and K=178 (the maximum). The trend is monotonic with model capability: GPT-4-Turbo tops both settings (95.0% F1@64, 66.4% F1@178); PaLM-2-L-IT trails badly (31.1%, 13.2%).

// does it hold up?

The eval read

The 72%/76%/20×-cheaper numbers are genuinely well-earned — this is one of the more carefully validated LLM-as-judge setups in the literature, with an actual disagreement-adjudication study rather than a bare agreement percentage. But look at who does the adjudicating: the same team that built SAFE also re-labels the 100 disagreement cases used to declare SAFE the winner. There is no independent third party in the loop anywhere in the validation chain. The 13-model leaderboard itself is graded entirely by SAFE with no parallel human-graded run on the same 250 prompts to cross-check against — the validation happened once, on a 496-pair subset borrowed from a different paper's dataset, and the main benchmark numbers are simply trusted to inherit that validation.

The professional-domain check in Appendix C.10 is a useful stress test but a small one: on 6 hand-picked law and medicine questions (133 law facts, 137 medicine facts), SAFE's agreement with a researcher-adjudicated ground truth drops to 79.7% for law versus 94.9% for medicine — a double-digit gap the paper doesn't dig into further, on a sample too small to generalize from. The open-source-model swap in Appendix C.9 is similarly thin: Mixtral 8×7B vs. GPT-3.5-Turbo is compared on just 20 questions (196 differently-rated facts), with GPT-3.5-Turbo winning 2-to-1 — evidence that SAFE's accuracy is sensitive to which LLM backs it, tested on too few examples to bound how sensitive.

There's also an unexamined circularity worth naming: GPT-4 writes the LongFact prompts, and GPT-4 (and GPT-4-Turbo, and GPT-3.5-Turbo, which grades everything via SAFE) are also among the models being scored. The paper never checks whether GPT-4-family responses get systematically easier prompts, or whether SAFE — itself an LLM — has any stylistic bias toward GPT-family phrasing when reasoning about search results. Nothing in the paper suggests this is happening, but nothing rules it out either.

baselines

SAFE is validated once, against one borrowed human-annotation dataset (Min et al., 2023's 496 pairs) — there's no second independent human-graded run on the actual 13-model, 250-prompt benchmark to confirm SAFE's ratings transfer beyond the validation set.

contamination

Not directly addressed. GPT-4 authors the LongFact prompts and is also graded on them via a GPT-family-run SAFE; the paper doesn't test for self-preference or stylistic bias in either direction.

what's missed

SAFE's own disagreement-adjudication ground truth is produced by the paper's own researchers, not a blind third party. The law-vs-medicine agreement gap (79.7% vs 94.9%) is reported but not explained. F1@K is explicitly gameable by repeating a supported fact up to K, and "irrelevant" facts are simply dropped from the precision calculation.

would raise confidence

An independent (non-author) human re-grading of a slice of the actual 13-model benchmark; a larger, blinded professional-domain study; and an explicit test for whether SAFE or LongFact favor GPT-family model outputs.

// bottom line

SAFE is a real methodological advance — fact-level, search-grounded, and honestly benchmarked against a disagreement study most LLM-judge papers skip entirely. But every rung of that validation ladder (prompt generation, grading, and disagreement adjudication) is built and checked by the same team, using the same family of models. The paper earns the "SAFE beats humans" headline on the slice it tested; it asks readers to extend that trust to the full 2,280-prompt, 13-model benchmark on faith.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. LongFact (2,280 GPT-4-generated prompts across 38 topics) is the first factuality prompt set that is both long-form and broad-domain — prior sets were either short-answer (TruthfulQA, HaluEval) or long-form but single-domain (FActScore's biographies).
  2. SAFE, an LLM search agent that decomposes a response into atomic facts and verifies each against Google Search, agrees with human annotators 72.0% of the time and wins 76% of adjudicated disagreements (vs. 19% for the original human labels) — at roughly 1/20th the per-response cost ($0.19 vs. $4.00).
  3. F1@K aggregates SAFE's per-fact verdicts into precision and a recall term that saturates at a user-chosen K supported facts. Across 13 models from four families, bigger/newer models are consistently more factual: GPT-4-Turbo leads at both K=64 (95.0%) and K=178 (66.4%); PaLM-2-L-IT trails at both (31.1%, 13.2%).
