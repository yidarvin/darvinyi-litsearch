NoLiMa: Long-Context Evaluation Beyond Literal Matching

A needle-in-a-haystack benchmark engineered so questions and their needles share almost no vocabulary — forcing models to retrieve via latent association instead of lexical pattern-matching. Eleven of thirteen 128K+-context models fall to half their short-context score by 32K tokens.

Ali Modarressi, Hanieh Deilamsalehy, Franck Dernoncourt, Trung Bui, Ryan Rossi, Seunghyun Yoon, Hinrich Schütze

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

Needle-in-a-Haystack (NIAH) is the default way to check whether a model can actually use the long context it claims to support: bury a fact (the "needle") in irrelevant text (the "haystack"), then ask a question that should retrieve it. The lineage of harder NIAH variants — multi-needle, fact-chaining, added distractors, in-context arithmetic — has pushed scores down, but the authors argue these benchmarks share a quiet flaw: the question and the needle almost always share literal vocabulary, so a model can often locate the needle by matching keywords from the question directly against the haystack text rather than by reasoning about what the question actually means. The same implicit overlap, the authors argue, runs through downstream long-document and multi-document QA benchmarks as well, not just synthetic NIAH-style tasks.

That overlap matters because attention mechanisms are demonstrably good at finding and copying repeated tokens (induction-head-style behavior) — a shortcut that has nothing to do with genuine long-range reasoning. To measure exactly how much surface overlap existing benchmarks leak, the authors compute ROUGE precision between each benchmark's questions and their relevant context. Table 1 in the paper is stark: vanilla NIAH scores R-1/R-2/R-L of 0.905/0.789/0.855, RULER's synthetic retrieval (S-NIAH) scores 0.571/0.461/0.500, and even down­stream tasks like ∞Bench QA hit 0.966/0.545/0.960 — all far above NOLIMA's own 0.069/0.002/0.067. If a model can solve a benchmark partly by literal string matching, near-saturated accuracy tells you little about whether it can actually locate and use information buried deep in a long, lexically dissimilar context — which is exactly the situation a real RAG system or multi-document analysis task creates.

// how it works

The method

NOLIMA keeps the NIAH skeleton — one needle hidden in a long haystack, one question that should retrieve it — but redesigns the needle set so the question and the needle share essentially no surface vocabulary. Each needle links a randomly-assigned character to a fact via a keyword Wn, and the matching question asks about a related keyword Wq that connects to Wn only through world knowledge or commonsense, never through shared words. The paper's own example: the needle "Actually, Yuki lives next to the Semper Opera House" pairs with the question "Which character has been to Dresden?" — the model has to know the Semper Opera House is in Dresden; no token in the question appears in the needle. A harder two-hop variant chains one more inference (e.g., asking about "the state of Saxony" instead of "Dresden," requiring the model to know Dresden is in Saxony), and an inverted-order variant places the character's name after the keyword instead of before it, testing whether the model's causal attention can "backtrace" as easily in that direction.

Haystacks are built from 10 open-license books (each ≥50K tokens), but to blunt memorization the authors don't use continuous chapters — they concatenate short (<250-token) snippets sampled randomly across books until the haystack clears 60K tokens. Before any needle goes in, a two-stage filter cleans the haystack: first, a Contriever embedding pass flags haystack words with high semantic or lexical similarity to the question keywords for manual removal (killing incidental distractors); second, an LLM (Llama 3.3 70B) scans the filtered haystack in ~250-token chunks against every question, flagging any chunk that could plausibly answer it, with a human reviewing every flag — repeated until no further removals happen. This is the pipeline in Figure 1.

As a positive control on the filtering model itself, the authors also place each needle in 100 randomly-selected chunks and check whether Llama 3.3 70B can still recover the answer when it's genuinely present — it scores 99.8% on this check, giving confidence that its "N/A" flags during redaction reflect real absence of an answer rather than the model simply failing to notice one. The final needle set has 58 question-needle pairs across 5 keyword-type groups (each with default/inverted variants, some with one-hop and two-hop items). At each tested context length (250 to 32K tokens, plus a 64K/128K extension for two models in the appendix), every pair is evaluated across 5 random haystacks with the needle placed at 26 evenly-spaced positions — 7,540 tests per length. Because raw accuracy conflates "can the model do this task at all" with "can it do this task at range," the paper defines a base score — the average, across all pairs, of each pair's best score at the three shortest lengths (250/500/1K) — and an effective length: the longest tested context at which accuracy still clears 85% of that model's own base score.

EffLen(m) = max { L : Score(m,L) ≥ 0.85 × BaseScore(m) } // per-model threshold, not a fixed absolute cutoff — adapted from RULER's effective-length idea (Hsieh et al., 2024)
// what they found

The results

Thirteen models were evaluated, every one claiming to support at least 128K tokens. Despite base scores mostly above 85% (12 of 13 models; only Llama 3.1 8B falls below, at 76.7%), effective lengths collapse: GPT-4o is the standout at an 8K effective length (base 99.3%, falling to 69.7% at 32K), but every other model tested lands at 2K or below — Llama 3.3 70B's effective length is 2K despite a 97.3% base score, and it drops to 42.7% accuracy by 32K. Command R+ falls hardest in relative terms: a 90.9% base score collapses to just 7.4% at 32K. Overall, 11 of the 13 models score at or below half their base score by 32K tokens.

The placement-depth analysis adds a mechanistic clue. In the full context sweep, one-hop tasks show the classic "lost-in-the-middle" dip when the needle sits mid-context at 32K — but two-hop tasks instead show the entire performance curve sinking toward zero as context grows, regardless of where the needle sits. To isolate whether this is a position-encoding effect (RoPE) or an attention-capacity effect, the authors re-run the sweep aligning the needle to a fixed relative depth within the last 2K tokens for every context length — so the distance between question and fact is held constant while only total context length changes. Performance still falls as context grows, which rules out position encoding as the explanation and points at the attention mechanism itself struggling once literal cues are gone.

Two further results narrow down the cause. Chain-of-thought prompting helps but doesn't rescue the collapse — Llama 3.3 70B two-hop accuracy at 32K rises from 25.9% (direct) to 34.3% (CoT), a 32.4% relative gain, but that's still far below the one-hop, no-CoT baseline of 56.2%. Even OpenAI's o1 — a reasoning model tested on the hardest 10-pair "NOLIMA-Hard" subset — falls from a 99.9% base score to 31.1% at 32K. And the ablation study confirms the paper's core claim directly: reintroducing literal overlap (a multiple-choice format where the correct character's name appears verbatim among four options) restores most of the lost accuracy — one-hop performance at 32K jumps from 56.2% to 93.1% once the literal cue is back.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

NOLIMA's core move — quantifying literal overlap in existing benchmarks (Table 1) and then engineering it out — is a genuinely useful methodological contribution, not just another harder NIAH variant. It reframes what "near-saturated" scores on RULER or vanilla NIAH actually mean: partly retrieval, partly pattern matching. But the benchmark's narrowness deserves scrutiny before treating its absolute numbers as a general verdict on "long-context ability."

The evaluated model roster (GPT-4o, Gemini 1.5/2.0, Claude 3.5 Sonnet, the Llama 3.x family, Mistral Large 2, Command R+, Jamba 1.5 Mini, plus o1/o3-mini/DeepSeek-R1-Distill as a reasoning add-on) was current for a February 2025 submission, but it's a frozen snapshot — the paper's own footnote concedes that newer models such as GPT-4.1, not covered in the main results, "have improved long-context performance," and defers their numbers to Appendix E rather than the headline Table 3, which means the widely-cited 69.7%-at-32K GPT-4o number is already dated relative to 2026 frontier models. The needle set itself is also small and culturally narrow: 58 templates built around Western literary/geographic commonsense links (opera houses, states, countries) drawn from a pool of 10 English-language books. A model's uneven world knowledge — not its context-length handling — could plausibly explain some of the score variance, and the paper doesn't report a human or open-book ceiling to separate "genuinely too hard for anyone without full attention" from "just an unfamiliar or ambiguous association."

baselines

13 widely-used 128K+-context models circa late 2024/early 2025, current for the paper's window but a frozen snapshot — the paper explicitly relegates newer 2025 models to an appendix rather than the main results table.

contamination

Stronger hygiene than most static long-context sets: haystacks are built from randomly concatenated book snippets rather than continuous chapters, character names are randomized per instance, and a 99.8%-accuracy control test confirms the filtering model reliably recognizes an answer when one is genuinely present in a chunk — evidence its "N/A" redaction flags are trustworthy rather than overzealous.

what's missed

Only single-fact and two-hop retrieval with one canonical name answer — nothing about synthesis, aggregation across many facts, or open-ended generation. The 58-template needle set is a small, book-culture-skewed pool, so a model's world-knowledge gaps can masquerade as context-length failure, and there's no reported human ceiling on the hardest condition.

would raise confidence

A re-run against 2026-era frontier models under the same effective-length metric, a larger and more culturally diverse needle-keyword pool, and a human or open-book upper bound on the two-hop/inverted/32K condition to rule out "just too obscure" as a confound.

// bottom line

NOLIMA is a sharp, well-instrumented demonstration that a large chunk of "solved" long-context retrieval was riding on literal-match shortcuts, not genuine associative recall — the ROUGE comparison in Table 1 alone is a useful diagnostic other benchmark authors should be running. But it's a narrow instrument (single-fact retrieval, small needle pool, one snapshot of models) whose absolute per-model numbers are best read as a relative signal — how much a model degrades once surface cues are removed — rather than a general long-context leaderboard.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. NOLIMA's questions overlap with their needles at a fraction of the rate other long-context benchmarks do (ROUGE-1 precision 0.069 vs. 0.905 for vanilla NIAH, 0.571 for RULER S-NIAH, 0.966 for ∞Bench QA) — quantifying, for the first time, how much "solved" long-context retrieval rides on literal string matching.
  2. Even GPT-4o, the best model tested, falls from a 99.3% base score to 69.7% at 32K tokens, and 11 of 13 models tested — all claiming ≥128K context support — drop to half or less of their base score by that length.
  3. The failure tracks attention capacity, not position encoding: holding the needle's relative depth fixed within the last 2K tokens while varying total context length still produces the same decline, and reintroducing literal cues (multiple-choice with the answer name as an option) recovers most of the lost accuracy (56.2%→93.1% at 32K, one-hop).
