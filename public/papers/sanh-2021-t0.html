Multitask Prompted Training Enables Zero-Shot Task Generalization

An 11-billion-parameter encoder-decoder, fine-tuned on 39 ordinary supervised datasets recast as diverse natural-language prompts, matches or beats GPT-3 175B on most held-out tasks — evidence that zero-shot generalization can be induced by explicit multitask training, not just summoned by scale.

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

GPT-3 showed that a large language model can do reasonable zero-shot generalization — answer a question, summarize a passage — having only ever been trained to predict the next word. The leading explanation, due to Radford et al. (2019), is that this is implicit multitask learning: a web corpus latently contains question-answer pairs, summaries, and the like, so next-word prediction quietly trains the model on many tasks at once. That story has two uncomfortable corollaries. It seems to require enormous scale (175B parameters), and the resulting zero-shot behavior is brittle — small changes in prompt wording swing accuracy wildly.

It also leaves a sharper question unanswered: how implicit is this multitask learning, really? Plenty of web pages are literally lists of trivia questions and answers — that is supervised closed-book-QA data sitting in the pretraining set. Sanh et al. ask the direct version of the hypothesis. If multitask supervision is what drives zero-shot generalization, can you induce it explicitly: take a pile of mundane, already-supervised NLP datasets, convert them into prompts, fine-tune a much smaller model on them, and get it to generalize to whole categories of task it never saw during training? And as a second prize — would deliberately diverse prompts buy the robustness to wording that GPT-3 conspicuously lacks?

// how it works

The method

The recipe is deliberately plain. Start from 62 public NLP datasets grouped into 12 task types. Convert each into natural language through several deliberately diverse prompt templates — an input template plus a target template. An NLI example, for instance, might be rendered as "If {Premise} is true, is it also true that {Hypothesis}?" with the target drawn from yes / maybe / no. To build these at scale, 36 contributors across 24 institutions in 8 countries hand-wrote templates into the Public Pool of Prompts (P3): 2,073 prompts spanning 177 datasets, averaging 11.7 prompts per dataset.

The model is T5+LM — the 11B LM-adapted T5 (Raffel et al., 2020; Lester et al., 2021), an encoder-decoder that, unlike GPT-3, is never trained to generate its own input. It is fine-tuned on the multitask mixture by standard maximum-likelihood. The design choice that turns this into a clean experiment is the held-out split (Figure 1): four entire task types — natural language inference, coreference resolution, word-sense disambiguation, and sentence completion — are pulled out of training and used only for evaluation, so any success is genuine zero-shot generalization to unseen task formats. NLI was picked because humans, too, generalize to it without being explicitly trained. The main model T0 trains on 39 datasets; T0+ adds GPT-3's evaluation sets (49 datasets); T0++ further adds SuperGLUE except RTE and CB (55 datasets).

θ* = argmaxθ  Σ(x,y) ∈ Dtrain  log pθ( y | prompt(x) ) // standard seq2seq likelihood over prompted (input, target) pairs from training tasks only; evaluated zero-shot on held-out task types Teval, with Teval ∩ Ttrain = ∅

Two methodological details keep the evaluation honest. Checkpoint selection uses only the training datasets' validation splits — never any held-out task — so the setup stays "true zero-shot." And rather than report a single prompt, each held-out dataset is scored by the median accuracy across all its prompts (with the interquartile range), which makes robustness to wording a first-class measurement rather than something hidden by a lucky template.

// what they found

The results

The headline: T0 (11B) matches or exceeds every GPT-3 model up to 175B on 9 of 11 held-out datasets, despite being roughly 16× smaller — and it beats GPT-3 on all five NLI sets (RTE, CB, ANLI R1–R3), where the identical-architecture T5+LM baseline, lacking only the prompted training, does not. Against that same-architecture baseline, T0 improves on every held-out dataset, which pins the gain to the prompted training itself rather than to the model or the prompts in isolation. The only two losses are Winogrande and HellaSwag.

On BIG-bench, at least one T0 variant beats all of Google's baseline language models on 13 of 14 tasks (all but StrategyQA), and accuracy generally climbs as more training datasets are added (T0++ > T0+ > T0). On robustness, more prompts per dataset reliably helps: moving from one prompt (p=1) to all original-task prompts (p≈5.7) raises the median on 8 of 11 datasets and tightens the spread on 7 of 11; T0's full mixture (p≈8.03, which adds non-original-task prompts) raises the median on 9 of 11 and tightens spread on 8 of 11. GPT-3, by contrast, is fragile — re-running GPT-3 175B (davinci) on RTE with T0's ten prompts, only the prompt matching Brown et al.'s reproduced near their number, and the other nine collapsed to roughly chance.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

Read narrowly, T0 holds up well: it is one of two concurrent existence proofs (with FLAN) that explicit multitask prompted training induces zero-shot generalization at modest scale, and that claim is reproducible — the T0 checkpoints and P3 are open. The headline framing, "beats GPT-3 175B," deserves three asterisks. First, it is a median-vs-single-prompt comparison: T0 is scored on the median across many prompts, while GPT-3's numbers come from a single prompt that T0's own RTE probe suggests was tuned (the matching prompt is the high outlier at 58.8% while the other nine collapse to a near-random 52.96% median). That cuts for the robustness story but against a clean accuracy claim — the honest version is "T0's median ≈ GPT-3 175B's best prompt, at 16× less scale," still a striking result.

Second, the GPT-3 comparison confounds three axes at once: architecture (encoder-decoder vs decoder-only), pretraining objective (masked LM then LM vs LM only), and pretraining data — so it is not a clean test of "explicit vs implicit multitask." The authors themselves invoke two of these — the encoder-decoder architecture and its masked-LM objective — plus greater prompt diversity to explain why FLAN found multitask training hurt at 8B while T0 found it helped at ≥3B (they do not appeal to differing pretraining data; that confound is mine). Third, "held-out tasks" really means held-out formats: sentence completion was held out partly because it is "too similar to NLI," and training includes paraphrase identification, multiple-choice QA, and the like — so there is genuine skill overlap, and generalization here is to new task wrappers, not to wholly novel competencies. The unevenness is the tell: the two tasks GPT-3 wins (Winogrande, HellaSwag) are exactly the completion-style ones, and the "fix" — deleting the task instructions, which lifts HellaSwag's median from 33.65% to 57.93% — is a post-hoc prompt edit lifted from FLAN — and the gain comes from deleting the instructions, not adding them, an awkward note for the instruction-tuning framing of the moment (the paper itself stays pointedly agnostic about why prompts help, and a T0 author co-wrote the work challenging the prompts-as-instructions premise).

baselines

The same-architecture T5+LM ablation is the strong, correct baseline — it pins the gain to prompted training. The GPT-3 comparison is the weak one: T0's median-across-prompts vs GPT-3's single (likely-tuned) prompt, across confounded architecture, objective, and data.

contamination

Good hygiene — GPT-3's eval datasets are excluded from T0's training and C4 is checked for held-out-task leakage (Appendix E). But T0 inherits T5's C4 pretraining, whose overlap with held-out sets (RTE, COPA, …) is unquantified, and "held-out task" ≠ "held-out skill."

what's missed

Every reported held-out metric is accuracy on classification / multiple-choice; the generative training tasks (summarization, structure-to-text) never receive a zero-shot evaluation at all. Median-over-prompts hides near-random worst-case prompts (e.g. CB's huge spread). More datasets raise the median but do not reliably cut variance. No few-shot comparison, no human baseline.

would raise confidence

Score GPT-3 with the same many-prompt median protocol on every dataset, not just RTE; evaluate the generative tasks zero-shot; disentangle the architecture/objective confound (a decoder-only T0 at matched scale vs FLAN); add few-shot numbers and a direct measure of train↔held-out skill overlap.

// bottom line

T0 is the more carefully controlled half of the 2021 instruction-tuning moment: a single 11B model, trained on ordinary datasets recast as diverse prompts, generalizes zero-shot to held-out task formats well enough to rival a 175B model — a real, reproducible, and hugely influential result, since P3 and the T0 checkpoints seeded much of what followed. Just read the GPT-3 headline as median-vs-best-prompt across confounded models, and "task generalization" as generalization to held-out formats with real skill overlap, not to new abilities.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. Explicit beats implicit, cheaply. Recasting 39 ordinary supervised datasets as diverse prompts and fine-tuning an 11B encoder-decoder induces zero-shot generalization that matches GPT-3 175B on 9 of 11 held-out datasets — you can train the behavior in directly rather than waiting for it to emerge at 175B scale.
  2. Prompt diversity is the active ingredient for robustness. More prompts per dataset (p=1 → 5.7 → 8.03) raise the median and shrink the variance across held-out tasks; GPT-3's single-prompt scores collapse to near-random when the wording changes (on RTE, 9 of 10 prompts ≈ chance). Robustness was measured, not assumed.
  3. The win is to held-out formats, and the comparison is confounded. T0 loses on the completion-style tasks (Winogrande, HellaSwag), the GPT-3 comparison mixes architecture/objective/data, and "beats 175B" is a median-vs-single-prompt reading. T0 + P3's lasting impact is the open recipe and data, not the leaderboard line.
