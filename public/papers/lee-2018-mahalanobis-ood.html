A Simple Unified Framework for Detecting Out-of-Distribution Samples and Adversarial Attacks

A pretrained softmax classifier is secretly already a generative model of its own features. Fit a class-conditional Gaussian to the penultimate layer, score any input by its Mahalanobis distance to the nearest class mean, and one confidence score — no retraining, no architecture change — beats the era's best out-of-distribution detector (ODIN) and its best adversarial-example detector (LID) at the same time.

Kimin Lee, Kibok Lee, Honglak Lee, Jinwoo Shin

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

Deep classifiers answer questions they have no business answering. Feed a CIFAR-10 network an SVHN digit, an unrelated photo, or an adversarially perturbed image, and the softmax layer often still reports a confident class — the paper's framing is blunt: "deep neural networks with the softmax classifier are known to produce highly overconfident posterior distributions even for such abnormal samples." Two separate research threads had each chipped at one half of this problem. For out-of-distribution (OOD) detection, Hendrycks & Gimpel's maximum softmax probability (MSP) baseline turned the classifier's own top softmax value into a free score, and ODIN improved it by temperature-scaling and perturbing the input before thresholding. For adversarial detection, kernel-density estimators and the local intrinsic dimensionality (LID) score characterized how adversarial examples sit differently in feature space. But as the authors point out, "most prior works on this line typically do not evaluate both OOD and adversarial samples… no universal detector is known to work well on both tasks." Worse, some of the strongest OOD detectors (e.g. confidence-calibrated classifiers) require retraining the network from scratch with a special loss, which is a nonstarter for an already-deployed model. The gap: one confidence score that (1) catches both OOD inputs and adversarial examples, (2) attaches to any already-trained softmax classifier with zero retraining, and (3) doesn't need a validation set drawn from the very OOD or attack distribution it will later be tested against.

// how it works

The method

The key move is a change of interpretation rather than architecture. A softmax classifier computes P(y=c|x) from a linear map applied to the penultimate-layer feature f(x). The authors show that if you instead assume f(x) is drawn from a class-conditional Gaussian distribution with a single, tied (shared) covariance across all classes — a generative classifier under Gaussian discriminant analysis (GDA), equivalent to classical linear discriminant analysis (LDA) — the posterior distribution this generative model implies is mathematically identical in form to the softmax classifier's own posterior. In other words: a pretrained softmax network is already quietly consistent with a Gaussian generative model of its features. You just have to go fit that Gaussian.

Fitting it costs one forward pass over the training set: take the empirical per-class mean and a single pooled covariance of the penultimate features,

μ̂c = 1Nc Σi:yᵢ=c f(xᵢ)  ,   Σ̂ = 1N Σc Σi:yᵢ=c(f(xᵢ)−μ̂c)(f(xᵢ)−μ̂c)⊤ // empirical class means + one tied covariance, from a single pass over training features

Any new input x — in-distribution, OOD, or adversarial — is then scored by its Mahalanobis distance to the closest class Gaussian:

M(x) = maxc −(f(x)−μ̂c)⊤ Σ̂−1 (f(x)−μ̂c) // confidence score: log-density under the nearest class-conditional Gaussian

A confidently in-distribution point sits close to some class mean; an abnormal point is far from all of them. Figure 1(a)'s t-SNE plot of CIFAR-10's penultimate features shows the assumption paying off visually — all ten classes separate into clean, near-Gaussian blobs — and (b) confirms that classifying by nearest Gaussian mean rather than the trained softmax head costs essentially no accuracy (94.1% vs. 93.7% on CIFAR-10, Mahalanobis vs. softmax): a generative classifier that, against "conventional beliefs," doesn't sacrifice discriminative performance.

Two calibration tricks turn the raw score into a strong detector. Input pre-processing perturbs the test point in the direction that increases its own Mahalanobis score before scoring it — the mirror image of an adversarial attack (which perturbs to fool the classifier), here perturbing to widen the separation between well-behaved and abnormal inputs. Feature ensembling computes the Mahalanobis score at every intermediate block of the network, not just the final layer — low-level blocks are sometimes the more informative signal for detecting LSUN, TinyImageNet, or DeepFool examples — and combines the per-layer scores αℓMℓ(x) via a lightweight logistic regression whose weights are fit on a small labeled validation set (Algorithm 1, below). Because both calibration steps are just pre/post-processing around a frozen backbone, the whole pipeline needs zero retraining of the original classifier.

The same mean/covariance bookkeeping doubles as a class-incremental learner (Algorithm 2 in the paper): when a new class appears, compute its mean from a handful of examples and fold its samples into the shared covariance — no gradient step on the backbone at all.

// what they found

The results

On CIFAR-10, CIFAR-100, and SVHN with DenseNet-100 and ResNet-34, tested against SVHN, TinyImageNet, and LSUN as OOD sources, the full method (feature ensemble + input pre-processing) beats both the MSP baseline and ODIN on every one of the 18 in-/out-of-distribution pairs in Table 2. The single clearest jump: detecting LSUN as OOD from a DenseNet trained on CIFAR-100, TNR at 95% TPR goes 16.7% (baseline) → 41.2% (ODIN) → 91.4% (Mahalanobis) — more than doubling ODIN's catch rate at the same false-alarm budget. An ablation on CIFAR-10 (ResNet) vs. SVHN (Table 1) shows where the gains come from: the raw Mahalanobis score alone (no calibration) already reaches 93.92 AUROC against the baseline's 89.88, but still trails ODIN's 96.65; adding input pre-processing alone gets to 98.30 AUROC, and the full combination reaches 99.14 AUROC and 96.42 TNR@95 — clearing ODIN's 86.55 TNR@95 by ten points.

Robustness is the more interesting claim. ODIN needs a validation set of actual OOD examples to tune its temperature and perturbation magnitude — "knowledge about OOD samples [that] is not accessible a priori." Re-tuning every method using only in-distribution data plus FGSM adversarial examples (no real OOD data at all), Mahalanobis still beats ODIN validated with real OOD data in several settings — e.g. ODIN's TNR@95 collapses to 39.8% on one DenseNet/SVHN pair while the OOD-blind Mahalanobis version holds at 62.2%. The method also degrades far more gracefully than the baseline or ODIN when training data shrinks to 5K images or is corrupted with up to 40% random labels.

On adversarial detection, the story repeats. Against four attacks — FGSM, BIM, DeepFool, and CW — on CIFAR-10/100/SVHN with DenseNet and ResNet, Mahalanobis outperforms both a kernel-density + predictive-uncertainty (KD+PU) baseline and the prior state of the art, LID, in most cases. The sharpest gain is on the hardest, most optimization-heavy attack: detecting CW on ResNet/CIFAR-10, AUROC goes from 82.2% (LID) to 95.8% (Mahalanobis). A logistic regression tuned only on FGSM — the cheapest attack to generate — generalizes respectably to detect BIM, DeepFool, and CW without ever seeing them during tuning, evidence the score is picking up something structural about "off-manifold-ness" rather than overfitting to one attack's artifacts.

Class-incremental learning is a bonus capability rather than the headline result: streaming in new classes one at a time (half of CIFAR-100 as base classes / the other half streamed in, or all of CIFAR-100 as base / 100 ImageNet classes streamed in), the Mahalanobis classifier reaches 40.0% (22.1%) AUC on the two respective splits after all new classes arrive, versus 32.7% (15.6%) for a fine-tuned softmax head and 32.9% (17.1%) for a Euclidean-distance classifier — all without a single gradient update to the backbone.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

The comparisons are fair for their moment: MSP and ODIN were the standing OOD detectors, KD+PU and LID the standing adversarial detectors, and the paper beats each on its own turf using the same architectures (DenseNet-100, ResNet-34) those baselines were themselves tested with. But two things are worth flagging about what the near-perfect scores are actually measuring. First, every OOD pair tested is far-OOD: SVHN digits, LSUN scenes, and TinyImageNet objects are all visually and semantically distant from CIFAR-10/100 natural-object photos, which is exactly the regime where a distance-in-feature-space score is easiest to make work (AUROC and TNR both push into the high-90s and even 100 in the SVHN-as-in-distribution rows). The paper never tests a near-OOD split — e.g., CIFAR-10 vs. CIFAR-100, or a held-out subset of semantically similar classes — where later benchmarks would show Mahalanobis-style feature-distance scores fare noticeably worse than they do here. Second, the "hyperparameter-free" framing is only partially earned: the paper's own robustness check still needs FGSM-generated adversarial examples as a stand-in validation signal when no OOD data is available — it removes the need for OOD validation data specifically, not for any held-out tuning signal at all, and the full method's headline numbers (Table 1's 99.14 AUROC) still come from a logistic regression fit on 1,000-image-per-pair validation sets, a mild form of tuning to the test distribution that a fully deployed detector might not get to see in advance.

baselines

MSP, ODIN (OOD) and KD+PU, LID (adversarial) were the correct, current baselines for 2018 and are run on the same architectures; fair fight for its era.

contamination

No train/test leakage in the classic sense, but every OOD pair tested is far-OOD (SVHN/LSUN/TinyImageNet vs. CIFAR) — an easier regime than the near-OOD splits (e.g. CIFAR-10 vs. CIFAR-100) the paper doesn't test.

what's missed

A single tied covariance shared across all classes (the LDA assumption) is a strong simplification; no near-OOD benchmark; the logistic-regression ensemble weights still need a labeled validation set even in the "OOD-free" tuning variant.

would raise confidence

Near-OOD evaluation splits, a fully label-free/threshold-only variant with no validation tuning at all, and reporting the per-layer covariance-inversion cost as feature dimensionality scales past ResNet/DenseNet-sized backbones.

// bottom line

A clean, retraining-free reframing of the softmax classifier as a hidden Gaussian generative model that comprehensively beat 2018-era OOD and adversarial baselines on far-OOD detection — but its near-100 AUROC scores describe the easy end of the OOD spectrum, its ensemble weights still lean on a labeled validation set, and within two years energy-based scores (Liu et al., 2020) would show much of this coverage was reachable with an even simpler, training-free statistic.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. A pretrained softmax classifier already implicitly defines a class-conditional Gaussian generative model over its own penultimate-layer features — Gaussian discriminant analysis with a tied covariance reproduces the identical posterior — so a Mahalanobis confidence score can be extracted with zero retraining.
  2. One score, two jobs: the same Mahalanobis distance detects both out-of-distribution inputs and adversarial examples, closing a gap where prior detectors (MSP, ODIN for OOD; KD+PU, LID for adversarial examples) were each only ever evaluated on one task.
  3. Feature ensembling across every network layer plus a confidence-increasing input perturbation lift the raw score from ~90 AUROC to 99+, and the identical mean/covariance bookkeeping extends for free to class-incremental learning — though the near-perfect numbers are all on far-OOD pairs, not the harder near-OOD splits later work would use to stress-test this family of methods.
