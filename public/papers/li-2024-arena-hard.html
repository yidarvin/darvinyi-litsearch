From Crowdsourced Data to High-Quality Benchmarks: Arena-Hard and BenchBuilder Pipeline

An automated pipeline that mines crowdsourced chat logs for the hardest open-ended prompts and grades answers with an LLM judge — turning 200K raw Chatbot Arena queries into Arena-Hard-Auto, a 500-prompt benchmark that separates frontier models better than Chatbot Arena itself and tracks its human rankings at up to 98.6% correlation, for about $20 a model.

Tianle Li*, Wei-Lin Chiang*, Evan Frick, Lisa Dunlap, Tianhao Wu, Banghua Zhu, Joseph E. Gonzalez, Ion Stoica · UC Berkeley (LMSYS)

What problem is this solving?

LLM capability has outpaced LLM evaluation. The static, ground-truth benchmarks the field grew up on — MMLU, MATH, GSM-8K, SQuAD, HellaSwag — are saturating: today's frontier models cluster near the ceiling, so the benchmark can no longer tell them apart, and because the questions are fixed and public, they steadily leak into training data and lose validity over time.

The obvious fix — pay domain experts to write fresh, hard questions — works but does not scale. The authors note that GPQA cost over $120,000 to curate 500 graduate-level multiple-choice questions, and being static it is still vulnerable to eventual contamination. Worse, these benchmarks are close-ended (multiple choice, exact-match), which fails to capture the open-ended, free-form way people actually use chatbots.

At the other extreme, Chatbot Arena crowdsources a never-ending stream of real, open-ended prompts and collects live human pairwise votes. It is fresh and human-aligned, but real-time human evaluation is slow and expensive — the reference ranking rests on over 1 million votes — so a model developer cannot run it on every checkpoint. And raw crowdsourced prompts vary enormously in quality: most are trivial ("hi", "write me a flirty text"), so the stream cannot be used as a benchmark without aggressive filtering.

The missing piece is an automated pipeline that continuously distills a cheap, challenging, open-ended benchmark out of crowdsourced data — one that both separates models with statistical confidence and agrees with human preference — with no human in the loop. That is the gap BenchBuilder targets.

// how it works

The method

The paper has two halves: a set of metrics for judging a benchmark, and BenchBuilder, the pipeline that builds one.

Two properties, three new metrics

A benchmark that approximates human preference should have two properties: separability (it should distinguish models with high confidence) and alignment (it should agree with human preference). Prior work fixated on alignment; the authors argue separability is just as important when comparing models of similar quality — e.g. two checkpoints from the same run. They show the usual Spearman/Pearson correlations are too coarse to capture either ranking variance or the magnitude of a gap, and propose three replacements:

  • Separability with Confidence — bootstrap each model's score, then report the percentage of model pairs whose 95% confidence intervals do not overlap. Higher means the benchmark confidently tells more pairs apart.
  • Agreement with Confidence Interval — for each model pair, score +1 if the benchmark and the human reference both confidently separate the pair and agree on the winner, −1 if they confidently disagree, and 0 if either cannot separate the pair. Average over all pairs.
  • Pair Rank Brier Score — rewards confident correct predictions and penalizes confident wrong ones, so a benchmark that says P(π₁ > π₂) = 0.90 is scored differently from one that says 0.60 even when both pick the same winner. This is exactly the calibration that Spearman is blind to.

BenchBuilder: from logs to a benchmark

The core idea is simple: score every prompt for quality, keep the hardest, and sample evenly across topics. An LLM annotator rates each prompt against seven quality indicators — specificity, domain knowledge, complexity, problem-solving, creativity, technical accuracy, and real-world application — and the prompt's quality score is simply how many of the seven it satisfies.

quality(p) = Σi=1..7 1[ qualityi present in p ] ∈ {0, …, 7} // an LLM annotator (GPT-4-Turbo) counts how many of the 7 indicators a prompt satisfies

To guarantee topical coverage, prompts are embedded with OpenAI's text-embedding-3-small, reduced with UMAP, and clustered with HDBSCAN via BERTopic. Low-scoring clusters (dominated by trivial prompts) are dropped, and the survivors are sampled evenly. Applied to Arena-Hard-Auto, the recipe is concrete: start from 200,000 Chatbot Arena prompts, strip duplicates / multi-turn / non-English, cluster into ~4,000 topics, score with GPT-4-Turbo, discard any prompt scoring below 6 and any cluster averaging below 5, then sample 2 prompts from each of 250 clusters → 500 prompts. The same pipeline run on 150K WildChat queries yields Wild-Hard-Auto.

Evaluation: LLM-as-a-Judge against a baseline

A model under test is compared pairwise against a strong baseline (GPT-4-0314). A judge model (default GPT-4-Turbo) reads both answers and rates its preference on a 5-point Likert scale, using chain-of-thought (the judge solves the problem itself first). To kill position bias the comparison is run twice with the answers swapped — a two-game setup, 1000 judgments per model. The pairwise outcomes are aggregated with a Bradley-Terry model and bootstrapped to produce a win-rate against the baseline with a confidence interval. Building the benchmark costs ~$500 with a GPT-4-Turbo annotator (or ~$45 with Llama-3-70B); evaluating a model costs about $20. The annotator itself was validated at 85.6% agreement with majority-vote labels from GPT-4o, Claude-3-Opus, and Gemini-1.5-Pro on 200 queries.

The results

Against a top-20 set of models (Chatbot Arena, 2024/04/13) with the Arena English ranking as reference, Arena-Hard-Auto leads on every metric. It separates 87.4% of model pairs with confidence — edging out even Chatbot Arena's own 85.8%, which is built from over a million human votes — at 90.9% confidence agreement with the human ranking, all for $20.

The most instructive number is MT-Bench's: a respectable 89.9% Spearman correlation hiding a 22.6% confidence agreement. Spearman rewards getting the rough order right while being blind to the fact that MT-Bench cannot confidently separate top models at all — precisely the failure the new metrics were designed to expose. Figure 3 shows the same story visually: against MT-Bench, Arena-Hard-Auto's per-model confidence intervals are dramatically tighter.

Three further results stand out:

  • Style control closes the gap to humans. When both the human ranking and the judge are decoupled from response length and markdown, style-controlled Arena-Hard-Auto reaches 98.6% confidence agreement and 98.6% Spearman against the Chatbot Arena English Hard-Prompt ranking. It also neutralizes gaming: instructing Llama-3.1-70B to answer in more "detail" lifts its raw score from 44.5 → 53.5, but under style control that advantage vanishes (it drops to 41.7).
  • The pipeline generalizes. Run on WildChat, Wild-Hard-Auto scores 88.6% confidence agreement versus just 36.4% for 250 randomly-sampled WildChat prompts — and swapping the GPT-4-Turbo annotator for Llama-3-70B gives a similar lift. The quality score is doing the work, not the specific source or annotator.
  • Judges differ, and an ensemble wins. As a judge, GPT-4-Turbo (90.9%) and Gemini-1.5-Pro (84.8%) agree with humans far better than Claude-3-Opus (66.7%) or Llama-3-70B (65.6%). Ensembling GPT-4-Turbo + Gemini-1.5-Pro reaches 91.5% agreement and the best Brier (0.065), while reducing the judge's self-bias.
// does it hold up?

The eval read

BenchBuilder is a genuinely useful recipe, and the separability metrics are a real contribution. But the headline framing deserves scrutiny, because the benchmark's validity is more circular and more judge-dependent than the abstract lets on.

The 98.6% headline is the friendliest of several numbers. That figure is the style-controlled agreement measured against the Chatbot Arena English Hard-Prompt subset — a reference deliberately chosen to be in-distribution with Arena-Hard-Auto's own hard prompts. The general-purpose number, against the full top-20 Arena ranking in Table 1, is 90.9% confidence agreement / 93.2% Spearman. Both are real and both are strong; the abstract simply leads with the most flattering.

Construct validity is partly circular by design. Arena-Hard-Auto's prompts are mined from Chatbot Arena and validated against the Chatbot Arena ranking. So a high correlation substantially measures how well a Chatbot-Arena-derived set predicts Chatbot Arena. That is exactly the stated goal — a cheap proxy for the Arena leaderboard — but it means the benchmark is an excellent predictor of one specific human-preference signal, not an independent oracle of model quality.

The "3×" separation is measured against the weakest baseline. The 87.4% vs 22.6% gap that motivates "3× more separable" is against MT-Bench, an 80-question, 160-prompt benchmark from 2023. The fair contemporary, AlpacaEval 2.0 LC, sits at 83.2% separability and 82.5% confidence agreement — close enough that the real margin over a strong baseline is single digits, not a multiple.

A single judge defines "quality" and grades the answers — and it has a measurable house bias. GPT-4-Turbo selects the prompts (the 7 qualities) and is the default judge, and the paper's own Appendix Table 10 shows GPT models ranking above human preference while Claude models rank below. The released leaderboard uses that single GPT-4-Turbo judge, so OpenAI models get a structural edge; Ensemble-as-Judges fixes much of it but is not the default. The "85.6% ground-truth" annotator check is itself a majority vote of three LLMs, so "quality" is LLM-defined end to end.

baselines

The headline "3× separation" is anchored to MT-Bench (22.6% separability), a weak 2023 set. The fair modern baseline, AlpacaEval 2.0 LC, trails by only single digits (83.2% vs 87.4% separability; 82.5% vs 90.9% confidence agreement).

contamination

The released Arena-Hard-Auto is a frozen 500-prompt, Arena-sourced set — it can be trained on and saturates over time. "Freshness" is a property of re-running BenchBuilder, not of the static artifact people download.

what's missed

English-only and single-turn (acknowledged); the 7 qualities skew technical, under-weighting creative/conversational use. The default GPT-4-Turbo judge self-favors OpenAI models (App. Table 10). The 98.6% headline is the in-distribution, style-controlled case — the general number is 90.9%.

would raise confidence

An independent (non-Arena) human-preference reference; Ensemble-as-Judges shipped as the default; periodic re-curation with contamination checks; and multi-turn / multilingual coverage.

// bottom line

Treat Arena-Hard-Auto as what it provably is: a $20, 87.4%-separability, open-ended benchmark that tracks the Chatbot Arena leaderboard closely — best used with style control on and, ideally, an ensemble judge. It is a fast, faithful proxy for Arena rankings, not an independent ground truth, and the default single-judge setup bakes in a small but real preference for the judge's own family.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. BenchBuilder automates benchmark curation. Cluster crowdsourced chat logs, score each prompt on 7 quality indicators with an LLM annotator, keep the hardest clusters, and sample. Applied to 200K Chatbot Arena prompts it yields Arena-Hard-Auto (500 prompts) for ~$500 to build and ~$20 to run per model — versus $120K of expert labor for a GPQA-style set.
  2. Separability belongs next to alignment. Confidence-aware metrics reveal that MT-Bench's respectable 89.9% Spearman hides a 22.6% confidence agreement — it cannot actually tell top models apart. Arena-Hard-Auto confidently separates 87.4% of model pairs, narrowly beating Chatbot Arena's 85.8% built from 1M+ human votes.
  3. Cheap automation carries an asterisk. The prompts are mined from Chatbot Arena and validated against it, and a single GPT-4-Turbo judge selects the prompts, grades the answers, and mildly favors OpenAI models. It is a faithful proxy for the Arena leaderboard — turn on style control, prefer an ensemble judge — not an independent measure of quality.
