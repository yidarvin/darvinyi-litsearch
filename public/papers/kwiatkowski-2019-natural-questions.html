Natural Questions: A Benchmark for Question Answering Research

307,373 real anonymized Google search queries, each paired with a Wikipedia page and a human-annotated long answer + short answer — the QA benchmark that traded back-written passage questions for genuine information-seeking, and built an achievable human upper bound to measure machines against.

// the gap

What problem is this solving?

By 2018 the reading-comprehension benchmarks driving NLP — SQuAD, SQuAD 2.0, NarrativeQA, HotpotQA, TriviaQA — shared a structural flaw: their questions were written by annotators who had already read the answer passage. That back-to-front authoring leaks artifacts. Questions borrow vocabulary from their evidence paragraph, so models can win on lexical overlap rather than understanding; Jia and Liang (2017) showed SQuAD systems could be flipped by inserting a single distractor sentence that shouldn't change the answer.

SQuAD 2.0 tried to harden the task with unanswerable questions, but those were designed to be unanswerable and can be flagged with little reasoning — a different skill from deciding whether a real paragraph actually contains all the evidence a genuine question needs. And by this point systems already outperformed humans on SQuAD; the field needed a harder, more realistic target.

The deeper gap was methodological. A QA benchmark is only as good as its questions, its answer annotations, its quality controls, and — crucially — its evaluation metrics and human upper bound. HotpotQA, for instance, reported a human ceiling computed by picking, per example, the partition of annotations that maximized the score — an upper bound that isn't representative of the eval distribution and may not be achievable. Natural Questions sets out to fix the whole pipeline at once: get naturally occurring information-seeking questions at scale, annotate them over full documents (allowing "no answer"), and define metrics with a human upper bound that a model could in principle reach.

// how it works

The method

Every NQ example starts from a real, anonymized, aggregated query issued to Google search. Simple heuristics filter the query stream down to information-seeking questions (e.g., requiring ≥8 words). For each question, a search is run; if a Wikipedia page appears in the top 5 results, the top such page is paired with the question, otherwise the question is discarded. So the input a model ultimately sees is a question + an entire Wikipedia page — close to an end-to-end QA application, not a hand-curated paragraph.

An annotator then produces two labels. The long answer is the earliest HTML bounding box — a paragraph, table, or list — that contains all the information needed to infer the answer, or NULL if the page doesn't answer the question. The short answer is the entity or set of entities within the long answer that actually answer the question, or a yes/no boolean, or NULL. If the long answer is NULL, the short answer is necessarily NULL.

Crucially, NQ treats "should I answer at all?" as part of the task. Across the corpus, annotators mark a long answer for 49% of examples and a short-answer span or yes/no for 36%; the remaining 51% with no labeled answer are kept, not discarded. Long answers are mostly paragraphs (73%), with the rest tables (19%), lists (3%), list items (3%), and table rows (1%). The annotation funnel below shows the path proportions on the training data.

The public release is large: 307,373 training examples with a single annotation each, 7,830 development and 7,842 test examples each with 5-way annotations, plus a focused study of 25-way annotations on 302 examples to probe human variability. Why 5-way for evaluation? Because naturally occurring questions are genuinely ambiguous: there is often real disagreement about whether a page answers a question, and several acceptable answers can coexist. Single-reference scoring would penalize a correct system for picking a different valid answer.

Measuring annotation quality

To audit precision, the authors sampled non-null answers and had four experts (the guideline authors) independently grade each into Correct (C), Correct-but-debatable (Cd), or Wrong (W), then meet to reach a consensus judgment. Annotation precision is the fraction of sampled non-null answers that are essentially correct (C + Cd):

A subtle metric idea: super-human is allowed

NQ also makes a sharp theoretical point about evaluation. If you score a system against a single held-out human annotation, a model that learns a good approximation of the answer distribution p(answer | question, document) can legitimately beat a single annotator — the Bayes-optimal prediction outperforms one random draw from the same distribution. The authors confirm this empirically by approximating the distribution from 20-way annotations and outscoring a single annotator, then operationalize it as a "super-annotator" upper bound built from the 25-way data. That ceiling is achievable and computed with the evaluation distribution held fixed — directly addressing HotpotQA's non-representative bound.

// what they found

The results

The headline is a large human–machine gap. On long-answer selection, the best baseline — a custom DecAtt + Document-Reader pipeline — reaches 54.8 F1 on dev (55.0 test), versus a single annotator at 73.4 F1 (80.4 precision / 67.6 recall) and a super-annotator at 87.2 F1 (90.0 / 84.6). On the harder short-answer task the best system (Document-QA) manages only 35.7 F1, against a single annotator at 57.5 and the super-annotator at 75.7. So machines trailed a single human by ~18 F1 (long) and ~22 F1 (short), and the achievable ceiling by ~32 and ~40 F1.

The annotation audit holds up well: precision is 90% for long answers and 84% for short answers. And the 25-way study validates the "variability is signal, not noise" thesis. When more than 80% of the 25 annotators give some non-null long answer, expert consensus judges those answers overwhelmingly Correct; when fewer than 20% do, they tend to be wrong. In the murky middle, the disagreement is largely the Cd "debatable" category — exactly the cases where reasonable people could be satisfied or want more. Inter-annotator agreement, in other words, tracks correctness.

The untrained baselines are revealing in their own right. Simply returning a page's first paragraph scores 27.8 long-answer F1, and two "copying" baselines — return the most frequent training annotation for a page, or the answer to the most similar training question — score 27.3 and 32.4. They work because 79% of the Wikipedia pages in dev also appear in training. That single statistic foreshadows the benchmark's main caveat.

// does it hold up?

The eval read

NQ's dataset design has aged extremely well; its difficulty claim was a moving target. The much-cited "large gap requiring significant advances in NLU" was measured against pre-BERT baselines — Document-QA (Clark & Gardner, 2018), Decomposable Attention (Parikh et al., 2016), and a DrQA-style reader (Chen et al., 2017) — which the paper itself calls state-of-the-art at the time of submission. It also points readers to Alberti et al. (2019) "for more recent advances in modeling," a BERT baseline that is a reference of this very paper. NQ, written in early 2019, makes no claim about how quickly its gap would close; but in hindsight BERT-era models climbed the NQ leaderboard fast, so the headline gap is best read as a snapshot of early-2019 modeling, not an intrinsic wall.

The leakage caveat is real and disclosed by the authors: with 79% of dev Wikipedia pages also in training and a question-level (not document-level) split, trivial copy baselines reach 27–32 long-answer F1. A model can exploit page-level regularities instead of reading. The honest framing is that NQ rewards a mix of comprehension and memorization of Wikipedia structure, and the headline numbers bake that in.

Finally, a single F1 hides genuine ambiguity. 51% of examples have no labeled answer, the single-annotator ceiling is itself only 73.4 F1 (humans disagree with each other a lot), and boolean yes/no answers are excluded from all baselines ("we leave boolean answers to future work"). The super-annotator upper bound is a genuine methodological advance — achievable and distribution-fixed — but it rests on just 302 examples with 25-way labels and a tuned threshold (α = 8).

baselines

Pre-BERT (Document-QA / DecAtt / DrQA); best long-answer F1 ≈ 55 vs. 73.4 for a single human. The paper calls these state-of-the-art at submission and points readers to Alberti et al. (2019)'s BERT baseline "for more recent advances"; in hindsight BERT-era models narrowed this gap quickly — so the headline number reflects pre-BERT modeling, not an intrinsic ceiling.

contamination

79% of dev Wikipedia pages also appear in training; a question-level split lets "closest question" / "most frequent" copy baselines hit 32.4 / 27.3 long-answer F1 with no reading at all.

what's missed

Boolean answers omitted from every baseline; a single F1 obscures real answer ambiguity (51% NULL, soft 73.4-F1 human ceiling). Long-answer selection is closer to NLI/evidence retrieval than extraction, but is scored with one number.

would raise confidence

A strong contemporary baseline (BERT/large LM) to recalibrate the gap; document-disjoint splits to kill page-level leakage; per-answer-type breakdowns; and releasing the 25-way data so the super-annotator bound is reproducible.

// bottom line

Natural Questions moved QA evaluation from back-written paragraph puzzles to real information-seeking queries over full documents, and contributed an achievable human upper bound — its most durable ideas. The famous human–machine gap was real but measured against pre-BERT models; in hindsight — a point the paper, writing in early 2019, could not make — BERT-era models narrowed it quickly. And NQ's own question-level splits over overlapping Wikipedia pages mean the headline F1 should be read as a starting point, not a verdict. As a dataset it became foundational; as a difficulty statement it had a short half-life.

// takeaways

What to remember

  1. NQ replaced annotator-authored passage questions with 307,373 real anonymized Google queries, each paired with a full Wikipedia page and a human long + short answer — the first large QA benchmark where the question is independent of the evidence, and where deciding not to answer (the ~51% NULL cases) is part of the task.
  2. Its lasting methodological gift is the achievable, distribution-fixed human upper bound (super-annotator: 87.2 long / 75.7 short F1) plus the insight that a model approximating p(answer | question, document) can legitimately exceed a single annotator — reframing what "human-level" means for ambiguous QA.
  3. The headline gap (best baseline ≈55 vs. 73.4 single-human long-answer F1) was set against pre-BERT baselines the paper calls state-of-the-art at submission while pointing to a newer BERT model (Alberti et al., 2019) for "more recent advances," and sits on top of 79% train/dev Wikipedia-page overlap — so treat the leaderboard with care, but recognize that NQ-the-dataset became a backbone of open-domain QA and retrieval (DPR, RAG, UnifiedQA, Contriever, and the BEIR retrieval lineage all build on it).
